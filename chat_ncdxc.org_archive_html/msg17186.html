Enphase micro inverters sold in the US are now built in China.


FWIW, I too have EnPhase micro inverters on my solar system, and notice no increase in local noise with the system on or off. 

I too was interested in verifying that there wouldn’t be an EMI issue from the panels once they were installed so, at my request, the company I worked with (Clean Solar) arranged for me to survey another customer’s installation prior to installing mine.  (The customer wasn’t a ham, but knew what ham radio was, and was actually interested in what I was doing.)  I used a KX3 and tunable whip with a wire counterpoise (not the greatest antenna, but adequate for the task). I didn’t detect any rise in noise level on the KX3, even with the whip within a meter of the panels. I tested on 80 through 10 meters, and multiple frequencies on each band.  I also walked around his neighborhood a bit. I found other sources of noise, but his panels were quiet. 

I’ve had my system for about 1-1/2 years, and have had no issues. My Hexbeam and 30/40m dipoles sit roughly 25 below and no more than 10 feet horizontally from one end of my panels. 

The EnPhase inverters are German made and so have to pass the tougher EMI specs that are imposed in Europe. I don’t know if that’s why they are as quiet as they are, but I suspect it doesn’t hurt.

73,
 Frank N6OI

And one more dot on the graph:   I have Enphase micro-inverters (18 of them) without any ferrites. I do hear them on the high bands as very broad QRN, but it raises the background noise from S0 or S1 to maybe S2 at most. Checking with a loop and battery HF radio, my main QRN source is NOT the solar panels, but rather my AC power entry to the house (different noise than the microinverters). If I shut off the PV system it's still there. Fortunately my antennas are about 150 feet away and usually aimed away from the house. So S0 stays S0 most of the time. I have to aim at the house to hear noise. Can't complain.

> On May 19, 2019, at 10:16 AM, Steve Dyer via Groups.Io <w1srd=yahoo.com@groups.io> wrote:
>
> I've been shopping for a radio silent solar system and wanted to quickly share a few recent findings.
>
> All of this is going to be relative to the level of noise you already have and the proximity of your receive antenna to any RFI generating part of the system.
>
> And to be sure, even the latest Solar gear generates RFI, some of it a lot.
>
> I was fortunate enough to find a local solar installer that would take me around to installations of the equipment he was proposing to verify noise which we did this past Friday. With a mini W6LVP loop and a KX3 running on battery we listened to:
>
> Sonnen Eco 3 battery system - extremely quiet, barely detectable with the antenna pressed against the chassis. Tested in both charge and discharge modes. All the noise in that install was coming from an ethernet over powerline system plugged in next to the Sonnen. Threw me for a few minutes until I saw it!
>
> Solar Edge inverter with optimizers - extremely noisy from both inverter and optimizers. I can get the exact model numbers if anyone cares, but the install is less than a year old. It was so noisy we left immediately after doing the shutdown to confirm it was the solar system. Coincidentally this was the installers personal home and the system he'd originally spec'd for me :-0.
>
> enPhase IQ7+ micro-inverter system (with ferrites) - detectable but not radio silent. This system was installed on a hams garage and he had the installer put 1 clamp on ferrite on each micro-inveter as well as put the AC output into the mains through multiple turns of mix 77. Not sure how much good the single small ferrites actually did, but with the test antenna within a few feet of a panel it was just a slight buzz on 80m. Since the op primarily operates at night, it's not a huge concern and his antenna is 100' from the panels so it is good enough for his needs.
>
> My solution is none of the above (except the battery). The best answer is still the old school string inverter with no electronics in the air. I'll run DC down to an SMA Sunnyboy inverter which is well known for being quiet and built like a tank. Also made in Germany.
>
> My methods were not particularly scientific, but it quickly confirmed my suspicion that putting active devices on the roof directly under the beams is a bad idea...
>
> After the fact I found this fantastic blog http://ka7oei.blogspot.com/2016/03/the-solar-saga-part-1-avoiding.html
>
> Perhaps I say fantastic because he came to the same conclusion 3 years ago, but the logic holds.
>
> Hope this helps anyone shopping.
