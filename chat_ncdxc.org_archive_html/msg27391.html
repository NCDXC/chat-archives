The ARRL didn't always approve contacts made via satellites for DXCC. All of the satellites prior to OSCAR-10 were in low earth orbit. While you could work some stations outside of North America, making 100 DX contacts was a pipe dream even if you worked just the horizon passes. When OSCAR-10 was launched, amateur radio finally had its first satellite in a high orbit that allowed some real DX contacts to be made. Memory fails me here, but I don't recall if DXCC for satellites was finally approved for OSCAR-10 or if we had to wait for OSCAR-13, another satellite in high earth orbits. Operators with a license that had VHF or UHF privileges were allowed to use the satellite. Both OSCAR-10 and OSCAR-13 allowed anyone in the world to work  DX for sometimes hours at a time. Some members of the NCDXC have DXCC on satellites; I believe K6KLY is one. I don't have DXCC on satellites, because I was too busy following telemetry and kept putting off working stations since I figured OSCAR-40 would be the next BIG satellite. OSCAR-40 is yet another story. 

To make a long story short, there was always discussion about a geosynchronous satellite, but the problem with them is they only allow one third of the world to be covered at one time. Whereas the orbits for OSCAR-10 and OSCAR-13 moved around so they provided coverage anywhere in the world. The AMSAT organizations kept dreaming about worldwide coverage, and there was discussion about ways to link the geosynchronous satellites. Unfortunately, the major problem was how to get a geosynchronous satellite launched. It was going to cost lots of money. Until "somebody" cold provide the funds for a geosynchronous satellite, that dream was going to be a ways off. I believe it was one of the middle eastern government that finally agreed to provide room for an amateur radio transponder to be included on their geosynchronous satellites. That became OSCAR-100 which is the satellite the station on Crozet is using. While that provided coverage over that "third of the globe," an offer to provide a geosynchronous satellite for the other two thirds has not been provided, yet. 

Only time and money will tell if that dream will happen.
