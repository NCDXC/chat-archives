David,

The grounding looks incomplete.  It MUST be connected to the house safety ground, with other grounds rods along that path (every 2X the depth of the ground rod).  Ditto every entry point of any wired service (cable tv, phone, dsl, satellite, etc).  Only then will you have the basics of lightning protection (and meet code).  Ideally a loop around the house (often not practical with asphalt, concrete, the XYL's begonias) with ample ground rods.  Keep that energy OUTSIDE.

On EACH wire on your aluminum plate (other metals would be a better choice, brass, bronze, copper), a lightning protection device (i.e. Polyphaser) must also be in place, to help shed static energy to ground.  It's not just lightning that is a concern, blowing wind, dust, snow also causes static, some of those charges can get seriously high in voltage.  Yes, including antenna control wires (SteppIR) and rotor cabling (EACH wire).

EVERY piece inside the shack also need to be bonded to ground (not in daisy chain, but directly).  Bring everything to a common ground, if you take a hit the potential rises equally.  ANY inequality will cause damage.

I would suggest getting and carefully studying the ARRL book, "Grounding and Bonding"; it goes into careful detail and is totally worth the cost.  There is also a Motorola guide (available online) that is very intensive and treated as a bible.

A bonus can happen in other areas as well; my noise floor FELL an average of 50 dB on each band once I grounded and bonded the station.  That's a lot of cotton removed from the ears!  It also proved (to me) how poor the house safety ground really was.  (I also use the copper pipe method, clean and simple.)
