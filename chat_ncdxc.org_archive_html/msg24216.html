The “Classic Exchange” is an event encouraging people to put those old boat-anchor radios on the air. It’s not a “contest” per se, but points are allocated to contacts equal to the sum of the ages of the transmitter and receiver. It’s very low-key, and short ragchews are commonplace. The event runs from 7 AM to 1 AM on Sunday Oct 24/Monday Oct 25, and again from 7 AM to 1 AM on Monday Oct 25/Tuesday Oct 26.

The main reason for posting this is to let folks know that I will be putting my 1952 Harvey-Wells Bandmaster on the air for at least a few hours during this event. I will be on CW only, 80-15 meters, depending on activity and conditions. No need to inform me that this transmitter has a terrible “whoop” (a chirp with increasing frequency), bad key clicks (even with a huge filter in the line), and other characteristics not present in most modern gear; I already know this! The event is scheduled for this time period in part because it does NOT conflict with any major contest. While the output of this transmitter is not the cleanest, I don’t believe it generates illegal spurious out-of-band transmissions, and is (at least marginally) legal.

Please DO inform me if I am inadvertently interfering with a DXpedition (like the 7P8 that just went active), or if I am interfering with some other activity that YOU are engaged with, e.g. a SOTA operation, the K1USN Slow Speed Test, etc. I shouldn’t affect Stew Perry, as the Harvey-Wells cannot operate on 160 meters. I will try to stay away from the busy portions of the spectrum, and will use crystals for frequency control as much as possible. (The Harvey-Wells is interesting because it can use either crystals or an internal VFO, or BOTH, with the VFO “moving” the crystal +/- a couple of kHz.)

Thanks for your indulgence, and feel free to put your own classic gear on the air, and give me a call. The “exchange” is REAL RST (and that third digit is often NOT a 9, plus add “K” for clicks and “C” for chirp, so you might give out a 477K, or 595C, etc.), Name, State, and the make/model of transmitter and receiver (or transceiver, if that’s what you’re using). Also, a lot of folks will be using crystal-controlled rigs, and may answer a CQ anywhere from +/- 5 kHz or even more from the caller’s frequency. (Remember when you did that as a novice?) Look for folks calling “CQ CX” (Classic Exchange).

More info at:

http://www.classicexchange.org/announcments/Fall_2021_CX_Rules.pdf
