Hi Frank,

Yes, its been almost 40 years already, amazing.  It sure doesn't seem that long but there have been a lot of changes since back then, so it seems possible.

Group, I apologize.  I didn't intend to hijack the thread into a discussion about the ARRL, rather just suggest that (in my opinion) they wouldn't be the best choice for gaining access to rare DX sites.  This is not a rant or ARRL bashing, but it IS a members opinion.  Here goes;

I am a member of the ARRL because it is the only national organization supporting the hobby that we love at the national level.  Not all of what they accomplish is done poorly but a lot is.  Being drawn in too many directions at once (save the frequencies, DXing, awards, legal assistance across the the country, technical support and reviews, etc.) makes it ineffective in general, too scattered.

Yes the ARRL is primarily political and I've spent a career and much of my life trying to be apolitical because honestly, politics reeks (while being an unavoidable fact of life).  It's trading favors, sucking up and paying off favors, often for personal gain; dishonest in almost every aspect and generally an affront to those placing or allowing anyone to be put in charge.  Yet, it cannot be avoided.

Ham radio is, first of all, technical.  As the lead organization for ham radio, the ARRL should be the BEST in technical issues related to our hobby, computer access and use being a large part of this.  I picked on LOTW as the example because while we strive to make it the main worldwide means of QSL (near instant gratification is cool), the last year has not created a high level of confidence.  There have been unexplained outages, the planned outage took 5 days instead of 12 hours and it was down again this morning (with no warning or update posted on the web pages that did work).  While I also submit that the past year is an improvement on previous years, it still has more downtime than is reasonable for a national organization (an opinion).  [Hint: run parallel servers, in the long run, it's easier.]

Most folks (US or elsewhere) that are not computer savvy do not want to face the means of obtaining the keys to use the TQSL software that is required.  They deem it too tedious and international mail is only part of the issue.  I can't argue against that but made my way through the process.  Like many here, waiting for THE QSL card was/is part of the fun.

Then take, for example that the encrypted ADIF file must be uploaded using an insecure (HTTP) transfer means.  Really?  SSH and variants have been around for decades and on every OS currently in use and they're secure.  Adding that level of security is simply done and is fairly state of the art.  SSH et al is also faster and less prone to error every time the ARRL makes a minor change on the page (as seen in the recent HRD quick release so LOTW access could be restored).  SSH is certainly secure enough to safely transfer an encrypted file into the server.  The ARRL places ALL the security load on TQSL (all eggs, one basket, bad plan) instead of layers (file and transfer mode).  For technical excellence, I grade them C-, improvement needed.

For political astuteness (admittedly an area I've said I try to avoid), the story improves some.  On the plus side, the ARRL has made some inroads to the folks that make decisions (at many levels).  They could certainly do more with lobbyists (professional panderers) and more money; which they frequently remind us by requests in email and postal mail.

On the downside, it is a machine that doesn't allow much voice (and we're supposed to be communicators).  They have NEVER to my knowledge asked the membership as a group, our opinions, on any subject before they took action on it.  They presume, make bold statements, on our behalf from what is THEIR opinion of what is good for us.  THEN they tell us what they've proposed, agreed to as a change or have done and isn't it great?  Yay us.  

I've come to expect that from our government and I don't like it there either.  If someone (singular or group) is going to stand up FOR me, ask ME what it is that is important to me before you open your mouth.  I don't need a nanny, I expect representation.  It can't be done without communication.

I grade them a C- in representation, a B in politics.

Getting to the point mentioned earlier in the thread, while the ARRL has some influence in the US, their opinion, pull or other abilities in other parts of the world, doesn't amount to squat.   Do you as example, really care what the RSGB says?  Great group, good resource of information, near zero influence here.  Why would you want the ARRL to operate on the international level if they don't even seek input from their paying members on national issues and they're ineffective from lack of focus?

I submit that the larger and long standing DX groups around the world (this is one of them) can provide better access at the international level, have more money to offer (collectively).  I further submit that if all competition could be put aside so that chosen leaders from the major DX groups (we know who they are) could gather and work together, 'magic' could happen.

If it means going in under the flag of another group, as mentioned here, such as environmental or "save the (critter of the day)" to allow DX site access then YES, by all means take advantage of that flag (improving environment is always a 'good' thing).  Almost anything that is moral should be employed.

One thing is clear in this country, those wielding the power we have granted them have forgotten who they work for and they simply tell people NO (stonewalling) instead of even considering anything contrary.  It won't be getting better soon.  The ARRL is on the same track with a deaf ear.

The ARRL has a lot of varied interests, all ham radio, that draws resources away from the small slice of what we like, DXing.  All efforts are scattered.  They have no proven track record at the international level (for DXing); they do not shine brightly on the national level to represent us and simply don't have the time, money, access or ability to open doors around the world.  I don't know of any group that actually does which is why I'd like to see a conference of DXers leaders around the world (and I doubt it would ever happen, politics rears it's ugly head again).

Paul, for example, has done more for gaining access to rare entities than the ARRL could only dream of. He's not alone, others (in this group and elsewhere) have done similar.  He and I have talked (he's quite generous of his time) and while we don't always agree, the DX fire is in him for accessing new entities and DX growth in general.  It is his drive and focus and I respect that.  He's passionate and not afraid, we could use more like him.

ARRL, the best game in town, because it is the ONLY game in town.  And a poor choice for opening rare DX sites, even in our own country.

We could do better.

73,
Rick, WA6NHC

Sorry Rick, but I have to agree with the apples/oranges comment.  How does the technical proficiency required to manage databases and websites relate to effectiveness in politics?  In my opinion, the ARRL is much more a political organization than a technical one (internally as well as externally, unfortunately) so lobbying is something they're likely to be (and occasionally have been) good at.

That being said, are there any examples where lobbying has worked as a means of getting permission to operate from an otherwise restricted island?  I can't think of any off hand, but maybe I'm just not aware.

I like the idea of partnering with scientific expeditions.  We're already seeing how that can work, the most obvious example being the upcoming Heard Island DXpedition.  It seems like that approach has a much better shot at success than just bugging political contacts until they give in out of sheer exhaustion.
