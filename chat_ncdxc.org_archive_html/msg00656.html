Hi Mike, yes its good that you point out that the ZL9HR pile ups were much better behaved than those of PT0S. I did not get the sense of pure pile up desperation that I did with the PT0S pile ups.
 
Also, the proximity of Campbell Island to that of North America and EU having propagation at different times, so there was less competition at the same time for contacts.
 
Only on the clusters did I see a lot of rude/angry comments about EU from others in EU, but I didn't get a feel of it during the operation. I think too, that the operating style of the ZL9 Operators was more effective, plodding along making their rates. There wasn't any of the erratic QSY/QRX activity or infusion of six meters that we saw with PT0S.
 
It will be interesting to see what their final QSO count is once Club Log is updated.
 
Paul N6PSE

I had a lot of fun trying to work them.   Thanks to Ken’s, K6TA, timely CHAT spot on 12/1 for them on 20M SSB, I was able to get an early QSO in the log which gave me an ATNO and sort of took the pressure off.   Through the rest of their operation I was able to log 17M CW, 20M CW and 15M SSB and CW QSOs.  
 
Propagation was frequently a challenge for me and if found their signals were often down in the noise, but when they were stronger into me, I was able to work them.   Also family obligations kept me out of the shack during some of the times when propagation was the best, but such is the life of a DXer … ;>)
 
I thought their ops did a great job.   The pileups were very large at times.  The behavior of the general population of callers seemed a lot better – certainly an improvement over what I heard during the PT0S operation.

Here is your NCDXC question of the week:
 
How was your experience working the ZL9HR Campbell Island DXpedition?
 
 
Did you have fun working them?     What were your challenges?
 
 
Thanks,
 
 
Paul N6PSE
 
 
 
Here is my response:
 
I had a blast working ZL9HR 10-40 meters!  Their signals were much stronger than I anticipated and I worked them on the higher bands with relative ease. It was a bit more difficult on 40 meters and I never heard them well enough on 30/80 meters. I was very happy for them that they were able to
remain on the island at night time. I feared that would greatly impact their operations and their ability to satisfy the need. I thought their pile up management was good. It was evident to me that several of their operators were possibly new/inexperienced DXpeditioners or DXPP's as our friend Martti Laine would call them, however they did well and maintained a decent rate.
 
The communications from this team was superb. Their pilots, including Rich KY6R really kept the DX Community informed as to the challenges and features of this DXpedition. One of those challenges was uploading their logs to Club Log. That can be a real chore from a SatFone and it appears that they are still missing a few days of activity at the tail end. Club Log currently shows 35,697 contacts and I estimate they made many more than that. Overall, a fun DXpedition to work and a much needed country for many of us!
 
Thank you!
 
Paul N6PSE
