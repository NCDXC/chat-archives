On 4/22/2013 4:35 PM, Charles K. Epps wrote:
Paul, I’ll wade into this debate with a contrarian position.

Not withstanding my good friend Rusty's reasoned position, I'll wade in on support of the PT0S guys. I have several responses to Paul's negative comment. In order of greatest weight:

The technical design and management of the expedition by George, AA7JV, can only be described as spectacularly good.  George's focus was the lower bands, specifically 160-40, which are the hardest bands to do WELL. Anyone can work the low hanging fruit,but George put together a station that could both hear and be heard everywhere there was mutual darkenss (or greyline plus darkness) to support QSOs. From >6,000 miles away, PT0S was easy to work on 160-40 if you took those bands seriously. They were easy to work because George's antenna designs were well developed, and he understood how to tame noise. 

George's power system design was equally spectacular. Everything in his station ran on 12V, including  power amps that produced 1kW in bridged mode, so his generators were able to run in "econmode" doing nothing more than charging batteries. As a result, their fuel consumption (and the amount of fuel they had to carry) was FAR less than for most trips. That concept alone is worth the award we gave them.

This was an operation with only four guys working under VERY difficult conditions, but they managed to have three stations on the air, and one of them made 7,000 Qs on 10M at 100W.  Not only that,those four guys put just short of 14,000 unique calls in the log.  That's 3,500 uniques per man, and they did it in less that 2 weeks. Try crunching those numbers on some of those mega-trips with a cast of thousands and budgets in the hundreds of thousands.

LOTW?  Same day service! 

QRX for 6M?  Have you ever WORKED 6M, Paul?  It's a VERY different band, and it must be worked when available.  More to the point -- if YOU'RE the guy living on 4 hours sleep a night in the corner of the same small space where your partners are running Qs, YOU get to make that choice! 

By contrast, I have a FAR less sanguine view of the 2-man crew at VK9CZ, who were totally clueless about how to operate any band below 20M, and varying degrees of clueless on the higher bands. In two weeks, they made 5 QSOs on 160M, 270 on 80, 1029 on 40M, and 913 on 30M.  Compare that to PT0S -- 3027 Qs on 160M, 4473 on 80M, 4504 on 40M, 3442 on 30M.  I heard WD5COV work VK9CZ SIMPLEX on 80M, and they were immediately swamped by JAs, simplex. I never heard them again.  They complained they had terrible noise on the lower bands. So did PT0S, but George did his homework and came loaded for bear with what he needed to kill the noise, and by the third night of their trip he could work S6 signals -- like mine.

ANYONE can get good results by throwing massive resources at a problem -- big bucks and a cast of thousands. But my hat is off to the guys who do it the old-fashioned way -- great engineering and smart operation. And, like some of the trips you've coordinated, it took some very savvy political work to make it happen.

AA7JV is one of my heroes!

73, Jim K9YC
