On Jun 4, 2023, at 1:47 PM, Rick NK7I via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

I agree with Paul.  We have enough 'lowering of the bar' these days and to do operations 'next to'  (how far?) would take away from those who have attained the ranks in #1 Honor Roll.  

DXCC is not about showing up (participation awards); it's about accomplishing something.  That something, is very specific.  The operations take place ON that location; a DXpedition has feet, antennas and transceiver (and wheels, boats, copter landing zone) ON the stated location; close just doesn't count (except horseshoes, hand grenades and nuclear weapons).

It doesn’t require “feet”, only the transmitter and antennas (hence the RIB model).

A RIB seems a viable compromise since the team must land the craft, set up the station, then leave so the environment remains (almost) pristine (simpler for controlling agencies to tolerate).  Human time on 'protected sites' would be less than a typical scientific study, mere hours.


Would you disallow a completely operational RIB that was dropped in by drone and operated by wireless remote from a nearby ship? That may not be practical TODAY, but is at least a theoretical possibility, and a great solution for very-difficult-to-land-on places, like Bouvet.

Since it was grazed on in this discussion, I repeat my premise that the only valid DXCC 'entities' that should be counted are only places where human life is present or possible (which deletes Bouvet unless it becomes a research facility and most reefs and atolls) and only include those governments that potentially will (ever) allow ham radio (unlikely in the DPRK in our lifetime).

Well, “ever” is a long time. DPRK didn’t exist before the Korean War (early 1950s), and will surely not exist indefinitely. Major conflicts tend to completely redraw national boundaries (WW2), and political change does so as well (e.g., the fall of the Soviet Union). One might have to wait a while, but the map is constantly redrawn over time, and the powers-in-control shift as well.
