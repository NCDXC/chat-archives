From my perspective, the verification scheme is a solution looking for a problem.

Sure, there were pirates during the Bouvet project. There have been pirates since the beginning of time. For obvious reasons, Bouvet was an exception and the pirates had a field day.

Most all callers work a DXpedition on multiple bands, multiple modes, and many make insurance contacts for the band/mode they “really need”.  The thought that they might miss a (real) ATNO because they worked a pirate is a stretch, especially  with today’s FT modes flooding the airways during a DXpedition. The coverage we've seen with most DXpeditions since 2018 has been nothing short of spectacular. The number of times I’ve seen a Not in Log response to a person with one QSO in the log is itself single digit.

Steve raises another interesting point. On Clipperton we were running 400+ Qs an hour using a standard version of WSJT-X (with some modifications-fixes), not Super Fox. With 5 Elecraft stations we hit 18,000+ QSOs a day. In one 4 hour shift they made 4,900 Qs on FT8.

My own observations with N5J and CY9C would indicate SF did not deliver the expected throughput. Will they fix it, of course they will. However, there are far more variables to throughput than meet the eye. For example, propagation, paths and frequency loading play a significant role in just how many Qs we can put in the log, per hour.    

But the real question is: "what problem are we trying to solve with verification?"

Cheers,
