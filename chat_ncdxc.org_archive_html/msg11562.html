On March 8, 2016 at 11:54:58, jeff kinzli n6gq (jeff@n6gq.com) wrote:

I do similar when I'm DX and trying to exploit a given path or band
that is not common. I.e 10m at midnight localtime.

From that perspective, if *any* skimmer hears you, I'd say there's a
good chance that a ham with a moderate station will hear you in that
geography. If its a 5dB S/N on RBN, then that's good enough for a
moderate station to hear you in the "real world" IMHO.

So I would say that when you are QRP, you can safely assume that if a
skimmer is hearing you at any level, then other hams in that geography
will hear you (albeit weak perhaps). The hard part is usually getting
someone to actually answer you ;) Or get noticed by more than one or
two callers.

And as K9YC pointed out, most skimmers are probably something on the
order of a ground-mounted wide-band vertical, so someone with a yagi
will probably hear you better than the RBN reports.

73 de N6GQ

On March 8, 2016 at 11:20:38, Jim Brown via Chat (chat@ncdxc.org) wrote:

Hi Dan,

The signal reports on RBN are usually dB referenced to the noise floor
in the receiver that is feeding RBN. I think that most RBN receivers use
a vertical antenna. So -- if you're spotted on the RBN, a station with
a reasonable antenna near that RBN reporter should be able to hear you
with a decent antenna.

Also, in a major contest, an RBN receiver may be running at reduced
sensitivity so that it isn't overloaded by strong signals. N4ZR would be
a good guy to ask about that. I'm copying him on this reply.

73, Jim K9YC
