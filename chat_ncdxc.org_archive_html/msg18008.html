OK. I'm sitting in a house in New Hampshire built in 1793. It is a standard farm house which has grown to 9 bedrooms. We are enjoying a power outage due to high winds courtesy of Everbright, the local power company. (Perhaps Everdark would be a better name.) The outside temperature is about 38 degrees this evening.
I'm currently using the 18th century heating system (a fireplace), but the kitchen has a 19th century heating system that can also cook and bake bread (a wood stove). The big missing piece of function is the well pump, so no water, and toilets only flush once. We could take a bucket down to the brook, but that sounds like work. We have a generator, but is isn't working, probably gasoline deposits clogging the carburator.
The long term plan for here is a propane powered generator using the propane tank which currently operates the 21st century heating system (hot water in the floor). We also have a 20th century heating system (oil fired forced air). Both of these heating systems require electric power to run. I'm just glad to have some heating systems that were developed before electricity was wired to homes.
The advantage of propane over gasoline is that propane doesn't clog the fuel system, and so is more reliable. A tank in the ground reduces, but doesn't eliminate, the risk of pipe breakage during an earthquake.
Instead of a generator, I would also consider a solar powered system. Yes, you're dependent on batteries at night, but how much power do you really need at night? Both refrigerators and freezers will keep food cold over night with no power. Air conditioning is most needed during daylight, when the panels are producing. With good insulation and a bit of thermal mass, you should be comfortable all night without running the compressor. Of course, you will need to power that 1.5 KW amp for top band...
