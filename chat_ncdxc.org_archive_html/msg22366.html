I'm no expert, but present this for discussion only, hoping others chime it.  It is an often unstated risk, easily ignored.

This group spans a vast membership area, I didn't look up your call (which sadly no longer points to actual locale) to see where you are located.  BUT, no where is safe from static events, even humid, salty, windy San Jose.

Because of my location (far north Idaho, north Rockies) one of the key points in my research was to plan for (seasonally frequent) lightning, BUT other environmental events happen too, causing static.  Wind (ugh), snow (not in the bay area often), dust and likely other causes can create a discharge (with resulting noise and damage).

I had to read several times, sections of the book, cross referencing other sources to get the basic understanding that I have.  I'm not stupid, can read at college levels but the topic is COMPLEX.  I applied what I learned to my new station (PLENTY of ground rods, copper wire buried; tied the tower to the shop safety ground AND the house safety ground; one run of wire and rods out into a field for later use, 'just because' for now; DENY static entry).

Lightning is routine here.  I've watched it, timed it to know the distance (as close as a quarter mile) and have even seen (have the video) a strike span the 8 mile valley I'm in (horizontal lightning; it was a mile away).  Light Rockies snow also happens, the static potential is high; humidity just doesn't matter (rarely <60% here); the same with dust (compressed glacial flour 'soil').  I've done what I can to mitigate the risks (and the noise floor improved).

I use a similar copper pipe in the actual shack, bonded to a ground rod on the other side of the wall.

Your house safety ground, at 20' away and a ground rod 10' down, meets the minimums, well done.  (Basic rule, 2X the depth of the rod for span.)  Tying ALL grounding together is mandatory (yeah, preaching to the choir here; consider the noobs in the group) by code (law).  Failure to do this may allow an insurance company cause to deny a claim (key point).

The same 'rule' applies to your Urban beam, ground rods each 2X the depth, you'll have to sink a few more rods along that path.  I buried mine to avoid snow blower, plow and mower risks (all coax/control wires in buried conduits, avoid the visual 'blight').

My concern of using aluminum is the bimetal corrosion, it reacts so poorly with other metals and naturally creates a layer of corrosion (why they last so long when used as an antenna, that corrosion protects the aluminum).  Using copper (shouldn't cost 'a lot' for a small plate, even up here in BFE), would minimize corrosion and resistance (an annual condition check for safety, measure resistances carefully, clean and polish); get every erg shed (lower that resistance to ground at every chance).

Being 'rigorous' is more being safer.  It's impossible to overdo it; though there is a point of diminishing return.

Again, ALL entry points of ALL services MUST be tied into the same grounding 'network'.  Missing one can be catastrophic.  You're reducing the risks by creating the best paths to ground, everywhere on everything.   Even then it's risk reduction, not elimination (a direct strike can ruin your week).

You want polyphasers (TM) on the roof mounted antennas as well, at the entry point to the house (even though they are DC grounded).  Keep that static energy OUTside; give it NO excuse or chance to come inside.  Having replaceable element designs may be a better choice.

Over planning is not a bad idea, no matter where one lives.  The investment in the devices you mention is less costly than the damage that may occur.   I have the Array Solutions (times two) at the base of the tower (SteppIR DB36/80 uses a LOT of wires, plus rotor cables); polyphasers as well (one per coax), with a secondary set of polyphasers on each coax just before it enters the house (belt AND suspender approach).

Rare means a lowered risk, not a removal of that risk; plan wisely for the worst case.  In the long run, it's cheaper.

Again I'm not an expert.  My language tends to be direct (teaching/sharing mode) but do your 'home work' and come up with a solution (even if it takes time to afford and install) that meets your station/building needs.  Then maintain it.

The cost of doing it right (while not cheap) is cheaper than replacing the station, home and contents.

Moving to other ideas for your shack; I have a (mostly) whole house generator (auto start/transfer) which is wired into the shack (a circuit JUST for the shack).  I also use a UPS (bonded to the copper pipe) to keep the shack alive during the power transfer; it also filters the power to the station.  With most radios being computer (and rotators, controllers etc); this stabilizes the station during an outage.

I do a similar UPS for my mesh network; being unwilling to accept the minutes it takes to reboot (modem, each node).  I can't run the KPA1500 on generator, but I can use the KPA500 since it's 120V wired.  During an emergent event, I SHALL be heard, hi.

Planning for the worst case scenario means better stability, until you find out what you missed, lol.
