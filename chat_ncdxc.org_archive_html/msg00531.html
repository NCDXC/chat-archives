I think I'd give an overall score of D+.

I know that seems harsh, but this DXpedition broke many of the rules of the DX Code of Conduct.

These guys get an A++ for their daily efforts to stay alive and provide contacts to the deserving.

But, that doesn't excuse the poor operating practices.

Was it really necessary to use ALL of the 12m phone band? TX 24940, RX 24945-24990 and 24930-24935 for (SA). Was it really necessary to use 65 kHz of 15m for RTTY?

And what about the emphasis on JAs? (I only wish the 1A and FR/G guys had shown such dedication for the West Coast) They spent an hour every night on 160m listening for JA without working a single one; then moved to 80m at sunrise where they worked about 10; before moving to 40m where they did have success. They should have abandoned their 160/80m JA efforts and put more stations in the log and/or put more time on 40m.

And what was the purpose of the QRX (terribly misused)? It generally meant QSY or QRT. Why not tell the truth?

Speaking of QRX, what is the correct response to "QRX K6U?" ? I wasn't sure if I was being asked to stop transmitting or not. I lost my 40m QSO by standing by; the next day on 80m, I waited 10 seconds and sent my call twice - that was my only good QSO.

The consequence of all of this was frustration in the crowd, which lead to them getting very unruly.

Not all was bad, the 160/80m operator(s) were excellent. I didn't get into the 160m log, but I did work RI1ANF at the same time.

All in all, it was not fun. It was great to have the hf activity end at 19Z (their sunset), so one didn't spend too much time in the chair. In the Pacific NW, their 160/80m signals peaked in the 05-07Z period, so one didn't have to spend the whole night chasing them.


Steve, K6UM
