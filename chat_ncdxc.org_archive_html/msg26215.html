For years now, we have had CW skimmers and the Reverse Beacon Network, making it trivial to “search & pounce” on CW during a contest (assuming that you want to operate in an “assisted” category, and that the contest allows such operation). Virtually EVERY station calling CQ is spotted within seconds, sometimes during the first call. OK, so now we have “near perfect”, automatic spotting for all CW stations.

Later, similar skimmer technology was developed for RTTY, making it again unnecessary for humans to spot callers. So S&P operation during a RTTY contest also becomes trivial in an assisted category, and even outside of a contest, every RTTY station calling CQ is automatically spotted.

And now, the same is true for many other digital modes, including the popular FT8/FT4, where the standard software decodes everything it can hear, and can generate spots either locally (for your own use) or via the Internet, for everyone to use. So far, so good.

And then there is phone operation. Unless someone is hiding some proprietary technology, I have never seen a skimmer for phone (SSB, in particular). Now, I understand the difficult challenges that phone “skimming” presents over CW or any digital mode:

* The actual suppressed carrier frequency of the transmitter is not known; it could be +/- a couple of hundred Hz, depending on the tonal quality of the speaker.

* Everyone’s voice is different, making speech recognition difficult, particularly in the face of noise and QRM.

* The speed at which people speak is different, even within a single transmission from the same speaker.

* Different nationalities will have different accents, even if speaking English.

* The use of non-standard phonetics makes things even harder (and I admit to being guilty on this count, using “Monkey Monkey Monkey” instead of “Mike Mike Mike”).

All that said, speech recognition systems DO exist, and “work” more or less well, particularly when the expected words are known, and conditions are controlled. We have all used automated telephone menus with voice commands, voice commands in our cars for navigation and entertainment control, etc. None of these systems are perfect, but they are better than nothing. And a “CQ” is a reasonably bounded set of words to decode. The CQ itself seems fairly easy (with ANY voice), and a callsign will always have at least one number and typically not more than 5 or so letters around it. (Yes, I know there are special-event calls that are much longer, and there’s always the “Country-Slash-My Real Call-Slash-Whatever”, but those are exceptions.)

Now, with the sophisticated AI and incredibly cheap processing power currently available, I suspect it may be possible to build a workable SSB skimmer. It would surely not be as accurate as CW/RTTY/FT8, but it should be able to “spot” a caller who is “in the clear”, with a natural-sounding voice. Again, not perfect, but better than nothing, although it might be a LOT of code.

But here’s another idea. On VHF/UHF repeaters, we regularly insert a low-frequency tone, inaudible to the user, but sufficient for the repeater to determine that the sender is “authorized” to use the repeater. What if we similarly inserted a low-frequency sub-band within the SSB audio, in the low hundreds-of-Hz range (inaudible to the listener), and used it to send one’s callsign in tone-coded CW at a fixed speed? A “SSB skimmer” wouldn’t have to worry at all about speech recognition, but would simply use the existing CW skimmer technology to automatically spot SSB stations equipped with this capability.

Now, this requires modification to the sender’s configuration, but anyone who WANTS to be spotted (e.g., contesters, DXpeditions, Special Event Stations, IOTA/SOTA/POTA, etc.) would want to add this feature, particularly if it was easy to do. There are a few ways to do this (probably a lot more, but here’s a couple):

- Virtually EVERY serious contester uses a recording, particularly for CQ, as it relieves a lot of tedious repetition over the many hours of a contest. It would be trivial to “mix” the CQ audio recording with the low-frequency tone-coded CW of one’s callsign, and save both in the .wav file used for sending CQ. 

- Popular contest software could automatically incorporate this “audio mixing” whenever a recorded CQ is sent, avoiding the need for the user to pre-mix the signals. (And the embedded CW is easy to generate automatically, since the software “knows” the callsign of the sending station, and CW generation is trivial.)

- Even non-contest logging software could mix the CW tone-code with transmitted audio, although it might require the user to mouse-click a button to include this while sending, since most non-contesters don’t use recordings for calling CQ.

I do understand that most SSB receivers band-limit the audio, so the low frequency sub-band can’t be TOO low, but I think it should be manageable. Plus, “SSB skimmers” specifically looking for this signal would not apply band-limiting at the low end; in fact, they should probably band-limit out the speaker’s audio to avoid aliasing problems with the CW tone-code.

So I’m just throwing out these ideas to get some feedback, or maybe someone will tell me that it’s already been done, or there’s a better way to do it, or maybe someone with more software expertise than me wants to work with me to build something.

Getting spotted on SSB during a contest REALLY helps; if it happened on phone like it does on CW, there would be more activity on SSB during contests.
