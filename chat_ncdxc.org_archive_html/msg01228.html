Rick, regarding the level playing field, the DXpedition has a certain responsibility to the DX Community and the DX-er has a certain responsibility if he/she is truly interested in working the DXpedition.
 
First, the DXpedition must plan a destination that is desirable enough for the DX community. My experience is that destinations that are not in the top 40 most wanted entities are poorly supported and are not viable for a proper DXpedition in this current economic climate. That is my own opinion. The DXpedition must be long enough to work down the pile ups and give everyone a chance. With ST0R because it was a new country, we were QRV for 21 days!   While 3D2C was largely needed only in Europe and 6.5 days QRV was sufficient.
 
In each of the DXpeditions that I have been involved with, we were often "begging" or calling CQ during the last two days of our QRV period. I have never left a destination feeling that there was unfinished business or an unmet need still awaiting our contacts.
 
The team must be made up of a good mix of experienced operators and inexperienced operators with the aptitude and the right attitude. Martti Laine calls these DX paying passengers. I'm not at all comfortable with that term and will just refer to them as newer operators or beginning DXpeditioners. We were all beginners at something at least once!
 
I have also found that it makes a big difference to schedule the right operators for the right shifts to make sure the need is met not only across the globe, but across the calendar and clock as well. Some operators like to operate at 3AM and some clearly don't.
 
There are many other things that a DXpedition can do to help increase the chances of a successful DXpedition. Selecting the right time of year is important.The Spring months of March, April and May and the fall months of September and October are best for higher band DXpeditions. The middle of the Summer is about the worst time to launch a DXpedition.
 
Each home DX-er also has to make his station as ready and as robust as possible. I have tried the technique of calling QRZ for QRP stations only many times and I have not found it to be productive. We are running a KW and gain antennas and the QRPer can hear us well but we just can't hear them from far flung places such as Iraq, South Sudan or Yemen.
 
The QRP operator is essentially handicapping his chances of working the DXpedition by running minimal power. The DXpedition cannot be expected to compensate for that.
 
This was a great question and I wanted to give my perspective on it. I'll add it to the list of questions for the DX Forum panel at IDXC in Visalia this April.
 
Hope to see you all there!

Hi Paul,
 
Sorry for the delay in response, it’s been a busy couple of days (still I added some ATNOs).  Just to reinforce my questions, everyone has a slightly different perspective on what is unique.  More input allows better choices; so I’m curious about the DXpedition point of view.
 
I do try to predict the pattern of the DX by watching the P3 scope, then get ahead of them and be better placed when they move.  But if I can’t hear the stations they are working, I just sit in one place I know they’ve listened (a few don’t tend to move often, part of the pattern).  Lightning strikes many places multiple times.  ;o)
 
As I am HOA limited, I love the concept of pausing for a few moments for QRP (not for me) and limited antenna stations (I have a stealth ~110 meter dipole, 30-50’ above dirt, that’s it).  But I suspect the reality worldwide won’t allow that to happen until the DX community adopts some sort of cross check with a known Big Gun list during those moments.  In short, while everyone operates with a sense of morality; not all have the same sense (i.e. a Russian KW may be 10KW actual or more).  We all want the DX; some are willing to cross the line to get it.  [ALL my DX has been on modest <500 watt stations.]
 
Which leads to one more question:  Should and how can the playing field be leveled to make it the same for all?  Not everyone has the space, money or simple ability to put up phased towers/arrays for each band.  Is there a fair means to compensate?  Yes, that is where technique comes in, which ties back to the first question I asked.

Hi Rick-WA6NHC.  This is a great question and one that is tackled at DX University each year. W9KNI also discusses his approaches in his book.
 
While I intend to submit this question to the DX Forum Panel, I can also say that from both ends of the pileups, if you are not a big gun station, try to stand out from the pack or separate yourself. Don't follow the pack. I rarely try to be where the DX just called. I use my P3 Panadapter to study where the DX is listening and I try to find a clear spot where he might listen next. 
 
When I am operating from a DXpedition such as 7O6T I notice that many now use their panadapters or "fish finders" to call right where you took the last caller. The days of working 4-5 guys from the same spot are pretty much over and you have to move around a lot to hear callers clearly.
 
Thanks everyone for the great questions, feel free to submit more up to IDXC in April.
