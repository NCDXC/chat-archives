On 8/6/2013 4:10 PM, Don Ferguson wrote:
I believe, as a club, we can make sure that any support we provide to DX groups includes the expectation that the group receiving our donation will upload QSO’s to LOTW within 6 months of the expeditions

When I asked the question, I was thinking about FAR more than DXpeditions -- that may satisfy old farts with 360 countries confirmed, but it does NOTHING for someone starting out from scratch, as I did when I moved to California 7 years ago.I'm chasing band countries for DXCC, CQ Fields, CQ Zones, 6M grids, and IOTAs.  I tried using the Bureau, but it so clogged with JA cards that an average round trip is three years, going on four. When a JA works me in a contest, he sends me  a card, sometimes one for each QSO.  There are a dozen or so JAs from whom I've received more than 30 cards each! 

So what I'm concerned about are the rank and file hams around the world, for whom I pay $1.10 to mail a card I paid $.10 for and add an average of $2.50 to the inside of the envelope for return postage. Add those numbers up, and you get $3.70 for a card, assuming a 100% return rate. I've spent well over $3k on postage that I would far rather have spent on rigs or good beer.

As I see it, there seem to be several reasons why folks don't use the cluster.  1) It's too much trouble -- from what I hear, it ain't easy for a ham outside the US to get started.  2) It's an invasion of privacy asking for the documentation that ARRL does. I've heard this from VK hams.  3) ARRL is making money off of it. I've heard this from several hams in the former Soviet Union. This seems to go along with resentment of the US.  4) Once data is entered, I can't change it. 5) Some hams who are making money by demanding a lot more than the actual postage from QSLing don't want to lose that income. A month or so ago, I posted an exchange with on OD ham who wanted $3 to buy a stamp that cost him less than $1.

So my question is, what can we do to break down these objections?

1) I think we ought to be exerting our influence with ARRL (through our Director, W6RGG, and by a carefully crafted letter to the League, to 1) make LOTW more accessible to hams outside the US, and to make the process more FRIENDLY and more user friendly.

2) I think it would help a lot for the League to do a PR campaign to attract more LOTW users, especially those outside the US. 

3) We should encourage the League to develop a relationship with RSGB for their awards, especially IOTA.  NI6T, another IOTA chaser, tells me there are about 1,100 islands.  Multiply that number by $3.70 and you get another very nice rig.

4) We should encourage RSGB to enter the 20th century (no mis-print -- they are still living in the 19th century, demanding paper cards, nothing electronic accepted! I don't have a lot of islands, but I just mailed more than 100 cards. I would much rather have spend that on radio gear.  And I have to mail the cards to a guy in Florida to have them checked! 

By contrast, W6OAT just checked my brand new submission of 175 CQ fields, using about 80 cards that I already had, with the remaining QSLs being from printouts of eQSL and LOTW confirmations. I only had to mail 11 cards to confirm those 175 fields. I handed to cards to him at the picnic, he returned them two weeks later when we met for dinner. And he didn't give me any breaks because we were friends -- he found errors with four of my cards. :) 

5) When we send a card, how about sticking a note in the envelope encouraging the other guy to use LOTW and eQSL? Tell him how much time it can save both of us, and how much money it saves the guy who needs the card.  Tell him how easy it is with free logging software like DXKeeper. Tell him that when you look at spots on the cluster, you're much more likely to respond to an LOTW user! 

73, Jim K9YC
