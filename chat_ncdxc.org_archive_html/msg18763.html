I've owned three SteppIR antennas (6-80 vertical, 3 element Yagi and DB36 four element  Yagi)

Like all antennas, they have their strengths and weaknesses. Yes the UV damages the black rubber boots but most of us have learned to cover them with 3M tape and 
they seem to last a long time. They are not an ideal antenna in areas of heavy ice or wind loads. 

The Remote Ham Radio guys (W2RE) have experienced significant boom failures with square booms and they are replacing all of their antennas with GXP antennas. Ray-W2RE who owns a tower/antenna installation
and service company and says the GXP antennas are far superior to anything else. GXP's are used by most of the big Multi Multi East Coast Contest stations. 

If I were to put up a new Yagi today, I would seriously consider the GXP line of antennas based on what I have been hearing.

I have heard a variety of “war stories” about Steppirs over the years. Everything from insulators that break down in UV light, beryllium tapes that get jammed, etc. When I embarked on my tower/antenna project 12 years ago, I researched everything I could find. A *LOT* of folks like Steppir, and it seems to be almost a cult following.

While I seriously considered the Steppir, I felt uncomfortable with a device having moving parts at the top of the tower, exposed to the environment. I ended up selecting and installing an Optibeam OB11-5 (https://www.optibeam.net/index.php?article_id=1&clang=1). It assembles like a precision machine. I especially like the idea of a SQUARE boom (with appropriate square U-bolts), so there is never a problem making the elements all lie in the same plane.

There are NO ADJUSTMENTS to be made, and none are even possible. All holes are pre-drilled, and there are no slip-rings or anything else to tweak resonance. Unbelievably, it was resonant EXACTLY where the specs say (once it is in the air, of course), with low SWR on all 5 bands (20-10). I have never had to make an adjustment or even lower the tower in the 12 years it has been up there, and it has consistently stayed in spec all these years.

If I were to do it again, I might have selected a larger model, perhaps with 30/40m capability, but this was my first beam and tower (in my life!), and I kept it fairly simple. (The OB11-5 is a 3-element monobander on 20/17/15/12m, and 5 elements on 10m, all on the same boom with a single feedline. It’s an interlaced Yagi, similar in concept to the old Force-12.) The antenna is heavy (almost 100 lbs), and not cheap (especially with shipping from Germany), but I have never doubted that I made the right decision. While I read everyone gripes about Steppirs, I just work the DX (326 entities including North Korea, all confirmed, in those 12 years).

Tom Schmenger, DF2BO, is the chief designer and president of Optibeam, and you can often find him on 40 or 20m SSB in the EU morning hours, before he goes to work.

(Disclaimer: I have no personal or financial interest in Optibeam or any of their distributors; I’m just a happy customer.)
