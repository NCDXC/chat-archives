On Sun, Jun 4, 2023 at 10:12 AM Steve Jones via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

Dear Ken,

I was struck by Dave Minster’s take on the Bouvet operation in his May QST editorial.  I fully support his comment questioning why being anchored 300 feet offshore doesn’t currently count for DXCC.  I have been chasing DX since 1962 when I worked my first JA from California.  I’m now at 333 current entities, with a few more worked but not yet confirmed.  My joy in working DX and chasing ATNO’s on DXpeditions is based on fine tuning my station so that I have the best chance of Mother Nature allowing me to work the distant station.  My personal analogy is fishing:  You can have the perfect Powell fly rod, and the perfect fly you tied that very morning based on the bugs you just saw hovering over the water.  But if Mother Nature doesn’t put that trout right there where it can see your fly, there is no catch!  Sure, part of my DX chasing is competitive, when I use my skills to place my call at just the right place and time to get ahead of all the other callers.  But my main reward is knowing my little signal went through all those thousands of miles of ionosphere, aurora, absorptive D-Layer, maybe a CME or two, to finally reach that distant operator’s ears and have him/her answer me back through all those same vagaries of propagation.  Whether the DX station was on dirt or rock or on a ship’s deck few hundred feet offshore makes absolutely no difference to my radio operating accomplishment.  And allowing DX credit for ships anchored off islands with sensitive ecologies, where access is prohibited by governmental agencies, would be to promote protection of earth’s delicate environment, a positive value we should all support.  Please consider changing the DXCC Rules to allow for offshore DXpedition operations.

Vy 73,

Steve Jones

N6SJ
