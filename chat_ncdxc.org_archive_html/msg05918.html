Yes, indeed, the trees are our major trouble! A group of hams at SLVARC evaluated numerous potential locations for 2.4-GHz links. We discovered that even when terrain was not an issue (according to Google Earth and a separate line-of-sight program that I wrote), field visits revealed that dense trees in this area nearly always got in the way.

That said, using directional antennas, we created a partitioned network with three links. One link crosses the San Lorenzo Valley, traversing 11 km between locations on opposite edges. This link needs to pass through some trees, and the link quality reflects this. We are aware of nearby station locations (less than 100 feet away) that allow a clear line of sight for this link. After a tower is erected at my end I'd like to move the antenna above the trees. 

There is a second link between my home and a station on the valley floor. Once again, this link passes through trees, this time at the other end.

A third, isolated link connects the SLVARC repeater site with the home of a nearby ham. This link is the link one of the network with clear line of sight.

Were we to have created more than just a partitioned network of three links, we would have continued to build out, but computer terrain evaluations and field visits of numerous potential sites showed that there are no more possible links with existing hams in the area.

During those experiments, I also attempted to invert my approach to the problem. Instead of evaluating potential links between the QTHs of SLVARC club members, I also attempted to identify the street addresses of homes in the valley that would make ideal station locations. The plan was to invite those homeowners to become hams! But the tree problem prevented me from using an automated approach to evaluate the quality of potential stations. I haven't thought of this problem recently, and I may revisit it again in the future.
