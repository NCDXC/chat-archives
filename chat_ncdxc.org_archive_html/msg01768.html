A group of us have been experimenting the last 15 years with 40M antennas
(among other things) at our summer property up in the Sierras. You can see
some pictures and other info at our K6XN post at QRZ.com. We have several
hundred acres of forest land up there acquired over 50 +/- years in a
wilderness area and we have built a cabin among other things up there and
installed multiple towers and antennas on top of a mountain/ridgeline at
approximately 5000 feet elevation.

On 40M we have installed and operated a 540 foot +/- (four wavelengths at
40M) horizontal loop antenna up between 60 and 100 feet, a 40M KLM dipole at
63 feet and a KLM four element 40m beam up 65 feet. 

The 4 element KLM 40M beam at 65 feet was wiped out by high winds the first
winter. While it was up it was fantastic!!! We may rebuild it this summer.

The KLM 40M dipole at 60 feet functioned well, survived the winters and we
could work anything we could hear with it.

The 540 foot +/- horizontal loop antenna works well on many bands (with a
full legal limit tuner) and on 40M it is low noise and high gain and has
been an outstanding antenna for chasing DX and for contesting. It is one of
our favorite antennas for Field Day, CQP, Museum Ships Weekend and various
other contests when we are not chasing individual DX contacts. It also works
well on 160M and other bands. It has substantially survived every winter so
far...but every Spring it needs some TLC and repair. We *love* this loop
antenna.

Using the 540 foot horizontal loop antenna at a 5000 foot elevation on top
of Remington Hill elevated 60 to 100 feet above ground and using a KW+ on
40M we have provided a lot of DXers and contesters over the years with
needed 40M contacts. Our "40M amplifier" up at the cabin is an old restored
beloved home brew 300+ pound antique dual 3-500Z amplifier from the early
60's powered by its own dedicated 4KW generator. (We have no commercial
power up at the cabin).

If you have heard and worked NN7NN, NC6DX, N7PR, K6XN, K6ON, K6YN, KJ6WQQ or
W6DOJ on 40M from Nevada County you have probably already experienced our
540 foot +/- horizontal loop antenna at 5000 feet on 40M. We *love* this
loop antenna.

73, Ted, K6XN

> What type of support do you have? 

That's always a great starting point.  Also, remember that an horizontal
loop is omni-directional in the horizontal plane, while a dipole has gain
broadside to it. That gives the dipole an advantage of several dB in it's
favored direction. If you have the supports and real estate to do it, a pair
of dipoles at right angles are a great option.  The first antenna I put up
when I moved here was an 80/40 fan broadside to EU and VK/ZL.  It started
out low (I needed help and tools to get it higher), and each increment of
height made it play better, especially on 80M.  
Eventually I got it up to about 110 ft, and added a second at 90 degrees to
it. A big Douglas Fir holds one end of both antennas.

There are lots of ideas about antennas for limited space on my website,
including various things I've learned about construction of wire antennas.
.

http://audiosystemsgroup.com/publish.htm

And if you have only a single support, check out the excellent ideas about
sloping antennas (and a lot more) by Rudy Severns (N6LF) on his website.
Google to find it.
