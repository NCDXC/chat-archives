Interesting range of comments (!) on PT0S. I guess the yardsticks vary. From my point of view, they did an excellent job, conditions on the rocks notwithstanding. The conditions only made the operating more difficult. George and Tamas are very experienced at what they set out to do. Likewise, PY5CC is said to be one of the best 6M operators in the World.

 

Sometimes, DXpeditions focus on maximizing unique callsigns. This was a specialty DXpedition. The announced goal for PT0S was to work the low bands and six meters. It wasn’t to maximize uniques. Few expeditioners have as much experience in their respective areas as these guys. The Yasme Foundation gave George and Tamas a Yasme Excellence Award a few years ago based on their low-band operations from the Pacific – including the Chesterfields. (Better weather and sea conditions there, of course.)

 

There were some rough edges in the operating on the upper bands. No doubt some of the pileups were very unruly; the reasons are pretty clear. That could use some work. Would it have been better not to try at all? Maybe. In any event, it may be that SP&P is a bit too rare -- #16 in the only ranking that matters – to do a specialty DXpedition.

 

But I am glad they went. Due to the DXCC conversion in the nineties, I needed everything but 15M. Because of a birthday and Thanksgiving in Montana, I only had a few days of operating, and I missed them on several bands. But 160 through 40 were not among the missed bands. They were there, and the pileups – though huge – were entirely under control.

 

In the end, I had lots of fun working them, and I am happy with the number of contacts I made. I am not gong to blame anyone else for what I didn’t work. For those who didn’t make Qs, I am confident as always that there will be activity there in coming years as there has been in the past.

 

73,

 

Wayne, N7NG
Jackson Hole

Here is your NCDXC question of the week:

 

How was your experience working PT0S?    Did you have fun working them?     Are you satisfied with your results/number of contacts?

 

 

Here is my response: 

 

I was in Europe during the first 3-4 days of this DXpedition. I could hear PT0S very well, however the EU QRM and overall bad behavior was extreme. I had hoped that things would be better when I got home to the West Coast. Upon arriving home, I assumed that the pile ups and bad behavior had diminished, but no!   It was nearly as bad as the earliest days.

 

I was well aware of the goals of this DXpedition and I know several of the Operators. I thought the two low band specialists did a great job. The other two operators had an erratic operating style where they would jump around to various bands very quickly and there was a clear preference for South America in their operating style. Quite often they were on six meters during our late afternoon periods which would have been the ideal time to catch them on the higher bands. Overall, there seemed to be an unusual level of desperation among those in the pileups to the bitter end. I think this was possibly brought on by the erratic and sudden QSY/QRT approach among some of the operators. It just seemed that you never knew how long they would be on a particular band or mode, so you had to work them when you could get them. No sitting back and waiting for the crowds to thin out. I never heard them begging for contacts as is often the case in the last days of a DXpedition.

 

To the Operators credit, they put in many long hours under arduous conditions. With only four operators they put in an incredible amount of air time and worked very hard. I think their number of unique contacts could have been increased had they been more consistent and operated on the higher bands more effectively, but again, their goals were the low bands and six meters and to that end they succeeded.

 

 

Thank you,

 

Paul N6PSE
