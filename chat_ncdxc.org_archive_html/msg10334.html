I've been helping Pat sell his ham
gear in preparation for a move out of 
their home (they plan to rent it) to
live on a sail boat that they have 
bought.

Among the stuff is a very clean
Heathkit SB300 that Pat said didn't 
work. I listed it that way and got no
responses, so during quiet periods 
of the VHF contest this weekend, I fired it
up. It wasn't easy -- it 
requires an older style line cord, and, as it turns
out, the switch 
contacts were so dirty they wouldn't even pass 120VAC!

Once
I got it running, I let it cook overnight to reform lytics, and 
this morning,
hooked an antenna to it. It was mid-morning, so there were 
several informal
nets on 40M. Other switches were quite oxidized, so 
operation was erratic
until I hit them with Deoxit and gave them some 
exercise. Ditto for the pots,
which I also exercised.

So, I pulled out the Windex, sprayed it on some paper
towels, and 
proceeded to clean the dust from exposed surfaces. The radio
doesn't 
hear much on the higher bands, so there's probably a weak tube or
more, 
and some caps that should be replaced. I made no attempt at alignment --

life has taught me that it if ain't broke, don't fix it -- and I don't 
have
the manual for it. The underside of the chassis reveals nice clean

construction, so this is no disaster that needs to be fixed. All the 
tubes
light up.

All the crystals are in place, and the filters are the "larger"
ones, 
which are considered "better" -- 404-M201 (AM), 404-200 (SSB), and

404-M202P23 (400 Hz CW).

Bottom line - this is a nice clean vintage RX. Ham
bands only, pre-WARC, 
so no 30, 17, or 12, and no 160M. It needs work, but it
is working. Six 
SB-300s in less than "good" condition have sold on EBay for
price + 
shipping ranging between $153 and $220.

This RX has to be worth
$175 if you want it, and I'd like to get Pat the 
money for it. I'd also like
to keep it local -- I don't know where I 
would find a box to put it in.
:)

73, Jim K9YC

I have a little music recording studio at
home, including keyboards, amplifier, external speakers, etc.   My 14-channel
mixer is a Mackie 1402VLZ, which I've used for years without issues.  
Lately
it's been acting up.  Poor switch contacts, intermittent faders, dirty pots,
etc.  I've tried dusting, blowing, and cleaning several ways, but never got it
right -- until I saw this
video.

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I95Zo6x-qJM

Encouraged and feeling
adventurous -- I negotiated with Celestine to use our dishwasher Saturday.  I
placed the Mackie upside down on the top shelf, popped in some detergent, and
set it on Pots and Pans -- Full Cycle.  Pressed start and walked away.  One hour
later took out the still hot but gleaming mixer and set it out in the sun to
bake for several hours.  Gave it a final compressed air treatment and plugged it
in.

Wow.  Amazing.  Works perfectly.  No sticky switches or faders.  Balanced
full output.  Sounds better than ever before.  And looks brand new.  The XYL was
happy too -- mostly that I didn't ruin the dishwasher.

So if you've got a
problem dust-collector and a little courage, give it a try.  Just be
careful.

Can't wait to try this method with my K3.  : >)

73,
John,
K6MM

50+ years ago I had a
summer job at Hewlett Packard stuffing PC boards and
soldering all of the
components and wires to the board.  Next step was a
trip to the dishwasher.  HP
ran all of their PC boards through the
dishwasher, the few parts that couldn't
survive the washer were put on at
the very end of the process.

Another
cleaning process that seems more radical than the dishwasher is
spraying the
really grimy ones with Gunk, an engine degreaser, followed by a
gentle wash
with the hose then a trip to the dishwasher.

73, Jim W6EB
