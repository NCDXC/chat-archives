On Thu, Feb 29, 2024 at 6:10 PM Rick NK7I via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

Hi Will,

 

There are a LOT of variables in your simple question (which IS a valid question).  This is something that all hams that operate on HF, have to learn.

 

As a GENERAL rule (not a law, rules change easily) the higher bands (10, 12, 15, 17) open our morning from the EAST and migrate west, with the sun (starting low, until finally 10M).  So on any given band you might work EI in the early (daylight) morning, EL Liberia in the late morning (some S AF too) then South America noonish followed by a dabble of Awz and Kiwi then Asia in the late afternoon into the evening.

 

Then 20 and lower (30, 40, 60, 80, 160), the opposite happens, they follow the shadow of the sun (although 20 and 30 can be open 24/7 for weeks; that’s a solar cycle thing along with time of year).

 

I presume you do not have a directional antenna (as I recall) which while you would be weaker, it’s simpler because you don’t care if its long or short path; just what you can/not hear.

 

When and if your station expands to a directional antenna and an amplifier; you’ll understand by then a little more how the bands play (which changes according to the solar cycle, solar storms and some of it guess and luck too; black magic).  Long path means more miles, which generally requires more signal (the amp).

 

The twice daily transition from short to long (and back) timing varies by band too.  So hopefully you’ll understand why this is such a complex question.  There is a LOT of stuff in play.

 

I have not found VOACAP (or HAMCAP) to be all the good at predicting coverage because there is always something not in the equation.  Just get on the air and LISTEN or call CQ to see who (where) answers.  Often, they are flat dead WRONG, leave room for magic (and disappointment).  SWAG = Scientific Wild Arsed Guess rules apply.

 

So again, the best tool, is get on the air (even listen); take some notes and learn where you hear, when, on what bands…

 

To REALLY understand propagation takes a long time, careful study and still; the radio can be magic.  And that, is part of the fun.

 

73,

Rick nk7i
