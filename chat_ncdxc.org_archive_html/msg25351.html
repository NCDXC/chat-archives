Paul,

As a relatively recent DXer, here are the benefits I've found from the QRZ logbook:

1. Some DX stations only use QRZ's log, and don't participate in LOTW or Clublog. When I work a station and don't hear the RR73 or 73 (or work them in a CW or SSB pileup and am not 100% sure they got my call right), it's great when they show up on the QRZ log. It saves having to send an email, or send a QSL card worrying whether it will come back with the dreaded "NIL". If they're in the QRZ log, I can send my QSL card (and greenstamps) with confidence.

2. You can create customized pages on the QRZ log. For example, I have mine set up to show only confirmed QSO's outside North America, and excluding JA. It reduces clutter and helps me quickly find the DX QSO's I'm interested in.

3. The DXKeeper log and JT Alert software that I use can be configured to automatically upload every logged QSO to QRZ.  I don't even have to think about it.

4. The QRZ log is super easy to sync with LOTW. 

5. Although the QRZ.com "awards" are sort of meaningless, they do have nifty online maps that show your confirmed QSO's in green (it counts both LOTW confirmation and QRZ.com confirmation), unconfirmed QSO's in yellow, and needed DX entities in red. Yeah, I know, half the club has wall-to-wall green around the entire globe, but for us "novices" with fewer than 300 entities, it's fun to track our progress filling in a continent with green. And it's a great visual aid to figure out which parts of the world you are reaching (or not) on CW, SSB, and digital.

5. If you're trying to figure out how to QSL with a station, or learn more about the op, it's just one click to open their QRZ.com page.

So in sum, it's not as useful as LOTW or Clublog on a day to day basis, but it is useful, and fun to use, especially if you can figure out a way to get your logging software to upload QSO's automatically. If not, it's easy to just upload from LOTW by just entering your password. And I'm pretty sure it's free to use.

Just my 2 cents.

73,
Andy K6ZP
