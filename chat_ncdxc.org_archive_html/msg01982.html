Paul, I’ll wade into this debate with a contrarian position.  I don’t think we should even have a “DXpedition Of The Year” award at all.  DXpeditions themselves are so different in their circumstances that I think it’s impossible to fairly compare them.  It’s all too subjective.  Personally, I voted for PT0S because I was impressed with the physical difficulties they faced and overcame.  But who is to say that made them a “better” DXpedition than 7O6T who figured out a way to cut through Yemini politics and get a valid license when scores of others have failed.  And is that “better” than NH8S who worked zillions of Zone 3 stations because they had a propagation pipeline to the West Coast?  Or the 6O0CW guys who had to make do with limited antennas because if they went outside the walls of their small compound they might get shot?

 

To my thinking, a “DXpedition Of The Year” award creates a competition where none should exist.  We should be thankful to all those DXpeditioners who went to great lengths to give us QSOs with rare places we needed.  There’s nothing to be gained by saying that one of these efforts was “better” than all the others.

 

IMHO,

 

Rusty, W6OAT

Was PT0S the best Dxpedition of 2012?
This past weekend, the NCDXC honored PT0S as our Dxpedition of the Year for 2012. This year was an incredible year for DXpeditions with superb activations by HK0NA, 7O6T, NH8S, ZL9HR and others.
 
Here are the overall results of your voting over January and February:

The first problem that I see is that only about 1/3 of the NCDXC members bothered to vote for this award. What is it about our club that 2/3 of the membership did not bother to vote? Only you that did not vote know the answers.
In 2011, when the NCDXC Dxpedition of the Year award was resurrected, the purpose was to recognize those DXpeditions that made an extra effort to work our members and to satisfy the needs of the Club. We had all witnessed DXpeditions in previous years visit much needed entities and either make no effort to work Zone 3, or give a strong preference to their home countries. Some of you grumbled quite loudly in the case of the FT5GA Glorioso Dxpedition in 2009 for example.
In resurrecting the Dxpedition of the Year award, we hoped to bring more visibility to the NCDXC and to create an awareness of the award to the extent that future DXpeditions would make an attempt to satisfy the needs of our members by working Zone 3.
We hoped that our annual Dxpedition of the Year Award would become coveted by DXpeditions and in time would become a prestigious event.
Those of you, who know me, know that I will tell you what I think. I think this year we got it wrong, and I don’t think the Dxpedition that we honored met the goals of this award and was far inferior to HK0NA, 7O6T, NH8S and ZL9HR who were all much more worthy of this award.
Those sentiments were echoed to me by some of my Visalia dinner and breakfast companions who were all leaders or participants in other major DXpeditions. They were quite surprised at our choice of DXpeditions to honor.
 
Was PT0S that good?
While two of the PT0S team members are world-class CW/Low band operators and performed flawlessly, there was some erratic operating by other team members on the higher bands. Some of us remember PT0S’s signal building on the West Coast, when suddenly they would announce QRX. Soon, they were found working their PY friends on six meters. Several times we saw them put their desire to work PY and South America on six meters ahead of their desire to work the West Coast. None of the other DXpeditions in the top tier did this.

As you can see, we have voted to honor the Dxpedition with the fewest operators, fewest total QSOs, fewest number of unique contacts and the fewest number of QSOs provided to Zone 3.
There are some that believe two items of Dxpedition performance measurement are the percent of unique contacts and the percent of Zone 3 contacts.
I don’t feel that these two items are valid measurement points. For example with percent of unique contacts this figure increases with the fewer total contacts that a Dxpedition makes. There are only so many unique contacts available to be worked. If a Major Dxpedition such as HK0NA, 7O6T or ZL9HR makes a lot of contacts on many bands and modes, their percentage of unique contacts will diminish as their number of overall contacts increases. Conversely, a small Dxpedition that makes fewer contacts will have a higher percentage of unique contacts. In my view, this is not a valid measuring device for Dxpedition performance.
The percent of Zone 3 contacts as part of the total contacts is also not valid as proximity and propagation are factors unique to each Dxpedition. A South Pacific Dxpedition such as NH8S will have a high number of Zone 3 contacts just based on their proximity to Zone 3. A remote Dxpedition such as 7O6T will have a correspondingly smaller percentage of Zone 3 contacts because Zone 3 is further away from Yemen. For a Zone 27 Dxpedition such as 7O6T to make many contacts on Zone 3, they must apply an extra amount of effort and focus on doing so. For 7O6T to make considerable more Zone 3 contacts than PT0S, indicates to me that the extra effort was made by the 7O6T team. PT0S was much closer to Zone 3 than 7O6T was. In my view, PT0S did not try to satisfy Zone 3 and fell short of the goals of this award.
We all know that the costs to carry out these major DXpeditions were enormous. As a Dxpeditioner, in my many presentations, we try to convey to you just how costly these major DXpeditions are to carry out. We try to help you understand all of the complexities involved in conducting a major Dxpedition.
In examining the tallies of your votes and given the number of outstanding DXpeditions to consider this year, I feel that your voting did not accomplish the goals of this year’s award. You’ve recognized a small team that fulfilled a niche need on the low bands. Some of our members with access to quite capable stations were not able to get a single contact with PT0S.
This was not a main-stream Dxpedition as were the others considered. They announced that they were going to focus on the low bands and indeed, they did.
After the award was made, I asked some of you why PT0S was the best Dxpedition of the Year. Some of you were rather shocked at our club’s results and felt that it was undeserving. Others were quite satisfied. Others felt the need to criticize their broad pileups taking up huge portions of some of the higher bands. Your comments indicate that you liked their LoTW upload relatively soon. A few of you told me that PT0S received your vote because you got a much needed 160 meter contact. Some of you told me that you wanted to honor George-AA7JV and Tomi-HA7RY for their past operations. Some of you enjoyed George’s blog posts and felt it added to the insight into the danger and difficulty of their operation.
There was considerable and spirited discussion among the Club’s Board of Directors after the voting was concluded. Some were quite happy with the result. Others, including myself questioned our process if only 35% of the club voted and the majority of those voters voted for other DXpeditions instead of PT0S.
As club members, we all get one vote. Some of us used our vote to recognize the best DXpeditions of the Year; however 17 of our votes have caused us to recognize perhaps something else. Perhaps we need to examine the process for future awards.
We all have opinions and now you’ve heard mine.
 
Paul N6PSE
