Dear Smart Folks,


We need some help.  I don't know if the City of Cupertino is playing games, or whether I'm just naive to this.


My friend, Walt (K6WGY) is trying to put up a tower in Cupertino, and I'm trying to help him with it.  He's applied for a permit, and so far things look good.  They want him to ensure that the tower is grounded, so they've specified a requirement of grounding rods at each of the (3) guy-line bases.  OK, a little strange, but no biggie.  But here's the catch:  they also want him to ensure that the resistance of the grounds do not exceed 10 ohms.  Does anyone know how to calculate this, or produce a measurement than can demonstrate this to the city?


From the City's Permit Review, the specific language the city used in this requirement is:

"Per TIA/EIA 222-G, builder will verify that the total resistance will not exceed 10 ohms, minimum.  The total resistance of the structure's primary grounds as referenced to remote earth should be measured or calculated in accordance with the [IEEE] Standard 142-1991, the standard states."


This IEEE Standard 142-1991 is basically a 215 page book, with fairly easy to read text, but poor instructions regarding the application of formulas used to calculate such things.  It seems orientated towards those who already have an EE degree.  And much above my head.


Anyone, please help!
