Based on practical experience, and with due respect to Joe Taylor, the break-even point seems to lie around F/H two-stream. Based on seat-of-the-pants observation, the SF stream is more vulnerable to random noise and QRM. A single brief tune-up tone or loud pop can defeat decoding. From a practical standpoint, and if I were a DXp operator, I would switch to SF when I began to see a significant number of stations not copying my reports while two-streaming.  

That said, if the path is favorable with good SNR reports, I would choose SF in a heartbeat simply for the excellent throughput.

73, Bruce K3NQ

Decoding standard FT8, stops at about -24 dB (-27 on the 'improved' version, different measuring system).  Decoding SF mode stops at about -16 dB.  That is 8 dB of difference.

So the potential +10 dB gain is almost completely lost (with an 8 dB hit) because it's wider.  Only those who can hear well, can win; which is not the majority of stations.

Wider makes it more fragile, easier to lose some bits in the packet (noise or DQRM or random tuning up).

Absolutely repeatable; SF provides almost no gain, except to those who can hear REALLY well.

73,
Rick nk7i

SuperFox mode provides a system gain of about +10 dB compared to Fox-and-Hounds mode. This is because SuperFox transmits a full power constant-envelope waveform, while Fox-and-Hounds transmits up to five streams, each 50 Hz wide.”
