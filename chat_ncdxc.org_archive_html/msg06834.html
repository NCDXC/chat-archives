First of all, I hope that some of you were able to work us on our recent Dxpedition to Antigua (V25M/V25N). As always, we had a GREAT time, with our usual combination of radio activity, tennis, local cuisine, and plenty of personal relaxation and fun.

Photos can be seen at:

https://www.flickr.com/photos/51562235@N04/sets/72157644881280411/

You can “mouse over” any photo for a description, and click on them for a larger version. Radio operating statistics are also included in the photo set.

We left from San Francisco on May 6th, traveling via Chicago to Antigua. Going to the Caribbean from the West Coast is usually a grueling trip: a "red-eye" flight to the East Coast, followed by a layover of 3-6 hours where you are trying to stay awake, and then another 3 hour flight, plus immigration, customs, car rental and finding your destination on unfamiliar roads with no road signs while driving on the wrong (left) side of the road. After having done this enough times, we chose to stay overnight in Chicago. This made life MUCH easier. Instead of a red-eye, we got a good night's sleep, and then caught a (very civilized) 11 am flight to Antigua where we got through the border formalities quite easily.

Finding the rental house was more of a challenge. Like many Caribbean islands, Antigua hasn't quite gotten the concept of road signs--there are (almost) none. Directions involve things like "make the third right at the big Rubi's (gas) station." Well, you're never quite sure of what constitutes an intersection to count, and we discovered that there is a big Rubi's gas station at virtually EVERY intersection!

We finally made it to the rented villa, and it was well worth it. As usual, Anna did an incredible job of finding luxurious accommodations with great radio capability. This was a spacious, airy villa right on the Atlantic coast, with a nice lawn area suitable for the antenna, plus a hugh "infinity" pool, and a den that was perfect for the "shack."


We operated 40-10m, CW/SSB/RTTY, using an Icom IC-7000 and a Deluxe Buddipole Kit. I generally use two 22” arms, and the long whips in an “L-vertical” configuration. It needs no coil on 15/12/10m, just a turn or so on 17m, and more on 20, 30, and 40m. The SWR is below 1.5:1 on all bands, with no tuner and no TRSB. Sometimes my home-determined settings will vary depending on the local terrain, but this time everything was “spot on”. We had absolutely no radio or antenna problems on Antigua. Of course, with the constant trade winds, you have to guy the mast, but you could (almost) get away with a single guy line, as the wind direction NEVER changes.

The weather was fine, with occasional short rain showers, but pleasant temperatures for the entire time. Radio conditions were GREAT!! We had regular daily openings on 20/17/15/12/10 meters to the USA, Europe, and lots of JA (especially on 15 and 12 meters). Evenings were good on 20 and 30 meters; we worked a few stations on 40, but we have never had really good propagation from the Caribbean on that band. Perhaps it's the time of year, or that we don't want to stay up all night while on holiday. 

Here's the numbers (combined from V25M and V25N, see photos for more stats, and country/continent breakdown):

Total QSOs: 6,215 in 8 days of casual operation

CW: 49%, SSB: 38%, RTTY: 13%. (Most of the CW is from me, most of the SSB is from Anna, who attracts pileups on SSB like flies to honey.)

10 meters: 11%, 12 meters: 13%, 15 meters: 14%, 17 meters: 27%, 20 meters: 26%, 30 meters: 8%, 40 meters: 1%.

As you can see, about half of the QSOs were on the WARC bands (12/17/30 meters), with 17 being our top performer. This was done consciously. Antigua isn't very rare, but a lot of operations from there are during contests. So while folks may have Antigua "in the log," not many have it on 12/17/30 meters (or RTTY). We got a lot of "thanks for the new band", much more than "thanks for the new one." 

Best hour: 156 QSOs/hour while running CW on 15 meters (that's 2.6 QSOs per minute, sustained). The challenge isn't getting people to call; it's being able to pull one complete, correct callsign out of the pileup without having to ask for fills or repeats. Without question, operating in a contest is the best way to practice for a DXpedition (and vice-versa). 


North America and Europe constituted 90% of our QSOs, but we had a surprising number of JA callers, particularly on 12 and 15 meters. They were very happy to work us, as the JA-Caribbean path is pretty long (almost 10,000 miles). Rarest callsigns--we were called by: 4K6FO (Azerbaijan), A92GE (Bahrain), A45XR (Oman), HB0CC (Liechtenstein), TZ6BB (Mali), and VP8LP (Falkland Islands).

We tried something new this time; the "N1MM Clublog Gateway" application. With this application running, and a working Internet connection, QSOs would be uploaded to Clublog in real-time. That is, within a second or so of hitting the "Log it" key in N1MM, the QSO would show up in the publicly-accessible Clublog database. We include a Clublog lookup widget on our QRZ page, so people could instantly check whether they made it into the log. This worked extremely well, and we will use it again. We saw a number of cluster postings noting this capability, from around the world.

Also, with cluster access, we were able to see all of the comments in our spots, which sometimes helps us out, e.g., to tell us that we are being heard by Asia/Pacific stations that can't get over the US "wall." We would ask the US to stand by for a minute or two to listen for the weaker stations.

Special mention has to go to David Ross, VA3MJR. It seemed like he was following us, on every band and mode. As soon as we started up, or QSY'ed, he would be there to get another QSO in the log. In the end, he made a total of 25 QSOs with both Anna and myself, on every band and mode. We designed and printed up a special QSL certificate and mailed it to him. Thanks, Dave!

On the road between the airport and our villa (which was at the far eastern end of the island), we passed by the V26B contest superstation a number of times. They had a bad fire a while back, and most of the shack was destroyed; I understand that it is partially rebuilt now. There are a few towers with some pretty extensive Yagi arrays (and a few antennas that need some repair). I suspect that most of the Antigua QSOs in peoples' logs in recent years came from that station. Interestingly, it is about as far from the water as you can get in Antigua, but they do pretty well nonetheless.


Of course, this was a holiday, so we didn't spend all of our time on the radio. We always find a tennis court, as we both love to play. We had planned to purchase a "day pass" at one of the local resorts that had tennis facilities, but while driving to our villa we saw this abandoned (and run down) resort for sale, less than half a mile from our house. There was a tennis court there, and it was in decent shape; no major cracks in the surface, and an intact net (the net is usually the first thing to go). It was also unlocked, so we just moseyed down there one day and went at it. No one ever stopped by, and no one bothered us. There wasn't even a place to sit down, but if you look at the photos, you'll see that I found a rusty old boat trailer and sat on the half-buried tire. So we were able to get our "tennis-fix" in.

On some of our trips, our rental villa is near to towns and restaurants. Not so here. Antigua isn't a huge island (unlike Tenerife, where we went last year), but it's about a 45-minute drive end-to-end, and we were all the way at the most isolated (and private) end. So we didn't go out much for dinner, and opted instead to buy food at the local markets and eat in. For one night, we hired a private chef to come to the house and prepare us a feast, including grilled local fish, and lots of little delicacies. (We also had her stock the kitchen with basics on the day we arrived, so we had dinner and the next day's breakfast already there.)

Anna also arranged a spectacular afternoon in the middle of the trip. She hired a 42' catamaran--including skipper and crewman--to take us out on the ocean for a four-hour cruise. (I know, you're already whistling the theme to "Gilligan's Island" in your head.) It was a gorgeous day to spend out on the water, just the two of us on the boat. We could (and did) dive off the side and swim to the beach and back, and just enjoy the wind and surf sitting up front on the "trampoline." For lunch, the skipper set up a barbecue on the aft deck, and prepared grilled, stuffed lobster, and a nice Chardonnay. Not a bad way to spend the day.

We also found a donkey sanctuary on the island. Apparently there is a large, neglected donkey population, and these folks bring the animals into their shelter. You can see pictures on the photo page, above. We "adopted" a donkey: for $25, you get a picture, "biography," and probably a Christmas card from your donkey. There was one sweet, blind donkey named Stevie; he doesn't play the piano, but he does sing in the "choir." (You should hear all of those donkeys braying!)

On August 16th, we headed home. We thought that a 2-hour layover in Miami would be enough, but it was closer than I like. First, it was about a mile walk from the gate to immigration/passport-control. We kept walking down a corridor, expecting that the next turn would be immigration, but it was always another corridor. There were "people-movers" but I hate to think what a physically handicapped person would do here (short of getting a wheelchair). Then there was the "automated kiosk" check-in, followed by a long line to an actual officer.

But that part was easy. Our bags took forever to come out, and apparently they directed my Pelican case (with all the radio gear) to the "special baggage" area without telling us. I had to chase that down, and then head for the customs line. Yikes!! The customs line circled the entire baggage claim area TWICE! There was no way we were going to get through that in time for our flight. Fortunately, a bunch of us in the same situation (transfer passengers with tight connections) convinced an agent to open another line. It still took a half-hour or so, and then a run to the gate, but we made it before they finished boarding.

I was glad to see that our seats weren't in the "crying baby" section of the airplane, but it still turned out to be the flight-from-hell. A woman in the adjacent aisle did not stop talking, loudly, for the entire six hours of the flight home. Everyone was trying to get some sleep, shushing her, but she kept ranting and raving, waving her hands and gushing obscenities. Even with earplugs, I couldn't get any peace. (It became clear that she was truly mentally disturbed, and the flight attendants couldn't do much about it.) But we made it home in one piece, and I have (almost) gotten it out of my mind.


Our next trip is back to Anguilla (VP2EAQ/VP2EAR) for Thanksgiving this November, and we are looking at some *really* interesting places for next year. All of the Antigua logs are uploaded to LotW, Clublog, and eQSL, and paper QSLs should be ready in about a month (they are at the printer as I write this).

If anyone has any specific questions about our operation, please feel free to contact me directly, off-list.

73 de Rich V25M (KE1B)
