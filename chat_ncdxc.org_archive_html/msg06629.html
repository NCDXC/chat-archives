I'm showing more of the extensive modeling I've been working on for the last year or so that is aimed at helping understanding how soil conditions, mounting height, and the availability of skyhooks combine to make verticals better for some situations and horizontal antennas better in others. 

http://paara.org/
This is the third talk in a series of three (so far) on the topic. At Pacificon, I presented the effects of mounting height on verticals with various soil conditions, and showed that on the roof is always better than on the ground.In a second talk, I showed the virtues and shortcomings of the recently popular 43-ft vertical.
While this talk is geared toward hams with relatively limited space and resources -- one or two skyhooks in the 30-40 ft range, and/or the ability to mount a vertical on a roof -- there's also a lot of meat for those who are not so limited. For example, it costs me $700 a day for tree climbers to put pulleys in trees for both ends of a wire antenna. The question is, what's the benefit in dB for getting the antenna higher? Likewise, what's the benefit, in dB, of another 10 ft of tower height on 20M? For 30 more feet? And if my choices are a 40M Yagi on a 70 ft tower and a pair of crossed wire dipoles at 120 ft, which provides the better return on investment? 

73, Jim K9YC
