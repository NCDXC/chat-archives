Paul; I do use some propagation predictions, those on the ARRL web site and I have the VOCAP Software to run more specific charts, only as their stated purpose as guides to "probability" of openings. Of course I know that these are always subject to actual conditions, my 100W power and antenna limitations. I also regularly keep track of the Gray Line status(I using DX Atlas 2.3 Software) Also I most often find that the best predictors are actual "spotting" reports. I regularly consult DXscape, the Reverse Beacons which provide actual, automated information of what is being heard in my general area and I check out spots that are placed here on NCDXC Chat.  I find that the N7TR(Reno, NV) and NQ6N(Pacifica, CA) skimmers tend to be fairly good indicators of what is likely to be heard here in the Northern San Joaquin Valley. In fact they have been very helpful in my recent achievement of DXCC. However I do not use the cluster or Reverse Beacons when in contest, in contests that define Single Operator as not allowed per the contest rules. When in contest I often will tune around and check out what the "big guns" are working and whether or not I can hear the stations they are working. I generally find in most situations I can work what I can hear. I will frequently CQ just a couple of KHz above the big gun and frequently get called by DX that has been drawn into my listening area by the big gun.  Often when I find I have "worked out" what I can hear, and make a band change, that I first check if "big guns" are on the band. If they aren't there the band probably is not open and I try not to waste my time. However I often will tune once through the band and occasionally find (as I have on 10m a few times) that the band is open and other nearby stations haven't recently checked the band. That's kind of my take on things. Bert, K6CSL
