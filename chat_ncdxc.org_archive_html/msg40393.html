Not looking to start WWIII here, but I recall reading that the FCC has rewritten regs to encourage experimentation with novel modes through primarily regulating bandwidth, and not actual emission type. Mea maxima culpa: I clicked first as well, before the frequency penetrated my thick skull. 
I support band plans as opposed to anarchy, but here’s Google AI’s take:


The FCC regulates the use of amateur radio bands primarily based on bandwidth, not by prohibiting data in the segments allocated for voice (phone). This approach fosters experimentation, a core principle of amateur radio, by allowing licensees flexibility in how they use the assigned frequencies, as long as they stay within the technical limits. 
Key aspects of the regulations
  • Emphasis on bandwidth: For most bands, especially those below 30 MHz (the high frequency or HF bands), the FCC has moved away from regulating the data symbol rate (baud rate). Instead, it has set a maximum bandwidth limit of 2.8 kilohertz (kHz) for data emissions.
  • HF band modes: The allocation charts for the HF bands specify sub-bands for different modes, including Morse code (CW), data, and phone (voice). While these are used as band plans to promote cooperation, the FCC rules authorize digital data on frequencies within the "phone" sub-bands, provided the emissions meet the 2.8 kHz bandwidth limit.
  • VHF and UHF bands: For the Very High Frequency (VHF) and Ultra High Frequency (UHF) bands, there are generally no legal bandwidth limits, enabling the use of wider-bandwidth modes for both voice and data.
  • Voluntary band plans: The FCC does not enforce band plans, which are voluntary agreements among amateur operators, often managed by organizations like the ARRL. These plans recommend which frequencies to use for voice, digital, or CW to promote good operating practice and reduce interference. For example, a band plan might suggest specific sub-bands for data modes to keep them separate from phone traffic. However, as long as an operator follows FCC technical rules, they are within legal boundaries even if they don't follow the band plan.
