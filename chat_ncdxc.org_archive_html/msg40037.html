One seemingly unknown fact is:

Regardless of where they live, LoTW is free to use by any registered user.  One does not need to be an ARRL member to use LoTW. 
The application keeps track of your DXCC accomplishments, match QSOs as they come in, etc.   
However, if a US user wants to apply for an award - ARRL membership is required for the US users, and there are associated fees for the transaction.

About 2 months ago the ARRL sent a fund raising  appeal to non members that are registered on LoTW. 
The email went to tens of thousands of users, last I heard the response has been very disappointing.

+ Every time some poor soul asks for help with LoTW on amateur radio social media, the result is torrent of messages lambasting the ARRL for failing to improve LoTW's terrible usability.  If the response to the ARRL's fund-raising appeal was disappointing, that's hardly a surprise.

My opinion:  ARRL should implement an annual non member user fee, $15 a year. 

+ Besides pulling the plug, I can't think of a quicker way to kill LoTW.

+ In late 2012, LoTW began losing submitted QSOs. As a result, users began pre-emptively resubmitting their QSOs, resulting in even more submitted QSOs being lost. Eventually, the LoTW Server crashed and could not be restarted. Fearful that ARRL management would permanently shut LoTW down, I drove to Newington, met with then-CEO Dave K1ZZ, and convinced him to let me help. An ARRL-LoTW Committee was formed with me as its only volunteer member. The recovery plan we developed was to first improve LoTW's availability, and then address its poor usability. We convinced the ARRL Board to fund two dedicated developers; those developers increased measured availability from less than 90% to more than 99.9%, and doubled the rate at which submitted QSOs were processed.

+ The ARRL-LoTW Committee also developed a plan to make LoTW self-funding: offer an optional annual subscription to a basket of premium services - like direct communication with QSO partners, log backup and recovery, immediate notification of ARRL award qualification, and access to realtime dashboards for popular on-air activities like NPOTA,  Centennial, and Grid Chase. Feedback from LoTW users - in person at Dayton, and online - showed enough interest to fund all of LoTW's annual operational expenses, including the two developers.

+ Sadly, in 2017 ARRL management re-assigned the two LoTW developers to other projects - a second attempt at re-implementing the DXCC system (which failed), and the deployment of Personify - Association Management software whose advertised benefit was "ARRL members will be able to renew, purchase, and donate in a single transaction". Disbanding the LoTW development team meant no improvements to usability, and no basket of premium services to fund LoTW's maintenance and continued development.  

+ Moving LoTW to the cloud last month means that LoTW will be easier to maintain, and will more rapidly catch up with post-contest QSO submission surges - but did nothing to improve LoTW's usability. It does prove that the current implementation of LoTW can be incrementally improved, if development resources are ever provided to do so.
