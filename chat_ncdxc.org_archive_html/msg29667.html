I never suggested deleting islands that were off limits to landing, only allowing an offshore operation.  And my point about propagation is that a few hundred feet is completely insignificant and undetectable over an HF ionospheric path.  I’m only talking about islands, whose governments do allow amateur radio but don’t allow landings, or for which landings are virtually insurmountable or cost prohibitive.  My suggestion has nothing to do with mainland countries that do not allow amateur radio at all.

Realistically “Close” doesn’t count – you need to be there – with a license and a permit and all that stuff.  The RIBs are interesting and probably good enough – but there are so many ways to “cheat” if you open it up that it really won’t be anything anymore.  If I fly over in my “permitted” Boeing 777 – does that count?  If I sneak up to the border with no license in hand and hop over and make a contact and hop back – does that count?  We don’t want to encourage the sort of behavior that will upset nation states and possibly get people killed or imprisoned.  You are free to suggest what you will but think about all the people who wanted to work Mt. Athos over the years – they finally got their chance and many got cards – I did.  Would I be happy that you now say screw it - it is deleted because I wasn’t home that day to work it?  Probably not.  It is a long game.  You can’t work em all in a weekend.  It may take 20 years to get DPRK – you might get it tomorrow.  Maybe that is part of the fun.  I remember when 5A and ZA and SV2/A were “impossible” – now they are all over the place.  Just deleting the hard ones so a bunch of newbies can get to the top of the honor roll in no time flat seems a bit much.  It is not WAS – in WAS you can work em all on 160 in a weekend – perhaps an evening – really not hard – I’ve done it.  But who cares about WAS?

On Sun, Jun 4, 2023 at 10:12 AM Steve Jones via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

Dear Ken,

I was struck by Dave Minster’s take on the Bouvet operation in his May QST editorial.  I fully support his comment questioning why being anchored 300 feet offshore doesn’t currently count for DXCC.  I have been chasing DX since 1962 when I worked my first JA from California.  I’m now at 333 current entities, with a few more worked but not yet confirmed.  My joy in working DX and chasing ATNO’s on DXpeditions is based on fine tuning my station so that I have the best chance of Mother Nature allowing me to work the distant station.  My personal analogy is fishing:  You can have the perfect Powell fly rod, and the perfect fly you tied that very morning based on the bugs you just saw hovering over the water.  But if Mother Nature doesn’t put that trout right there where it can see your fly, there is no catch!  Sure, part of my DX chasing is competitive, when I use my skills to place my call at just the right place and time to get ahead of all the other callers.  But my main reward is knowing my little signal went through all those thousands of miles of ionosphere, aurora, absorptive D-Layer, maybe a CME or two, to finally reach that distant operator’s ears and have him/her answer me back through all those same vagaries of propagation.  Whether the DX station was on dirt or rock or on a ship’s deck few hundred feet offshore makes absolutely no difference to my radio operating accomplishment.  And allowing DX credit for ships anchored off islands with sensitive ecologies, where access is prohibited by governmental agencies, would be to promote protection of earth’s delicate environment, a positive value we should all support.  Please consider changing the DXCC Rules to allow for offshore DXpedition operations.

Vy 73,

Steve Jones

N6SJ
