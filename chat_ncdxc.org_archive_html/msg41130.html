some years ago, I had a repeated series of such interactions with the HRD crew, which lead me to adopt DXLab - and I'm very glad I
did.

+ For members not familiar with DXLab, it's a free suite of 8 interoperating applications that I began developing in 1990 just after
getting my Novice ticket, striving to confirm 100 DXCC entities so I could join the NCDXC. K6LLK's packet cluster was just starting
to replace the club's repeater as the primary venue for tag-team DXing. Even at slow packet cluster speeds, I found it difficult to
ingest and exploit information rolling in one spot at a time. So I created SpotCollector, which in real time creates a database with
one entry for each active station operating in a particular mode near  particular frequency. One can query these Entries in many
ways. In particular, you can

1. identify the operating pattern of a needed DX station

2. identify actual propagation openings between your QTH and a needed DX station

The frequency-time intersection between operating pattern and propagation openings identifies where and when to listen, often
enabling you to make the needed QSO before the needed station is spotted and the cluster hordes descend on the frequency.

SpotCollector automatically interoperates with DXKeeper, the Suite's logging component - so it knows what awards you are pursuing,
and can highlight/announce active stations with whom a QSO would advance your award progress. Log a QSO with a needed station, and
the knowledge of what you need is automatically updated.

This video I made with Bill AJ8B describes these capabilities in more detail:

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-0q3lJS65ds

If you're interested, take a look at

https://www.dxlabsuite.com/dxlabwiki/GettingStarted

In terms of software development philosophy, my priorities are answering questions from users and correcting reported defects in
both applications and documentation. Across the entire DXLab Suite, the total number of reported but uncorrected defects is at this
moment 0. I practice iterative, user-driven development: frequent releases bearing new functionality often suggested and refined
during online discussions with users. During 2024, I made 22 public releases of DXLab applications.
