On Feb 3, 2023, at 12:17 PM, Sam S. <wb6rjh@gmail.com> wrote:

I expect our local pilot will give an update during the meeting this evening, but I just saw the latest update on the Bouvet DXpedition on DXnews:

https://dxnews.com/3y0j/


I don’t have much more than what has been said on Facebook, The Daily DX, and others. The Internet connection is down (Starlink; they were/are preparing to move it from the ship to the island. The *service* is still there.) All communications have been coming via satellite telephone from Ken to Morten (in Norway) and Steve (on the East Coast). We share all of that on a private Slack channel, dedicated to the worldwide pilots.

Most of the sat phone bandwidth is being used for ship-to-shore coordination, and they don’t have a lot of time (or a lot of information) to pass to the pilots.

Bottom line, it is apparently more difficult than they had hoped to get all of the people and gear onto the island, and then to haul it to the top of the glacier. The plan was to spend today (Friday, EU time) in an all-hands-on-deck, ten hour day effort to move as much as they could. The landing site is making that difficult (rocks, rough waters, cold and high winds), and they were able to get only four people over on any given day. And now a storm is moving in, with freezing rain and  60 knot (~70 mph) winds. 

They are abiding by their rule that there must always be 3 days of food, water, and shelter for every person on the island at any given time (in case of a rapid weather change that strands the group), and that’s why they only had 4 team members instead of the whole gang; they couldn’t move enough food/water/shelter for more than 4 people in those rough conditions. 

The good news: They are there. They are safe. They have a plan, and a backup plan, and a backup to the backup plan. It will take longer than hoped for to start operations, but they have a lot of time. They may start more slowly, with fewer stations and ops, as they use the rest of the team to continue transferring gear to the island.

There is a reason why this is such a rare destination; it’s REALLY hard to get there, and REALLY had to set up a big operation. And this is Antarctic SUMMER! Winter? Fuggedaboutit!

I will inform everyone I can if/when I get new information, typically relayed from sat phone conversations with Steve, N2AJ. Steve, however, has been very good about posting the latest info on the team's Facebook page, so if you have Facebook access, that’s probably the best place to look. (And I fully understand if you don’t have or want a Facebook account; I don’t have one either, for privacy reasons.)
