Hi Jonathon – what a mess.  Glad to hear the Force-12 held up just fine.

 

I have a Force-12 C3S at 40 feet.  A number of years ago (2004?) here at the lower end of the Los Gatos gulch the wind was recorded at 83mph peak.  It blew 5 cement tiles off my roof and $10k later I had a new roof.  The Force-12 was nested at around 25 feet and did just fine – tips of the elements point into the wind.  But the gust were making it howl!

 

Two-story house.

 

BTW – one of those tiles was a rake-tile, hangs over the edge of the roof.  I came down and blew a hole in the garden window.  The double paned top section exploded throwing glass almost 20 feet from the kitchen into the family room!  Sheila had just left the kitchen.  I went out with a piece of plywood and two bricks to cover the hole to stop the rain from coming into the kitchen.  The wind was sucking the air out of my mouth.  Getting a little scary!

 

But through all that the Force-12 was undamaged and ham radio activities continued.

 

Emergency fix to the roof was made the next day (tarps) to stop the water coming into the master bedroom.  This is California – what’s up with that!

 

73,

Bob – W6OPO

I would love to have a Steppir.  But I live in Colorado at 6,000' ASL and we get 100mph winds once in a couple years and 70mph+ several times a year.  I don't think a Steppir would survive 100mph.  On Jan. 9th I lost my Bencher Skyhawk in a 109mph wind storm.  My standard (100mph) Force 12 Delta 230/240 survived.  I ordered a custom heavy duty Optibeam OB9-5 to replace the Skyhawk.  I don't know the wind survival rating on my new Optibeam however I do know the 20m element starts off at 35mm or 1-3/8" OD.  Attached is a picture of my destroyed Skyhawk.  You can also see the slight bend in the 2" boom on the Skyhawk.  The elements were pointed into the wind.  The undamaged Force 12 sits just below it.
