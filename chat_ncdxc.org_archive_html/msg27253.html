Paul -

   Thanks for passing along this really neat trick - it gives a small of ray of hope to those of us Little Pistols looking for any advantage! But I have a naive technical question about why it works:

   If I'm doing my math right, in your original example FT8WW was transmitting FT8 tones at 10,131,400 Hz (10.131 MHz + 400 Hz). You tuned your rig to 1000 Hz below 10.131 (10,130,000 Hz), and then transmitted your FT8 tones at 700 Hz, which would be 10,130,700 Hz. This would be 300 Hz below FT8WW's rig tuning, or "-300 Hz" on his receive waterfall? 

   My rig here consists of: Rx audio out (analog) to a PC sound capture "card" (SignalLink USB) and into my PC. Assuming my receiver has an SSB audio bandpass of 3 KHz, my normal FT8 waterfall displays (roughly) 0 - 3000 Hz, relative to my Receiver's tuned frequency. So if my Rx is tuned to 10.131 MHz, I think I am "seeing" 10,131,000 - 10,134,000 Hz on my waterfall. So it seems like your trick wouldn't work if you called me - your tones at 10,130,700 Hz would look like a "negative frequency" (relative to my Rx tuned frequency) that wouldn't be within my Rx audio passband. Granted, if I were to also detune my Rx frequency I could bring your 10,130,700 Hz FT8 tones into my received audio passband, but since my audio passband is fixed width (3 KHz), that would be at the expense of losing callers at the higher end of my original waterfall.

So my questions are:

   a) do you know something about FT8WW's receiver that tells you he is capable of "seeing" (decoding) FT8 frequencies below his base Rx frequency? If so:

   b) does that mean he can't "hear" (decode) up to a full 3000 Hz above his (announced) base Rx frequency, because he has traded it off to be able to hear "negative" FT8 frequencies. Or does his rig have a wider audio passband? (and how would you know?)


Or, (c) do I just not understand how this works?  I am pretty new to FT8, so this is entirely possible  ;-)    Not doubting your results - just wanting to learn why!

   Thanks,

   - Richard, NC6RJ

Guys, this technique really does work. I received this confirmation from Rusty W6OAT this morning:

I have just spent about an hour calling FT8WW on 20m FT8 using my "normal" method of calling about 1000 Hz above his frequency.  NADA!  So I decided to give your method a try.  I tuned my transceiver to 500 Hz below his announced freq (which then put his tones around 800 Hz on my waterfall and called him about 200 Hz below his transmit freq.  He answered me on my second call!  THANK YOU!

73,
Rusty, W6OAT

Also, yesterday morning Richard WB6EWM used this technique to call FT8WW at 144 on the waterfall and after two calls, he made his contact. The key is to find a clear spot as close to but lower than FT8WW's lowest transmit frequency. If he is at 312 for example, call at 250 or lower by moving your VFO to 14.084 and calling at 1250 (assuming you see FT8WW at 1350)

Good luck!

On Sun, Dec 25, 2022 at 6:14 PM Paul N6PSE <pauln6pse@gmail.com> wrote:
I've worked FT8WW on 30 and 20 meters with about five minutes of calling. 

Here is how I did it:

1) I decoded him as ODD so I called Even (critical step) 

2) I saw that he was spotted at 10.131 and was 300-400 Hz on the waterfall. 

3) I purposely put my VFO at 10.130 which put him 1400 Hz +/- on the waterfall. 

4) Recognizing that he is NOT Fox and Hound but is using MSHV, I called him below his QRV frequency on the waterfall. Around 700 Hz. I had no competition and a clear signal to him. 

5) Amid the huge pileup and overlap of signals, he saw my signal in the clear and worked me quickly. 

6) I did the same on 20 meters. He was at 14.085. I put my VFO at 14.084 and saw him around 1300 Hz on the waterfall. I called around 800 Hz, completely in the clear and quickly had a QSO. 

Try this technique, it works marvelous as long as the DX is NOT Fox and Hound. 

73 and good luck!

Paul N6PSE
