First of all, I hope that some of you were able to work us on our recent Buddipole-Dxpeditions to Tenerife (EA8/KE1B and EA8/W6NN) and Gozo Island, Malta (9H3MMM and 9H3NN). I know that it was a LOT more difficult to work North America (and the West Coast in particular) from there, as opposed to the Caribbean. Regardless, We had a GREAT time, with our usual combination of radio activity, tennis, local cuisine, and plenty of personal relaxation and fun.

Photos can be seen at:

http://www.flickr.com/photos/51562235@N04/sets/72157635390513391/

You can “mouse over” any photo for a description, and click on them for a larger version. Radio operating statistics are also included in the photo set.

Anna actually went to Tenerife alone on August 2, to spend a week with some of her family that met her there from her native Hungary. They spent a week without me (and without ham radio), at a family resort on the south side of the island. It's along trip from here on the West Coast--San Francisco to Chicago (4 hours), then non-stop to Madrid (8 hours), then a plane change for the 3 hour flight to Tenerife. You leave here at 9 am, and arrive in Tenerife around 2 pm the next day. Tough trip!

I followed a week later on the same itinerary, and brought the radio equipment with me. Anna's family went back to Europe, and we moved into our rented "finca"; a VERY traditional, Canary-style cottage up at the top of a very steep hill, around 600 meters elevation. (It's the first photo in the set, above.) It was (intentionally) NOT the sort of luxurious place we have rented in the past in the Caribbean; rather, it was kind of rustic, with a very old-world feel. The house was quite typical of the older Canarian homes all over the island.

As usual, we operated 40-10m, CW/SSB/RTTY, using an Icom IC-7000 and a Deluxe Buddipole Kit. I generally use two 22” arms, and the long whips in an “L-vertical” configuration. It needs no coil on 15/12/10m, just a turn or so on 17m, and more on 20, 30, and 40m. The SWR is below 1.5:1 on all bands, with no tuner. Sometimes my home-determined settings will vary depending on the local terrain, but this time everything was “spot on”. We had absolutely no radio or antenna problems on Tenerife. 

Unfortunately, radio conditions on Tenerife were simply terrible. (But read on, things were much better in Malta.) From the Caribbean (e.g., our last trip to Anguilla), we can make over 5000 QSOs in a week of casual operation. From Tenerife, we only managed to make about 450 QSOs in six days. A few things contributed to this:

-Conditions were simply poor. Solar activity was low, and the A/K indices were high. A bad combination.

-Our QTH was not directly on the water. We were about 20 km from the shore, although we did have some elevation.

-The Canary Islands (EA8) is not a particularly rare DX entity. There are quite a few hams on the islands, and they are very active, so calling CQ with an EA8 call doesn't exactly generate a pile-up in-and-of itself.

Interestingly, it was easier to work North America than Europe from Tenerife. (More than 2/3 of our contacts were NA.) While Tenerife is on the other side of the pond, it is still about 4000 km from there to central Europe. And even though we were at 600 meters elevation, there was a 3700 meter high volcano directly in the path from our location on the southwest part of the island to Europe. (The entire island of Tenerife is one big volcano. In fact, the Teide volcano is the highest point among all Atlantic islands, and the highest point in Spain.)

We tried to make a few contacts during the Worked-All-Europe CW contest over the weekend of August 10/11. In that contest, all contacts must be between a European station and a non-European station. The Canary Islands are (officially) in Africa (IOTA AF-004), so we couldn't work all of the strong US stations we were hearing, but had to push through the volcano to Europe. I think we made a grand total of 35 contacts in that contest. (In contrast, I logged over 1200 QSOs in the 2012 WAE CW *from the West Coast of the USA*!)


We met with another local ham, Jorge EA8TL, with whom we had made dozens of contacts over the years, from various locations. He is a great guy, and showed us around some of the island, including a wonderful dinner at a restaurant owned by a friend of his. (Jorge is one of those guys who has friends *everywhere*. We would just walk down the street and have to stop because we ran into someone he knew.) The restaurant was right down at the waterfront, and when we walked in, fishermen were bringing in the catch from boats just a few meters away. There was no menu at this place. They had the fresh fish and seafood on open display, and you would just point out which ones you wanted, and how you want it prepared (grilled, deep fried, pan saute, whatever). You could not get fresher fish that that. Add in some Canary potatoes (boiled, wrinkled, whole new potatoes with a crystallized salt crust) and a local wine, and you're in culinary heaven.

Given that radio was lousy, we occupied ourselves with other things. There was a beautiful five-star resort about 15 minutes away, and we used their world-class tennis courts every other day. I was hoping for some Spanish red clay courts (like at the French Open), but these were all hardcourt. Much easier to maintain than clay, and much less dust and dirt on the players, too. It was hot every day, starting off around 28 C (82 F) in the morning and getting as high as 35-36 C (97 F) by mid-afternoon. Add in the humidity, and we didn't have to watch our waistlines on this trip. We burned it all off (and more) on the tennis courts. (Every go on vacation and *lose* weight? I dropped almost 5 pounds on this trip.)

Lots of sightseeing, including some pretty scary drives through narrow, steep mountain passes. I don't know how those big tour busses make those trips; it looks like they can't even get around some of the turns. Those of you who are familiar with our contest callsign (K6MMM, "Monkey-Monkey-Monkey"; check out http://www.qrz.com/db/k6mmm) know that Anna and I are into monkeys. We found a primate rescue preserve on the island ("Monkey Park") that allowed visitors. I went, wearing my K6MMM T-shirt, in search of monkey friends. We knew to go first thing in the morning, when the monkeys are hungry. I brought some grapes, and they all came running, particularly some lemurs that were out loose. The pictures show me with one lemur who climbed up on my head.

Other than that, there was great food, Canary beer and wine. We left Tenerife on Saturday August 17th, and spent a night in Madrid before catching a flight the next morning to Malta. Malta comprises two populated islands, the main island of Malta and the smaller island of Gozo. We rented a villa on Gozo, but it wouldn't be ready for a couple of days, so we spent two nights in a small hotel on the main island just being tourists. There are some photos in the Flickr set referenced earlier.

On August 20th, we took the ferry over to Gozo (about 20 minutes), and got settled in our villa. It was in a brand-new development ("The Hamlet"), and had every conceivable modern convenience, including: private pool, A/C, WiFi Internet, indoor spa, etc. In fact, some of the "conveniences" were rather annoying. In order to maintain energy efficiency, the air-conditioners in the living/dining area would shut themselves off if the motion sensors (all over the house) decided that there was no one in the room. Also, they would shut off if any outside door or window was open for more than a minute or two. Well, we had to run the coaxial cable from the shack/dining-room to the pool patio outside, and that meant leaving the patio door open a crack. This shut down the A/C in the shack. Yuck. Fortunately, it wasn't nearly as hot on Gozo as it was on Tenerife, and we could operate with the natural breeze coming in.

Radio was MUCH better on Gozo, for all of the opposite reasons that it lousy on Tenerife. We were closer to the water, we were closer to Europe (heck, we were IN Europe!), and a 9H callsign is much more desirable than an EA8. In addition, we consciously try to operate on bands and modes that are less popular, including lots of RTTY, and operation on the WARC bands (which are never used in contests). So even if 9H isn't an all-time "new one", it might very well be a new one for some folks on 17M RTTY, or 12M SSB.

You can see the full stats in the photo album, but here's the summary:

3279 QSOs total over six days; 62% CW, 25% SSB, 13% RTTY

81% EU (with European Russia and Germany the top countries), 12% NA, 6% Asia, and a smattering of SA and Africa (but not a single VK/ZL contact!)

40M: 5%, 30M: 10%, 20M: 32%, 17M: 21%, 15M: 24%, 12M: 7%, and 10M: 2%

Anna had callsign 9H3NN (corresponding to her home call of W6NN), and I had 9H3MMM (Monkey-Monkey-Monkey), just for fun. There were LOTS of Russians, EU and East Coast, less so for the West Coast. Interestingly, there were times when EU, US, and JA were all coming in simultaneously, at about the same signal strength. Surprisingly, I didn't get overwhelmed with Italians; they can be quite loud, and call persistently, even when it's not their turn. I think Malta is too close, and our signals skipped over Italy for the most part. There seemed to be a "pipeline" to the UK; when everyone else was in the mud, those guys were 20 over 9, even running low power.

Just for fun, I got on in the YO DX contest for a few hours, and had a non-stop pileup the entire time. I think I was the *ONLY* 9H station in the contest, so when I got on and called CQ, the skimmers picked me up and I immediately showed up in *red* as a new multiplier on every screen in the world! It was near the end of the contest, and it seemed like EVERYONE wanted that new mult at once. Good thing I have lots of contest experience, and pretty good ears. I logged almost 400 QSOs in just a few hours.

Almost nothing on 10M, and very little on 12M. I guess conditions just weren't favoring the high bands. We did make a LOT of contacts with US stations, more East Coast than West. My friends out here told me later that they saw me spotted, and could hear the pile-up, but just couldn't hear my signal.

On the first morning there, they had a HUGE thunderstorm. (We were warned a day or two in advance; this was definitely NOT normal for this time of year. Like California, they don't usually get any rain from April through October.) I'm originally from the East Coast, so I know the sound of a real thunder-boomer, and this was a doozy. (We don't get thunderstorms in California, and I kinda miss them.) Of course, I shut down the radio equipment, dropped the antenna, and disconnected the coaxial cable. The storm started about 8-9 km away, counting three seconds per kilometer from the flash to the sound. Then it kept getting closer, until the flash and sound were nearly simultaneous; the lightning was coming down (or is it going up?) just a few hundred meters from the house. Then we heard a loud BOOM, and the power went off. I'm not surprised, and figured that a transformer or a power pole had taken a hit. We were comfortable, and decided to just wait out the storm and the return of power. About an hour later (after the storm had passed), a maintenance worker for the villa came to the door, and said that there was no problem from the power company. This was another of the "modern conveniences" in this house. Turns out that they have a surge sensor on the power lines, and it automatically shuts down and isolates the house when it detects a power surge, to protect all the electronics in the house. All it took was a reset of the breaker, and we were back on line. This is a pretty good idea, and I'm surprised that I haven't seen it elsewhere.


Again, we had time for tennis (OK courts, not great), sightseeing, and more local food and wine. Anna arranged one dinner that was simply spectacular. As a military stronghold over the centuries (Malta is strategically located, right in the middle of the Mediterranean), Gozo Island is ringed with a series of watch-towers that were used for centuries to warn of enemy approaches. They are more of a tourist attraction today; the Dwerja Tower can be seen at:  http://www.islandofgozo.org/what-to-see/watch-towers. Well, I don't know how she arranged it, but Anna managed to rent out the Dwerja Tower (the one in the picture on that website) for just the two of us, and hired a private chef to set up and prepare a dinner on the top of the tower! This was pretty amazing. We arrived about 45 minutes before sunset, and our chef had already set up our table (white linen, china, and crystal service), and had hors d'oeuvres prepared (Sweet sun-dried tomato paste with slices of sheep's milk cheese on Gozitan bread) along with a nice Italina Prosecco (sparkling wine). We watched the sun set, and could also see the famous "Azure Window" from the tower. (It's a natural bridge with a beautiful view of the Mediterranean through it. It's visible in the lower right portion in the picture on the website, above.) The first course was an incredible spread of local seafood: prawns, langostinos, mussels, clams, grilled fish, and whitebait cakes, with a Gozo Chardonnay. The main course was a local specialty; braised rabbit, and a local Cabernet blend. Coffee and dessert, then the chef had to turn on the headlights on his truck so that we could see the path down to the parking area, as it was now pitch black. (The chef brought a small generator so that we could have lights during dinner, and so that he could cook some items on site.) This was quite a treat.


That’s about it. On August 27th, we headed back home, again with an overnight stay in Madrid. There are some more pictures of the sights on Malta and Gozo, but we had a great time, particularly with the better radio conditions on Gozo. Anna has already booked a trip to Antigua for next May, and we may squeeze in something else between now and then. All of the logs are uploaded to LotW, Clublog, and eQSL, and paper QSLs should be ready in about a month (they are at the printer as I write this).

If anyone has any specific questions about our operation, please feel free to contact me directly, off-list.

73 de Rich 9H3MMM (KE1B)
