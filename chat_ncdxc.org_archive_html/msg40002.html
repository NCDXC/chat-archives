Just remember, every time you work a new one, there are fewer new ones to work. The first 200 are easy (relatively). The last 10-20 can be nearly impossible, depending on: radio conditions, political conditions, financial conditions, etc.

Part of it is the “rules”, if you accept ARRL as the ruling authority. In addition to the “deserving/underserving” article in the DXer, carefully read Jim’s dated (but still absolutely valid) editorial on the hubris of ARRL. As Jim says, the ONLY questions to ask are: (1) Is the ham licensed to operate?, and (2) Is he/she where he/she says they are? Whether ARRL considers that location a “country” or “entity” is irrelevant. And even the issue of “what constitutes a valid confirmation” is not up to the ARRL.

For example: ARRL *ONLY* accepts paper QSLs that they have “certified” (either by sending the cards to them, or getting approval by one of their “approved” checkers), or Logbook of the World (which is operated by ARRL, and for which they charge a fee to “credit” any LoTW confirmation towards their published results.

Personally, I accept almost any reasonable form of confirmation. If I get an email confirmation from a QSO, that’s “good enough for me”, although it isn’t good enough for ARRL. Of course, anyone could “game the system”, and make bogus entries in their log and create bogus email confirmations for those QSOs. And who are you fooling? Just yourself. I know who I worked, and I know who has confirmed it. I don’t need to prove it to ARRL.

And then the question arises of “what constitutes a valid QSO with a given entity”? Example: I am a Director of the Northern CA DX Foundation, the most recognized authority for funding of DXpeditions in the world. Not long ago, we received a request to help fund a fairly low-budget DXpedition. It was a Phillipine operator who wanted to operate from Scarborough Reef (@2 on the world’s Most Wanted List). Scarborough Reef is quite near the Philippines, just over the Mariana Trench (the deepest part of the sea in the world). His plan was to take a small boat, essentially run it aground onto the Reef, and operate from the boat (which, being on land, even meets the ARRL criteria for “being there.”)

Now Scarborough Reef is a disputed territory. There are claims of sovereignty by the Phillipines, the People's Republic of China, and (I think) Vietnam. ARRL has a bunch of rules that define what constitutes a separate DX entity, one of which is the distance from the “home nation.” So Alcatraz Island isn’t a separate DX entity because it’s too close to the US mainland. But Alaska and Hawaii are far enough away to count.

Scarborough Reef isn’t far enough from the Phillipines to “count” as a separate entity with respect to the Phillipines, but it IS far enough away from both China and Vietnam to count as separate with respect to those countries. So ARRL declared that a Filipino, operating under a (valid) Filipino license on Scarborough Reef was really operating from the Phillipines, and it didn’t count as Scarborough. But if a Chinese or Vietnamese operator (or anyone else) operated there under a Chinese or Vietnamese license, then it WOULD count as Scarborough. Of course, that makes absolutely no sense, as noted by Jim K8JRK. (And why is the ARRL getting involved in deciding whose sovereignty claims are more valid that others?) In all cases, the operator has a valid license, and is where they say they are. But if the license was issued by the Phillipines, it doesn’t count as Scarborough, but if it was issued by China or Vietnam (or anyone else), it WOULD count as Scarborough. Nonsense!!

That’s why I ignore the ARRL completely as any sort of authority on DX’ing or even on ham radio in the USA. Again, I know who I worked, I know where they were, and that’s all that matters (to me).

That said, enjoy every ATNO as you run your count up from (now) 101 to wherever you take it. Remember, it’s a game, and you get to choose the rules under which you play. I recommend you don’t let the ARRL define those rules for you.

Rich KE1B
