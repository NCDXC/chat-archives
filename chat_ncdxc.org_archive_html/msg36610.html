Just to add to Dave’s comments, and address a couple of issues.

Being part of the N5J DXpedition as a remote operator was a pretty amazing experience.  There were 4 primary ops on the Magnet and 40 remote operators from around the world, working around-the-clock to put Jarvis Island on the air for the first time since 1990.  

The first QSO was made just 4 hours after landing on the island - unheard of with traditional tent+generator trips.  Following a carefully thought out Band and Propagation plan, the team made almost 107K QSOs in 13.8 days.  

CW = 43,516   (41%)
FT8 = 49,820   (47%)
SSB =   1,112  (13%
Total = 106,883

Of the 49,820 FT8 QSOs, I was able to log 4,858, almost 10%.  For my Bay Area friends, although I wasn’t able to give you a “KB”, sometimes I had a moment to send a screen snapshot as a QSO souvenir to some of you. 

The low percent of N5J SSB QSOs isn’t surprising.  SSB mode is fun but challenging.  It’s often much slower than FT8 or CW because of callsign repeat requests (dialects and accents), unruly pileups and intentional QRMing — especially the Italians. : >)    SSB on Jarvis was then reserved for the bands with the fewest problems and best openings, primarily 20M-15M-17M-10M.  Remember the recent FT4GL Glorioso DXpedition?  Total Qs = 61,141.  SSB Qs = 5,200 (9%).

The N5J Jarvis Island DXpedition is a success on several levels.  SuperFox is a game changer.  The crew on the Magnet kept 7 RIB’s running smoothly on the island with very few problems.  Communication between the Magnet and remote ops was tight — there were almost no unfilled remote operating time slots during the entire two-week operation.

DXpedition leaders George, AA7JV and Don, N1DG have demonstrated how to leverage complex technology and dedicated teamwork to change the face of DXpeditioning for the better…..maybe for a long time.
