On 12/17/2012 8:46 AM, Andreas Junge wrote:
I have been wondering why it always has to be done in form of Mega Expeditions. There are lots of groups of two and three and they are activating fairly rare places.
YES! See my expanded comments below. As others have noted, some entities may demand the large scale treatment simply because of the cost of getting on a very difficult or remote island or to some other very remote location, but AA7JV has proven that many others do not.
Why not do 4 smaller expeditions every other year vs one Mega Expedition every 10 years? Pile-ups would be smaller and maybe the expeditions can focus on low bands vs high bands …
I'm afraid that the "I've got to work all the slots" attitude may have gotten us to the point where many hams are trying to fill them with every trip. With a couple of the recent South Pacific trips, I had already worked one or more previous trips for most band/mode slots, so either went after them QRP or stayed out of the pileup for those slots.
My view is that its time for those leading the mega-trips to learn from George Wallner, AA7JV, who has led at least four very successful trips in the past three years, and filled at least 40 band-slots for me (and ALWAYS the more difficult 80 and 160). George has proved that, as we used to say in the jazz world, "it don't take no lot of folks to have a good time" -- what is left out is, "if they know what they're doing."
One of the most important things to be learned from George is the SYSTEMS ENGINEERING aspects of his trips. One very simple example that makes a BIG difference in logistics -- to run 1kW, he straps together a pair of 500W power amps that run on 12VDC from big batteries that are float-charged by light weight Honda generators, and because the batteries are running the radios, he can run the generators in econo mode, greatly reducing the number of gallons of fuel he has to carry. George has also done some excellent antenna design work for his trip, as proven by his big signals and big ears on the lower bands.
Another important thing to be learned from George is the critical importance of having at least one damned good (and very resourceful) engineer both in the planning stages and at the site. K3NA is another guy who's an awfully good engineer, and done some spectacularly good systems design for his (much larger) trips.
On the other side of the coin, I think Paul has done a great job of the "political" aspects of trips to difficult places -- getting the permissions to GO there in the first place, and organizing the bigger trips. 

73, Jim K9YC
