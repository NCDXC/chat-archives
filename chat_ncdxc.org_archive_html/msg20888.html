The problem is different between contesting and DXing. In contesting, you both have to log the QSO or someone gets dinged for a logging error. In DXing, you log it and let Clublog/LOTW/paper QSLs etc. straighten it out. The only downside is you can't be sure about the contact.
Also, on time skew: If you are chasing a DX, you don't care about NIST time. His clock is the only correct definition of correct time. I think if your clock is a few deciseconds early, then you will be able to decode him because you were listening when he sent the start of his message. But when you transmit, he may not have started listening yet and so miss the start of your message and so not decode you.
I do know that I have a lot better chance of being heard when my clock is within a few deciseconds of correct.
