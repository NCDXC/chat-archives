One more point often ignored...

MSHV can be told to IGNORE worked calls.  So IF a station is sent RR73 (now logged at the DX site), but the station doesn't hear it, MSHV will ignore that call to the point that another RR73 may not be sent.  All dupe attempts, just add band noise.

So what I do in that case; if my given report is loud enough that I'd expect to be heard again; LOG IT.  Then wait another 20 ish hours (the next day) for a dupe, IF the logbook is updated and I'm not there.  (A couple nights ago, zs8w on 80 ft8; but I got emails showing the wanted RR73 that was missed up here.)

[I watched as one station didn't hear the RR73, started again to be ignored completely (not a busy station) then changed their call to t1ts which got an immediate report to prove the real call was ignored.  The DX op only sent that once; caught onto the situation (and minor joke in English).  The real call was shown in the log the next upload.]

Livestream=Millennial instant gratification (not arguing a moment; I love it too; WHEN it works).  That's an awesome transition state for the hobby.

On a recent DXpedition, a CW contact was shown (livestream) as nk7U (one keystroke to the left of I) then went QSY.  Looking up that call, noting it was the ONLY log entry for that call (and QRZ showed nothing DX for the op); one quick email and the log was repaired without a headache; the next update all was well.  The op actually emailed me and accepted that he fat fingered the entry.

Multiple access points for a major team (more than one Starlink portal); would seem a no brainer (redundant everything!) but contact with the ISP (Starlink) to make SURE the entity had coverage should be on the list too (else, why would the birds transmit over, say, Antarctica when no one is there?  That costs battery power, for nothing).

Bandwidth; you'll use MORE than ever expected (as the team members phone/text/email AND operator time); plus more means the precious valuable log can be uploaded more often (for us belt and suspender types).

Still, the point is to have fun and we're doing that.  After 50 years, it's still fun to watch the tech evolve and find ways to best integrate it.
