Very well said Richard. 
A DXer has to be able to make a contact long distance through RF propagation (no DMR internet radio of course).
FT4 FT8 accomplishes that and there are subtleties to be learned with practice to be effective. 
Operators who spent their life learning and perfecting cw might enjoy more that mode and its own subtleties. 
In any case,  with all the hoa restrictions,  QRM from neighbors and lack of propagation WSJTx has been a blessing. 
I activate SOTA and enjoy cw there and the joy of being able to copy stations with a simple wire. 
I'm yet to score a DX though but I know people who did in CW a few years back.  Tempted to bring a raspberry pi loaded with WSJTx to the summit...😋

On 8/14/2020 3:35 PM, Bruce Bern wrote:
> My first love remains CW, but don’t underestimate the discipline and strategy required to nail a rare one on FT8 or FT4. Frequently, DX stations change frequency or timing. Guessing what audio tone frequency will be clear at the DX station’s QTH is important, as well as locating the frequency of the current station in QSO with the DX (just like a SSB or CW pileup).
> I recall the sunspot minimum of 1963, when I was a junior high student on the air for the first time. With 100 watts and a loaded vertical in a valley bottom, low end 40 CW was my only hope. A single VK was a triumph. With the WSJT modes, modest stations can enjoy the thrill of DX with no sunspots. I think it’s kept DXing alive, especially for new hams.
> de Bruce, K3NQ
