There are a number of applications available called "YouTube downloaders"  Some install on your computer, some are simply websites wherein you type the Youtube vid URL, wait a minute, and it presents a downloadable file.  Do a search for "Youtube to mp3" downloader and you'll find the website I usually use.  It pulls the audio track without the video.

The mp3 file format used in YouTube vids doesn't have much in the way of quality anyway, so I don't worry about file translations causing issues.

Youtube has gone on the warpath about copying from them.  Any youtube vid more than ten minutes long is "protected" somehow and the apps will refuse to download/copy it.  In those cases, you'll have to do what Jim suggested: use an outboard recorder.  You still don't need to worry much about audio quality: even if the youtube vid did have any, the in-built sound card in a computer suffers in the noisy environment of the computer.  Laptops are much worse.  But, if the purpose is for downloading and/or saving educational materials, ham radio snippets, etc, it's all good enough.  I even use some of these tools to download songs that my band wants me to learn.

        My question is: What is the best way to capture Youtube

        Should I connect a digital hand-held digital stereo
          recorder to speaker out on my sound card?  Or should I use
          some form of capture/mixing software?    I would prefer to
          make an MP3 file of each videos audio. 
      
    

    As the resident audio guy, I should have an easy, inside the
    computer answer, but I don't, because I'm not that much of a
    computer guy. :) I'd bet that if you search long enough, you'll find
    software to drag audio (and video) from youtube to a computer file.

    

    An outboard recorder, or even the sound input of another computer
    should work just fine if you set levels right -- that is, not too
    low, not too high, just Goldilocks. :)  One thing I do NOT recommend
    is doing the intermediate step as an MP3. MP3 is LOSSY compression,
    and every time we play and record MP3 we throw away a bit of stuff
    and the MP3 player guesses at what the MP3 recorder threw away. The
    more complex the audio, and the greater the level of compression,
    the more is thrown away.  

    

    You'll probably make the final audio recording an MP3 (although it
    wouldn't hurt to leave it as a .WAV except for disk space), and you
    don't lose anything when you COPY an MP3 without converting it to
    audio so you can listen to it, edit it, or combine it with other
    stuff.
