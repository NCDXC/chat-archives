I am also working on completing 10BWAS but I still need about 20 more confirmations to have all 500 needed QSLs for the ten band WAS.  Working them is one thing but as you know actually getting the  QSLs and/or LOTW confirmations are something else after working them.  The hardest bands for me to complete have been  6M, 12M and  160M.  The great thing about the ARRL Centennial is that many of the hard to find WARC band modes have been on and active and plentiful this year and so far the ARRL QSLs have been 100% via the LOTW. Wow! Thank you ARRL and thank you volunteer operators such as you and K7ZS and W7YAQ!  J
 
I have been on a number of DXpeditions over the years. The first really challenging one for me was back in 1988 (?) when four of us including my then teenage daughter, Ashley,  N6MTJ, operated in the CQWW from the Island of Montserrat as VP2MU and we finished third in our category for the CQWW running a modest  Icom IC-730 transceiver to a Heathkit SB-200 linear amplifier. Wonderful experience and we learned a lot! We set up a station near the water on the island and put up a triband beam and a couple of inverted Vee antennas for 40M and 80M. We had a wonderful ham radio experience. We all learned a lot and we had a lot of fun doing things with the local hams and touring the island and the volcano (long story).!!!! We had some *serious* pileups to deal with from Montserrat during the CQWW and we learned how to manage them. Great experience.  
 
Have fun and thanks again.  73, Ted, K6XN
 
PS I have made more 12M, 17M and 30M stateside contacts on more band modes in the last five months than in the prior ten years as a result of this ARRL Centennial event. Thank you ARRL!

Ted,
 
It was mostly K7ZS, W7YAQ, and me on the WARC bands today.
 
We were in constant communication via texting (Gchat, twitter or Skype) all day long. If one band seemed hot we could quickly alert the others. We could also swap modes if one of us was getting burned out. It worked well and you might consider it for the upcoming CA and NV operations.
 
I've been chasing the W1AW/x operations for 10BWAS (need 3 more to complete that) and just about every contact on 12m has been via backscatter. I kept moving the antenna around and would find different stations in different directions. A lot of the stations in the SE USA were actually pointing East to work me. I didn't have the late afternoon shift, but perhaps beaming SW might be better.
 
I've never been on a DXpedition, so this is my first experience with huge pileups. This W1AW/x thing has far exceeded ARRL expectations. Looking at the LeaderBoard there  appears to be around 7500 serious W1AW/x chasers. That's a lot of stations chasing only two W1AW/x operations each week.
 
So, if you haven't experienced a huge pileup, I encourage you to take part in the CA or NV operations. It's a gas.
 
73,
Steve, K6UM
