On 7/31/2021 11:46 AM, Paul Booth - WA6IBU via Chat wrote:

I resisted for a long time in trying this out.  There’s probably more controversy over this with comments such as “is this real ham radio” etc.  We do like new technologies and in this world of low sunspots it fits the bill.  After using it for a bit I think my only objection is how it’s in the same classification of RTTY.  I think it should be separate, they are just not the same and the rewards of achieving RTTY contacts are devalued by those on FT8.  But then again, maybe RTTY will be going away.  I also find it very impersonal especially in USA to USA contacts.  So, a couple of observations from this neophyte on what I’ve observed in working the FT8 world although I’m sure there’s a lot more experience out there you may want to add:

  1. Getting Odd/Even Mixed up:  many times, I hear stations calling the DX on the same even or odd instead of the opposite.
  2. The DX Switches:  Sometime the DX goes from Odd to Even and visa versa.  Maybe instead of taking calls they are calling the DX and switch accordingly
  3. Call w/o copying:  There are hams calling the DX who in no way can possibly copy them.
  4. Finding the right frequency:  Don’t select an open frequency on the odd/even the DX is on, make sure to pick one on the opposite.  I’ve seen people call on the DX frequency while he was transmitting too.
  5. Calling too high:  I’ve found that if you can, a frequency to transmit below 2500Hz is sometimes better.  Some of our transmitters if not set right roll off too much by 2800Hz
  6. Above 1KHz:  I like to keep my transmit above 1.5KHz as most of the DX seems to be below that;  not sure why.
  7. Missing the DX calling you:  Sometimes someone sits right on top of the DX, hopefully because they didn’t hear them, washing them out so you can’t decode
  8. The DX missing you:  similarly, sometimes the DX can’t hear you because someone started transmitting on top of you;  it pays to check once in a while whether your frequency is still free.  If no success switch frequencies.
  9. Receiving direction:  I’ve noticed that you can copy stations in a direction not normally thought of say on CW.  There may be other directions too but I’m not talking long path either.  I’m saying something at 335 degrees may be similar to 35 degrees, especially to the middle east.  So sometimes I’m confused as to where to point the antenna since I can hear from multiple directions.
  10. Receiving but not transmitting:  Antenna direction appears to be more sensitive in transmitting than receiving.
  11. Transmitting during QSOs:  One nice thing is that you can keep calling before the DX qso is done, he may see you and pick you up without having to call CQ, besides everyone else is calling all the time too.
  12. Not getting a 73:  A number of stations do not respond with a 73;  don’t understand that so I wonder if they logged it (as I did).  Even a recent DXpeditition didn’t send 73.  So I don’t if all the contacts are real, and whether they never copied me.  Sometimes I’ve tried a second contact though and did get a 73, but in observing them they sometimes didn’t do it 50% of the time.  So not sure what gives.
