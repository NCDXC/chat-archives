I agree with Kevin and Paul.  One of the great satisfactions of being a DXpedition operator is to not only work the “easy ones” from the pileup — but to also spend time digging out smaller stations running 100 watts and a simple antenna.

Getting those little pistol stations in the log for an ATNO, is a real thrill for me personally.  I don’t need a CW skimmer for that…..just patience and good listening skills.

On Jun 28, 2016, at 8:26 AM, Paul N6PSE via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

Hank, a skimmer approach might help a poor/inexperienced operator but it also raises the ethical question, if the skimmer heard the caller and the Op did not, is it a valid contact?  Many would say it was not. 

The skimmer is also going to pick up remnants of the other signals being transmitted from the other DXpedition operators. 

As Kevin said, skimmers only decode the best, most clear signals. We've recently seen a few DXpeditions with unskilled operators. They either did not have experience with pileups or were unfamiliar with their rig (or both)

 One of these DXpeditions were making on average around 4,000 contacts a day while DXpeditions just before and during were making up to 12,000 contacts a day. 

It is critical that a costly DXpedition to a remote place that wont be activated again for many years have the right amount of skilled and experienced operators. Proper team member selection is one of the most important aspects of the DXpedition.

Based on what we have seen lately, I am advocating a "Back to Basics" approach in our DXpeditions. We will never use any gimmicks or tricks to fund or operate our DXpeditions. 

DXpedition Leaders should strive to assemble the best team possible. Proper equipment selection, testing and setup and proper antennas add greatly to the mix. 

If you gather the best operating team possible and give them good technology that they know and understand, a skimmer really adds no value. 

73, 

Paul N6PSE

On Tue, Jun 28, 2016 at 7:30 AM, Kevin Rowett via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:
yes, it helps.  Not as much as you’d think.

IMO - Good DXp operators need to work the entire audience - Loud, weak, slow, super fast (hi hi), chirpy, nervous, etc.  CW Skimmer tends to pick out the “best” signals.  Yes, gets the rate up, but, excludes too many folks that you should work.

Put another way, as a contester, I work down the pileup in an order that does the best for my score - louder, rarer mults, etc.  That’s not the best practice for a DXp operator.



73, K6TD

I'm not a big DXer. I'll have a go at nabbing a DXpedition for the thrill of the chase, not for a new-one. There are clearly many things I don't get. I've never been on the other end.

But ...

It seems that many times DXpeditions have a hard time pulling a call out of the pileups. Sometimes it will be many, many seconds before they come back to someone.

A Wild Idea?

How about a local skimmer at the DXpeditions site. The skimmer would have to be configured to respond to just calls. The skimmer would put spots on the bandmap. The DXpedition operator would click on a skimmer spot and work the guy (still split).

Would it be solving a problem that's not a problem? Would it work? Would it help?
