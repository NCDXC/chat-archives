There is a lot at play with yagis on hilltops or stacks that has to do
with the band opening and timing. You'll often find that when the band
is just opening the arrival angle is very high, and a low yagi will
work better than a high yagi (or the one on the edge of the ridge). As
the band opens, longer-haul signals will often get lower in angle of
arrival and then the high yagis and the ones on the ridge get better.
Of course as with everything in radio there is no sure thing, but this
is what I've found over the years of operating at numerous stations
with those sorts of systems on 40M. Angle *diversity* is key though,
and having switchable stacks or antennas with varying angles of
takeoff capability is going to be a huge advantage. Just like having a
dipole and vertical in a small station gives you diversity, when one
is working great, the other might be marginal, but if you have both,
then you cover many other situations.

The same sometimes goes for RX antennas - one of the best RX antennas
for opening 80/160 (long-haul) that I've found is a low dipole - 20
feet off the ground. I've found this works VERY well right at sunset
or right before, as long as you have a TX antenna that can get a
signal off to that long-haul station.

The yagi options don't have to be expensive, either. If you can't
afford a tall tower to get the horizontal yagi up to 1/2 wave or
higher (assuming the flat ground in-town QTH rule of thumb of
half-wave+ height), then there's always the phased verticals approach,
or vertically-polarized loops, etc. that will often do as well or
sometimes better than a high yagi. Wire verticals or loops can often
be fit in smaller areas, more discrete, and cheaper, and will often
work very well.

The station that I use in YN has a 2 element quad loop on 40M pointed
to EU, and a 3L wire yagi pointed on JA (both fixed), and a dipole
broadside to the US. 40 is a very, very strong band from down there
with this setup and I've always been amazed at how well that little
set of quad loops works. They're not high off the ground, were
probably built for less than $50, and I can open and close the band
with those antennas with relatively low power (500w). Most pileups I
can crack with 2-3 calls even if the station is weak, and while the
QTH is relatively good, it's not a place with huge sloping
foregrounds, but I do believe there's something to be said for soil
quality :)

YMMV, but I'm all about building cheap wire antennas that work well
and in this area, there are lots of options.

> On 2/4/2014 8:28 AM, Andreas Junge wrote:
> Even just a rotatable dipole up 50 feet gives you an advantage by
> being able to point exactly to the maximum signal.
>
> I am currently using a 6-20M rotatable SteppIR dipole up 60 feet. It
> loads up on 30M and I can make a S5 signal go away in the null.
>
> Over the last year or so, I've been using NEC to study the effect of
> mounting height. The BEST 2-element antenna is good for 4 dB as compared
> to a dipole at the same height. Indeed, much lot of the benefit we have
> historically assigned to a beam is really the result of mounting it higher.
>
> For example -- raising a 20M dipole or Yagi from 33 ft to 67 ft is good
> for 6 dB at 5 deg elevation, 5 dB at 10 deg, and 3 dB at 15 degrees.
> Raising it to 100 ft is good for another 3 dB at 5 degrees,1 dB at 10
> degrees. Tom Schiller, N6BT, has long maintained that, based on his
> experience, improving our signal by only 2 dB can make a big difference
> in what we can work. The point I'm getting to is that I agree with Rich
> that there's more than one way to skin the cat, depending on the
> practicalities of our real estate and budget, and that we can get gain
> from height, from adding elements, and from doing both.
>
> Before I had found a clearing in my dense redwood forest large enough to
> rotate a beam, I had only wire dipoles at about 100 ft for 20-10, and at
> 110 ft for 80 and 40. 80 and 40 are still high wires (two for each band,
> at right angles to each other), and I've added a reflector to both NE
> dipoles to make them 2-el with peak gain to about 50 degrees. My guess
> is that I'm getting about 3 dB from these wire Yagis.
>
> I'm continuing work on a presentation that looks at these issues in a
> way that helps us make better decisions on antennas based on the
> limitations of our real estate, possible skyhooks, ground conductivity,
> and budget. It compares verticals and horizontal antennas at various
> mounting heights, studying the effect of these factors on their vertical
> pattern. I plan to show this work in the Paciifcon antenna forum this
> fall, and would be love to do it for our club.
>
