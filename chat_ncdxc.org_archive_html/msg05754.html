My computer is set to sound a bubble "pop" any time a needed DXCC is spotted and reported through one of the major clusters.  Happened to be up and near my computer at around 0800 the other night when it started to go mad with alerts - sounded like a small fire-fight.  Jumped on to see if my Mac was crashing, or if the bands really were going wild.  Opened my cluster monitor and saw a steady stream of spots (including the P5) being posted by the station identifying himself as IK7JTF. 

At peak, the jerk was posting spots at a rate of about 20 per minute. Kept up for about half an hour on and off. The typing and posting alone would've been taxing - but his qso throughput seemed... unreal. Then I noticed that many of the posted spots were for DXpeditions from last year, and at least a few from several years ago.  

I checked, and "spotter's" call is legit. The guy (if it was really him) has a profile, complete with photo, on DX Watch and I think on QRZ as well. 

All of the bogus spots were coming through DX Watch, but they were echoed through every cluster on earth. Looked like some kind of Deliberate Denial of Service Attack after a while.

If DX Watch can't verify the license status of their users, can't they at least administratively block a user whose call is obviously being used to spam the system?  It was mid-morning in Northern Europe, you'd think they would've seen what was going on and done something about it?
