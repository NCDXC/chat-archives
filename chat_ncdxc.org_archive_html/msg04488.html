VU7AG finally made an appearance on 17m CW this afternoon. By the time the minimum TOA was high enough for me to hear him---about 1 PM---there was a substantial pile, which grew like a rampaging virus, reaching 15 to 20 kHz  wide! As it got denser, it got crazier--almost like watching a hurricane form--and behavior disintegrated. The VU7 faded here after about an hour but others were calling a while longer.

Unfortunately, despite the west coast having been locked out of previous 17m appearances by ill-considered timing of the 17m ops--they show up and leave too early or too late for us---the viral pile contained all of North America. We could have used a W6/W7-only event, even for a few minutes, but it did not happen. Or, if it did, I failed to hear it over the clamor of the pileup on my B VFO.

I don't know who in the club got through. I did not--although I thought he came back to me at 2156Z; someone stepped on the VU7 and by the time that ended, that possibility was history. Even with the aid of a P3 for finding clear spots and successful callers, there were just too many in the pile, the QSO's were too short, I saw no repeatable pattern for the operator and I did not succeed. I suspect my time will come after the weekend, when only us cranky old farts are home on the radio.
 
Having 40, 30 and 20, and 3 modes confirmed going back 20 years(!), I have largely stayed out of the fray, logging only a quick and easy 20 CW Q a few days ago, while (im)patiently waiting for an opportunity on the lowest bands and above 20m. This has been extremely frustrating, as their operators seem not to understand or care about putting the western half of the continent in the log on those bands. On 80 and 160 we have so far seen appearances only at their sunrise---not ours---and I have tired of listening to NA stations from the Atlantic Coast to W5 and W0 swinging away on 80 while of course not hearing the DX on the SE path, followed by non-events on 17 and 15. My insomnia-driven attendance in the wee hours ---their sunset is at 1240Z and  our sunrise at 15Z--has not brought any joy. That is, they have so far ignored the 2 hours and 20 minutes of overlapping darkness that spells opportunity for us.

KY6R has tried to communicate these needs to the team; it remains to be seen whether the team will show up on the NW path at all at the right times on the lowest bands. At least this afternoon there was something to chase. I have no argument with heavy emphasis on the most productive bands--20, 30 and 40--to provide maximum ATNO's, but for those of us long past that all-important first QSO, their chronic failure to show up elsewhere has been torture.

Garry, NI6T
