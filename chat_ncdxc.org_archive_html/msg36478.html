On Wed, Aug 14, 2024, 10:11 AM Lucas Ford via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

I have heard of these, never used one though. Expendability is the only drawback I can see but for many this will do. Wish they had more USB ports. As is I added a startech USB expansion card on my end. You can use hubs but they will never work as dependably or with the full speed.

 

For those looking to experiment with Linux, I mentioned this in my book as well: https://sourceforge.net/projects/kb1oiq-andysham/

And this is what I use on a secondary machine (my old Dell): https://linuxmint.com/

Of course there is the raspberry pi if you want to get your feet wet for cheap, version 5 just came out. https://www.raspberrypi.com/ I am still on version 4 and still run v3 for my hamclock, Pi-Star (DMR) and pi tablet for the RTL-SDR.

 

For the computer end, you can actually make a bootable USB drive as well, so you do not have to wipe your windows if you want to test the above. Just make sure you know read up on it, so you don’t wipe out anything accidentally. Meaning do not install just run unless you are ready to make the move. You can also dual boot but again, make sure you know what you are doing!

 

I use a KVM switch to go between 2 PCs, a mac and a Linux machine and the occasional Raspberry pi so I use the same monitor(s), Keyboard and Mouse, etc. Might be a good option for those looking to experiment. Startech (A brand I trust mostly) makes nice 4K dual monitor KVM switches even. Get the 60Hz version to be future ready unless you already made the move.

I was going to chime in but looks like Jeff beat me to it. Figured I have a few minutes before heading out to work so now is a good time to do so.

 

All very good point, especially on the Win11 and OneDrive situation. Microsoft has a history of pushing products on people (as does Apple) so this is no big surprise, however is frustrating for sure. And yes, be careful with updates, especially of drivers. Stay away from “driver updaters” unless you really understand what they do. Not for everyone.

 

If you still work in the corporate world of have done so semi-recently, you have likely encountered SCCM (Now InTune) or JAMF on the apple side of things. Winuntil kind of does this on a local level, at least the initial steps. I personally do not use it as I have requirements which this software does not meet but I do think it is a good tool for many. Perhaps we should develop an amateur radio branch on Github. What do you think Jeff?

 

I keep 2 documents. One where I have screen captures of settings I do not want to lose and the other my personalized checklist. Since I reinstall my computers about once a year and replace them about every 3-4 years, this is helpful. This way I avoid downtime and rarely do I have issues. “OS rot” as they call it real. And yes, it happens on MacIntosh as well.

 

The software packages, licenses and most everything else I store on my Synology NAS here at home. I used to have a windows server, then a Linux server but they were overkill, and the new NAS devices do everything they used to with a lower electric bill and less time-consuming maintenance. Actually, they pretty much run themselves. This also keeps a backup of my radio logs and I keep a set in the cloud. NOT one drive, but google drive. Dropbox is fine too. I use my NAS as my document folder. So if my computer ever just drops dead, I can get to anything I need and not stress about loosing files. Important documents I mirror to the cloud.

 

For desktops, I go between Dell precision and building my own. About a year ago when I switched, I built my own again. Though since I work in IT, at times when I get home it is the last thing I want to be doing. If you want a turnkey solution, I always had good luck with the precision line and we give those to “power users” at work. They will do great with most anything you throw at them. Just don’t get the bottom models. Stay away from the lower (entry level) end Dells and HPs. Frankly, they make better doorstops than computers. Also if you live in a warmer place, make sure you have airflow. This was one of my deciding factors even though I am in Pacifica. My system is on sometimes for days.

 

If you build, keep in mind that throwing more memory at it is not going to speed up you much if any. If you are using 10-12GB and you have 32, increasing it is a total waste of money. I see many people do this. I generally get 64GB in my machines. More than most people need. Memory speed matters! DDR5 is good currently.

 

Spend your money on a GOOD SSD (lot of crap out there, look for brand name and specs) and a good processor. I7 or i9, latest generations if you go Intel, but there are good alternatives. The number of cores is more important than the “speed”. Meaning, a 12 core with a slightly lower speed will outperform, at least in 99% of the cases an 8 core cpu at a faster speed. As an example…. Cache also matters. A lot in some cases. Get a good fan for it, like a Noctura if you are a power user or just want a quiet case.

 

If you build your own I (and most IT guys) love the Fractal design cases. I have the Torrent myself. Easy to work on and is a work of art. Plus, if you can overheat it, I would be really impressed! Has large fans that run slower so it is very quiet. But when I turn on my Flex PowerGenius its game over anyway so on the end of the day does not matter.

 

Also, a good GPU (graphics card) is nice to have, especially now with the world of SDRs and WSJT-X. You will likely want to run 4K, multi monitor. And at this point, a GOOD monitor is a must. You get what you pay for here, to a degree at least. I use the Dell Q series, but the Ultrasharp are nice as well. The “best buy specials” will look special but not the way you want them to. Samsung has a nice line as does LG. I run an Nvidia Geforce RTX 3060 which is now a bit dated, but runs 3x 4K HDR monitors with ease and high bit color enabled. I do graphics and video editing on it as well. Sometimes rendering 4K videos for YouTube while decoding WSJT-X on two bands and never had an issue.

 

I like the QST idea but I am also a little hesitant. When I was writing my book, I had to be VERY careful not to bad mouth any manufacturers or vendors….Like I just did above. Instead, I just glanced over or looked for positives. Don’t want any legal issues and some of these companies are very touchy! But maybe one day and while really biting my tongue.

 

Hope this helps a little, if you want to chat or reach out to me, please feel free. Usually, evenings are better.

 

Take good care,

Lucas W6AER

Hi Mike,

 

It will be exiting and frustrating to upgrade. 

 

While the rest of the world seems to welcome or at least tolerate regular updates, hams seem to prefer a stable platform that, once proven to play well with various radio drivers and amateur applications, remains unchanged.

 

Once you find your PC vendor you might consider discussing the “unique” needs of ham operators when planning your initial setup, as well as security strategies that avoid, or at least minimize, the number of automated updates of the operating system and every software application you use try to force.

 

One set of tools you and they might find useful in setting up your new system is Chris Titus Tech Windows Utility

https://github.com/ChrisTitusTech/winutil

Here is a YouTube video that is a very detailed review.

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pldFPTnOCGM&t=1038s

 

One point to emphasize with Windows 11; stay well clear of Microsoft OneDrive and avoid using the predefined My Document, My Pictures etc folders. Microsoft practically forces new users to use one OneDrive and, without using tools like the Chris Titus utility, it can take days to untangle the convoluted registrations to avoid it.  This is a “necessity” in my book if you plan any offline computer enabled radio operation like field day, etc.

 

Once you set up and de-bloat your new system, I recommend a conservative update strategy in which you choose to specifically permit or deny each update attempted by the OS, drivers, or apps. Do this in conjunction with regular full system imaging and save several generations of system images on network storage of some sort. (There are a number of good imaging tools, including Windows built is version.)

 

System imaging allows you restore you system rapidly if an update - forced or otherwise - breaks any of your amateur application. It also serves as a backup to virus and other threats, in that you can have minimum protection to minimize the intrusion of “security” and antivirus programs that often themselves interfere with applications and can be system resource hogs. This also allow you to recover when an OS or other update cannot be rolled back.

 

If something - update, virus infection, whatever - breaks your system, restore your system using you last working image and get back to DXing in minutes rather than days and figure out what broke your system later when there is no DX.

 

All that said, what hath our own Lucas Ford have to say on these topics?

(Seems like there might be a good QST article in there somewhere – Eh, Lucas?)

 

Good DX’ing Mike!

 

-Jeff AJ6LG
