On November 14, 2018 at 7:54 AM Gene K5GS via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

I’ve read the comments on the NCDXC (and other) reflectors, THANKS.

We had a great team with a great crew on Braveheart. It all came together like a finely tuned race car and kicked off with a bang, ~10,000 Qs a day.

Two quick clarifications:

DXA busted calls:

DXA didn’t bust calls.  DXA is a copy of the N1MM QSO record. If it’s wrong in DXA it is wrong in the VP6D log. Of course, there may be a one-off situation where that isn't true. DXA worked exactly as intended. For near real time reporting DXA has a dependency on the BGAN satellite and our local area networks being available.  We had some network issues that disabled near real time reporting.

Our on-line log was updated once per day. This caused confusion when people expected to see their Q in the on-line log and on DXA within minutes of each other. We will review the wisdom of using both.

N1MM and WSJT-X didn’t play well together. We had to do a lot of gyrations to remove almost 5,000 WSJT-x / RR73 generated dupes and merge the N1MM and WSJT logs. This was very difficult to do while the DX-pedition was underway. Thanks to Pista HA5AO and Steve W1SRD for doing all the heavy log lifting, merging and auditing. And of course, thanks to Tim M0URX for untangling the problems.

Why did they leave early, the weather maps didn’t show bad weather:

Ducie has no natural harbor. In order to get to water deep enough for the Zodiac to operate you must walk out into the water. At low tide we walked out on a rocky coral (slippery) sea bed with extremely poor (dangerous) footing. At high tide in knee high+ water. We formed a chain gang to get the equipment from the Zodiac to the beach at high tide.

On about Oct 30th the skipper reported that over the next few days the seas and winds would make it very dangerous to extract the team and equipment. He decided not to take chances, he was right.

The extraction process took a long time and looked like something out of a Hollywood action movie with the team members being pushed into the Zodiac at just the right moment, and the skipper hitting the gas. It didn’t seem funny at the time, but in retrospect it was “very” exciting. Ask W1SRD about his 2nd day adventure when he “volunteered” to go back to the island and help retrieve the equipment. This is definitely a young(er) man’s game.

 The other risk was missing the only flight from Mangareva to Tahiti. There is only one flight a week on Tuesday afternoon, miss that flight and you have a real problem. The skipper didn’t want to take chances with the weather by waiting until the last minute to remove us from Ducie. In retrospect, of course he was right. We had plenty of time to make the flight without worry.

Many of the team will be at Visalia where we’ll tell you the rest of the story.

Cheers,
GS K5GS
