When I got licensed in the 00s, I looked for an antenna that I could deploy in my small Palo Alto lot that wouldn't upset my neighbors or my XYL. I ended up with a Hustler 6BTV mounted to the center-of-house utility chimney; I used the DXE tilt-base to make it "easy" to tune and maintain, and laid out two tuned radials for each band across the roof.

It worked much better than I expected, especially since I was doing everything QRP. I could get pretty much everywhere ... except Europe.

The noise level in my neighborhood has gone up and up. I had a OCF dipole up for a while that worked really well as a receiving antenna (well, much better than the vertical), but the tree that it was tied to died and I haven't put up something new yet. I have a receiving loop in the garage waiting for an install.

But I digress - I think that a roof-mounted vertical with tuned radials can work great (and the DXE tilt-base mount is a big win).

VY 73 de chris K6DBG

On 2/22/21 10:07 AM, Bruce H. Bern via Chat wrote:

Truman,

 

I had not worked 80m since my teenage years in the 60s. I boasted a grand total of 7 countries confirmed. Andy, K6ZP, was kind enough to pass along a spare HF9V to me a few months ago. After a little polishing with 3M pads, copious Noalox, and a fresh 200 pf 80m capacitor (from DXE), I was finally back on 80 about 3 months ago. With the antenna ground mounted in my front yard, I'm currently at 57 confirmed on 80... so it works, but a few caveats:

1. I gradually built my radial field, but started with only 12. Ground return loss was so high my FT8 reports were 10 dB down from my received SN ratio. I'm now up to 29 radials (using approximately 1400 ft of assorted wire) and the improvement in my received SN ratio has been dramatic. So lesson 1 is that you need tons of radials. Mine are of assorted lengths from 15 ft to 70 ft, but you just need lots of wire on the ground. As a rule of thumb, many shorter radials are superior to fewer longer radials, but the more there are and the longer they are, the better. Local ground conductivity plays a huge role.

2. Despite the optimistic power handling capabilities from DXE, at least on 80, I note gradually increasing SWR at levels above about 700w- that indicates capacitor overheating, and new ones are $50. I attempted to source beefier, US made items, but there is only one manufacturer remaining in the US, and they quoted $437 for a single doorknob.

3. Tuning absolutely for sure requires an antenna analyzer outdoors beside the antenna. It'll still take a morning for all 9 bands, but at least you won't need psychoactive medication when you're done. Don't even think about an HF9V if you don't own an antenna analyzer. Tuning the higher bands does require dismounting the antenna from its base a few times ( which would be difficult if you're teetering on the roof), tuning the lower bands can be done from the base of the mounted antenna with access required only to about 7 ft from the ground

4. On 80m, I note a usable bandwidth of 40-50 KHz. I retuned to the CW segment (from FT8) with a loop of coax out my shack window connected to the analyzer (RigExpert AA-230 Zoom) 15 feet from the antenna- it took 10 minutes.

5. That said, the HF9V is a fabulous antenna. It is roughly equivalent on 40m to my 46 ft. center loaded rotatable dipole at 44 ft, and perhaps even better at lower angles of radiation. I've used it on all bands with success, and although it's roughly 10 dB down from my Navassa 5 on 20-10m, in terms of cost effectiveness, it's a winner. It's Andy's only antenna, and he's confirmed over 200 countries in less than 2 years.

Feel free to contact me off-list if you have any other questions.

 

73, Bruce K3NQ
