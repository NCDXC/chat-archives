To close out this thread, a recap and ending:
 
In the end, the problem was simple; worn brushes.  But the trip to get there is never simple.  This is posted solely as future reference for a prop pitch owner.
 
The weather finally cooperated (early first spring in N Idaho, two is the norm), so the rotor was extracted from the tower.
It and the controller went into the shop of KJ7TEA, Tom who has extensive knowledge in with motors, mechanical and other things.  With a test set up, it was able to rotate, but very slowly and the motor was 'growly', clearly unhappy.
 
Tom took it apart (upper gear unit; lower gear unit and motor) because we didn't know what the problem was.  The gears (planetary, not taken apart) looked almost virginal; brand new for 70+ years old.  (Hurrah to the war years work quality and toughness!)
Kurt had opted for grease, not oil for lubrication along with sealed bearing instead of wet.  He added an arm on the bottom end of the motor, for a Hall sensor (count the rotations).
 
Then we got to the motor with heavy carbon dust almost everywhere, REALLY thick in the armature and mag coils.  The armature copper contacts had scoring and wear as well (0.08"? it's in the photo).  Since I got this from an estate sale, built circa 2006, (Kurt was literally a back fence neighbor) it's not known if this is 20 years of wear or 70+ (since we can't ask Kurt).  [His old email address didn't bounce, but there was no response so I assume his son isn't watching the old account.  Someone is still making the clamps under the company name but ???]
 
The brushes for the motor were well worn and at least one spring, wasn't extending much.  Uh, Houston?  There are six, two never see current (which two, depends on how the motor is set up).
 
In that interim, Tom took one of his K7NV clamps that he had on hand, made a steel plate with the rotor pattern on it, with the clamp holes.  That assembly was installed on the tower rotor plate, mast tightened down and tower raised (one FIXED position but on the air).
 
Brushes were found online, wait ten days because shipping systems and routes make NO logical sense (reassemble the newly greased gear boxes while waiting, a few small clean ups made of connectors).  The armature was lathed down and the gaps cleaned out (the carbon dust also cleaned out of everything else).
Once they arrived, the brushes were set in the lathe and light sandpaper applied to bring them down to 0.24" (spec) because they were sold as 0.25" and were slight wider.  Test fit, successful.
 
Once the new brushes were installed, the motor was tested, put back into the gear train and it was immediately noted that the rotor (still on the bench) ran MUCH faster (like when I first got it).  It was still noisy but not the grumbly growl (the cut gears won't be silent).
 
Run for a while (20 min?) to seat the brushes with no load, then this morning replaced into the tower.
 
The mast collars are set above the thrust bearing so that they take the downward weight (transfer to the thrust bearing, then tower).  This is done by leaving a gap at the bottom of the K7NV clamp, set and tighten the collars, THEN tightened down the mast clamp (the prop pitch rotor only has to bear rotational loads, as originally designed [bevel gear still in place]).
 
The controller put back in the shack, a couple quick tests while the tower was tilted, SUCCESS!
 
The tower was tilted back up (whew! a workout by arm!), then raised.
 
I'm back on the air, have pictures and videos to post, which I'll make available on request.
 
Two months of downtime, mostly weather caused.  We'll see how long this works out but for now I can 'see' more than north or south (180 in SteppIR speak).  Fortunately, I didn't miss any major DXpeditions; what was there I used my 80 GP and my 160 L (which was pretty ugly).
 
Thank you Tom, it was well out of my wheelhouse and you have the shop and tools with the skills to manage them.
 
73,
