I've recently been studying the effect of height on the DX performance of horizontal 80M antennas. I modeled a simple half-wave dipole over two kinds of soil -- "Sandy" and "Average," that are pretty much the range of what most of us have -- and at heights from 40 ft to 130 ft in 10 ft steps. For "Sandy" soil, every 10 ft of height is worth about 1 dB below 70 ft, and 0.9 dB per 10 ft between 70 ft and 130 ft. For the better "Average" soil, the numbers are 5-10% larger. In other words, raising a dipole from 40 ft to 70 ft is worth 3 dB, going from there to 130 ft is good for another 5.5 dB.
These numbers come from comparing radiation at 10 degrees elevation. In general, the differences are pretty consistent from 15 degrees down to 1 degree. That is, if the advantage of height is 3 dB at 10 degrees, it's also about 3 dB from 1 degree to at least 15 degrees.
A good way to think about this is to realize that a low dipole (less than a quarter wave) is a cloud-warmer that will work pretty well for short distances, while the closer the antenna gets to a half-wave, the more the radiation is concentrated at the low angles we need to work most DX.
Obviously, these height numbers can be scaled to 40M by dividing by 2. That is, 65-70 ft is a close to ideal mounting height, and every 5 ft below that loses 0.9 - 1 dB.
Think about this when you're deciding whether to use a beam or a high wire, and/or how much the cost of a tower (or tree-climbers) is worth. A 2-el Yagi is good for about 3.5 dB, a 3rd element adds a dB or so. That 2-el Yagi at 40 ft is roughly equal to a dipole at 70 ft. Do both and you're 6-7 dB better than the dipole at 40 ft. Raise an 80M dipole from 40 ft to 130 ft and add a reflector and your 100W radio yields 900W ERP.
