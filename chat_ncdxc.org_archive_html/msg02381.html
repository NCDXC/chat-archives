Thanks. I have the StackMatch II and 180 degree phasing unit. I have posts
in concrete out in the back 40 and am finishing building two 24' top hatted
verticals. 

I had a dipole up 65' and it was the best 40M antenna I have ever had.
Unfortunately, a squirrel kept eating through the thick Dacron rope - but it
was a blessing - the neighbor chopped down that awful Monterrey Pine. It was
a great 70'+ antenna support - but was leaning towards my house and getting
dried out and dropping branches. 

So - now its back down low to the ground for me - hi hi. 

I have attached an EZNec file where I have modeled the 2 verts, and am
_almost_ correct. I don't know how to model the load - but the two sources
are either going to be phased at 90, 180 or out of phase by 180 degrees. The
patterns in my EZnec model look right - the gain is just under 1 dBi lower
than I have read about - so I'm maybe 90% there in my model. 

My design goal is to get something up on 40M better than a low dipole and
better than a single vertical - with decent end fire pattern and half way
decent broadside. The end fire figure 8 that the Array Solutions 180 degree
phasing box is icing on the cake. My definition of "better" is 2 dB . . .
with noticeable pattern.

Hi Rich,

I bought W6NF's very nice phased 40M vertical package when he moved to
HC2 last year. I then did some modeling, and compared the results with my
high dipoles (at 110 ft). Thanks to their height, they interact well with
the earth to provide strong low angle radiation, so they outperform what a
2-el vertical array can do.

I plan to use it for Field Day and CQP expeditions, where it is typically
difficult to rig high dipoles. Optimum dipole height varies a bit with
terrain, but in general, my modeling shows that the dipoles are better when
you get them above about a half wavelength.

I haven't looked at the DXE box, but W6NF did his phasing with transmission
lines. For our CQP and FD setups, we typically use a fixed
70 degree aim, so I plan to simply make a simple Yagi array, with the
reflector a bit longer. This sacrifices a half dB or so of gain, with the
advantage of simplicity -- I'll simply drive one against radials, and
connect the reflector to radials. I expect to see an advantage of about 6dB
to the east coast as compared to the low dipoles (20-30 ft) we have been
using.

73, Jim K9YC
