I can’t vote, but I have an idea on how to quantify the success of a DXpedition.  Success is highly subjective and often defined by an individuals needs (and could depend on which coast is deciding).  The only part that can’t be quantified is the ‘fun factor’ which opens the door for discussion.

 

How about creating a “score” expressed as a math formula that takes the total number of contacts made divided by the number of simultaneous stations operated divided by the total bands operated but multiplied by the inverse of the clublog need rating ( if #2 of 341 possible countries, then 341/2 or *170.5)?  It would create a baseline ‘value’ or score for discussion of the portions that are hard to quantify; QRO/P (mile per watt); best use of propagation; pileup management; best antenna utilization etc. (those are subjective).

 

That means that the smaller low band only station contacts have more point value; the greater needed locations also makes more points but a 10 station 24/7 group with a lower need country gets a lower score.  It’s an even field then.

 

There are other factors (number of zones worked, modes worked, Q’s per dollar spent, trip goals met) that could be included as well but my point is that it’s possible to create a numeric scoring ‘value’ for each trip.  Someone with actual math skills could probably express this better.  All that’s left to consider (discuss) after determining the ‘score’ is the personal aspects; it was easy or fun or needed by individuals.

 

73,

Rick wa6nhc

 

From: Charles K. Epps

 

It might be an interesting number, Craig, but one you have to be careful with.  For instance, the C82DX DXpedition set as its goal to work stations on the low bands where Mozambique was most needed.  That meant low rates night after night trying to work stations through all the QRN found in that part of the world.  Sometimes, at the end of a four hour shift, the operators had added only 10 to 25 QSOs to the log -- but they all were on 80 and/or 160 and were new band/countries for the guys they worked.  They could have easily made a few hundred QSOs during that same time period on 30m or 20m, but they mostily would have been working stations who already had Mozambique confirmed on those bands.  So, in my opinion, while sheer number of QSOs might be one measure of a DXpedition's success, it certainly is not the only measure.  More important, I think, is what were the DXpedition's stated goals and how well did it achieve those goals.

 

73 de Rusty, W6OAT
