Paul,

This IS a superb article! Just about all one needs to know about common mode
chokes and their role in noise reduction  is contained in Councelman's article and 
Jim Brown's K9YC web site (http://audiosystemsgroup.com/CoaxChokesPPT.pdf).
Unlike many articles these explain the how, where, what and why of using these
devices to kill the sorts of noise pollution that all hams who live in heavily populated
areas face.

I find that the best way to proceed is to get a good handle on the incoming noise level
on several bands and then place a choke at the antenna's feed point. If it helps (it will)
place another at the receiver input. A third at a voltage point on the coax outer conductor
may also help. These best locations for chokes must be found by experiment.

Core material is of course very important. Choosing a low loss core material such as mix 61
used to build RF transformers is just the wrong thing to do when trying to realize high choking
impedance. Mix 31 (1.8-25 MHz) and mix 43 (10-200 MHz) are great choices. . Each time the 
coax passes thru another core the impedance goes up so that n cores results in ~n times the
impedance of a the coax passing thru a single core. When n turns of coax is wound around a 
large core, the impedance goes up as ~n squared. Lots of cores and lots of turns on the correct
ferrite is the key to very high impedance chokes. Placing the chokes at voltage points on the
outer conductor of the coax results in maximum noise reduction. Be sure to use cable that will
tolerate tight radius bends such as RG213.

I was able to reduce my noise level on 80-10 meters from S7 down to S2 (a 30 dB reduction)after 
a few days of playing around with various mix 31 chokes as described by K9YC. Try it you will like 
the results!
