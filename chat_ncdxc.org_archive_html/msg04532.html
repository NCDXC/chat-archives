Having two antennas to compare is a great strategy, and the results may surprise you.

My only 160 TX antenna for many years was what I called a Perverted Vee, to suggest its unorthodox shape. It was basically a 160 dipole, but the spacing of the trees that supported it was such that I had to make it an inverted vee with the high point off-center and the ends bent. Maybe half of it was sort of horizontal. That part of the antenna was at 130 ft--the trees are redwoods. It reminded me of Quasimoto (Charles Laughton's version) by how bent and distorted it was.

Some years later I also erected an inverted L with the bent top at 80' and two raised radials 10 ft. off the ground. Over several seasons, it never beat the Perverted Vee on any path, but did occasionally equal it, so I took it down. In 2012 I took down my treetop beams, and the PV came down with them. I needed another topband TX antenna so I built another inverted L in the same place as before, but with a K2AV folded counterpoise (FCP). Last year I got it up in time to work PP Rocks and Malpelo for 230 and 231 confirmed on top, but that was not exactly a comprehensive test. (Yes, there are receiving antennas.)

This year has not yet been particularly productive. I miss the Perverted Vee to compare with, because it had a history. So I have no idea how the new antenna would have compared. Even at season end it will be a guess.

So I am scheming on where to put another TX antenna, given a heavily forested odd-shaped hillside property of modest size. The old Perverted Vee is not a candidate for resurrection.

The 80m loop is more than a delta loop. It is also a 40m delta bisquare. It has been up for 20 years, except when winter storms have ravaged it. Recently I redesigned the matching scheme, using SimSmith, which did not exist 20 years ago, and now it shows low SWR across most of both 80 and 40. I have 330 ocutners on 40, mostly with that antenna. In a pinch, it works on other bands, too, but not all of them.

With limited space for radials, you might want to look at K2AV;s folded counterpoise scheme as an alternative.to several raised radials. He has designs for both 80 and 160.

Garry

Your pattern would still be mostly upwards with the dipole at 100'. That is not necessarily bad. My 80m loop, apex down, has its horizontal at 100' and I am pushing 300 counters with it. Max radiation might be  straight up, but that does not mean you won't have significant radiation at moderate angles.

If you are considering an inverted L, you did not mention your planned ground system, which is absolutely the most critical factor.

On Dec 1, 2013, at 12:10 PM, Jim Brown wrote:


On 12/1/2013 12:00 PM, Andreas Junge wrote:

Your numbers are valid for flat terrain. I have played with N6BV’s HFTA program quite a bit and it has been quite an eye opener. If you consider “your” specific topography, higher might not always be better:-)  
Have you tried HFTA?

Yes, extensively, and I swear by it. I've modeled every direction on every band in 5 degree increments of azimuth and 10 ft increments of height. For my QTH, which looks into dirt from about 0 degrees around to about 130 degrees, higher is better on every band. HFTA tells me that 120 ft is my best compromise in those directions, and that's where my SteppIR is. HFTA tells me I'd have to go to at least 300 ft to get any better. :) There's a secondary higher angle peak from my terrain for the higher bands in the range of 30-45 ft, and it's near ideal for working into the Pacific, and that's where my second tower is.
