I'd agree, the greyline *can* be awesome. You've probably seen what can happen, 20-30dB signal improvement sometimes over a matter of minutes, only to fall again as the enhancement moves with the terminator. The lower the frequency, the shorter that enhancement window usually is.

I'd venture two guesses about why you didn't hear anything (complete guesses with a little bit of experience thrown in):

1. Overall conditions weren't sufficient at the time for you to experience the holy grail. Maybe there was 20dB of enhancement, but that enhancement + primary signal was still much below your MDS  (minimum discernable signal).

2. Maybe there really was no greyline ducting happening at that time and therefore there was nothing for you to hear. Certainly W6<->VU on short path on 80 would be most difficult, and even with greyline enhancement its tough, however the greyline mostly-over-water-path would be much preferred *IF* its viable. 

From my experience, while this is the time of year to expect greyline, its not always there. Sometimes for a reason (no one with directional antennas are pointing that direction), and sometimes it just seems that the traditional short path is the only path that works.  

One of my favorite stories of greyline is this. Years ago I was doing CQWW SSB SOAB at KH6LC. I don't remember exactly what time it was, but it was shortly after sunset (still a little light outside). I was on 20M running UA9's, etc. - conditions were quite good. Over the pileup comes UU7K, he was easily 20 over 9. He asked me to move to 80M, and I happily made the move. I moved to 80 and within seconds heard him calling me. I was using Lloyd's 4-square and was listening NE (short path). On that direction he was maybe S7 or S8. When I switched LP, he was way over S9. The QSO completed easily. Awesome. Of course I know big tetrodes and such are the norm in that part of the world, but still, way cool. 

I contrast this with other forms of ducting, etc. - for example, equatorial spread-F - which I've successfully exploited a lot over the years - WHEN its there its awesome - and not that many people know it or exploit it - but its not always there. It is, however, more reliable I think than traditional greyline longpath, but you have to be at the right latitudes to see that consistency. I've experienced it at the high of the cycles as well as the dead lows.

For those that haven't heard of Equatorial Spread-F, I sort of think of it like sporadic E, but different.  Essentially, at lower latitudes (maybe 27*N or less), you point your antennas a few degrees (10-30*) west of due south (close to a straight long path, but not always, usually 10-20* off straight longpath), and at high frequencies (typically 10/15M) around local midnight, you'll hear zone 14/15/16/17/18/19/20/21 depending on time. This path has been great for me from KH6 as well as zone 7 (YN). Of course gain helps, and that's what makes me think its not traditional long path, its a few degrees off, as verified by a quite directional antenna on 10m (7L M2). The other thing is that on the far end of the circuit, the enhancement is usually about +2 hours AFTER local sunrise or so. 

I have recordings somewhere of enormous pileups of that ESF zone 18/17/16/15 progression over a couple hours - most stations calling are not strong because they're 10-50w guys with verticals or low dipoles that have no idea whats going on - but when someone spots you as "Longpath" then you hear the guys with yagis show up and they're super loud when they get the right direction. 

Anyhow, sorry, I digressed. Love talking about this stuff.
