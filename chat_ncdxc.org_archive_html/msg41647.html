I had many antennas over the years and most had pluses and minuses. Some had serious minuses, at least for me and my situation.

 

For the lower bands, meaning, mainly 160.80 and 60 I had best luck with verticals. I do have ground radials and since I have 2 verticals, I have the radial systems connected together which is like magic, if you have some room to do so. I used to have an Alpha Delta dipole which allegedly covered 160m even, but all I could hear was maybe Texas on a good night. I had no way to put it up high enough and so maybe that was not so much the antennas’ fault. I also tried a variety of dipoles and long wires and good for local chit chat but for DXing it can be rough. I only have a 30m dipole still at times since the Urban Bean 30m is TERRIBLE. This is a know issue. High SWR and needs calibration often, at least with my high wind situation as the elements are extended longer on 30/40.

 

As for other antennas, the one I liked the least was my cobweb antenna I had over a decade ago before going to a hex beam. In fact had two, one prebuilt and one I made based on plans. However, I hear they are not all created equal, so take that with a grain of salt. I will take a hex beam any day, however with high winds here in Pacifica, I spent more time repeating the antenna than using it to be completely honest. When they are in one piece, they are wonderful for what they are. Not for high wind areas, as even if they do not break, the wires can touch and that can be an issue obviously. I had mine seriously modified with extra zip ties and some added safety measures for windy situations. By the time I got it tweaked enough, I changed it out. Go figure! You do want to coat them if you are in a high moisture area, and good to replace the parts as they start to get brittle but still would get another.

 

I also had the Butternut multiband HF9V I think is called with the 160m addon coil. Very hard to tune (if not impossible) if you have the 160m coil on it. However, it worked on 160m and could make contacts from 6-160m. Even used it on 60m. Needed a lot of radials to work well though and that is not a plus. I had a few radials early on and did not realize what I was missing until I added more. So, if you are patient and are OK with questionable SWR at times, room for radials, they are OK. Will do the job, but are noisy. For FT8 use great, Phone not so much in my opinion.

 

Currently using 2 SteppIRs. Urban beam which is OK except for the 30m issue and sad to see the brand go away and a BigIR Mark IV with the 80/60m coil add-on. Which is just OK, this has a persistent 60/80m issue, apparently common as when I called them they acted like it was not unusual. I had issues with the add-on box but for 6-40 it is good as a backup for my other antennas. My Urban beam can take a better beating than the hex beam could. However, I did have to cycle the elements in it more than a few times after heavy rain/wind, same is true for the SteppIR vertical. The sponge on the tips does get wet and can be an issue. Also, if you have rodents, they seem to find the control cables for the SteppIR delicious. I had to do several repairs, basically annually. They seem to leave my rotator cables and coax alone so something about the cable they sell maybe is different? Not sure. If you have cats outdoors, might not be an issue. Hi hi

 

I also have a M2 5 element 6m and a helical wound vertical for 160m both of which are wonderful. Can’t complain there, so no negatives. I did have to add extra supports for the 160m for high winds, but that is likely not an issue for others as much as here in Pacifica.

As we all sit around waiting for KP5/NP3VI to fill the bands, 

 

Here is a question: What antenna do you regret buying? 

 

For me, it was a Cushcraft R7 vertical.  It was a very poor vertical antenna, very noisy and just very ineffective. It was one of my first HF antennas but was quickly replaced by a G5RV dipole that was much quieter and much better. 

 

What is your antenna regret?
