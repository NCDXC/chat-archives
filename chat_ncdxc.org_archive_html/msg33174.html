Hi Vijay,

Great questions. 

I have used nothing but solid state HF amplifiers for the last 5 years.
These have been both homebrew 1.5 kW LDMOS amps I have designed
and built and commercial units such as the KPA1500 and Icom PW1.
That said my first thought is that you should very carefully weigh the
question:  "Is a solid state high power amp right for you?" If you are a very
deliberate and careful operator who does not get flustered calling a very rare
DX station or changing bands and antennas during a contest then maybe
you are a good candidate for solid state. In the the heat of the moment you
switch to the wrong antenna or worse overdrive the solid state amp you run
a good chance of destroying one or more FET's. I have built 4 LDMOS solid
state amps and have blown several LDMOS parts in the process. I have also
built and used several home brew and commercial vacuum tube amps. The
tube amps are much more forgiving of operator boneheaded errors. I have never
blown up a solid state amp on the air but I am that careful deliberate operator.
If you are as well then solid state may be for you. Another advantage of tube amps
is they are often available used from SK estates or from guys who upgraded to
solid state. However after some of these guys who "upgraded" blow up the amplifier
they seem to send them out to be fixed and turn around and sell them. You can
watch for those as well. You should never buy a solid state amp that is not specifically
protected against RF overdrive, overcurrent, overvoltage, device operating temperature
and high load SWR. The SWR protection must be fast (<50 usec) to save the FETs.
The KPA 1500 is well designed and well protected but Elecraft still gets quite a few back 
with blown FETs. The KPA 500 and KPA 1500 are both excellent amps. Like all solid
state amplifiers they require a careful operator.

Finally on your 120V vs 240V question. You can run a 500W amplifier either tube or 
solid state on 120V. A 1500W amp is at best 60% efficient and requires 2500 to 3000
watts of ac power. That means an ac current draw of up to 25 amps. Some homes have
20 amp 120V outlets (look for 12 gauge wire to the outlet) but most homes are wired for
15 amp service. I strongly suggest you drop 240V service into your shack so that you
can consider the full range of amplifier options.

Good luck with your amplifier project. I know that you will enjoy whatever unit you
purchase.

73,

John Eisenberg K6YP
