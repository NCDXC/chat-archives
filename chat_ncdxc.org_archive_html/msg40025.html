On Jul 15, 2025, at 6:07 AM, Gene K5GS via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

Paul wrote:
"The small fees that we pay go to support the overhead for the program.”


There is absolutely no evidence of the truth of that statement. ALL of the fees charged by ARRL:

-Annual membership dues
-LoTW credit redemption fees
-Fees for plaques, certificates, endorsement stickers, and lapel pins
-Fees for Contest Gavels (yes, they now charge the winner of a contest for the gavel!)
-Fees for receiving a physical copy of QST, NCJ, and all of their other publications
-Fees for sending them Bureau QSLs

and much, much, more, all go to the general fund of the ARRL are are not earmarked for any particular purpose. They pay for the salaries of staff and officers, travel budgets for directors and others, etc. Like most long-standing, bloated organizations, they exist for the sake of existing, and for paying themselves to do so.

And with all this revenue from all of these sources, they STILL request MORE MONEY in the form of donations (revenue with no corresponding service provided), testamentary requests, free legal counsel (Volunteer attorneys who help hams with legal issues, typically antenna zoning problems), free QSL card checkers, free license examiners, etc.

Then they took a big pile of this money and gave it to international scammers who were able to perpetrate a ransomware attack on the organization because they had no clue how to build or manage an IT infrastructure. (Compare to Clublog, which asks for NO MONEY for ANY of its services, and whose IT infrastructure is both clean and secure, and has never been hacked or ransomed.)

Perhaps 75-100 years ago, when Amateur Radio was new and not well-understood by the public, ARRL performed a service by becoming the spokesgroup for a bunch of (admittedly nerdy, especially for the times) folks who were providing an important public service while pursuing their hobby, both for fun and for advancing the leading edge of technology. Not so much now. Amateur radio is still an important public service, both for emergency preparedness and for fostering communications between PEOPLE (not nations or diplomats) worldwide, but ARRL has little to do or say about this. Their influence on the legislature is minimal at best, and their support of the amateur community, as we have all seen, is only done in exchange for money.

They have become, essentially, a publication house (selling books, plaques, trinkets, and made-up services like DXCC) masquerading as a 501(c)3 charitable organization.
