Hi Kevin, Wayne et al,

I recall hearing BY1PK on the air occasionally prior to 1982.  They would call long CQs and answer no one ... and certainly no one in the USA.  What I have heard (but cannot verify) is that some stations would reply to those CQs by sending their callsigns and a signal report, and then wait for a BY1PK QSL to show up some months later via the QSL bureau.  Wayne, you probably know better than any of us whether or not anyone ever tried to submit one of those BY1PK "QSLs" for DXCC credit.

In late 1982, Kan JA1BK, somehow got permission to go on the air from BY1PK.  He opened up on 15m CW at 00:00Z on November 28, 1982.  He quickly worked a bunch of his friends in Japan, then turned to the USA.  His first two North American QSOs were with Bill, W6TPH, at 00:11Z and W6OAT at 00:12Z.  I don't know who he worked next because I was busy pouring a glass of vodka!

Three weeks later, I was on my way to Nepal to operate 9N38 in celebration of the Nepalese king's 38th birthday.  I had a day layover in Japan and met Kan for dinner that night.  He had his BY1PK log with him (plus a tape recording someone in Japan had made of his QSO with me!).  I asked him if I could fill out QSLs for my friends in NCDXC and take them back to California with me.  Unfortunately, he said he had only one blank BY1PK card available, and that I could NOT have it!  So I finally convinced him to let me make multiple photo-copies of the QSL on 8.5 x 11 inch sheets of plain paper, filled in the QSO details for every NCDXC member I could spot in the log, got Kan to personally verify and sign each sheet, and passed them out at the January, 1983, NCDXC meeting.  I got lots of vodkas bought for me that night.  So, as far as I know, NCDXC members were the first folks in North America to get DXCC credit for a valid post-war QSO with China.

73,
Rusty, W6OAT
