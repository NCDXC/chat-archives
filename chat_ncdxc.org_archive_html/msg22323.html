My first antenna as a ham was a butternut HF6V and it worked great elevated at my QTH in San Diego.  I think the thing I had most challenge was to get it tuned properly (especially on 15m) and as an elevated antenna it is an especially very challenging antenna to tune.  Here in Texas, I picked up another HF6V with the stub tuned resonant radials (sent to me by a friend in NCDXC) and I had a lot of challenges again getting it to tune.  I took it down recently and opt to use my 53’ monopole with 2000’ of radials and a MFJ 2910 tuning unit at the base.  I use the stub tuned radials with it also.

 

In San Jose when I lived there, I ran a steppir dipole on a chimney mount and a R7000.  It was a pretty good setup.  I missed a couple key dxpeditions (like Tromelin) but I don’t like working DX at any other station but my own (some fine gents offered their stations, but I am obstinate about my internal rules for DX, lol).

 

Coincidentally, my 53’ monopole base is from that R7000 antenna I had in San Jose.  I ended up selling the traps and have been using that base in this monopole fashion for years.  I finally got DXCC on 80m a month ago so that was pretty cool.  I use the MFJ2910 for tuning antenna at the base.  It is the best MFJ product I have ever owned.  I don’t have a lot of regard generally for MFJ because most of the stuff I have ever owned from MFJ either did not work out of the box or eventually succumbed to early death.  This one however has never failed me. 

 

https://www.dxengineering.com/parts/mfj-2910

I share most of Paul's experiences with the R7 (in my case a R8). I certainly worked my

first 150 countries with that antenna. Like Paul I found that a few elevated radials really

Hi Truman, 

 

I started out with verticals in my backyard as a new DXer. The Cushcraft R7 was my first. It got me on the bands and I could work the South Pacific, Asia and South America but I could not work Europe with my vertical. 

 

Cushcraft claimed that the R7 did not need radials, but guess what?  When I read that adding 3-4 elevated radials would improve any vertical, I gave that a try and saw remarkable improvement. I've not used the Butternut but a lot of guys use them.  I like the Hustler 4BTV and other verticals and we use those on DXpeditions with 3-4 elevated radials. 3-4 elevated radials is much better than many more directly on the ground. 

 

If you install your vertical in the corner of your yard, you can run elevated radials along the top of your fences and no one is the wiser. 

 

Verticals can be fun but just keep in mind that they are very much a minimal antenna or a compromised antenna. Verticals also have a higher noise level than a horizontally polarized antenna. 

 

Good luck with your project, 

 

Paul N6PSE
