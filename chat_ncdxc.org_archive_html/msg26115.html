Paul,

I have your set up - 4x4x12 ft buried about 4 ft in the ground and used as an anchor for my 30 ft push up mast with my 3-el Mosley TA-33jr at about 25 ft. Guy wire anchors along the side yard go into large "screw in dog stakes" I picked up at a flea market. The first 8 ft of the mast is strapped onto the side of the anchor post. 

Has been there 15 years still going strong. That said, Paul N6PSE's H30 and the larger sleeve pipe is easier and if you have the guys you really do not need the 4x4 or 4x6 post. We did the 4x4 post because we didn't think we'd have space for the guy wires, but once we got it in the air we realized we could fit them in the side yard - just some steeper angles 

a Hexbeam at about 25 ft is going to work well... 

73

John NZ6Q


On 2022-06-14 8:40 pm, Paul N6PSE via Chat wrote:

Paul,  I don't think you will be able to find a 4X6X16' that is pressure treated. Non pressure treated wood in the ground will rot within a few years. Even my pressure treated 4X4 fence posts rot in 10 years. 
 
I recommend a Rohn H30 push up mast. Instead of extending it to 30', you can extend it much shorter for additional strength. The Rohn H30 also has guy collars every eight feet and makes it very easy to guy. Also easy to install a rotator on the top. 
 
To install the H30, I recommend buying a 2' long larger pipe that the bottom of the H30 can slide into. Put that pipe into the ground flush with the surface and you can easily remove the H30 if you ever need to. 
 
The Rohn H30 is $110 at DX Engineering:  https://www.dxengineering.com/search/part-type/telescoping-masts
 
Good luck, 

Paul N6PSE

Does anyone in the club currently use or have experience in mounting a hexbeam on a push-up mast?  If so, I'd like to pick your brain.  I have a very narrow backyard and was thinking of using 4x6x16 lumber, buried and cemented 4' into the ground as both a mast post for the rotor and a thrust bearing, as well as 2 more of the same as tie-offs for elevated guy rope, with the other two guys tied off to the roof eave.  

I'd like to do this once and do it right, and to not lose sleep about a mast coming down on my home or my shared fenceline...  Anyone have any thoughts on this setup or relevant experience with the lateral forces that might be placed upon my elevated guy posts by a Hex - or to the lateral strength of pressure-treated lumber that has dynamic force applied to its endpoint?
