Yes, it’s not difficult to build a team covering #1-8….but you forgot about #9 Mother Nature and #10 Murphy.

Bouvet is a Two-Part challenge:  (1) Get there safely with the right team & equipment and (2) hope that Mother Nature takes a nap while you’re there.

2018 - 3YØZ - Outstanding DXpedition leaders Bob (K4UEE) and Ralph (K0IR) spent 2 years putting together what they thought was a solid DXpedition plan for Bouvet, including taking 2 helicopters.  Engine failure on the Betanzos + Mother Nature prevented them from ever landing on the island.  20 of my good friends spent one month on the ocean with no QSOs.  A Failure?  Bad Planning?  Bad Luck?  Combination?  
Score (10 Pts. Max):  Mother Nature 10; DXer  0

2019 - 3YØI - After two weeks at sea, and only 70 nautical miles from Bouvet, Dom (3Z9DX) and his team on the Atlantic Tuna had to return to Cape Town due to a violent storm causing radar and navigation damage.  A Failure?  Bad Planning?  Bad Luck?  Combination?  
Score (10 Pts. Max):  Mother Nature 10; DXer  0

2023 - 3YØJ - On January 30, 2023, Ken (LA7GIA) and team on the Marama anchored at Bouvet.  Challenging weather conditions and rough waters prevented them from transferring all of their gear to the island.  8 operators did land and set up a small camp but were hampered with little fuel and no amplifier.  They logged 18,845 QSOs (8,590 Unique Calls) and likely handed out 2000+ ATNOs.  A Failure?  Bad Planning?  Bad Luck?  Combination?  
Score (10 Pts. Max):  Mother Nature 9; DXer  1

As for these two recent announcements…...

2024-2025 - 3YØI = 2nd attempt by Dom (3Z9DX).  https://www.dx-world.net/3y0i-bouvet-island/
2025 - 3YØK = 2nd attempt by Ken (LA7GIA)  https://3y0k.com/

….their success will depend on how well they handle the curve balls.

It is not difficult to build a team of 14 men to go to Bouvet IF:

1) You have a good track record.
2) You have good Leadership with good people and project management skills. 
3) Your Leaders have a clear and attainable vision. 
4) You under promise and over deliver. 
5) You have a sturdy and sound vessel. 
6) You have a secure landing plan that does not involve swimming ashore. 
7) You cover all contingencies, medical emergencies etc. 
8) You avoid unnecessary shortcuts and inefficiencies. 

Three amateurs and four helpers with no Physician is shortcutting in a big way. 

Paul N6PSE

> Rich, I have been studying their plans listed on their website. Based on what I am seeing, I am not sure that they are going to correct prior errors or restore a reputation.
>
> Here is what I see:
> Three man team with four non ham helpers. (Why not a large team?)

Because: (1) It’s hard to find people that want to go to Bouvet. Last time, quite a few “team members” refused to leave the ship once the landing conditions were determined. And (2) At least 4 of the stations will be remotely operated, either from the ship or the other side of the world, which reduces the number of people that have to be there.

> Camp 1 (Main camp)
> Radio 1 + Amplifier CW/SSB
> Radio 2 + Amplifier CW/SSB
> Radio 4 + Amplifier  FT8
> Radio 5 (No amplifier) FT8
> RIB 1  CW (No Amplifier)
> RIB 2 CW (No Amplifier)
> RIB 3 FT8 (No Amplifier)
> RIB 4 FT8 (No Amplifier)
>

Be careful NOT to conflate what Ken is proposing (essentially, a remotely operated station) with the “RiB”, a specific implementation of a full DXpeditiion station (including amplifier), developed and demonstrated by George Wallner, AA7JV. Ken (at least at this point) is not planning to use George’s RiBs, although my understanding is that George is consulting with Ken on best practices for remote DXpeditioning, having a fair amount of experience now.

> Camp 2-North America Camp  (Cape Fie)
> Radio #3 + Amplifier CW/SSB
> Radio #5 FT8 (No amplifier)
>
> So of their TEN radios, only four will have amplifiers and of that, only one amplifier at the Camp 2 North America Camp.
>
> For those small US stations that did not eek out a contact with 3Y0J, this cannot be very encouraging.
>
> Paul N6PSE
>

Well, 10 radios with SOME amplifiers is a whole lot better than last time. (Which was two radios, no amps, and very modest wire antennas.) It remains to be seen if he will find funding, even at the claimed $150K level.

And for the record, *so far*, I have no personal association with this mission (last time I was a pilot station), and NCDXF has not even begun a formal assessment for funding.
