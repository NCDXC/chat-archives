Same here. Back in Massachusetts, I put up a Hy-Gain 5-BTV vertical in my nice, flat, 1/2 acre yard. I even had access (through my employer at the time, Digital Equipment Corp.) to a state-of-the-art HP Network Analyzer. Probably cost $50K back then, now it’s a few hundred dollar device.

Was able to adjust the antenna for perfect resonance and pure resistive load on all bands. Didn’t matter. That’s when I learned that a vertical antenna is a poor propagator, equally in all directions. Of course, my lot was kinda sandy soil, poor conductivity, and that’s the main problem. I have had great success with verticals, even short ones, at my various Holiday DXpeditions, as long as I was adjacent to salt water.

And I second the motion of another poster who touted Optibeam. I also have an OB 11-5 (which really is a 17-element antenna, not 11 elements, if you include the parasitic elements for 12 and 17 meters). That antenna went up here in 2008, and has NEVER NEEDED the slightest adjustment or repair. It performs (performed) flawlessly, per the specs, from the day it was raised to the current day. High-quality German engineering, everything assembles EXACTLY as it should. All holes line up with millimeter precision, and (believe it or not), there are NO ADJUSTMENTS required (or even possible) to bring the antenna to resonance on all bands, 20-10. No insulator failures due to UV exposure, no loosening of parts, nothing but lots of QSOs.

I especially like that it uses a square cross-section boom (not a round pipe). It requires square-shaped U-bolts to attach the elements (which are provided), but it means that all of the elements line up in the same horizontal plane without ANY adjustment. That’s often a challenge with conventional round-boom Yagis.

Tom Schmenger, DF2BO (the founder and long-time owner of Optibeam) recently retired and sold his product line, but as long as the new owner simply builds and ships the product as he designed it, it should continue to be great.
