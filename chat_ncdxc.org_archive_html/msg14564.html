Unfortunately, this post along with the eHam post referenced only increase the confusion!

 

It’s not clear from the wording what “high” or “low” mean.  Do these words refer to the audio tones?  Do they refer to the Mark and Space RF frequencies?  Do they refer to the amplitude of the tones?  Adding 2.13 (I think the author means 2.125 kHz) or subtracting 2.28 (I think the author means 2.295 kHz) only applies for one specific audio tone pair.  Not everyone uses the commonly assumed “default” of 2125/2295 Hz.  I sure don’t.

 

Regardless of how the RTTY signal is generated (AFSK, FSK, LSB, USB, reverse, etc), the higher of the two RF frequencies is the proper logical Mark and that is the frequency that is the standard for specifying a RTTY frequency for spotting or meeting someone for a schedule.  If your radio doesn’t tell you the correct RF Mark frequency, then you need to understand enough about how the signal is generated to calculate the correct RF Mark frequency to communicate clearly with others about what frequency you are operating RTTY on.

Also by convention, RTTY is sent using the LSB (FSK or AFSK) so Mark is the low tone of the tone pair. If sent using USB (without REV), Mark is the high tone.

What can be really confusing is looking at a panadapter. When properly done the Mark is *always* the higher frequency peak of the pair in either LSB or USB. It’s pretty easy to spot someone with wrong polarity since the Mark usually is the higher peak (more average energy I believe).

The convention for specifying RTTY frequencies is to use the RF Mark frequency.   It is irrelavent whether the transmission is AFSK or FSK.  When either method is properly configured and adjusted they are indistinguishable on the air.

 

The reason some spotted frequencies are wrong is that the operator uses their radio dial frequency which may not be the Mark frequency.  Often it is the suppressed carrier frequency which differs from the Mark frequency by whatever local tone frequency is being used.  Each operator on the two sides of the QSO can use any local audio tone frequencies.  They are independent and can be arbitrarily different. 

 

When an operator fails to understand these frequency relationships, they are likely to be confused and specify the wrong frequency.  The error could be up to about 2 kHz off since most people use local audio tones at about 2 kHz or lower.
