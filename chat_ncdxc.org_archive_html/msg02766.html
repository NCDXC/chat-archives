The fund raising goal is on the FT5ZM web site – which is a fantastic idea.. Their “fund-ometer” says they have raised $268,918K out of $400k, and just yesterday I saw an email where they expect (with QSL donations) that they are $75K short right now.

 

Heard Island will cost more. I’m not at liberty to discuss right now – but all I can say is that a pattern for polar (south seas) DX-peditions might have us at the “tipping point”. The Marion DuFresne, (which was used for VK0IR in 1997), now quoted us a price near $1 million (said in my best Dr. Evil impression). The reason the team was able to do this same thing in 1997 for roughly $300K is that they were able to “tag along” on a partially filled boat. But even so – everything cost a lot more now.

 

EVERYONE who goes is pay per seat – that’s why that is really kind of a silly term. You take the total cost, divide it by how much to charge per seat. If you make the seats too expensive – you might exclude some of the best operators. Not to mention – it’s not just cost. If it takes 9 days to sail each way, that’s 18 days just travelling to and fro. Now – add another week or two – plus the flight to get to and from where the ship is berthed, and you are talking maybe 6 weeks off work. This means you use all of your vacation and maybe lose a pay check or two. What do you charge each person? 15K? 20K? And at these rates – what kind of person can afford this? I can’t even afford to get away from work that long – so will never be on one myself.

 

I personally think the DX community is going to have to get quite “creative” in being to pay for such ventures from now on. The entities I wonder about are in or below the Roaring 40’s. These make the F&W entities look like a cake walk . . . .

 

In 10 years – how many people will be planning such trips? In 5 years?

 

It will only happen if we all work together – and not criticize anyone’s effort. Personally – if someone even tries – regardless of “Q Rate”, I am very much thankful. There is room for all manners and levels of expertise. The ATNO’s from the best operations count the same as the one’s from non-record breaking operations.

 

Some of my most memorable operations weren’t even large team ops – just one person “on the road” two people in a sailboat or someone working for the UNHCR . . . .

 

73,

 

Rich

Unfortunately, Martti’s explanation of the term DXPP is woefully incomplete and as such inadequate. Ralph, K0IR might remember our discussion back in the late 80s when he was planning for Peter I and how to raise funds for that one. Peter I was terribly expensive even back then. My suggestion at the time – and I don’t claim to have originated it – was that they needed some operators who had the time, the money, and a good attitude. Of course adequate time was necessary, as was the money.

But, from an operating point of view, they needed operators with a good attitude so that they would be willing to learn the finer points. Operators who knew they needed guidance. If they just thought they could operate and in fact couldn’t, that could be a disaster. You all have heard examples. You don’t need an operator on 15M SSB working guys at a rate of one every 2-3 minutes because it is a new country. Even newcomers can do better, but they need guidance. That help will be provided as long as there aren’t too many. I learned a lot at XF4L, and it didn’t take that long.

Having said all of that, it is obvious that we need to help newcomers learn. The Finns are particularly good at bring newcomers along, although mostly in contesting. A few of us from Wyoming and Utah are planning a trip to the Pacific to do just that. But, it won’t be to a country in the top 50.

I have had a discussion about the term DXPP with Martti. I think he likes the acronym, and maybe it translates better in Finnish, I have no idea. (Risto?) Nevertheless, whatever you call it, the need remains.

73,

Martti Laine-OH2BH has written several recent articles about DXPP’s or “DX Paying Passengers”. Martti describes a DXPP as an operator with only a minimal degree of operating expertise, but who contributes to the Dxpedition financing to help the Dxpedition.

Martti writes that DXPPs can seriously damage the credibility of an operation and create chaos on the bands. He is critical of the K5D Desecheo Dxpedition where a strong operator was relieved by a lesser skilled operator and problems resulted. Martti also criticizes the TX5K Clipperton Dxpedition, writing that “a seat was available for anyone who wanted to pay”. Martti suggests that the number of TX5K DXPPs added to the stress level of the Dxpedition team members and the Dxpedition audience.

Martti suggests that DX Foundations consider the number of DXPPs included in a Dxpedition when funding them. He writes that a Dxpedition may not qualify for support if the percentage of DXPPs is too high..

 

Here is your Question of the week: As members of the DX Community, are you concerned about DXPPs?   Do you feel that a Dxpedition suffers if there are a few lesser experienced operators on the team?   Should the DX Foundations offer less support for a Dxpedition to a rare place even if they have some lesser skilled operators on the team?

Give us your thoughts-

 

Paul N6PSE
