Frankly, I find at least half of our own politicians despicable and offensive in how they think and what they stand for (and don’t stand for) and a disgrace to most of the traditional (and Constitutional)  American values of substantial justice and fair play I believe in……….and I find offensive the policies and politicians of some of the  many countries apparently despised by some in the Club regarding monitoring of private (or ham radio) personal communications…but notwithstanding the commendable moral outrage expressed here…I am still going to work US stations… I will also continue to work DX stations.    Back to working DX…even DX from countries who have politicians I may detest (in private). I will try to restrain my moral outrage and simply enjoy a hobby. I have sympathy for those of you with heightened moral outrage that will preclude you from supporting the efforts of others to encourage getting DX stations on the air or working DX entities having policies and cultures you disagree with…and thank you for making the pileups smaller for the rest of us when the rare ones come back on the air. I love this hobby. Politicians not so much. I can tolerate diversity in cultures and politics…if they QSL.

 

73, Ted, K6XN  Call me radical.

Bob,

Wake up to reality--a substantial number of rare entities monitor/spy on DXpeditions to their countries.

I recall YK9A, where the operation was actually from the Ministry of Telecommunications, with monitors listening in the next room, paranoid that someone might answer an Israeli station in the pileups. Most of the ops on that trip were NCCC members.

I am sure every totalitarian host has monitored ham expeditions.

I truly doubt that a ham DXped would convince anyone with a brain that DPRK is a warm and fuzzy place. Ham radio tries to open closed doors.

If we have litmus tests for DXCC host countries, no one will ever make it to HR#1 again. The world is what it is.

Garry, NI6T

On 10/14/2014 11:33 PM, Bob Lanning via Chat wrote:

Thinking about what has been said I am reluctant to support a P5 operation.
Although the general population is peace loving the government and its
controlled media can use the operation to demonstrate a false image of
openness.  And the general population appears to be so mesmerized by their
Dear Leader they may turn hostile upon command - not knowing the true nature
of the world outside their country.  No shortwave receivers allowed!
 
The family run and hostile kingdom of North Korea has the power and skill to
use a well-intended ham radio operation to show the world how their country
is open and free.  So why can't people leave the country?  Why can't they
listen to shortwave radio?
 
In Paul's presentation of his DPRK visit, Paul told us how at the end of his
tour the pictures in the cameras of those on the tour were confiscated by
the government "watcher" on the tour.  Sure the general population class
tour guide later showed how to recover the pictures but one has to know he
did that at some risk.  The tour guide's actions also show the two faces of
the country, the general population and the government.  The face that is
the government is the dangerous one. 
 
I'm also reminded of Paul's Yemen operation.  In his Yemen program, Paul
described how there were one or two government representatives that were
assigned to his operation.  They stood over the station operators, to
monitor what was said on the radio and to enforce no contact with Israel.
Not an example of an open country.
 
I don't want to participate in an operation that has a high probability of
being used as propaganda putting forth a to the world a false image of
DPRK's closed and brutal kingdom.  Our neutral ham radio operation could be
turned into something none of us want.  
 
73,
 
Bob - W6OPO

This certainly is disturbing.  However, I personally don't have knowledge or
evidence of now much of these clandestine reports are true.  Maybe reality
is even worse than the reports.
 
More important, however, my life experience in seemingly oppressed countries
around the world, is that the vast majority of the people are peace-loving
and desirous of a conflict-free life like all of us.  These are the people
that we need to support and encourage.
 
And while I respect the rationale of boycotting, it is not the only valid
action an amateur operator can take.  Quite the opposite, ham radio for
decades has stood for human connections worldwide, despite political,
cultural, religious and other differences.
 
I personally support a neutral ham radio operation from North Korea.  While
I don't think an operation should make a positive statement about the
country politics, neither do I think a boycott should make a negative
statement.  Ham radio should continue to be a positive and neutral worldwide
ambassador.
 
Ed W0YK
