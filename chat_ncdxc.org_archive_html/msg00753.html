The Columbian navy has provided transportation to Malpelo a number of times in the past. In the case of PT0S, the Brazilian navy granted permission for the group to be taken by a fishing boat. But, from the PT0S web site:

We are on schedule to leave for St. Peter and St. Paul Archipelago on November 5. The weather forecast is favorable, which is good news, as seas can be rough along the 620 nautical mile passage. We -- and all our gear -- will be travelling on the 70 foot fishing boat Transmar II, along with 8 fishermen. It will be tight and the lighter the seas the more bearable the passage will be. 

Such shared efforts can certainly be successful. (The captain always wants to leave early, of course…”bad weather” saved us about $8K at Chesterfields.) One Peter I effort used TWO Russian icebreakers with helicopters. One to leave them and one to pick them up. That would make me nervous, and I am not sure if it would be attempted again, but it was successful. Trips to Clipperton have been shared successfully with sports fishermen.


A certain amount of ingenuity can save money. “One size fits all” doesn’t sound good to me. Anything else is fair game. Let the market decide.

My responses woven in below, hopefully the text is visible.

 

From: paul@n6pse.com

Jim (below) suggests a shared pool of equipment. That makes sense, but do you really trust a bunch of equipment used by another group on previous trips? You would have to do extensive set up and testing to make sure everything works as it should. We normally do that anyway, but greater care would have to be applied since another group has used some of the gear. Getting equipment really isn't our main challenge when planning to do go a rare island entity. It is money for fuel and boat costs that is our most significant challenge when considering these ultra rare destinations.

 

NHC:  Perhaps one organization (NCDXC) could make a purchase of equipment, palletize it for easier shipping (or flying) and another (ARRL) could insure it for damage and repairs.  That way it is located in one vicinity, can be made ready quickly and the extended costs are spread out.  This would also play out for emergency ops (Sandy) so buy in from some groups (ARRL) could be easier (not without further politics etc.).

 

John-K6YP suggests a way to economize by hitting several islands during one trip. While I think that might make the DXpedition more popular by providing multiple entities instead of just one, I see it also adding to the costs of the trip by adding fuel consumption and extra dates for the boat booking. It is hard enough getting the DX Community to support a DXpedition to one rare entity, by adding a second entity and increasing your costs, I have doubts that the DX Community would step up and do their part.

 

NHC:  Multiple sites could increase the cost exponentially.  Weather seems to be a major factor (or high/low/rough seas) making the trip far more complex.  Also how many of the team could make the time commitment for such a voyage (it could easily be a month or more)?  Planning with the added variables would be a nightmare; failure (even partial) brings the rapid wrath of the DX community regardless of reasons.

 

Dave-NE5EE suggests that he would donate $10 to a rare entity that he needs for an ATNO. While that would normally be considered a generous donation for an entity such as 3D2C where our cost per QSO was probably $2 per contact, a DXpedition to Bouvet/Crozet could easily cost the team $5 to $7 to provide each QSO!  So a $10 donation would probably not cover the costs to provide a few contacts.

 

NHC:  While that holds true in the US (the cost of a foo foo cup of hot beverage), in other countries, $10 is a significant amount.  Hams are inherently cheap and I don’t see them paying for a QSO and if the conditions don’t favor them, there WILL be harsh words and feelings.  Asking for donations will likely be the sole means of paying for it, corporate or private.

 

Jim-K9YC likes the DXpeditions done by George-AA7JV. George just finished activating PT0S with a group of three other guys. Transportation was provided by the Brazilian Navy and I tend to think their costs were minimal. Not much of a chance that our US Navy or the French Navy will take us where we want to go.

 

NHC:  Perhaps couch it as a propagation study?  ;o)  Has anyone actually approached them with the idea of assisting?  USN helps out during disasters by providing power, medical aid etc. so the basic foundation has been laid.  Since we are ‘at peace’ with most of the world, they have been doing some retasking.  They are certainly well qualified (and getting some engineer assistance (SeaBees) could make some locations [SP&SP Rocks] more inhabitable).
