On 11/20/21 10:52 AM, KE1B via Chat wrote:

About 10 years ago, I put up a Modified Extended Double Zepp antenna for 40 meters. This is a 170’ long wire antenna, with some series capacitance. I strung it between two trees on my property, using the classic “potato launcher” gun, and using the fishing line to pull up some 3/16” black, UV resistant, poly line which attached to the end insulators on the antenna. It is center fed with 450 ohm ladder line through a balun. This antenna has served me very well for a decade. (It’s got quite a bit of gain over a dipole, and it’s oriented for US/EU on one side, and VK/ZL on the other.)

The capacitors (9.1 pf) were made using a few inches of LMR-400 cable, as they must withstand rather high voltages and currents; conventional capacitors are inappropriate for this application. I weather-sealed the capacitors with RTV, and encased the assemblies in PVC pipe with endcaps, and sealed the endcaps where the wire passes through. So far so good. This antenna worked perfectly for a decade.

Over time, and given the big storm a few weeks ago, I believe some water finally made its way into the capacitor housing(s), and the caps are now arc’ing over when power is applied. This is apparent as I can set the tuner for a very low SWR at low power, but once it gets up over 700 watts or so, the SWR abruptly changes, and the amp shuts down to protect itself. OK, I can understand that nothing lasts forever (including my aging body), so I figured it’s time to take down the antenna and replace the capacitor assemblies.

So I loosened the ropes that are holding up the antenna (it’s about 70-80’ up at the ends, and droops to perhaps 40-50’ high in the middle where the feedline attaches), and lo and behold, the antenna won’t drop down. I strongly suspect that the trees have grown and essentially embedded the supporting rope within the now-grown tree branch where they passed through. Pulling on the end ropes does nothing but shake a few branches.

So my problem now is, “How do I get the antenna down?”. The entire expanse of the antenna is exposed but it’s at least 40’ up at the lowest point, and I can’t reach it, even with a ladder on my truck bed. At this point, my thinking is to toss some weight attached to a rope over the middle of it, and get a couple of strong guys (people, not wires!) to pull the sucker down. At this point, I don’t even mind destroying the antenna as it is, since it’s just wire and can be cheaply replaced. Once the middle is pulled down (or the wire or rope breaks and the antenna falls down in two sections), we can yank it out of the tree, using either human force or perhaps tying the end to my truck bumper. I’m sure the wire or rope will break before the tree comes down; they’re pretty big and mature. I could also rent a “cherry picker”, but that’s a pretty big expense just to rip down a piece of wire.

I can then remeasure the wire, rebuild the capacitors, and prepare to re-install the antenna. I’m thinking that I have three options for the re-install (assuming we can get the bugger down in the first place).

One, I can just do what I did before, and hope it lasts for another ten years.

Similarly, instead of a potato launcher, perhaps someone has a drone rigged for this type of thing, which makes placing the ropes much more accurate and likely to succeed.

Or I could hire a tree climber to go as high as they can, and install a pulley at each end, which would avoid a repeat of this adventure ten years from now. I’m OK with any of these three options.

But of course, I first need some help getting the antenna down, either in one piece or more than one (at this point, I don’t really care which).

Any other ideas, or does anyone care to help with this task? I think two or three guys should be able to easily do it, especially if someone has a drone (or just a good throwing arm) to get the pull-down rope over the middle of the antenna. We can schedule it almost any time, preferably on a day with decent weather. It can even wait until after the holidays, as I have an inverted-Vee dipole for 40 meters, which works fine (although not quite as kick-ass as the Zepp). And of course, I’ll need similar help to put it back up. (I don’t think it can be dropped, rebuilt, and re-installed the same day, unless someone has a good idea.)

Who is up for a day in the beautiful Santa Cruz Mountains? (It’s around 50 degrees right now, but bright sun and clear skies. We get a lot of days like this.)

Rich KE1B
