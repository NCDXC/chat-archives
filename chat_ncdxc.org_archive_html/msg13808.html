I am putting together a one-man DXexpedition.
I am planning to sail from Invercargill, New Zealand on February 9, 2018, aboard the M/V Spirit of Enderby. There are a number of island stops along the way and the culmination of the trip is, hopefully, a landing at McMurdo Station, Antarctica.
The cruise ends March 9 in Christchurch.

The M/V Spirit of Enderby is a re-purposed Soviet 'trawler' that was used for signals intelligence and shadowing NATO Fleet activities during the Cold War. It is now an 'intimate' expeditionary vessel designed for 50 passengers + as many more as can be financially accommodated. It makes 10 to 12 knots and routinely traverses the roaring 40s, screaming 50s and howling 60s with Zodiac excursions to permit you to wade through penguin and walrus poop and take photos and make memories.

The only reason I chose this vessel is because they were the only ones that would let me operate my ham radio. Thus my joining this list is to earnestly request your DXexpedition expertise.

The ship does not have any HF antennas or equipment. They will allow me to pay for the installation of an antenna but I have not as yet  determined what type it should be. I have in mind a Shakespeare High Frequency Marine Vertical or an end fed or dipole wire antenna that will shrug off 75 knot crosswinds. Both may be auto-tuned by a feed-point tuner - an SGC 237. For static discharge I will wind 10 turns of coax around a 12 inch long by half inch diameter ferrite rod connected between the antenna feedpoint and the tuner ground with the tuner mounted to a steel roof or side wall outside the, at present, empty and unused Radio Room behind the Bridge. There is some sort of pipe to allow entry of coax cable into the Radio Room.

The rig will be a Yaesu 897, an SCS Pactor 4 modem for Winlink and a West Mountain Radio Bluetooth Rig Blaster. I will have two 500 watt, 220 volt, uninterruptible power supplies, one for the rig and the other for the accessories. The mains supply is subject to serious spikes and sputters when other equipment on the ship is turned on or off. I will also use a 220 volt air conditioner surge protector between the mains outlet and the UPS's.

The Worldwide Antarctica Program (www.waponline.com) has assigned me a unique designator in anticipation of my cruise. I will be able to participate in at least one special event, Antarctic Awareness Week, beginning February 22, 2018.

With this tentative and very sketchy outline I am really eager for suggestions and ideas as what to bring and what to expect from the ship and the solar flux index. My main goal is to get as much of my 100 watts RF going out the antenna and let the shore based stations up north use their log periodics and amplifiers to make the QSO with me.

On the near horizon I will try to make a personal visit to SGC and seek their input. I'll be on long planned Australia/New Zealand cruise for two weeks that leaves Sydney, Australia on October 22, 2017, The Enderby's home offices are in Christchurch and I will try to have a meeting with the shore based crew and get things figured out then during that port call.  Holland America and Princess Lines are the only folks that allow amateur radio operation at sea from a passenger's balcony cabin. I will have the radio operational on that cruise also.

I'm flying Business Class into Christchurch for the Antarctica cruise with three maximum sized Pelican suitcases. I've got 50 pounds apiece for each one while the radio, modem and Rig Blaster will all fit into a Pelican carry-on case that goes with me into the cabin..

If you have any comments, please send them my way.
