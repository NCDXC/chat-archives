Jack was special. I remember belly laughing as I read many of his stories in QST. He had a way of pointing out some of our pomposities in a way that was both poignant yet kind. When I married Debra in April 2000 (on a Sunday) we both attended an NCCC meeting at Ken Menabe's restaurant in San Jose the very next day. Jack was there with Marguerite, and the newlyweds had dinner with them. It was a great evening, and Debra never forgot Jack. When I joined FOC in 2005, and first participated in its annual 'marathon,' Jack was my first 6 band QSO - and we did it in about 5 minutes. I worked him on 15 meters, first, and he said "let's do 10." So, we did. Then, "let's do 20... 40... 80... and 160." Working 6 bands earned you lots of extra points. Jack did it again with me the next year, too. Recently, Bob, W6CYX, has stepped in to become my 6 bander. It has been many years since I last worked Jack. But my memories of him are very vivid, and will remain so. He was one of a kind, for sure.
 
Rob K6RB
 
 
 
 
 
John G. Troster, W6ISQ, became a Silent Key on January 11, 2014. His wife of 65 years, Marguerite (KC6NFE), was at his bedside when he passed away.
 
Jack, along with Vince Chinn K6KQN (now W6EE), Lee Shaklee W6BH (SK), and Don Schliesser K6RV, founded the Northern California DX Foundation in October, 1972. Jack served as an NCDXF Board member for 26 years before stepping down in July, 1998. He was the Foundationâ€™s third President, serving from 1975 until 1986.
 
Jack had an enormous passion for amateur radio. He was first licensed in September, 1935, at age 14, as W2ISQ in Yonkers, New York. He received his W6ISQ callsign when he moved to California shortly after the end of World War II. Among Jackâ€™s many radio accomplishments and awards were DXCC #1 Honor Roll on the Mixed, CW and SSB modes; 5B-DXCC; 5B-WAS (Certificate #4); and USA Counties Award (for working all 3,076 USA counties, which he did completely on CW!). Jack was a member of the FOC and the A1 Operators Club; he served as president of both the Northern California DX Club and the Northern California Contest Club; he was a Contributing Editor of QST magazine and was the Pacific Divisionâ€™s representative on the ARRL DX Advisory Committee. He was a director of the Quarter Century Wireless Association and for years wrote the monthly QCWA column published in World Radio News. Jack was named NCDXCâ€™s DXer of the Year, and in 1988 he was inducted as the 27th member of the CQ DX Hall of Fame.
 
 
As impressive as were all his radio achievements, even more so was Jack himself. He was the consummate gentleman. He was warm and welcoming to everyone he met, and many amateur radio operators today enjoy the hobby as a direct result of his nurturing, encouragement and enthusiasm. Jack was known for his quick wit and encyclopedic knowledge of amateur radio history. His passing leaves a huge void. Amateur radio has lost one of its giants.
