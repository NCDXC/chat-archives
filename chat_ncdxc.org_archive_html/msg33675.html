Excellent suggestions. My “happy place” is usually about 2450 Hz, but I’ve often found success by going between 2500 and 3000 Hz, and sometimes higher. Technically, the hounds are suppose to be able to listen up to 5000 Hz above the base freq, though most radios have narrower audio passbands. 

For non-f/h MSHV, going below the DX’s audio freq can be a good strategy, as taught by others in our club.

On Feb 17, 2024, at 12:07 PM, Robert Kincaid via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

I thought I’d share the experience I just had with 8R7X. I’ve been trying off and on all morning to score a contact on 17m with FT8. On my waterfall it doesn’t seem like that much of a pileup. Lots of empty slots to transmit from. It slowly dawned on me that as has been observed before, they were working a lot of European stations. But I wasn’t seeing as many stations in my waterfall as they seem to be logging. I realized I wasn’t seeing what 8R7X was seeing. So I moved way out to 3000 and within 3 or 4 calls I got ‘em. So just a word to the wise that if you’re having an oddly frustrating time getting a contact, even IF you don’t see a big pileup, try moving somewhere that would be unlikely to be occupied but still within a plausible passband. And sometimes just take a chance on an extreme location.

It’s the same trick John Owens NN6U mentioned recently, but it wasn’t obvious that it was needed in this case.

It also might be the case that they haven’t been favoring Europe. It may be that they are just seeing more of a pileup coming from there that we don’t see. It correlates a bit how they seem to work more east coast stations when they do work the US.

I hope this helps.
