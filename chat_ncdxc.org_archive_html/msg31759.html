Heh, for cost is no object, take a look at the QRZ page for KE5EE.  And that is lightning and hurricane prone Florida...  Multiple stacked arrays, rotating towers and more.  And he is HEARD on every band.

I'm fairly well pleased with my DB36/80, even with the history we've had together.  I find the additional couple dB of gain for the DB42 to be offset by it's complexity (FAR more cables, relays and parts to fail plus another 25 lbs and greater wind load).  But it also depends on your lot size, it's a challenge to tip over a tower on the typical city lot; while being only 15 feet above your roof, is not a great plan.  All antennas need maintenance and repair, does it need a crane to move around?

Providing gain, is simple to a point, more power and/or elements.  But that means heavier parts (tower, cabling etc).  Sometimes, it's better to concentrate on lowering your noise floor, again a challenge on a city lot.  Higher antenna gain can help you null out some noise; unless it's in the direction you want to hear.

So ultimately, it's not just budget, but many other factors rolled into the package.  Nothing comes for free; a station is a collection of compromises, constrained by space, budget, time and ability.

Most just do the best they can.  In my case, I moved away from cities and postage stamp lots to rural and a few acres; enough to build a station in a quiet (in all respects) area.  That is part of my retirement plan and not everyone is ready or can get there.

As for other antenna styles and models, trap arrays also have issues and compromises (traps fail, aluminum can't take some environments); all antennas can be soaked, ice laden (where aluminum fails forever but fiberglass can recover).  Log periodic have a lot more weight, less gain and often 2:1 SWR (so a tuner is required or a tolerant tube amp)...

If it supposed to move, one day it won't.  It's its stable, one day it won't be.  If it's outside, the weather will eat it.  The bugs WILL invade it.  That's with ANY antenna.

Some are for DXing, others are for local (NVIS) coverage; the goals of the operator are part of the decisions.  One size, doesn't fit all needs.

In other words, it's not a simple 'this is best' antenna.  Goals, intent are confined to budget, available space, location, HOA, permits, spousal approval rates, noises... there is no simple answer, ever.

SteppIR has some good antennas (and other brands too).  Are they perfect?  No, no antenna is (except isotropic).  I would still look hard at replacing this one, most likely with another DB36/80 (or just buy more parts to keep this one alive).  Because it covers 80-6M with one feedline, with enough gain to simplify making contacts; and my station goal centers on simplicity (and THAT is a LOT of effort).  Yes, this antenna has compromises too, but in the areas I want (reasonable gain, relative simplicity, ease of operation); while I can still work on it myself (lower the tower, tip, work with feet on the ground).

There are solid, absolute reasons to NOT get a SteppIR too.  So we're back to 'do the best one can do', no one antenna is best...

When antennas work (any brand/model/material) peace; but there is always the nagging 'when will it fail?'  The simple answer is when you need it most hi hi.
