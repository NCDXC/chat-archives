Of course not!  No ham would ever run more than 200W on 30m, I should have
added "except on 30m" to my posting.  Actually I think that I worked them on
a couple of bands with only 100W as it takes longer to turn on the amp and
auto tuner at my remote station.

But this does raise an interesting topic:  I quite frequently have a much
harder time cracking a pileup on 30 than on the other bands.  This was also
true on the recent Swains Island expedition.  Conventional wisdom would say
that this is because 30 is frequently open to large areas of the world at
the same time and hence bigger pileups.  Also G5RV antennas don't work very
well on 30 and 15.

However who can say that they haven't thought about switching on the amp
after sitting in a 30m pileup for a few hours?  No one will ever admit it
but I often wonder how many of the signals in a typical 30m pileup are
running high power?  Could that also be a factor in cracking those pileups?

73,
