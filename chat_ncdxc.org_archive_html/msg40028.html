On Tue, Jul 15, 2025 at 11:48 AM Greg De Hoogh via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:
Hi Gene,

I agree that LoTW needs money for necessary upgrades and continued operation, and I won't argue that there's a great solution to the problem. (If there is, I certainly don't have it.) Thanks for your response - there's great value in getting other folks' perspectives and opinions.

I get no real benefit from using LoTW as my log, but I do get benefit from the matches that it generates. So, maybe the system could be set up so that uploading QSOs and verifying they're there is free, but all other access (including seeing whether those QSOs have been matched) requires a yearly fee? The guy in Tajikistan who's short on money can still upload to LoTW for free and save the hassle of having to deal with QSL cards, but if he wants to play the DXCC (or WAS, or VUCC, ...) game he's going to have to scrape up the money, even if all he wants is to see if he got a confirmation on LoTW.

As a life member, I don't think I can argue for a dues increase to cover additional LoTW costs. Plus, why should US hams be the ones covering so much of the cost. ARRL is mainly US hams, but DXCC is international. What about asking other amateur radio societies - RSGB, DARC, URE, JARL, etc. - to foot part of the bill? Could more corporate sponsors be obtained, or does Yaesu have exclusive rights to sponsorship? Could they get more individual sponsors (something better than a one-time email)?

I certainly won't disagree with you on the bureau system, especially ARRL's outgoing bureau, but as someone who chases JARL awards I do get some benefit from the incoming bureau. I quit using ARRL's outgoing bureau a couple years ago when I discovered QRURE, the URE's outgoing bureau system. For about half what it would cost me to buy cards, mail them to ARRL, and pay the ARRL fee, and with far, far less effort, I can simply upload an adif file to QDURE. They print the cards with the QSO information on them and send them out.

Total brainstorming here, so please don't attack me: How about if DXpeditions with more than a certain number of QSOs had to pay per QSO to upload? Necessarily coupled with that would need to be NCDXF and other foundations dropping the requirement of free LoTW uploads after a given time. So, the DXpeditions could require everyone who wants a confirmation to pay for it. As it is now, I dislike the fact that so many of the folks who get value from the DXpeditions don't share in the expenses. You guys do all the work and make all the sacrifices, and they get the benefit without so much as getting out of their chairs.

73,
Greg N6PM

"The small fees that we pay go to support the overhead for the program."

One seemingly unknown fact is:

Regardless of where they live, LoTW is free to use by any registered user.  One does not need to be an ARRL member to use LoTW.
The application keeps track of your DXCC accomplishments, match QSOs as they come in, etc.  
However, if a US user wants to apply for an award - ARRL membership is required for the US users, and there are associated fees for the transaction.

About 2 months ago the ARRL sent a fund raising  appeal to non members that are registered on LoTW.
The email went to tens of thousands of users, last I heard the response has been very disappointing.

My opinion:  ARRL should implement an annual non member user fee, $15 a year.
There is no free lunch, LoTW provides a worthwhile service, 15 bucks today isn't going to break anyone's ham radio budget.
Although, the usual suspects will loudly complain.

Cheers,
GS K5GS

Source: ARRL Website:

Membership and costs

US amateurs must be ARRL members to use LoTW for award credits.  Cost of credits is determined by the price structure surrounding a particular award.  There is currently no charge for an individual to extract results from LoTW, though this is used as a basis for awards by other issuers.

Uploading logs is a free activity for all amateurs.  DX stations are particularly encouraged to upload their logs.

Currently (July 2014), the only fees are those required when contacts are used for supported awards. Premium services may be developed for LoTW which would require user fees.



On 7/14/2025 7:43 PM, Paul N6PSE via Chat wrote:
Will, don't get discouraged. The DXCC program is one of the best amateur radio awards programs, with strict rules and a system of checks and balances. The DXCC program has evolved in large part due to cheating by DXers seeking fame and glory but by not actually following the rules or being where they claimed to be.  Government permission as in this case with Cambodia is essential. On my DXpeditions, we always furnish our government licenses and a photo of our GPS with date and time to the ARRL DXCC program to prove that we are where we said we would be. 

Even today, there are well known DXers that are still cheating, operating from rigs on their boat. Then there is the whole issue of DXers gaming the system by operating from remote stations that give them a huge propagation advantage. We see that on every DXpedition. I am not against using remotes within the rules but some well known figures use remotes continents away from their QTH to game the system. 

So the tough rules and strict process to sign up to use LoTW is necessary to keep the DXCC program valid and worthwhile to participate in. The small fees that we pay go to support the overhead for the program. I am happy to do my part to help support the system. 

As I have written before, many of us will evolve in the way we enjoy the hobby. For a long time, I was satisfied to work the rarest of DXpeditions for a single contact.  Peter 1 comes to mind. I saw no point (at the time) to work them on multiple bands and modes.  With the creation of DXCC Challenge, I now challenge myself and try to work DX on as many bands and modes as possible. I have actually come to enjoy this "chase" for DX.  

Prior to Covid, I was still logging on paper. That made it very difficult to easily apply for awards. During Covid, I spent hundreds of hours entering old paper contacts into my computer logging program. I then uploaded to LoTW and I was stunned at the results. I am now happily logging and uploading to LoTW nearly every day and I submit my contacts for various awards when the urge hits me. My DXing has evolved and I am enjoying the hobby more than I ever have. 

I think if you stick with DXing and participate in the DXCC program, your methods will evolve as well. You will look back on these days and likely realize how much better the program will be in the future. Let's help build and maintain that program and not tear it down. 

Best 73, 

Paul N6PSE
