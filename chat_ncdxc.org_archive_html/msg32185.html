In my short year and a half of being a ham I thought I needed to understand all the stats and numbers.

Now I do the same thing that you guys have already said.

I get on the air.  If I'm not sure, then I get in the CW portion and call CQ and see with RBN spotters pick me up.  

If none, then I get on FT8 and send a couple of CQs to see who can hear me.

In the night time (8pm Pacific and later) I get very little activity on 40m at my QTH.  It's just quiet.  If I can get the RBN to spot me it's usually only one spotter.  Sometimes 2-3 spotters.  When that happens I try calling CQ longer to see who can hear and I will call CQ on the SKCC frequencies because there are usually a lot of ears listening.

Will

On Tue, Nov 21, 2023 at 1:16 PM Rick NK7I via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:
That's a benefit of watching FTx mode; it gives you a real time view of
prop, at least in the direction that you're looking.  You don't even
have to transmit.  What calls are scrolling by and whom are they
calling?  I've added more than a few slots by noting who is being
called, turning that way and making a contact.

VOACAP adds a singular data point.  Having said that, VOACAP (online or
app) is often wrong, or early/late.  It's a SWAG (scientific wild arsed
guess) based on variables that science and math don't account for
quickly in real time (like CME or flares). So look and listen are still
the best tools.  Spotting lists and hamalert alarms help too, over as
wide an area as you choose (what's heard in MO on 80M has potential here).

Even with all of that, the magic of radio is true; more so when you
least count on it.
