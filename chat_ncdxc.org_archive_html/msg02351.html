First of all, I hope that some of you were able to work VP2EAQ (Rich) and/or VP2EAR (Anna) on our recent Buddipole-Dxpedition to Anguilla. We had a GREAT time, with lots of radio activity, superb Caribbean cuisine, and plenty of personal relaxation and fun.
 
Photos can be seen at:
 
http://www.flickr.com/photos/51562235@N04/sets/72157633556915881/
 
You can “mouse over” any photo for a description, and click on them for a larger version.
 
We arrived on May 8, traveling from San Francisco to Newark, then Newark to Phillipsburg in Dutch Sint Maarten (PJ7). Anguilla is only about 5 miles from Sint Maarten at the closest point, and there are a few options for getting over there. We chose to go out on a very short flight (total flight time: 7 minutes), partly because it meant not having to lug our bags to a shuttle bus and then to the ferry boat, and partly because Anna didn’t want to take her chances on a boat trip after a long, red-eye flight. (We took the ferry on the way back, and it was no problem at all.)
 
Anna is a master planner on these trips, and always arranges for spectacular accommodations, as well as figuring out where to go, what to do, and where the best restaurants are. We stayed at a beautiful private villa with pool, lush landscaping, and a nice open backyard directly on the water. That huge salt water ground plane makes a vertical antenna really shine. Noise levels were non-existent (except on 40m), and we could easily work folks that didn’t even move the S-meter, in all modes. We operated 40-10m, CW/SSB/RTTY, using an Icom IC-7000 and a Buddipole, just as I presented a few months back in my "Holiday DXpedition" talk.
 
In the Caribbean, the trade winds are ALWAYS there, and they seemed more intense in Anguilla than last year in the Turks & Caicos. While the vertical doesn’t offer much wind load, I put three small tent stakes into the ground, and guyed the antenna with some lightweight rope at the top of the 8’ mast (below the VersaTee). Although the winds whipped up pretty fiercely at times (and we got heavy rains a couple of times), everything held perfectly well.
 
Prior to the trip, we received a number of emails from folks requesting RTTY QSOs, and also operation on the WARC bands (30/17/12m). There are very few active hams on Anguilla, and the ones that are there tend to be more casual SSB ops. There have been some contest operations from the island, but that still leaves lots of folks with no QSOs on the WARC bands. So we gave the people what they wanted, and had some incredible runs on 12m, 17m and 30m, plus lots of RTTY contacts.
 
There are some operating statistics in the photos linked above, but here is a summary:
Total QSOs: 5510, with about 3500 unique callsigns, over 8 days (part-time, single transmitter)
Band Distribution: 40m-1%, 30m-5%, 20m-25%, 17m-14%, 15m-27%, 12m-17%, 10m-11%
Mode Distribution: CW-47%, SSB-37%, RTTY-16%
Continent Distribution: EU-64%, NA-25%, SA-5%, AS-3%, AF-1%, OC-0.3%
 
Europe simply BOOMED in, constantly. Even though we were closer to the US, we logged 2.5 times more Europeans than North Americans. Of course, it was a clear shot over the water to EU. We figured that we could work more US stations when the Europeans went to sleep, but THEY NEVER DID. We worked EU at all hours, including their middle-of-the-night. Lots of Italians, Russians, Germans, and the like. The top of the "leaderboard" was RX3XW, who worked Anna and me on 19 different band/mode slots. 
 
At one point I started hearing JAs on 15m CW around 2300Z (their morning), and stopped working anything else. It’s can be hard to work Anguilla from JA, and I wanted to give them as many QSOs as I could. I ran JA non-stop for about 45 minutes, and made quite a few folks happy.
 
Anna and I had a great run on 10m SSB one afternoon, and at one point I got three calls, back-to-back, from the Canary Islands (which is a straight shot over the water, due East of our locations). Then, over the next 45 minutes, I counted no less than FOURTEEN different Canary Island callsigns! It started to be a joke for Anna and me. She was listening in the background, and every minute or two I would say “Thanks for the call from the Canary Islands!” and she would laugh. Maybe I’m a conspiracy theorist, but I figured that they must be calling their local friends on the phone, asking everyone to get on 10m and call this Anguilla station. In August, Anna and I will be operating from the Canary Islands, and I think we should be admitted into the Canary Islands Amateur Radio club after that run.
 
It’s really great fun being on the receiving end of the pileup, and the pileups here were non-stop. I was able to run CW at ~150 QSOs/hour, with the limiting factor being not the availability of callers, but the ability to pull out a single callsign from the cacophony. On SSB, we occasionally went to working “by the numbers”, just to thin out the mass of simultaneous callers.
 
I was watching the cluster while operating, and had to laugh when I saw a posting from A65CA (United Arab Emirates) saying that we were strong in the Middle East, but he couldn’t get over the “EU Wall”. Out here in California, we are used to being stymied by the “East Coast Wall”. It’s nice to know that it happens the other way, too. By the way, Yuri/A65CQ did finally get through, along with an Omani station and a number of Kuwaitis. Another high point was when both Anna and I got called by VU2NKS (India, on both SSB and RTTY). And all this with 100W and a Buddipole.
 
Of course, we had lots of time for tennis (our passion), lying on the beach, and sampling the local food and drink. Anguilla is considered a “foodie’s paradise”, which is partly why we chose to go there. The local specialties are crayfish and lobster;. The crayfish are much larger than the Louisiana variety; the lobster are smaller than the New England version, and they don’t have any claws. Both are very tasty, fresh off the grill. We consumed more than our share, along with lots of Jamaican rum and Caribe beer (the local brew).
 
We also made contact with a local ham, Teddy VP2ETE. A few weeks earlier, I worked Teddy from our home in California on 10m SSB, and told him that we would be down there in May. We exchanged emails and telephone numbers, and got together a couple of times while we were on the island. I visited his shack (TS480-SAT, to a hex-beam and some wire antenna), and the next day he came out to our villa. The entire island is fairly small, and nothing was more than about 20 minutes from our relatively central location.
 
That’s about it. It’s amazing how much fun you can have with a simple, low-power radio and portable antenna, particularly if you can get it next to a large body of water. Of course, the Anguillan callsign didn’t hurt, and Anna gets another virtual S-unit or two with her YL voice. In August, we will be heading out to Tenerife in the Canary Islands (look for EA8/KE1B and EA8/W6NN from August 11-16), and then to Gozo Island in the Malta group (9H3MMM and 9H3NN) from August 21-26.
 
If anyone has any specific questions about our operation, please feel free to contact me directly, off-list.
 
73 de Rich VP2EAQ (KE1B)
