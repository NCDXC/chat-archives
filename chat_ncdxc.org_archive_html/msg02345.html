I will reply from my personal experience.

I co-led the 2000 DXped to Kingman Reef--then and now very high on the Most Wanted list. There were physical challenges associated with getting to and operating from a venue 25 ft. wide in the middle of the Pacific. One squall almost blew away the camp. Antennas were crowded into a short ribbon of broken coral and clamshells. With a small team by today's standards, we logged almost 81K QSO's--a record at the time for boat-and-tent operations. We spent our funds so carefully that money was returned to team members and to major sponsors. The QST article won the plaque for Best of Issue.

No one has been back in 13 years.

At the 2001 Dayton DX Dinner, we expected to be named DXped of the Year. It went instead to a Bhutan expedition led by W0GJ that had just missed being included for the previous year. My sense of disappointment lasted about ten seconds, if that. Why? Because the Bhutan operation and every effort made that year, in prior years and subsequently that surmounted difficult challenges---political, weather, propagational, etc.--is a DXpedition of the Year. We did not need a plaque to know we had done a helluva job, and neither did HK0NA, 7O6T, PT0S or any other expedition--large or small--in 2012. They were all different, and appealed to the audience in different ways. Today, in one case, it might be just the achievement of getting permission to go--as in your DPRK effort; in another, the achievement of getting people and gear ashore without a tragedy, another to turn a pile of marine rocks into a DXCC entity.

This is not to say that all DXpeds are run efficiently or even fairly. It is to say that widely different expeditions should not be evaluated by a process akin to voting for your favorite baseball player or ice cream flavor. Ultimately, audience appreciation will be measured by donations and perceptions of the operation.

I agree with W6KM--let it go and get on with it. The awards after the fact will take care of themselves.

Garry, NI6T
