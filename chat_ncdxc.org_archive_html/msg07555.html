Hi NCDXC'ers:
 
I want to, if only for a moment, try to divert the chatter from the recent WebDx roasting ...
 
For me, amateur radio has always been about making the long-distance contact and wondering what the other op's conditions were like, where he/she was located and what (exactly) was being used ... So, following Paul's (N6PSE) great precedent, from his time at XZ1J, I thought to give you all a little insight into A25TQ/A25CF (the Jim & Frosty show ...).
 
1) At first glance, Botswana reminds me a lot of western Texas and New Mexico, with high buttes and plateaus, very arid weather and its red (ok orange ...) sands. It is Africa *HOT* here right now, with highs in the mid 90s and absolutely zero chance of wind. This seems to foster a very favorable insect environment (sans misquitos thankfully ...) and, at any given moment, when I'm operating, I can count 3-5 different insect specie trying to find a home. So, if I seem flustered, it's probably because something just flew in my ear. 
 
2) The QTH here is at the Lotsane Safari & Game Lodge, along the Tuli Perserve, in Eastern Bostwana. If you want to see the actual locale (in detail) open up the map on my A25TQ QRZ.com page and zoom in. The Lotsane Lodge is located on a plot of land which is basically 5 mi x 12 mi, approximately 30 miles from the nearest town (Sherwood). On the land, the lodge has two paddocks, one for game hunting and one for game viewing. Thankfully, we are situated on the game viewing side and can, at any given time, have a few antelope, wart hogs, porcupines (or other ...) passing by ...
 
3) The set-up here is TS-590 + Expert 1K Amp (runs 900-1000w) and a Teledyne T6 LP up about 25', a Butternut HF2V 40/80m vertical and an old Cushcraft R7000 vertical. Both the Butternut and Cushcraft are ground mounted.  This area is very flat, offering a good takeoff in all directions ...
 
4) To get here, I flew into Jo'burg (JNB), was met by Frosty (K5LBU) and then we proceeded to drive 4 hours to Gaborone (the capital of Botswana) to visit with the Bostwana Communication Authority (BCA) and collect our A25 ham licenses. In typical Africa fashion, we waited 4 hours at the BCA, for them to print out our licenses and sadly discovered they couldn't seem to correct that dreaded "server error!"  So well pick these up upon our return. If anyone's interested, the A2 license costs about $5 USD and can be made for any length of stay up to 6 months and, if you ask nicely, that can be extended to one year.   Upon leaving Gaborone, Frosty and I drove for 4 hours, up the main Bostwana artery, arriving in the small truck-stop town of Sherwood, where we camped out for the night ... Then another hour to the Lotsane Lodge the next day ...
 
That's all for now. Greyline awaits.
 
73s, Jim Colletto, A25TQ.
