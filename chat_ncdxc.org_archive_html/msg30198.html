Understand that this is just an effort to save a little time – a very little amount of time  - in doing something that is already done at nearly the speed of light using fiber (and switches/retransmitters) with NO effect on hams or anyone else.  They want to bypass the fiber and switch delay to save, perhaps, a couple of milliseconds and carry out flashier “flash trades”.  The cost will be large but their potential profit can be shown to be huge (coming entirely at the expense of the flash traders already on fiber).  I don’t see a reason to give up ham radio for this nonsense.  It only benefits one group at the expense of another and if it is NOT allowed no one suffers.  It is not allowed by ITU so it cannot be allowed by FCC without violating the ITU regs – or changing them.

 

A good way to block it would be to do what was required by the FCC for Digital television – a 50db BP filter proposed by industry which the FCC then “doubled” to 100db.  Yes they did that.  It is very effective – but it is also very expensive.  These filters are the size of a large bus and located inside skyscraper radio rooms.  For HF they might wind up being a bit larger.  Make them build a 100db bandpass filter to contain their signal and don’t let more than 0.001uW/meter appear within 10 MHz of the sides of that filter.  Technical solutions are always the best way to go.  They avoid all the unpleasantries of motivation and intent.  Build it so we can’t hear you if we try or don’t build it at all.

 

These guys should pay a neutral third party expert such as  NIST or AEROSPACE Corp to come up with a technical solution, test it, and prove that it will be environmentally stable over a WIDE range of weather and atmospheric conditions.

 

W6DR

Art,

 

Thank you for bringing this important matter to the attention of our club members and other amateurs. 

 

Here's what I sent, based on your suggestions - others may find it useful as a template to revise and add their own comments:

 

"I am writing in opposition to RM-11953. This would allow a consortium of wealthy investors to transmit very high power (more than 10 kilowatt) signals adjacent to the frequency bands used by amateur radio operators in the US and around the world to provide emergency communications and other volunteer services that are important to the public.

I request that additional time be allowed for detailed and thorough study of the impact of this proposed rule. There are likely other alternatives that the petitioners could use for their business needs that do not require high power utilization of the limited radio frequency spectrum. If spectrum use is granted, the power and spectral limits should not exceed those of the adjacent amateur radio frequency bands.

Thank you for your consideration in permitting ongoing access by amateur radio to our valuable frequency spectrum.

Respectfully,
Andrew F Calman
Amateur radio callsign K6ZP"

 

73,
