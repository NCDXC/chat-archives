>> On Fri,2/6/2015 6:33 PM, Robert Harmon via Chat wrote:
>>  Is the size of the P3 display large enough to pick out the station worked ?
> 
> Yes and no. It depends entirely on 1) your skill in setting it up, which is directly related to your understanding of how the averaging of FFTs can bring signals out of noise; 2) the nature of the pileup. The K1N pileup is so dense, and SO undisciplined -- MANY, MANY stations are calling almost blind-calling, or calling nearly continuously, calling when the DX is either working someone else or calling a partial that isn't even slightly related to them.
> 
> The P3 does many other things. It will, for example, let you see when the DX QSYs a kHz or so to get away from cops and jammers. It will help you identify noise. It will show you exactly how dirty a dirty signal is. :)  And you can save the screen to email to the guilty party. :)
> 
> I'm primarily a contester, but have gotten sucked into DXing.  For both pursuits, the P3 is much more important to me than the second RX. And for both, it's worth adding the SVGA option. The SVGA provides about 4X more detail, and about 20X the time in the waterfall as compared to the P3 built-in display. The latter is also quite helpful in finding clear frequencies -- I can get about a minute in the waterfall!
> 
> The P3 frequency span is continuously adjustable from 200 kHz down to 2 kHz. The vertical scale is continously adjustable from about 6 dB to about 80 dB. I usually use 24 dB for most operation, 42 dB for high power contests, and 18 dB for finding weak signals on a dead band, or waiting for the band to open, Like 10M and 6M. :)
> 
> On 6M, I set the P3 to scan 50,080 to 50,280. This lets me see CW (below 50,100), the DX window (50,100 - 50,125), the SSB activity (50,125 to 50,250), and JT65/JT9 (50,276 - 50,280). I've made a lot of QSOs and worked a lot of new grids because I had that capability. I just got a QSL from CX5BL for a 6M JT65 QSO in October. In a brief opening that lasted about 30 minutes, I worked three grids in CX and LU, some on CW, some on JT65.
> 
> Last year, N6PSE set up skeds with EP. His rig was pretty unsophisticated, and he didn't know his operating frequency very precisely. We were all listening where he thought he was calling us, but in reality he was 500-700 Hz down. I saw that signal on the P3, tuned down, and sure enough it was him. I and about a dozen other guys got in his log that night, thanks to the P3.
