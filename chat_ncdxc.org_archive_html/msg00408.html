I think PT0S can do a better job of notifying the masses when they plan to QSY to another band, and not leave everyone hanging, like they did this morning on 15M RTTY, saying "QRX" -- and then not returning.

However, Jim's 5-point summary is right on.  Conditions on St. Paul are the same or worse than HK0NA (Malpelo Island).  This is one of the first DXpeditions I've seen where the operators move around the bands in a somewhat systematic pattern based on need and propagation.  For that, they deserve much credit.

I have them in the log 5 times.  The 80M (CW) and 40M (CW + SSB) QSOs were relatively easy later in the evening/early morning.

The 17M (CW) and 15M (CW) were much more difficult as the pileups were larger and it took more time and patience to pinpoint the operator's split freq. listening pattern.  FWIW -- I note that the CW operators tend to move UP the band at about 5 kHz steps each time.  Setting the 2nd Rcvr on the K3 to jump 5 kHZ at a time before transmitting helped snag both CW contacts.

They'll be there until Nov. 22 --  five more days to get them in the log.  But as Jim says, I'd rather see the JAs get through rather than pile on more band-modes myself.

73,
John, K6MM

On Nov 17, 2012, at 12:56 PM, Jim Brown wrote:

On 11/17/2012 12:00 PM, paul@n6pse.com wrote:
Many times, I have seen the PT0S Operators have a massive pile up only to QSY to another band or mode just leaving the pile up hanging.

Some thoughts on that.  First, their focus is specifically the lower bands.  Second, they are in quite limited space, with noise from scientific equipment on the island, and they've noted that it's quite difficult to sleep. Third, it's a rather small group, and they need to maximize resources. Fourth, I think their goal (beyond the low band emphasis) is more toward getting more unique calls into the log rather than working more band-modes.  Fifth, their most difficult path is to JA and Asia in general, so they try to work that when they can. 

With those goals in mind, and their operational conditions, I don't find their operating choices all that strange. For example, last night several noted on the W6TI reflector (I was the scratchy guy calling to note their QSX on 75M, and to report other places I was hearing them), noted that while there were lots of callers, rate was quite low, and they QSYed to 80M. That seems smart -- if your goal is uniques and you're not making rate, switch to a band where you are making rate to areas where you're harder to work. Your own efforts in that direction have been greatly appreciated when you've been the DX. Note also that 160, 80, and 40 in their hours before and after sunrise favor North America and points west, and mostly take EU out of the picture. 

FWIW -- I've found it far easier to work them on the lower bands -- I'm in the log on 160 CW, 80 CW, 75, and 40 CW, but have nothing above that, and 160M was the only one that took very long. When I hear them on higher bands, propagation is significantly better to other continents, so although  I can copy them, they're working JA.  According to posts I've seen, they're #4 most wanted there, and while I'd love more band-slots, I wouldn't want to begrudge those JAs getting an all time new country.  .
