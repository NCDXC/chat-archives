I do not believe it will be productive for Marek to be turning either of his beams looking for long path openings that may/may not happen. On Wed, May 29, 2024 at 10:05 AM Rick Bates, nk7i via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

Rich,

It is recognized that Marek is relatively new to ham radio (clearly with other qualifications and is on the 'not stupid' side of the line).  He has done VERY well for a noob showing both caring and some understanding.  But he may/not have the depth of understanding wanted, realizes this and is asking for help (that's wisdom).

Even when the Acom is/was working, his FT8 signals are often meager, because of the number of streams.  I suggest he set and leave max streams at 3, never higher than 4; a fair trade off for working further/weaker stations vs the rate/hour.  Using a tone 400-600 would help also (avoid the rolloff of the modern audio filtering).  [I'll ignore the choice of FT8 app, we've had that discussion and it won't change.]

So far, he has been heard in NA and much of the west coast on most bands that he offered (10 M not so much, not even a hint here).  His largest issue, is hearing outside EU (or the stuff sent meant to block non-EU from being heard).  

Hearing is not his issue, definition is.  The pileups have clearly been consistently, intense.  That much is obvious.

For FT8, there is a zone filter to help that (and a CQ zone message); on phone it's a rough ride (since there isn't a lot of cooperation).  We can tell how well he hears, from the reports he sends; he can HEAR.  The rest is just pileup depth and us working through it.

For a solo op of any experience level, he is doing quite well.

Two words:  GRAY LINE.

His dawn and west coast sunset, are near alignment (20-30 minutes off, depending on where you look on the west coast).  It is a potent time (duration varies by band) on any HF band.  During this time, I suggest his focus be on the furthest away stations (NA in general, specifically the west coast).

The path to the west coast is his furthest (90% antipodal) and bands like 40M (him using a vertical, alas) will require the use of gray line magic.  Those openings often last less than 15 minutes (up here at least); so (mostly on 40) he will want to note the US calls during the window of magic.  This is burden on HIM, since (hopefully) only those that hear him, call him.

EU he can work most any time of day; it's the non-EU that will need more attention.

The gray line opening (SP) on 12 M FT8 was 4 minutes wide here; I was fortunate; gray line made the difference.  Phone is highly unlikely for 10/12M (here, this far north); certainly if his Acom has utterly died; it has not been allowed by prop, he's just not that loud (except spotlights).

*During the gray line window, Marek will want to use the distance filters in MSHV plus pay attention to the calls; manual selection, not robot.

Two more words:  LONG PATH

While his high band antenna does not have a lot of f/b (to null out loud EU or AS), hopefully he is aware of long path to NA (17/20 M, ~1200 UTC+; 15-10M ~00 UTC).  His low band (40) is a vertical; so it's on him to be aware of where the callers are from (no robot mode, manual selection and use the distance filter in the app).

It would mean that he reverse the azimuth on both hexbeams to better use long path.  Those few dB of difference, could matter.

Long path and gray line at the same time (on lower bands) would be amazing for NA, west coast in particular.

I would not suggest any strong focus on 30M (the QRP band) occur, that will most likely only be heard in EU/AS/AF at the low power output available.  Try it at various times of the day, see what happens (it IS an interesting band, always open to somewhere).

I am grateful for the 20-12 FT8 and 15 phone so far, prop has not been good up here (and SP polar distortion appears too).  I'm not sure that the average ham would tolerate conditions and the treatment he receives (abuse) as well as he is doing.  Kudos.

Hope for better prop (more spots, less CME spits at us).  And get some serious rest; no one alone can manage this pressure (and his other duties on site) for long.

(The prop apps, are almost never accurate this sun cycle, not worth a lot of time spent on them.  Actual observation and operating time are the better tools.)

Thank you for your service as a pilot; hoping he has the endurance (and thick skin) to continue on.

73,
Rick nk7i
