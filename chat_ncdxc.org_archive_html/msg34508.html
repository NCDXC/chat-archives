Paul (N6PSE),

For potential discussion/s at IDXC:

First a happier topic:  What makes any DXpedition 'good'?  What makes one become the BEST DXpedition?

Yes, we all want band mode slots or the ATNO at the least.

I submit that equality to all zones (not playing favorites or phoning home too often), taking full advantage of propagation and a wide variety of mode balance and a biggie, PILEUP MANAGEMENT TO ACHIEVE that goal are some of the main points to be considered.

In recent weeks, while giddy over the sheer volume of teams in the field; we've seen favoritism (oh, EU closed, we're done now), NO pileup control (utter bedlam); little to no use of propagation leaving some continents literally starving and other unsavory 'features'.  I'm not finger pointing but suggest that multinational teams do tend to be more balanced with who gets worked (EVERY team member wants to be the hometown hero).

I also suggest that total QSO counts is not the best measuring tool; but the number of uniques matters more (and yes, I collect ALL that I can, all the time, sometimes upwards of 20 slots on one team).  At the end of the trip, the stats should show that the team made a serious effort, to envelop ALL areas on the planet (and sometimes, prop laughs and defeats that effort); uniques proves that effort.

Teams should apply the old business model:  Under sell, over deliver.  (And that way, if an early escape is needed from the site, you never bragged.)

IDXC is the correct place to extol the virtues of 'the best' so the words are spread throughout the DX community; so what makes it 'best'?

Another (never ending, continuous and painful) topic:  Poor operating practices and how to change the behavior of the lids that use them.

My initial premise:  They are IGNORANT or they CHOOSE the poor behavior.  Ignorance is resolved through education (stupid lasts forever).

General behavior has gotten bad over the decades and is not heading in a positive direction.  We see it daily on any DX team in the field and for single DX op station calling.  

Part of the blame (opinion) is lowering the bar to get a license; noobs are taught to the test answers only and get a license knowing zilch about how to best use it.  (I can verify this having been a dual certified proctor in many testing sessions, they know test answers, ONLY.)  Most do NOT attend a follow up class, if available and we all lose from their misunderstanding or ignorance.

Local clubs RARELY address this (and there are fewer clubs each year).

For DXers, examples of poor (rude) behavior include (but are not limited to):

  • Tuning up ON the DX before split engaged (makes zero sense and may not tune correctly from the freq shift 'up') and taking several minutes to complete the tuning (should be < 10 seconds).  (Worst case, the DX is now answering YOU but you can't hear through the tuning up.)
  • Not going split, consistently.  We all make the same errors now and then; but really?  If you hear 'up', check YOUR settings, software belches now and then, don't be the problem.
  • Up cops, name callers and worse.  HINT: You are NOT helping after one polite 'up' message is sent.
  • NOT LISTENING, which includes YOUR call recognition (CW), continuous calling (ID machines), answering when a partial call is not even close to your call; calling when your continent is excluded (EU EU does not mean NA should call).  A good DX op will TELL the pileup what they need to know (up, up 5-15, spread out).  LISTEN.
  • NOT finding a clear spot to call (can be a challenge on phone and a best guess most of the time) which reduces the chances for everyone to get through (the DX can't decypher the blend).  It doesn't HAVE to be a dog pile, loudest mouth wins.  [Make it simpler for the DX to hear YOU, by being in an open spot and watching the DX movement patterns (phone/CW/RTTY) to be ahead of that pattern.  Hint:  Purely random over time, is not a natural human ability, the op HAS a pattern or stays put.]
  • Calling when the DX is not heard, at all (calling blind).  (They went QRT 24 minutes ago!)
  • In short, ignoring or violating ALL of the DX Code of Conduct, consistently.
  • On the DX side; does QRX mean stand by or does it really mean QSY/QRT?  Use the correct messages; EVERY time.
  • Little or no pileup management (hint: 'by the numbers' is SO out of vogue and ignores propagation entirely; filter by zone instead and shift that around to use the prop to work all areas evenly and fairly; you are not ET and have to phone home ALL the time).

Mistakes happen, sure.  So does DQRM (from clearly capable stations, able to produce loud signals; making ZERO sense to use that ability to deny others some enjoyment).  Poor behavior frustrates everyone in hearing range.  EVERY.  SINGLE.  DAY.  Take some personal responsibility to be a better more responsible operator.

  • Polite mentoring is the best answer, IF the offending (or ignorant) station/s is/are willing to learn.  How many make the effort to be a mentor?  If a mentor doesn't have all the answers, it's an opportunity for everyone to learn together.
  • Speaking at local ham radio clubs (with an introduction to DXing for the newly licensed or simply unaware ops) about HOW to present your BEST appearance on the air (repeaters too, oy!) would be another starting place.
  • Someone with the time, ability and tools (AND KNOWLEDGE) could make a series of SHORT (< 10min) presentations uploaded to YouBoob to help teach too.  Topics should include repeater use; what (NOT) to talk about and the how and why a clean(er) transmitted signal helps everyone having to hear it (a full day session there).  Some have attempted this, but either talk above or below the target audience (and some clearly don't know how to teach and a few are dead WRONG).

I guarantee you it would be a lively discussion at IDXC, the trick is to not allow it to devolve into a gripe session, but makes an attempt to find solutions and deploy them so we all win.  But as most attendees are seasoned DXers at IDXC, understanding the problems all too well; is it the right venue?

I submit that it's the best place to encourage mentoring.  We are mostly seasoned communicators; pass along what is expected for correct use of the license so we can all enjoy it more.  To that end, everyone wins.  If we don't, the hobby ends; it's that simple.

73,
Rick nk7i
