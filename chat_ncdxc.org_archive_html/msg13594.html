Actually, the new law supersedes the old version, and does away with the “offending language.”

The original (2008) §23123.5 said”

"This bill would also prohibit a person from driving a motor vehicle while using an electronic wireless communications device to write, send, or read a text-based communication … .” 

To my legally-trained reading, that prohibited mobile CW, as one normally operating CW is using a wireless communications device to send text (i.e., words and letters from an alphabet). Of course, this interpretation had never been tested by a court, which is the ultimate authority on what it covers. (Someone would have had to get a ticket for operating mobile CW, fight the ticket, lose, and then appeal that the law doesn’t apply to mobile CW. A ruling from an appeals court would be a definitive interpretation of the law.)

But that never happened, so we don’t know for sure what it meant. In any case, that language is gone in the 2017 amendment.

Now the relevant language is: 

"A handheld wireless telephone or electronic wireless communications device may be operated in a manner requiring the use of the driver’s hand while the driver is operating the vehicle only if both of the following conditions are satisfied: 

… [part 1 is about mounting the device, and is easily met]

(2) The driver’s hand is used to activate or deactivate a feature or function of the handheld wireless telephone or wireless communications device with the motion of a single swipe or tap of the driver’s finger.”

I believe that, when operating CW, the driver’s hand can be used to actvate a function of the wireless communications device (e.g., sending a dit or a dah) with the motion of a single tap of the driver’s finger. The wording would seem to prohibit the use of iambic paddles, however, since their use requires multiple taps of multiple fingers to activate certain functions (only single taps are permitted). Again, it’s for a court to decide. I seriously doubt any peace officer would have the slightest idea of what that paddle on the console was used for, and would be unlikely to issue a citation under this code section for operating mobile CW. Maybe it’s time to resurrect those old Russian straight-keys with the leg strap that were used for aviation in the World Wars. That’s a single-tap-only device (and looks cool on your leg).

Of course, if you drive off the road and hit a tree while chasing that rare DX, you may have bigger problems.
