I think it’s great to share techniques. After all, digi is new and evolving. I’m sure the transition from rotary spark to CW was sped along by the ham spirit of cooperation. Ideally, we’d have access to a WebSDR at the same locale as the DX. That probably wouldn’t work for Bouvet, or even Burundi. I find myself pausing my transmit cycle very few minutes, and “listening” to callers. It’s helpful to see if the DX is favoring callers in any particular slice of the passband, and as Paul said, trying to correlate successful callers and their chosen calling frequencies. I also try to listen for successful callers that have received a POOR SNR report, assuming that that’s a quiet and open channel in the DX passband. Jumping into a spot whose last inhabitant had received a +10 may not help unless you will be a +10 too!

 

In the original K1JT article entitled “FT8 DXpedition Mode User Guide” in 2018, Joe envisioned Hounds calling between 1000 and 4000 Hz, not 3000 Hz. I did find it helpful to read the original article… I’d forgotten many of the details, and appreciated the thought that went into F/H design that much more.

 

73 de Bruce, K3NQ

On Sun, Feb 5, 2023 at 10:11 AM Paul Booth via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

Maybe this is well known and I just discovered, but here is something that worked for me this morning with a large pileup.  We all look for an open spot to transmit on FT8 in a pileup.  But we can't always see (hear) what the DX station is working and what we think might be a clear frequency is not on his/her end.  So just like in CW I listen and watch for a 73 given to a station that I can hear, and then occupy that frequency knowing the odds are better that the frequency will still be clear.  Does that make sense?  Is there a better way to find a clear frequency in a big pileup?
