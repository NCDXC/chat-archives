I agree with Jim.

FWIW, many years ago when I was a teenager I helped my uncle out during the
summers at his ranch in Nevada. I loved being there and with him and working
on projects at his ranch. When he died I inherited his ranch. A group of us
thought it would be great to put up a tower and some beam antennas and
provide people with contacts from his ranch in Nevada as Nevada at the time
(over 20+/- years ago) wasn't very active in several major contests. Three
of us put up a 72 foot self supporting tower, a monster 10M beam....and
promptly earned the number one spot in the ARRL 10M contest three years in a
row for the multi-single category for the State of Nevada. What call sign
did we choose for our club? NEVADA NEVADA SEVEN NEVADA NEVADA. There was no
mistaking what state we were operating from.

Not only were we able to give many of the deserving DXers and Contesters
needed contacts (and QSLs) from the State of Nevada but we were also able to
provide hundreds (thousands) of Japanese with needed contacts and QSLs from
the State of Nevada. For the Japanese we used Japanese phonetics NIPPON
NIPPON NANA NIPPON NIPPON. They *loved our callsign and our *Japanese
phonetics*! How did we call CQ when working Japanese from Nevada? We called
MASHI MASHI NIPPON. We loved working stations and providing DXers and
contesters with needed contacts and a unique QSL from the State of Nevada.

Ultimately we sold the ranch to my brother in law at his request. But by
then we had acquired several hundred acres of forest land at 5000 feet in
Nevada County, California. You guessed it....we often use NN7NN now when
operating from our cabin in Nevada county (and from other locations as well)
but especially when DXers or Contesters need contacts from Nevada County.
There are many county hunters and also various 80M and other nets that
appreciate contacts and QSLs from specific Counties. In addition to HF
contacts 160M through 10M we also provide contacts on 6M through 1296 MHz
from Grid Square CM99 with monster VHF and UHF arrays we have up at our
NN7NN club site in Nevada County. Our monster 6M beam for example is an M2
11 element 6M beam on a military crank up tower with 10 cubic yards of steel
reinforced concrete in the base. It took over three years for a number of us
to get that tower up and the Heliax feed line installed from the tower to
the cabin. The local rattlesnakes were not amused. Over the years we have
been pleasantly surprised that NN7NN is appreciated by DXers and Contesters
alike for earning  various CQ WPX awards. We have seven towers up (so far),
several loop antennas (including a 546 foot horizontal loop antenna up 70 to
100 feet in the pine trees) and we also have our own NN7NN 2 meter repeater
up and running from our Nevada County site using solar power and batteries.
Its call sign is NN7NN/R. A club can do a lot with several hundred acres, a
5000 foot elevation, lots of pine trees, no paved roads or power lines for
miles and the only neighbors being primarily a few small bears, deer and
rattlesnakes. Most of us are now old farts in our 60s and 70s and it has
taken years to build up our club stations and antenna infrastructure. Every
Spring we have to clear fallen trees and/or tree limbs from the dirt roads
into the property.

Similarly, our club call NC6DX was selected over 15 years ago for primary
use in the California QSO Party (CQP) and for Field Day e.g. NEVADA COUNTY
SIX DX. If you would like to see our home made cabin/club house, some of our
club station equipment and some of our club members check out our pictures
posted at QRZ.COM for NC6DX.

Our club call N7PR was selected years ago primarily for contesting and we
still use it a lot....if you check our QRZ.COM post for N7PR you will find
over 29,000 hits for N7PR as we have actively used it over the years. Over
the years we have sent out *thousands* of N7PR QSL cards to the deserving
and we continue to do so.

Each of our club stations have their own ARRL LOTW accounts and we post our
club logs in ADIF promptly after each club operation. Each club has earned
their own Club DXCC awards, various WAS awards, contest awards and I think
we have made a lot of DXers and contesters happy over the years. We have
also provided some pretty nice Ham Radio experiences for Boy Scout and
Explorer Post organizations, guest operators and others over the years as
club functions.

We also host operations every year for W6DOJ, the Department of Justice
Radio Club, which is an Amateur Radio Club composed of active and retired
Special Agents, civilian employees, and friends of the California Department
of Justice, Division of Law Enforcement. (We also have our own modest rifle
and pistol range up there for members to use when they are not DXing or
Contesting).

Frankly, I was surprised that some DXers may have no idea why there are
justifications and good reasons for club call signs to be used by active
clubs and their members to provide needed DX contacts to both DXers and
Contesters alike.....or to work new DX for their Club to earn DXCC and other
awards for their club. 

73, Ted, K6XN 

PS Today you may hear Nevada Seven Peter Rabbit on the air from several
locations, bands and modes....N7PR is usually very active every Easter.....

On 4/17/2014 4:07 PM, Paul Ewing wrote:
> I would like to see other hams call for a "give back" of less than 
> legit club call signs and more concern and policing of hams by hams.

I was surprised to learn that there is, indeed, a shortage of 1x2 and
2x1 calls. That was certainly not the case the last time I looked, about
three years ago when I filed the application for KU6W.

There are very good reasons for contest clubs to hold a csllsign. 
Indeed, CQWPX is built around unique prefixes, and a rare prefix is worth a
lot of points. It's sort of like being DX without having to spend $20K and
12 weeks to go to an island. :)

Perhaps you are not aware of the US callsigns held by hams from other
countries, especially JA, where I suspect they are a status symbol. I
receive a lot of JA cards showing US calls, many of them 1x2s.
