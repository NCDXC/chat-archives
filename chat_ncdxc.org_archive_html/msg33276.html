The problem reasons of (not) getting through are straight forward.  It is NOT mode dependent and it doesn’t really matter who is being called.

This morning, TX5S on FT8 10M is an example to demonstrate constants:
1) 10M opened ‘early’ to NA (while it was still VERY open to EU/AF) you could watch the opening by the calls of the team answering, the opening swept west with the sun.
2) 10M opened WIDELY to much of the world, no spotlighting; an exceptional opening but not rare (same as other bands over the span of time).  HEAVY calling load.
3) The antenna/s at the site (or most stations) do not have razor thin patterns (3 dB point, great F/B etc) so they hear IT ALL VERY LOUDLY.  (My 4 elements on 10M has 3dB points at 45 deg, not that narrow.  Lower bands, wider patterns.)
4) They can only work what they can and it’s rarely ‘fast enough’ for the callers.  (DUH!)
5) On FT8 they CAN limit to ‘hear’ only the continent/s they choose for balancing the playing field; equal share of total callers worked per zone (they are not doing this on FT8 but DO call “EU EU” on other modes; frustrating to NA but it’s fair to all others; bigger picture view).

The answer also has constants for working ANY DX/ped;
1) Be THERE EARLY before the pileup grows (use alerts, spots and such for early warning); fewer in the crowd equals faster response.  (See point 5)
2) Be LOUD(er) than others to ensure you’re heard (amps, gain antennas) “bust the wall” as best you legally can and 
3) BE PATIENT; when the DX hears the entire world calling at once, it’s hard to pick out one single player in the band to answer.

This morning it took 30 minutes of calling, to get heard, answered and logged.  That isn’t my longest window, by FAR.  It was also near legal limit output.

[Lesser constants:
4) While propagation tools are ‘nice’, they often don’t match present reality so explore and push the envelope; listen to the spots away further than your expectations of the band.  That’s one place the magic of radio, lives every day.
5) Improving (lowering) your noise floor as much as possible, means you hear more of what you WANT to hear which allows you to utilize the often MUCH quieter floor of the DXpeditions.  You can slow your improvements, when your floor reaches MDS (as in never).
6) MANY DXpeditions have an MDS (or atmospheric) noise floor; so call if you hear/decode them anyway; magic happens when they can REALLY HEAR WELL!
7) Propagation paths to/from the other station MAY not be equal; try a skewed path and remember that one way prop, is REAL.  See point 3 answer again.]

That is how I play the game, always, for the rarer DX.  While Clipperton is a chip shot for the left coast; 22 slots in the log (so far) shows the above statements to be consistent and true as do my successes (and failures) with other DX and teams over time.

GL! 73,

I am by no means an FT8 basher, but boy! there's so little operator skill in this mode. 

I waited more than one hour on 80m FT8 last night, doing all those things that we believe help (find a clear spot, which is bogus because it's MY clear spot, not necessarily theirs), etc. In the meantime, I took a break, went to 40m CW and in 5 minutes I found the op modus operandi and got the Q. Went back to 80m FT8 and eventually I made it through.

The only operator skill I can think of is having a friend at the other side who can get you in the queue faster 🙂

Don't throw stones at me, I believe in FT8. It's just so helpless.

Here’s something that’s absolutely hilarious, at least, in my opinion.

I have been calling Clipperton for probably 2 1/2 hours on 10 m using my stepper, three element in 180° mode. 

I’ve been moving around all over the spectrum making sure I was calling in an open spot.

After my transmitter timed out, I decided to rotate the antenna 180° and take it out of reverse mode.

While I was in the backyard making sure the antenna was rotating correctly, I came back into the shack to find that I had worked Clipperton, while I was gone, off the side of my beam.

Apparently I was in the queue when my transmitter timed out and, fortunately, foxhound mode, will reactivate your transmitter if you get a response.

 It’s better to be lucky than good.
