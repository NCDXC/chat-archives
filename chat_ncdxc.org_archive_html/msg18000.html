I am in the process of converting my generator to natural gas--tapping the line that feeds the house.  I am of the belief that an extended outage--now a new PG&E gift--will ultimately see no power at your local gas station.  The natural gas is cheaper and hopefully the last power source to become unavailable. To load balance and avoid extension cords over two floors I am (properly) connecting the generator to my main power panel.  When power goes out I  mechanically throw a switch (200 amp rating) to route the generator output directly into the main panel.  Last step is to than actively open the circuit breakers on the garage compressor, dryer, etc to avoid inadvertently hitting the generator with an excessive load.  JIM

Alan,

We have a Honda generator rated at 1500 watts.  Our refrigerator/freezer in the kitchen needs 1100 watts.
Our freezer needs 800 watts.  When we have a power outage the generator is connected to the refrigerator and a line to the living room for 
our computers and cell phones.  I switch from the refrigerator to the freezer every 4 hours, supply the freezer for one hour than back on the 
refirgerator.  You can get a larger capacity generator but the bigger the noisier and more gas consumption.  In retrospect,  a little larger capacity 
would be nice for our needs but this is a good compromise.  The Honda is not that big and very quiet.  Had good luck wth Honda's.  This one 
is a Field Day veteran.  hihi
