All;

A good friend of mine, Alan Biocca, W6AKB, recently posted the following to another email list that I belong to.  Knowing that there is a fair amount of interest in SDRs in the NCDXC, I thought I'd pass his thoughts along to the group (with his permission, of course).  Here it is:

High Frequency Software Defined Radios have been here for awhile, but there are some pretty big changes recently and coming up in the near future. The latest crop of these rigs have 120+ MHz 14-16 bit Analog to Digital Converters (ADCs) directly sampling the 0-55 MHz RF after minimal filtering and possibly preamplification. This 100+ megasample datastream is processed by Field Programmable Gate Arrays initially and then by high performance Digital Signal Processors (DSPs) or by standard PCs that do the conversions, filtering and demodulation via software.

So, something like this has been going on for awhile, what's different?

The early SDRs operated at IF frequencies, not directly on the RF. This includes Flex 1000 through 5xxx, and also Icom ProII and ProIII, IC7600, KX3 and K3 among others. Direct Samping RF is more recent, examples include some newer Flex radios in the fairly expensive 6xxx models. Last year at Dayton Flex introduced the lowest cost model of the line, the 6300 at about $2500, so hardly an entry level price.

So what's really changing is the cost and availability of these direct sampling architectures is about to explode, or at least expand rapidly. Two key product lines are either here now, or will be soon at near entry level prices using this advanced technology.

One is based on the open hardware and software development by TAPR and others (including our own KA6S) and now being commercialized by Apache Labs. These radios, called the ANAN series are already on the shelf for sale at HRO and GigaParts, and they start under $1000. The lowest cost model is the ANAN 10E which has 15-20W output and somewhat downscaled ADCs and FPGAs, but it still has receiver and transmitter performance that place it near the top of the charts and better than just about every conventional radio out there. Direct sampling avoids most of the problems in heterodyne receivers such as images and nonlinearity. The PureSignal pre-distortion transmitter algorithm creates RF that is significantly cleaner than other Amateur Radio transmitters by compensating for the nonlinearity in the transmit RF amplifiers, both internal and external. This radio requires a decent computer to run the signal processing and transfers the digital stream via a Gigabit ethernet interface, but any reasonable i3 CPU is adequate for the job, some have done it with dualcores but that is probably marginal.

The other product that is about to hit is the Icom IC7300. This rig has been shown and talked about for months and is due for release soon (maybe it will slip a bit, perhaps till Dayton 2016?). Icom indicated the price would be well under $1500 for this IC730 midsized rig with a large 4.3" color screen with realtime high resolution PanaFall display. Of course no computer is needed, all the processing is internal via FPGA and DSP. This radio has knobs and displays unlike the Flex and ANAN (though Flex has an expensive external front panel and knobs that can be added to their models). We don't yet know quite where the performance of the IC7300 will fall, but the architecture is capable of (and likely to have) extreme performance as has been shown in the TAPR and Apache Labs as well as the Flex radios. This radio could outperform the $14K IC7851 in most ways, as well as the IC7600 and the IC756ProIII. Icom will probably differentiate it by leaving out features, unlike the ANAN's that have any feature that the hardware will support available. But for operations like Field Day the 7300 should have a much better band display, a cleaner and harder to overload receiver a clean transmitter, all in a package that is smaller and lighter than the usual ProIII we have been using.

It remains to be seen of course, and there are many ways to get something "wrong" in a new radio, but more than ever the 7300 is firmware/software based and can be fixed with an upload, which Icom has been supporting on their high end radios for awhile now.

Anyway, this is old news to some of you on this list, but for others it may be informative. Take a look at the ANAN's, Flexes and IC7300 and see what you think. 

And if anyone is in the market for a lightly used IC756ProIII drop me an email. :)

-- Alan, w6akb
