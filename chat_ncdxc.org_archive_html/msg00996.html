Folks, in tuning on Paul's question of the week I don't know whether this would be right on frequency as my most memorable qso but here goes:

It was August 1982, when I was researching industrial development in Salvador, Bahia, Brazil's Northeast, a blossoming petro-chemical industry area, I wanted to call my xyl (Anita)
and our kids who, at the time, were staying in Los Altos Hills with her folks. 

As landline cost to and from Northeast Brazil was unreliable, to say nothing about being prohibitedly expensive, a friend suggested "Why don't you see if you can find a ham?" 
Puzzled my friend redefined "Oh that's a radio amateur guy."
Soon after that I was introduced to a Luiz Carvalho, a.k.a. an attorney, plantation owner and PY6SL, who one evening invited me to have supper with him and his family. 
At the time they lived in a large apartment on the top (6th) floor of a high rise.

After supper, about 8 PM, Luiz took me to his study, pushed a button on his desk, and a wall slide aside revealing what appeared to me as a whole bunch of radios or stereo receivers.
Luiz proceeded to turn his antenna (that I later saw was a huge yagi on top of the partment building) north. He called a "CQ DX for any station in the USA". Within a minute or two a W4 replied and Luiz
asked if he could kindly "phone patch" to California .( SRI  I cannot recall the W4's exact call or name). 

He did so. My xyl answered but when asked if she would accept a collect call from Miami she said "no". It goes without saying that I was embarassed, doubly so when I soon realized that hundreds were probably listening.
Yet set the evening was rescued as soon after the "qrt" another W4 from Tennessee said "Hold on. I have a Watts line." He called. Again Anita answered and before she said a word he said "Don't hang up I have a free Watts line."
I the had a brief but happy chat with her and our (at the time) young boys.

To me, although assisted, that was my 1st qso. That fall, one Saturday, when strolling on Stanford's north campus near Searsville Road I came across a huge open trailer adjacent to larger but similar to PY6SL's  antennas.
In the trailer's "shack"  was Stuart Neubarth, WA6FSI and Pete Mahowald, K6TJ, a.k.a. EE PhD candidates, who invited me to join the club (W6YX), where I was "trained" to become, early in 1983, KA6OJI.
