I'm one of those guys that does not put a lot of faith in computer generated propagation predictions. I've seen them be "off" too many times.

Before I go on a DXpedition, I run the predictions. Then during the first 3-4 days of the DXpedition I make careful notes and I often compare them. Sometimes my findings are quite different than the predictions.

Sometimes I also look at the solar numbers and band openings depicted on DX Summit ( if there is Internet) before my shifts on a DXpedition.  During 7O6T and XZ1J I often found bands that were open and quite usable that showed closed on DX Summit only moments before.

At 7O6T, 17 meters was phenomenal with global propagation world-wide.

At XZ1J, 15 meters was the reliable band for USA contacts. 20/17 meters were very poor. 10/12 meters was surprisingly good to the US East Coast via the long path at their sunrise/our sunset. That was not predicted.

Anyone in the USA who had just a monoband Yagi for 20 or 17 meters was probably sorely disappointed with their ability to work XZ1J.

The computer generated predictions can be an indicator but my experience tells me to hit the band switch and be prepared for anything. I would never want to be limited/confined with a good antenna on only one band. A Tri-band Yagi is a safe bet at minimum to work Amsterdam.

I would not expect 30 meters to be the ideal band as practical antenna limitations on a DXpedition make it difficult to achieve much directivity or gain. A four square is about the best they could do on 30 meters and we are (legally) limited to 200 watts on our end. It might make the distance but I would not expect it to be better than the higher bands.

My goal is to get one SSB, one CW and one RTTY contact with Amsterdam Island. I will be very happy if I can achieve that.
