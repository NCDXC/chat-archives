The most important characteristic of an RX antenna is its directivity --
that is, its ability to separate one signal from another, or a signal
from the noise. I often hear DX better on my Beverages, but I wouldn't
want to transmit on them.
Verticals and horizontal antennas respond rather differently to mounting
height, and also to terrain. HFTA author N6BV has said that the math
used for HFTA applies only to horizontal antennas, and that the math for
vertical antennas was too complex for him model.
At Pacificon last fall, I presented some work I've done studying the
performance of ground-mounted vertical antennas with the same antennas
at mounting heights ranging between 20 and 45 ft. The DX performance of
virtually all verticals is improved by putting them 20-40 ft off the
ground, provided that they have adequate radial systems. The degree of
improvement depends greatly on ground conductivity -- the worse the
ground, the greater the improvement. In rocky, mountainous terrain, in
sandy terrain, and in cities, the improvement can be as much as 6 dB.
In preparation for the Pacificon presentation, as a sanity test and
proof of concept, W6GJB and I built an end-fed vertical 20M dipole that
he hung from a pulley tied to a support rope for his 80M dipole (up
about 80 ft in a redwood). Glen is 5 miles from me, over rough terrain.
I measured his field strength on my K3 connected to a vertical ground
plane as he varied the height of the bottom of the dipole from 0 ft to
40 ft. The improvement was 10 dB.
I'm just finishing an article for NCJ (National Contest Journal) about
this work, as well as work comparing verticals to horizontal dipoles at
modest heights. For a "flatland" QTH, the low angle radiation of an
elevated vertical can be within a dB or so of that from a small beam at
modest height (and may beat the beam if it has traps!).
