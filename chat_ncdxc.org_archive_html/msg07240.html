Absolutely - it's fun to keep turning over every "stone" you can to find
another gold nugget. There have been a few steps up that I took that really
did make the difference between hearing a very rare ATNO or not. It took a
while and a lot of work - but I have crawled up from the "alligator swamp"
by trying new things - all tweaking, tuning, and dropping old "wives tales"
that I used to believe were true.

The learning has been the fuel that brought new and better DX. I'm not an
engineer, and don't play one on TV - but am a "serious amateur" - hi hi. And
it seems there is always something new to learn - which makes it so much
fun, and keeps me in the game. 

My most recent project is building a drill jig that lets me pin element to
boom plates onto round aluminum boom stock - and which makes the elements
all line up perfectly. So - there is the electrical as well as the
mechanical that get a work out around here. However - if I didn't have so
much dang aluminum on hand - I would follow Tom, DF2BO's "Optibeam" approach
and use a square boom. But heck - I have built 6 very good antennas out of
$300 that I salvaged from Carl, AI6V's "back-40" when he lived up in Nevada
City, and even had spare aluminum left over that I gave to a buddy who is
building his own mono band yagi!

You're not alone, Rich. I think most guys who are serious about DXing or
contesting and who are also at least somewhat technically competent are
continuously tweaking their systems -- antennas, rigs, feedline, etc. to
squeeze out every half dB, simply because those half dB add up, and because
when things are marginal (especially close to the other guy's noise), a dB
or two can be the difference between in the log or not.

Something like six years ago, I replaced the RG8X that was feeding my high
80/40 dipoles with Belden's 8213 (their best RG11) and picked up more than a
dB (a couple of dB at the band limits on 80). As I was able to find hard
line, I ran it all the way to the top of my tower (a 350 ft run), picking up
another dB on the higher bands, half a dB on the lower ones. Going from a
very good 3-el to a very good 4-el is not much more than a dB, and if I
could turn the 4-el SteppIR in my trees I would. :)
