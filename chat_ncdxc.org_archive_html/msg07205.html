Besides the longer vertical radiator – I have found that the circumference of the capacitance hat wire had an even bigger effect on being able to reduce the coil size. Sevick suggests that you add two times the diameter of the cap hat to the length of the radiator – so – if this applies to a rectangular hat like mine – then we are talking about adding 21 feet to the 60’ vertical. 81 feet is below the 80M band, and hence – when I bypass the base LC circuit and measure the actual resonant frequency of the antenna – it ends up at 3.1 mhz. This means I might have gone from that massive MA160V coil to something that is a bit too short. Luckily, I have a killer “edge wound” coil in my tubs of high power L-C components – and because this antenna is very easy to raise and lower – I might have some more tweaking to do. I use this calculator all the time:

 

http://www.csgnetwork.com/freqwavelengthcalc.html

 

OH – remember, the cap hat doesn’t add real radiation “real estate” to the vertical – because it doesn’t radiate but cancels – but what it does do is allow you to reach your resonant frequency. It does this with very little loss – hence – why making a giant top hat and having no coil is the holy grail. If I didn’t have so many trees nearby – my top hat would be twice what it is now – hi hi. I barely clear the trees as it is.

 

And per Sevick – who only used the most efficient coils (i.e. Air Dux / Mini Dux), I switched to Mini Dux coils. I might have the spelling wrong here. But these coils are really expensive – but I did have some in my tubs of antenna parts under the house.

 

My final goal will be to eliminate the matching circuit at the base of this vertical, but this will take some thought and more tweaking. I do know increasing the hat and the vertical as well as futzing with the coil could get me resonant on 1.821 instead of that 3.1 mhz – so I think this will be the next step in my march towards the “best” short hatted 160M vertical. But I really like the antenna as it is – resonant on 1.821 and 3.506 (with just one click of a relay in my KAT-500), and on the air tests prove that it is at least “good enough” to get to DXCC on 160M and work FT5Zm on 160 and 80M.

 

So I won’t “Let the perfect be the enemy of the good”, I will continue to use this antenna as it is until I know what my exact next step will be. And I am marching forward towards DXCC on 160M – I am now up to 62 entities. [YAY]. I am a computer programmer and not an EE – so I’m stumbling and fumbling towards the finish line . . . and enjoying every step. Funny how much time you can spend on a “silly little vertical”.

 

Jim suggested stub tuning – but on 160M ¼ wl would be huge – and I’d have to coil up those stubs – so this is where I am confused about that recommendation. ON4UN suggests that the circuit that I am using is essentially the same as stub tuning – in fact – the base LC circuit IS right out of the ON4UN Lowband DX-ing Book. But making ¼ stubs is very easy to do.

 

Onward!

On Aug 9, 2014, at 21:01, SPWoo via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

In the past I was once confused with Q and antenna efficiency.  I had thought that higher Q (narrow bandwidth) = higher efficiency.  I was wrong.  One could maximize radiation efficiency and broaden the bandwidth (lower Q) at the same time.  I experimented with my vertical by using a larger hat and smaller coil (similar to what Rich did).  The end result is a significant broadening of the bandwidth and higher radiation resistance (lower loss at the coil = higher radiation resistance).  So in the nutshell for a fixed radiator one needs to use as large of a cap. hat as possible in order to reduce the amount of inductance (coil).  The cap. hat is almost loss free and the coil is lossy.  Perhaps someone could explain it in technical terms.  Someone did explain it to me in technical terms however I can't regurgitate it now, hi.
