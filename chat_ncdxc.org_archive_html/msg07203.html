On 8/9/2014 9:26 PM, SPWoo via Chat wrote:
Mobile screwdriver antennas have very high Q but they have very low radiation resistance. T
Yes. Yet another example of trying to use one (inappropriate) characteristic to describe the performance of a system when there are many characteristics at play. With antennas, that often becomes SWR, but here it is Q. In audio systems, it is often frequency response. As one of my heroes, the late Dick Heyser (his day gig was at JPL, where he helped put us in the moon) once said, "trying to describe an audio system using only frequency response is like trying to write poetry with only one word in your vocabulary."
Indeed, one thing Rich has done is to increase Rr (radiation resistance), which is the "good" resistance that accounts for radiated power. The radiated TX power is, essentially, the voltage divider ratio between the radiation resistance and the combination of wire resistance (which includes any loading coils) and ground resistance. See my tutorial on 160M antennas and radial systems that explains this. I showed this to NCDXC at a meeting earlier this year, and at Pacificon a year or so ago. And although I didn't discuss it, the effect of the R in the coil depends on how much current is flowing in the coil, when depends on where the coil is. Near the base, a lot of current. Up high, not much current. And power is I squared R.
