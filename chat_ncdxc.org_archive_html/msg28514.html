Paul, et al,

If you want to use YOUR receiver OR an online SDR, your shack computer likely already has BlueTooth (BT).  You can use (almost) any BT listening 'speaker' device (Alexa, hearing aids, headphones) to hear it.  Since you operate JT modes, the audio is already to the computer, you connect that to the BT utility; then the BT to the 'speaker'.

If you want direct from your rig to BT, be aware there is a new(er) version of BT (needs both tx/rx devices) with a much lower latency; so if you make a purchase for this need, that is what you want.  (Still a tad slow for high speed CW and QSK)

BT sending from the rig means a power source or battery recharge for the TX device.  It would be simpler to connect your 'speaker' to your means of hearing from the computer.

There is also free software that I use (RadioFeed) for streaming scanner audio online; which can set up a web page for your home, then use any browser (on your LAN) to listen in.  That DOES have SIGNIFICANT latency (~20 seconds) for which I've not found a cure.  (The online streamer server, Broadcastify, adds almost 2 minutes of delay to the local delay if you listen to my scanner online).

If you run RadioFeed, with headphones, your walk around coverage area is you wifi range.

LOTS of options.

73,

On Feb 24, 2023, at 9:58 AM, Paul N6PSE via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

NCDXC friends, I am looking for a technology solution to solve the following problem. 

I enjoy listening to a morning 40 meter net but it comes at a busy time of the morning for me and I often miss much of it. 

I am looking for a way to hear the net as I move about the house.  I considered a set of AM baby monitors but looking for something better.  Is there a bluetooth device that plugs into the speaker out or headphone jack of the rig that would broadcast bluetooth throughout the house?

Any other ideas?


There are LOTS of these:

https://www.amazon.com/s?k=portable+bluetooth+transmitter


They take an audio output and transmit it over Bluetooth to any wireless earbud, headphone, or the like. They are very popular for folks who want to listen to TV (or radio) without disturbing anyone in the house. The range for Bluetooth is generally about 10-15 meters max, but if that’s OK for you, this is a cheap solution.

Word of warning: These devices are NOT very good if you are looking for two-way, real-time audio. They inpose a slight delay (on the order of tens of milliseconds) to encode the analog audio into Bluetooth. I discovered this because I bought one for Anna to be able to play/practice on her electric violin, without using heavy or wired earphones. It worked perfectly to get the audio from the violin to her lightweight bluetooth earbuds, but the delay between the sound made by the strings (acoustic) and what she heard in her ears made it unusuable in that application. But listening to a morning net a few milliseconds behind is no problem.
