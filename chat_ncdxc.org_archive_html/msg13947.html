Wow,

The 430 MHz feed is WR26 brass or copper wave guide (26" on the inner broad wall dimension). You can virtually
crawl thru it and each section weighs a bunch. The wave guide run is supported by cables and also carry a
cat walk from the side of the bowl up to the feed platform. Breaking that run of wave guide must have been a
terrible event. Replacing it will be expensive as it was custom made in about 1963. Even more difficult and time
consuming will be patching the holes in the dish's surface and then aligning each new panel and checking the
entire antenna for surface accuracy. The surface must be a perfect section of a sphere cut by a chord as the dish
is 1000 feet in diameter. It will be a while before the observatory and radar are back up and running.

I wonder how the rest of the facility fared?

I was at the observatory for a year in 1967-68.

john k6yp

Hi all,

Some on this list will be interested in news from Puerto Rico -- and
particularly about people at the Arecibo Observatory, and the big dish
itself.

I spoke with my good friend Angel, WP3R, on 14.260 MHz around 6:15 pm
this evening. His news was that things on the island are generally very
bad: no power and essentially no communication except via ham radio. He
and his wife Eva are OK, and as far as he knows all Observatory
personnel are safe and OK. The 430 MHz line feed on the big dish broke
in half, fell down, and made some holes in the big dish. Roads are
mostly impassable. It seems fortunate that more lives were not lost,
but it will clearly take a long time to get things back to normal.

-- 73, Joe, K1JT
