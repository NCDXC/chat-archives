>From one DXer and contester to another...  Jim I must say all the work you have done to your station and your operation have paid off.  Amazing Job.  Congrats. 

I decided to work with my elmers at N6RO this weekend.  There are few places in the West where you can get CW running experience.  I took over the 10m op position from N6BV (Truly one of the greats!) at Noon Saturday and carried it all the way to Sunday afternoon with some time operating the 80M 4 square Saturday night into Sunday AM.

For DXers looking to improve the following:
1.  Operating Knowledge and Skill
2.  Propagation Knowledge
3.  Dxpedition operating potential
4.  Using top quality Stacks/Arrays/Listening systems
5.  Opportunities to work with active DXpedition operators.. and maybe develop opportunities to get on one yourself.

As well as having the opportunity to work with Amateur Radio Superstars and Greats..  I highly recommend becoming involved with Stations like K9YC/N6RO/W6YX/K6LRG.  Its an experience unto itself.  We need ops.  you will be welcome if you have an open mind and are willing to learn and are dedicated to the competition.

  At N6RO we just missed working a 5BDXCC over 48 hours.  I wish I could convey the magnitude of the 10M EU pile up calling on Sunday morning.  What a rush!  I wish I could have taken them split and spread them up over 10 KC's..  but ethics and available bandwidth prevent this in contests. (for all but one zone 12 Chilean who decided to go split anyway).

On Sun,11/30/2014 9:42 PM, Al N6TA via Chat wrote:
> But why was the V31 so tough to work at the end of the contest??

Because he was a multiplier and had not been active much earlier in the
contest. In a contest logger, calls are plotted on the bandmap in
colors. In N1MM, grey means he doesn't count or that you've worked him
on that band, blue means you can work him, red means he's a multiplier,
green means he's a double mult (country and zone).

The key to working these pileups is to avoid them and come back when the
feeding frenzy. But even with those pileups, I've worked a lot of
stations I needed by 1) being early  2) carefully timing my call  3)
sending fast (we want to finish the Q fast, so will respond to a fast op
before a slower one) 4) moving to the fringe of the pileup.

Here's my summary for the weekend. I ran full power, assisted (that is,
I used the cluster) and SO2R. I haven't figured out if I picked up any
new band-countries, but I did work a handful of EU long path on 80M this
morning, perhaps a new one. I worked nearly a dozen EU on 80 short path
Friday and Saturday night, but no new ones.

Note the QSO totals on 80 and 40 -- I've always been able to run Asia
and Oceania on those bands, and also on 160, but it's gotten even better
since I raised the high dipoles about 25 ft. I haven't done the stats,
but I'd guess that half of the Qs on those bands are JA and other Asia.
Not much 160M activity, and nothing exotic -- I spent about 30 minutes
there for the 11 mults. It's like Sweepstakes and Field Day -- if you're
on 160M, you're losing. :)

One bonus thing though -- if you're into band-countries, guys who do
DXpeditions for contests often spend time before and after on the WARC
bands and 160M, so it's a great time to boost your totals. I picked up
something new on 160 Thursday evening.
