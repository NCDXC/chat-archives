Follow up re “how did you work them”.

 

Back in 2000 and 2002 my station included  a KLM KT-34XA six element triband beam antenna, an Alpha 76PA linear amplifier and a combination of a Kenwood T-820S transceiver and a Kenwood R-820 receiver. The P5/4L4fn North Korea operation was SSB only but the 7O1YGF Yemen operation was both CW and SSB.

 

In order to work North Korea I took several vacation days from work and basically lived in the radio room for the better part of three days until I had solid contacts on two different bands. J At the time I thought this North Korean operation might be the only opportunity in my lifetime to work North Korea. J My associates at work thought I was pretty radical to take several vacation days in the middle of a work week for an “urgent ham radio emergency” to just play ham radio from home. My kids however didn’t think it radical at all as they both had their ham radio licenses by then and they had both been on DXpeditions with “Dad” and they understood completely!

 

73, Ted, K6XN

I have a tie for my most challenging DX entities. Working North Korea P5/4L4FN back in 2002 was a challenge actually working. Wow what pileups.

 

My other most challenging DX entity was Yemen, 7O1YGF, back in 2000. The real challenge wasn’t actually working Yemen or getting the QSL cards from the QSL manager but rather the challenge was in waiting for 10 +/- years after submitting the QSL cards to the ARRL for the ARRL to eventually recognize and accept the documentation the German group had been issued from the previous regime in Yemen for their operation. J Thanks to whomever at the ARRL ultimately sorted out and finally accepted the documentation issued by the  prior Yemeni government officials who authorized the 7O1YGF DXpedition.

 

73, Ted, K6XN
