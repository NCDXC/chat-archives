Paul and Team

 

I have had many memorable contacts…but two stand out.

 

The first was back in 1960 +/- when as  a new teenage Novice operator with the call sign WV6LDC using a converted 40M ARC-5 WWII Army Air Force aircraft receiver from a B-17, a home made crystal controlled CW transmitter running four 6L6 metal tubes mounted upside down in a coffee can of water (it was ugly and dangerous but it worked) exercising a long wire antenna from my bedroom/radio room to a tree in the back yard in Sacramento. I called a long CQ at about 10 WPM on 40M with an army surplus J-38 straight key and I heard someone coming back to me on another frequency. He had a seven in his call sign (Wow!!!!) and he was on a rafting trip at the bottom of the Grand Canyon. While they camped for the night he thought he would try out a crystal controlled portable CW rig running on batteries that he had put together for the trip. He said he worked for the government and he loved the outdoors and ham radio. It was for me one of my most enjoyable rag chews on CW as a Novice operator. When I received his QSL card I found out I had worked Barry Goldwater, K7UGA, a United States Senator and a retired Major General in the U.S. Air Force. Years later after returning from a combat tour in 1968 and 1969 I had the opportunity to work K7UGA again but by then I was a regular Army Captain on active duty and selected for  General Staff for General Latta but among other things I was also assigned to be the radio officer for K2USA at Ft. Monmouth. At that time we operated five stations and we were running phone patches using ham radio for our troops in Vietnam back to the United States using Amateur Radio and available  Autovon and Stratcom lines so troops in combat could literally speak with their wives and children from Vietnam. Long story. I have a lot of personal respect for K7UGA and all that he did for amateur radio and for our troops for many years. I also have a lot of respect for those who volunteered to operate our K2USA stations and the K7UGA station and the phone patches…most operators at K2USA had tears in their eyes as they operated a typical two hour shift running  phone patches back then….particularly for conversations from wounded veterans to their children and spouses……I don’t know how some of those guys could handle what they heard day after day…but they handled the phone patches and added real value and IMHO provided needed and valuable public service with their amateur radio contributions….

 

Many years later working P5/4L4FN (North Korea) on 20M and 15M were two other  memorable contacts that stand out for me.  

 

Ted, K6XN

Here is your NCDXC Question of the week (QoTW)

 

 

What is your most memorable DX Contact?   What contact sticks in your mind as most memorable to you?

 

 

 

One of my most memorable contacts occurred only a few weeks ago during the last hours of 2012. I saw SV2ASP/A posted on DX Summit on 40 meters SSB. Having met him some weeks earlier and having seen his antennas, I had doubt that I would ever work this entity, however I decided to go into my shack and fire up all of my equipment and take a listen. Much to my surprise, I heard Monk Apollo working W6's and W7's. I fired up my amplifier and it only took three calls to him. He said "Hello N6PSE nice to meet you again. You are 5-6 5-6 QSL?   I was speechless!

 

Other quite memorable contacts for me are my CW/SSB and RTTY contacts with T6AF/WA2EWE Jim McLaughlin (SK) from his QTH in Kabul Afghanistan. He was from the West Coast and he was so eager to work all of us out here. He was really patient and he operated very well. I had met him only two weeks before his death and I still think of him often.

 

 

Paul N6PSE
