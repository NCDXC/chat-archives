Over the years we have used both NoAlox and Penetrox as anti-oxidants for our Aluminum antenna rebuilds up in the Sierra. Our experience has been that both are effective when making copper to copper connections ,aluminum to aluminum connections and aluminum to copper connections. Over the years for example our multiple antique 20+ year old KT-34XA beams as they arrived from KLM used to routinely come with Penetrox included with directions from KLM to use the Penetrox for aluminum to aluminum connections e.g. between telescoping element connections, between aluminum clamps and portions of the element tips and between the phasing lines and their connections to the elements and even to the balun to driven element wire connections. Both Noalox and Penetrox are available e.g. they can be purchased from Amazon and Home Depot usually has Noalox but not Penetrox. We are still using both Penetrox and Noalox for rebuilding our antenna systems as needed up in the Sierra after every winter.
 
We have never used either NoAlox or Penetrox anti-oxidant on *coax connectors* as we simply didn’t think about using them for coax connectors as we haven’t had any oxidation problems over the last 25+ years up at our cabin site with connectors using *good quality coax connectors*. Putting a *thin surface coat* of anti-oxidant on the connectors sounds like a good idea to me…we just never thought of doing it. To put our experience in context we have nine towers up there at a 5000 foot elevation, many beam antennas for 12 bands,  a 546 foot monster loop antenna and over 25 antenna coaxial feed lines up there with a lot of connectors.  FWIW we use DX Engineering professional tools for preparing and installing all of our coax connections and after the connectors are installed we tighten them with pliers and then coat the outside of the complete connection with antenna seal.  I agree with Jim, K9YC, regarding tightening the connectors.
 
73, Ted, K6XN
 
PS These anti-oxidants have also worked well for us for electrical connections used for our solar panel arrays, electrical distribution systems, generators, diesel tractors, trailers and a 1943 WWII Army Jeep up in the Sierra.  J
 
PPS What are the weather conditions our antenna systems and coax connectors experience at our cabin every winter? Check out the pictures posted for NC6DX on QRZ.COM. See if you can spot the jeeps buried in the snow.
