As for:

"Especially when NCDXF funded virtually ALL of his out-of-pocket costs."

Rich,

What does this statement mean?
NCDXF’s mission includes funding DXpeditions to make rare DX available to hams worldwide. That said, we ARE primarily a US organization, and have historically been based in Northern California. We do not require DXpeditions to guarantee any particular geographic distribution, but we do encourage DXpeditions to consider how to maximize their presence in NA and the West Coast. That includes offering suggestions as to bands and times of operation (from propagation predictions), as well as station setup and antennas. A good example is the announcement that Ken’s Bouvet 2026 DXpedition will include a second operating position, on the west side of the island, which is particularly suited for propagation to North America. NCDXF is providing up to $200K in funding to this operation. ($100K outright, plus $100K one-for-one matching contribution. If you want to support this DXpedition, making a donation through NCDXF will double your impact.)
Marion Island was an oddball, almost “accidental” DXpedition. Yuris’ primary activity was to provide radio engineering services to the island research station. Almost as an aside, he arranged to obtain a radio license to operate in his spare time (or perhaps, to run an MSHV bot while he slept; you can’t prove otherwise). His travel expenses, room and board, etc. were provided as part of his work contract. He brought along his own radios and antennas.
His only real out-of-pocket expense was a significant “license fee”, which was completely reimbursed by NCDXF. (And yes, we did check whether this was a legitimate license fee, and not a bag of money passed under the table to some bureaucrat.) I don’t believe he raised any other significant donations beforehand, although he will recoup his QSL expenses through QSL fees.
So yes, I would have expected at least SOME attempt to focus more on North America and/or the West Coast. Almost the opposite occurred; as many have noted he would be working NA on 40m each night (our night), and you could see how the pileup moved from the East Coast westward over a few hours. But then he would often go QRT before any West Coast opening. 
Note that Marion Island may have been Clublog #11 Most Wanted on a worldwide basis, but it was #5 for North America (i.e., more needed) and #18 for Europe (less needed). It will be interesting to see how that changes as a result of this operation.
