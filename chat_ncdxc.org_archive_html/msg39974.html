Thank you for this insight on how Texas works, I appreciate that.  

For those that don't know, and want to know, California works in much the same way.  Most counties have Memoranda of Understanding ("MOU") with neighboring counties, which is why you'll see Fire and Sheriff departments respond on routine mutual aid calls to neighboring agencies.  Where I live, near the Ventura and Los Angeles County line, both departments get automatically dispatched to calls near either side of the line as a result of an MOU.  It also allows for a rapid saturation response for brush fires or other responses.

Beyond that, if a county needs more resources, such as on a large brush fire, the request first goes to the county's Office of Emergency Services ("OES"), who then sends it to the state OES coordinator, who then approves it and sends the request to all the County-level OES coordinators in the state (or outside of it as with the recent LA County fires).  Out-of-county resources are to be cared for by the requesting agency and they are under the direction of the requesting agency.  The longest part is the drive time from one end of the state (or from out of state) to the other end.

Every large brush fire I've assisted on (not as a firefighter but as a civilian support volunteer), has had responses from other states.  It really is a remarkable program.  The smaller ones I've assisted on have had units from all over California.

Me: Former IT guy, now a real estate guy.  23+ years and counting of volunteering with multiple emergency response agencies (in civilian roles).  Training on the mutual aid system from the (at the time) State OES Coordinator.  I've personally deployed through the OES Mutual Aid system back when I was on Search and Rescue.
