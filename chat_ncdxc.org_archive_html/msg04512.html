Given that choice:  80M dipole at 100ft or a vertical ... I'd pick the dipole. Can't think of any exceptions based on terrain. Getting something .. anything .. horizontal at 100ft would be wonderful. I can only wish!

Any vertical suffers ground absorption well beyond the radial system. A vertically polarized  wave induces eddy currents in the ground out several wavelengths until the energy becomes an established sky wave instead of a ground wave. So even if you have the world's best radial system, it still suffers compared to a high dipole. The only exception is if the vertical is close to salt water (like within 1/4 wavelength). Even then the "goodness" only extends out over the water .. not the other way.  That doesn't mean a vertical won't work .. it just wouldn't be as good as the high dipole.

73, Alan  AD6E

> On 12/1/2013 12:49 PM, Bob K6UJ wrote:
>> What do you guys think about this:
>> I could try a horiz dipole for 80M at about 100 feet at my qth. I think this is almost 3/8 WL.
>> Would I be wasting my time and be better with an inverted L with the horiz leg at 100 feet ?
>
> It's time to do some modeling.  Both will likely be effective antennas if done well. My guess is that the dipole may have a few dB advantage broadside.
>
> A crucial component of any end fed antenna (including the inverted L) is an effective counterpoise or radial system. Without it, the earth becomes an effective attenuator that can easily burn more than half of your transmitter's output power.
>
> Another point.  I wouldn't make it an L unless you also want to load it on 160M. Why? Because the added physical length of wire will make the feedpoint Z rather high on 80M, which will make it difficult to load.  You can easily load a 100 ft vertical on 80M by adding series capacitance to tune out the inductance, and you'll be not far from 50-75 ohms.
