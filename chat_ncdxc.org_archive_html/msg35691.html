That coarse 60 Hz buzz is a happy sound! It usually means that the multi streaming DX is strong enough to be easily decoded and worked. 
By convention, F/H (including the MSHV variant) spaces the streams at 60 Hz. Each stream is 50 Hz wide and uses 8-tone frequency shift modulation. The average beat frequency is 60 Hz, so you get a faithful recreation of an exciting CW DX station from the sixties. 
Without clusters, 2m spotting networks, and the internet, DXing in the early sixties was far lonelier than today. It’s a ton of fun chasing DX with the ravenous Wolfpack on W6TI. Back then, horrible AC hum, chirps, clicks, and auroral flutter were the tip-offs that the station was rare DX. I prefer W6TI!
Thanks for the trip down memory lane, Steve!
73, Bruce K3NQ (K3FNQ 1962-68)

I’ve noticed when Marek switches to multi-stream there is a distinctive sound generated by the streams’ audio tones beating against each other.  I noticed this the other day as I watched my spectrum display on WSJT-X.  I was reminded of the rough CW tone on almost all Cuban hams’ signals back in the 1960’s.  When I heard a CO7 calling CQ on 40 meters back then, I didn’t need to wait for him to sign his call before I knew where he was…Cuba!  My ham buddies and I had a joke that when Castro took over in 1959, the U.S. immediately slapped an embargo on any electrolytic capacitors going to Cuba.  All CW from Cuba had a loud buzz or hum, from rectified AC that was poorly filtered.  I checked Marek’s multi-streams and each one appears to be 60 Hz from the adjacent stream.  So when those streams are loud enough to hear, say a minus 10-15 signal level, they create a crisp growl that sounds just like a 1960’s-something unfiltered CO7 on 40 meters! 

Anyone else notice this?
