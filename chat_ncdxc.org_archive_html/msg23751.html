On Jul 31, 2021, at 11:46 AM, Paul Booth - WA6IBU via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

I resisted for a long time in trying this out.  There’s probably more controversy over this with comments such as “is this real ham radio” etc.  We do like new technologies and in this world of low sunspots it fits the bill.  After using it for a bit I think my only objection is how it’s in the same classification of RTTY.  I think it should be separate, they are just not the same and the rewards of achieving RTTY contacts are devalued by those on FT8.

I agree that operating RTTY is quite different from FT8 (or almost any other digital mode). Different skills, the option of “free-form” exchanges, ratchews, etc. However, ARRL so far considers everything except CW and Phone as “Digital”; perhaps that will change at some point.

  But then again, maybe RTTY will be going away.  I also find it very impersonal especially in USA to USA contacts.  So, a couple of observations from this neophyte on what I’ve observed in working the FT8 world although I’m sure there’s a lot more experience out there you may want to add:

  1. Getting Odd/Even Mixed up:  many times, I hear stations calling the DX on the same even or odd instead of the opposite.

If you are S&P’ing, clicking on the target’s CQ will ALWAYS put you on the correct half (even or odd).

  2. Calling too high:  I’ve found that if you can, a frequency to transmit below 2500Hz is sometimes better.  Some of our transmitters if not set right roll off too much by 2800Hz

My Icom 7600 can set the bandwidth as wide as 3600 Hz. In any case, if you use either “Split” or “Fake it”, the software will move your virtual carrier frequency in steps of 500 Hz, so that your tones are always in the center of the transmitters passband. The issue then becomes whether the target station can RECEIVE your actual tones, so 2500 Hz is probably a good number there.
