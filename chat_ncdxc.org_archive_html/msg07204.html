Wow – lots of great points regarding this discussion, and Jonathan and I have both been “in pursuit” of the best [short] vertical for 160/80M that we can put up. Paul’s questions were excellent – as were all of the responses. Jim, K9YC, has one slide in his 160M Antenna presentation that I always remember:

 

“Don’t let the perfect be the enemy of the good”.

 

So, I started out with an MA160V, and in  years past had the MA8040, and I feel these are fine antennas as they are – for what they are trying to do – which is to get you on those bands on a small lot. In my case they were great “starter” antennas, but certainly are compromises. The MA160V much more so than the MA8040V. But I like to squeeze every drop of performance out of an antenna that I can – and love “tinkering”.

 

So the question becomes “How good is good?”. My own answer is to “go the distance” and do everything possible to increase efficiency over what the MA160V gave me. I ended up with no MA160V parts – and a 100% home brewed 160/80M vertical with 100 radials. So – to Paul’s point – yes – it’s pretty easy to build your own and improve over the MA160V. I’d love to do A-B tests between what I have now and an inverted L made using this plus one simple wire attached to the top:

 

http://www.dxengineering.com/search/department/rigid-tubing/part-type/aluminum-tubing-kits/product-line/dx-engineering-telescoping-aluminum-antenna-tubing-kits?autoview=SKU&N=4294951163%2B4294951089&sortby=Default&sortorder=Default

 

But I don’t have the right lot to run a wire that would have to be horizontal at 65’ up. Drooping the wire down defeats the purpose for me – it just doesn’t seem like it would end up being any better than what I have. It might be a really decent DX-pedition antenna though (?).

 

My guide has been reading the ON4UN Lowband DX-ing book – and I have read chapter 9 many times now. I then read possibly the best book on short verticals – the Sevick – W2FMI book(let) on short hatted verticals. There are also two vertical articles worth reading in the Vertical Classics book, K9YC’s web site and Rudy, N6LF’s web site. Dean, N6BV also has done some great studies on radials – I used his recommendation for radial lengths, and I also read about the Minooka and Battle Creek Specials. All of this was for Transmit antennas – I also have done lots of research regarding receiving antennas.

 

My entire antenna farm is home brewed, but the designs are right out of the ARRL books. If there is any “innovation” going on in my back yard – it’s the fabrication of some of the parts on these antennas. I have built many of my own parts – and mostly just using a hack saw, drill press and pop riveter. I stopped using hose clamps – and either use stainless steel screws or pop rivets – depending on the antenna.

 

“Don’t let the perfect be the enemy of the good”. For me – this meant that I had to work within many constraints on my small lot. Radials went down where they could. I also used ¼” screen, but not “galvanically connected” to the wire radials. I used a big top hat on my 160/80M vertical and also on the pair of phased (Christman) 40M verticals. None of what I did is textbook perfect, but – everything I have done has increased my chance of meeting my goals:

 

1)      Make Honor Roll #1

2)      DXCC on 160M

3)      Have monobanders with as much gain as I can on 40 and 20M – by far the two biggest “money bands” as far as what my log tells me

4)      Do not create nasty interactions between antennas – which would circumvent all of my hard work on my antennas

 

I am sure I can still improve in several places – but I don’t let being perfect from going on the air. I have a pretty good idea if an antenna is working or not – there are all of the “scientific” tests (using various meters) and RBN tests – but then there are on the air tests – and how you do in pileups as well. I have used RBN more and more when I need to do “Proxy A-B Tests” – meaning where I couldn’t A-B say a Moxon at 55’ vs. a full sized 3L monobander or two 160M antennas. I even use the NCDXF Beacons – and all of this data is in my trusty notebook(s).

 

I carry a notebook with me and listen to music while “dreaming” on my daily BART ride to work in SF. It’s amazing what strange ideas pop into my head – and I now capture all of these in my “old school” notebook. Yes, I have a Smartphone, and even have Evernote on my smart phone – but the old school notebook becomes a memento – I laugh at some of my ideas from the past. The best thing about K9YC, N6BV and N6BT are that they will tell me straight up when I am going down a rabbit hole – and I have certainly made some mistakes. It’s been great to collaborate with Jonathan, W6GX,  and my two very close friends N6JEB and W6DEI. Some great stories too – like this one:

 

http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BfVRNwQLSZQ

 

So – sharing ideas like this with so many people has been the best classroom I’ve ever had, and the learning continues. It seems like there is no end to what you can learn in the ham radio hobby – and this is the real reason I stay interested. In fact – I am having more fun now than when I was 13 as WN2QHN back in “Joisey”.

 

Let’s not even start talking about propagation and Stu – K6TU’s fantastic propagation service YW.exe, the N6BV Propagation Charts, HFTA or antenna.13 files. I’m having too much fun these days.

 

Thanks for all of your input – funny how a silly aluminum pole can be so technical!

 

73,

Mobile screwdriver antennas have very high Q but they have very low radiation resistance.  They make the coil to have a very high Q because they can't increase the radiation resistance through other methods (i.e. lengthening the radiator or add a cap. hat).  In Rich's case the Q is less important as the radiator length and large cap. hat make the antenna very efficient.  For a given inductance one should make the coil having as high of a Q as possible.  Increasing the diameter of the coil will increase the Q.  However to keep the inductance the same a large coil will have fewer turns.  In Rich's case he used a smaller coil (one having less inductance than the stock Cushcraft coil) but the Q doesn't necessarily is lower than the stock Cushcraft coil.  In fact I believe the Q is much higher on the new coil than the stock coil since the new coil is air wound.

In the past I was once confused with Q and antenna efficiency.  I had thought that higher Q (narrow bandwidth) = higher efficiency.  I was wrong.  One could maximize radiation efficiency and broaden the bandwidth (lower Q) at the same time.  I experimented with my vertical by using a larger hat and smaller coil (similar to what Rich did).  The end result is a significant broadening of the bandwidth and higher radiation resistance (lower loss at the coil = higher radiation resistance).  So in the nutshell for a fixed radiator one needs to use as large of a cap. hat as possible in order to reduce the amount of inductance (coil).  The cap. hat is almost loss free and the coil is lossy.  Perhaps someone could explain it in technical terms.  Someone did explain it to me in technical terms however I can't regurgitate it now, hi.
