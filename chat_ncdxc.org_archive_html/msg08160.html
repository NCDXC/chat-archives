Garry, al,
 

I'd be curious to know how many members have more than a ‘trombone’ on 30M. I have one and it is as good as a dipole at the same height. But it turns and you do get directivity broadside as expected. I love it.

I have always felt that my trombone was a modest improvement over the typical sloper, Vee or dipole and that the guys with a 2 or3 element somethingorother on 30 M. They are those that I envy.

 

But I do agree that things are different on 30 now than before. Until December 2003, I had only three 30M contacts from SA or OC/AS. Number 4 was T04E on Europa at 8 in the morning after sun was 2 hours up. All I had was a sloper with the lower end 6’ off the ground and on the same feedline as my 40 M sloper. I ws stunned to work that one.

 

It was 2.5 years later that I made another QSO on 30M. Only until mid 2009 did I realize you could work a lot of DX on 30 with modest antennas. When I missed the last DXpedition to 5A due to their bad noise and my weak signal did I decide to upgrade to something which was the SteppIR. Still no 5A worked on any band but lots of 30 M fun since. But I still envy those with more than a trombone or fixed dipole.

 

73 de Al-N6TA

Gerry,

Tonight on 30m, the op was easy to copy for a long time. Unfortunately, he was taking all comers and most were Yurpeans and NA from the east coast through the Great Plains, with just the occasional zone 3 station and you can bet the Yurps and the right coast guys were adding to substantial band-slot totals. The pileup was so big and the calling so continuous that even with a  P3 I was at a loss to pick out the current lucky caller. And if I did pick him out--and I did many times--- bigger signals pushed me aside.  It's hard to cover 30 kHz worth of stations layered on one another, especially when the DX stations response to  one station triggers a flood of simultaneous responses.

In the early WARC days fancy antennas were not necessary on the WARC bands--DXers already had triband yagis for 20/15/10 and added wires for WARC. and it was easy to build one's totals on 30, 17 and 12. Now everyone has a SteppIR with trombones and  pushes the "200W" limit on 30. I swear there were west coast callers on 30 so huge they were pushing the K3 front end were way wide to boot, while   most of the pile was inaudible.

Garry

Its interesting to read the many varied experiences among the guys on the West Coast.
 
I’ve managed to make only one 20 meter SSB contact with FT4TA. I have been in the pileups at sunrise and sundown each day and have only missed one morning.  I thought I had them on 17 and 30 CW but Clublog says no. Its not their fault. With polar flutter and jammers in the mix, I will just have to try again.  I have listened to them from remote big contest stations in W2 but chose to work them from home.
 
Their signals have greatly exceeded my expectations. I feared that their verticals only approach would fail them (and us) but their signals are superb.
 
The quality of the operators is excellent and they are putting in an incredible amount of times on the bands. One has to wonder when they have time to eat and sleep.
 
I feel disappointed in their almost complete lack of attention to the West Coast during the grey line. Yes a few times they have asked for the West Coast but not enough. The West Coast made a major financial contribution to this team and I have expected more from them. Most of the time, they seem content to work the strongest signals. Tonight for example, they came up on 30 CW just after our sunset. Their signal was tremendous. Not one time did the operator ask for the West Coast, he worked the strongest signals and those came from EU and the area east of the Rockies. After about an hour, he abruptly left for what ever reason. I didn’t get a contact and when he disappeared he had a tremendous pileup.
 
A few times, they have been on six meters when they could have been on 20 meters for the West Coast.
 
If I was running this Dxpedition, I would have a station dedicated to 20 meters SSB/CW for the better part of the first half of the Dxpedition. Possibly the same approach for 17 or 15 meters. We would not have been on 160 meters the first night, nor RTTY right away. We might do six meters during the second week, or not at all. Six meters should not be a priority for this operation.
 
I hope they pay attention to messages from their West Coast Pilot. I hope that they make a better effort to give the West Coast guys a chance without having to compete with the East Coast and EU. This next days could either be more of the same or it could be really great,
 
Let’s hope!
 
Paul N6PSE

I'm not a member but Paul suggested that I post this to the list, it was originally only to him...

The operators seem to be doing well but are lacking in two areas (from the little I can gather when I hear them).

1)  They aren't taking advantage of some of the propagation fully.  VOACap says we should hear them long before/after they appear; yet they are working bands that favor other areas, consistently.  As you noted, 20 is open nearly 24/7 to somewhere yet they're not around much of the day.  With 5 stations available, this isn't reasonable.  There hasn't been much (any?) contacts made on 160-40 here, but it is possible.

2)  They leave suddenly, sometime for minutes, sometimes for good.  A random dropout, I would understand from a generator needing fuel, operator change etc but this is consistent happening (also according to the DX spots).  An announcement would help; QRT, QSY to [mode:band]; QRX for op change, pit stop etc.

MORE CW; being the more efficient mode, it gives the little guys a better chance of making their first Q (besides me).  While on phone they are calm, even and slogging away, some TX EQ (cutting the lows in particular) would certainly help on the fringes.  The op sounds half asleep, voice rich in lows; very hard to hear.

I also note that, right now, the spread of the QSX is 10-35 KHz and spreading.  If they keep increasing the edges by only working the edges, it's not really helping anyone.  Pick a spread, stick to it and if too much, start working EU; NA; AS etc in rotation (paying attention to the propagation as it changes to not exclude someone).

I'd also like to see a mass exodus of all the Up Kops (as in Keystone Kops), grrr.... they are NOT helping and they likely know it.  I also don't get what part of UP is hard to understand; the same with JA only or EU only...  It's either stupid or rude or both, yet ALWAYS with the 59 reports.

If what I read is accurate about their antenna choices (two element compact beams, requiring salt water to be efficient); one needs to know which side of the island the antenna is, to know which direction it would favor.  I suspect they could have made better antenna choices than fiberglass two wire antennas.  While transport would have been more difficult, a few yagi antennas (SteppIR) with some masting would have been better choices; more efficient, more easily turned to work with propagation; heck you've been there, you know all this.

And they just dropped out again, without comment.  I only NEED one and since I worked one of the ops while he was waiting on Madagascar last week, I know it is possible for me...

But I'm happy for those able to make it through the walls.  If not this trip, perhaps the next will be more productive and by then I might be able to move and put up a real antenna (104 meter dipole now).

Darn, I'd really like to make them #241...

73,

Rick, WA6NHC
