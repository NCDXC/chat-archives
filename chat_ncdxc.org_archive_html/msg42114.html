I agree with Rick, good in theory, in practice maybe not so good.
I've listened to the arguments for, and don't buy-in.
The J51A team is doing very well with MSHV.

While on Clipperton we did upwards of 400 Qs an hour with WSJT-X, operating within the DXCC rules - no robot ops. In my opinion, good propagation and putting out a big signal are the DXpedition's life blood. When propagation is hot the pileup never diminishes.  SF is not in my plan for future projects. 

Personally, I think all this towel wringing over DQRM and Pirates is a waste of time. 
These days most people are making multiple Qs with a DXpedition, missing one Q to Slim - they still get the ATNO (if that's their goal).

Jammers and pirates are part of the adventure, people need to learn how to deal with them and move on.

Cheers,
GS K5GS

﻿ WSJT-X will NOT prevent someone from transmitting even if they haven't decoded the SuperFox if the user is in regular FT8 mode. Those people Paul listed most likely just typed 3Y0K into the DX Call window and started transmitting in the time period their software was already in - possibly without decoding 3Y0K first. If 3Y0K were in SuperFox mode, those people would then be QRMing 3Y0K, since the SuperFox uses a wide bandwidth. Which brings up yet ANOTHER advantage of SuperFox:

The software (at least WSJT and it’s “improved” variants; I’ve never used MSHV) will NOT ALLOW a station to transmit until it has decoded the SuperFox. You can’t manually enter the callsign in the call window and hit “Enable TX”, it simply won’t transmit. So only those who are really hearing the Fox can try to work him.

I recently gave a fairly in-depth presentation on the operation of SuperFox to my local club (San Lorenzo Valley), including the underlying encoding, the mathematics involved, and the authentication system. I would be glad to repeat that at a future NCDXC meeting, or perhaps at Santa Maria. I really dug into the guts (source code) to get all the internal details.
