It would have to be working Gus Browning in Bhutan on his Hammarlund expedition in 1965.  I was a teenage WA6SVY then and his call was AC2H.  He was working 20M SSB and I had no sideband capability, only CW.  I called him anyway, and he immediately, in his gentle southern accent, asked the other guys calling to standby for the “corn whiskey” station.  I called again and he gave me a 579.  This was around the same time Jack Troster W6ISQ had just published one of his hilarious QST articles involving Gus drinking “Yak-buttered Coke”.  I could just imagine that greasy glass bottle sitting next to his Hammarlund rig!

 

My second most memorable Q was working 3Y0X in 2008 on 160M with 100W and my dipole feedline shorted together to make a crude vertical.  The Peter I signal faded very slowly into and out of the noise with about a 3-minute period between peaks, when I could just hear him around S4.  I must have called 10 times through these slow fades, and finally he came back as our path peaked, and gave me a “5NN” (right!)  Whenever I see Bob Alphin I remind him that Q ranks up there with my Gus QSO!

 

73,

 

Steve

Here is your NCDXC Question of the week (QoTW)

 

 

What is your most memorable DX Contact?   What contact sticks in your mind as most memorable to you?

 

 

 

One of my most memorable contacts occurred only a few weeks ago during the last hours of 2012. I saw SV2ASP/A posted on DX Summit on 40 meters SSB. Having met him some weeks earlier and having seen his antennas, I had doubt that I would ever work this entity, however I decided to go into my shack and fire up all of my equipment and take a listen. Much to my surprise, I heard Monk Apollo working W6's and W7's. I fired up my amplifier and it only took three calls to him. He said "Hello N6PSE nice to meet you again. You are 5-6 5-6 QSL?   I was speechless!

 

Other quite memorable contacts for me are my CW/SSB and RTTY contacts with T6AF/WA2EWE Jim McLaughlin (SK) from his QTH in Kabul Afghanistan. He was from the West Coast and he was so eager to work all of us out here. He was really patient and he operated very well. I had met him only two weeks before his death and I still think of him often.

 

 

Paul N6PSE
