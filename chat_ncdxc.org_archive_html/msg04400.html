Today started as one of those special days.

Any kind of sleep is always a premium on a DXpedition. Almost everything
else is more important. With the help of the NCCC guys, we got two RTTY
stations on the air and we are blazing away, steadily giving out new ones.
We stayed up all night on 30 meters RTTY. My friend Champ E21EIC seems
almost super human and gets by with just a few hours each day. He says he
would rather operate than sleep.

After a few hours sleep, I walked across the road and down the drive to our
SSB shack. The police came out to see who was walking in the darkness.
Thankfully, his two German Shepherds were on a leash because they seemed
like they wanted me for breakfast. We skipped the high five as the dogs had
other intentions.

At the SSB shack, I waited until the sun started to come up and I swung the
beam around from long path North America to short path.

I started calling CQ on 14.190 listening at 14.240. I knew this would enable
the US General Class amateurs to call me. I wondered if this would be the
day when I would hear K6SSJ/M or my friend N6NKT.

Sunrises in Myanmar are rather spectacular affairs. The sky is full of
bright colors as the sun clears the high peaks dividing us and Thailand.
Within minutes, 20 meters comes to life. I wonder to myself, this is our
last full day, will the band hold?  Will the Chinese radar come along and
trash the band as it often does?

I call CQ and within minutes I'm working a K6, then a W4 and soon followed
by a W1. Soon, I heard the vibrating sound of the Chinese Radar. It's now S2
and I work as fast as I can to make contacts. Within about 25 minutes the
Chinese Radar roars to full strength. Soon it's S9 and I can't hear anyone
but the always present JA's.  The JA's Remind me of seagulls out in the open
ocean. They are ever present or not far away. No one shares their level of
excitement or interest in this hobby. In between contacts I'm checking the
other bands to see if they are open. If I hear the ever present JA's that's
a good sign.

First ten meters. I listen for those familiar sounds to give me a hint. None
to be found. I make more twenty meter contacts while checking twelve meters.
No sign of an opening. I continue working over the dull roar of the Chinese
Radar. My Q rate is really dropping off and I can't hear anyone. The guys at
home must think I'm a lid, I know I'm strong to them.

Darn, I forgot my cup of coffee on the way to the shack. I could really use
it now. I try a piece of Peters candy.  We've been joking with Peter about
running out of candy at PT0S so we have plenty of candy on hand. There is no
hardship or suffering at XZ1J except for the many mosquito bites. 

Fifteen meters sounds good. I blindly tell my twenty meter pileup that I'm
QSY to fifteen meters.

Hot Dog!  The band is good!  I make contacts all across North America. What
a great way to start our last full day!

Tomorrow we will start taking down our five stations at two sites,
consolidating only to keep two of them on the air. But first, we will fully
enjoy and relish our last days together. I get a kick out of watching some
of our team members making their first RTTY contacts and enjoying the mode
very much!  They remind me of kids on Christmas morning with a new toy.

We will start the arduous job of packing up in preparation for our flights
on Tuesday morning. Tuesday will be a long day. After our morning flight
back to Yangon, we will lounge around the airport awaiting our late evening
flights to Seoul or Tokyo. Then the long flight back to San Francisco, in
time for Thanksgiving dinner.

This has been an amazing adventure in an amazing place. This team has really
clicked from the first day and old Murphy has largely stayed away. Let's
hope our good luck holds!

See you in the pileups!

Paul N6PSE
