On Sunday, December 31, 2023 at 12:57:25 PM PST, Fred ["Skip"] Jensen <k6dgwnv@gmail.com> wrote:


2017 all over again.  I've come to the conclusion that ARRL's days are numbered, that number is smaller than most of us thought, and it is looking more and more to be unrecoverable:

a.  Membership as a fraction of licensees is around 20% and has been declining over the last decade.

b.  It will continue to decline, probably at an accelerating pace, due to the age of the great majority of members.

c.  2022 operating revenue fell short of operating expenditures by $15-20K [it's difficult to pin these things down due to overwhelming lack of transparency].  The shortfall was apparently covered by liquidation of unrestricted investments.

d.  The recent ham-handed handling of the dues increase and QST isn't going to help.  It will take 5 members who were paying $49/yr to remain members at $59/yr to offset every non-renewal just to break-even.  Sorting out the effects of the QST goat-rope is going to be harder than partial differential equations but will surely result in further membership and revenue decline.

e.  The rate of newly licensed hams is pleasantly positive ... however they predominantly have a utilitarian view of ham radio using it in support of their other life activities and hobbies, not as a hobby in itself.  They're also not teenagers or even remotely close.  Email me if you're interested in how I know this.

f.  In the last week or so, an ARRL Director publicly stated that [paraphrasing] 'now we have to turn them into actual hams which will take education.'  That's a view that seems to be shared by other directors, officers, and management.  "Congratulations on passing the test, but you'll now have to be (re?)educated ... and spend multi-kilobucks if you want to be a 'Real Ham'."

Maybe it's time to wave goodbye to ARRL.  It seems to be infected with an incurable disease.  While advocacy with the FCC is always raised as a prime reason for it's existence, advocacy actions have been very very few over the last decade.  Could a Phoenix arise from the ashes for the 21st century?

Rick Tavan wrote on 12/31/2023 10:35 AM:
Some thoughts on this matter:

I know Ethics Committee members K6WX and K9LA and have never noticed any "evil" tendencies although K6WX has mentioned disapproval of some of Dick's actions. That being said, expulsion from the member-elected board of a membership organization should be very difficult - only "for cause" and requiring unanimous decision of an Ethics Committee, a super-majority or unanimity of the full Board minus the defendant, and ratification by the members in the division represented by the director proposed to be expelled (or "recused" to use the legal fiction being promulgated here). In fact, a recent posting quoted CT law requiring membership voting to recall their own elected rep. Furthermore, the rules should specify that only "egregious, willful, and unwarranted" violations should be considered grounds for removal. 

A Code of Conduct is a good thing to have but ten pages seems excessive. Although directors of an organization can be required to comply with Board decisions that they did not approve of, gagging their dissent is undemocratic and should be illegal. (I'll leave that to lawyers fluent in CT and US non-profit law to determine!) 

Debate is a healthy component of democratic decision making. Forced illusions of unanimity are unhealthy. Companies do not have to be democratic although public ones are required to disclose certain important actions and finances. Their boards are "elected" by shareholder vote (by shares held) which is quite different from membership voting (one member = one vote). Elected non-profit boards should be democratic and be required to answer to their constituencies even though their reporting responsibilities may be less constrained than those of public companies.

ARRL is heading in the wrong direction with this proposal which must be defeated.

73,

/Rick N6XI
