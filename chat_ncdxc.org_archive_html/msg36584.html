On Aug 20, 2024, at 3:52 PM, Rick NK7I via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

They were on 40 phone, early in their effort.  But it was 3 hours into our daylight, they were still loud (here) but focused solely on AS/EU.

I happen to KNOW just how loud I am there, that time of our daylight.  They had to have heard me (equally loud or more compared to most JA stations); but I was ignored.  Then <pop> they quit, gone.  

(Once again QRX is NOT NOT NOT NOT NOT the same as QSY!!!  Say what you MEAN!  Mostly it meant they were changing bands.)

At their ending, 40 phone was the only slot offered, that I didn’t get (well 6M too but who’s dreaming now?).  Some slots (10 phone) were very difficult and not all of the blame goes to the solar storm (wiping out the northern tier of NA for days, but we had aurora).  Some slots were reruns from when I had a meager station (Palmyra 2016).  40 Phone should have been a one call simple Q… had they been there for NA.

I still lack a few slots to close out the entity, that should have been doable this trip (low bands I worked easily, except on phone).

Much of their operation, was EU centric, look at the stats (AS was a given, short distance, all water).  Had they shown interest in NA/SA, they would have appeared an hour before their sunset, prime time.  They did manage that on 40 CW (after they were griped at).

On the plus side, it was refreshing to see a major focus on CW.  It was an interesting test of SuperFox, with mixed results.  Seeing up to 9 streams, a hoot.

But, SF is not as deeply heard (or the SNR reports are not adjusted correctly); -16 dB was the lowest signal decoded (normal FT8 goes down to -24).  The ‘verified’ line is counted as a stream (correctly) but if the first couple seconds are not decoded, the entire packet is dumped (no repeats like ft8 streams).

IF the SNR is computed correctly, it hints that SF mode comes with an 8 dB low signal hit, offsetting much of the gains of one louder threaded stream.

Some folks didn’t update to RC6, as firmly suggested.  RC5 doesn’t decode as well (consistently) so there were other arenas that required attention (the WSJT-X groups) for better success.

I liked the verified (EVERY transmission), it negated much of the pirate noise and issues of the other branches of JT mode coding; everyone on the correct page.  I loved that there was no (obvious) robot mode.

Kudos for using normal F/H mode though it likely lowered the rate.  There are tricks that can be used for FT8 to compensate for the multistream signal losses (to a point).  Those do not work (as well) on SF mode.

That being said:  Most DX teams appear to pay little attention to the reports they get; which of course shows current propagation, but also should be useful for setting max number of streams (in classic F/H mode).  (Short version:  More than 3, not useful)

[to8fp, consistently low reports issued, all bands; they didn’t hear well.  I once sent them a +17 report, to get a -17 answer.  Uh, Hoooston, there be a problem.]

I have other thoughts for improvements (antennas choices, audio management, use of prop) but they haven’t asked.  It could have gone better.

We all know that DXpeditions COST real money.  Most solo or team ops beg donations because of the expense.

I submit that it would be useful (and entertaining) for a large group (NCDXC?) to adopt and sponsor a scoring format of each DXpedition (including Sylvia waltzing through AF and the one man op at Crozet; others on a case by case, TBD).  

1)  It could be an actual award; 2)  it could be bragging rights and (key point) 3)  useful for funding at the next event (low score, low hopes of a major donor).  It would reward excellence in operations with a baseline that is quantifiable.

Criteria could be very simple; based on bands used; zones worked and modes used.  Fairly and equitably distributed to all (as prop allows); the higher score.  Focus on a couple bands to one zone; lower score.  Early rapid departure (Bouvet); lower score (but high because it made ATNO for many possible).

I do similarly as an individual when donating to the teams; but coming from a large group/organization would make that public knowledge and a challenge to do well.  [And people love to rate and argue.]

I just returned to Idaho from WA state, the last few n5j  (and to8fp) contacts (all modes) were managed running the station remotely.  Previously, my remote ops were limited to FT8 (and an 80 morning group on phone).  This was done with an eclectic assortment of apps (not pretty) and some careful macro writing.  High speed internet was used at both ends, I’m not sure that cell would have been as solid.

One day, THE one app, will exist.  It is not that day.

73,
Rick nk7i
