On Jul 22, 2013, at 9:21 AM, John Miller wrote:

> Some facts:
> 
> Kosovo is not recognized by the UN yet -- currently a requirement for DXCC approval.  Russia and China have vetoed their membership.
> http://www.dxcoffee.com/eng/2012/09/26/kosovo-dxcc-entity/
> 

OK. But the question should be whether UN recognition should be the criterion. Why should Russia or China (or the US for that matter) have veto power over an ARRL decision?

> The ITU has not assigned the Z6 prefix.  It was self-assigned by the Kosovo telecommunications authority.
> 
> http://www.itu.int/online/mms/glad/cga_callsign.sh
> 
> http://dx-world.net/2012/the-republic-of-kosovo-soon-on-air/
> 
> So....reactivating amateur radio in Kosovo seems like a good idea.  But transmitting from Kosovo using an illegal prefix probably isn't OK.
> 

There is nothing "illegal" about the Z6 prefix. The ITU is not a law-enforcement agency, nor do they have any authority over anyone who does not accept that authority (e.g., the Kosovars). Kosovars can transmit with any prefix they like; the issue raised is whether they should be considered a separate DXCC entity.

> Unfortunately, the ITU -- and not the ARRL or IARU -- is the authoritative organization and the key here.
> 

Nonsense. It is the ARRL that sets up the DXCC program, the ARRL that sets the rules for the DXCC program, and the ARRL that decides who gets to play in their playpen. That the ARRL has chosen to defer their decisions to the UN or the ITU speaks to the leadership model of the ARRL, and nothing else.

Note that we are not talking about international cooperation on the use of shared spectrum space. No one is suggesting that the Kosovars want to operate on frequencies or modes that are not generally assigned to amateur use around the world, or in any other way to violate the conventions of amateur radio. The ONLY issue is whether the ARRL sanctions their operation for what is, essentially, a private game/contest, namely DXCC.

> For ARRL to change their entity admission process requires rolling up their sleeves and squaring off with the ITU.  Not likely to happen.
> 

Not at all. The ITU does not care about DXCC awards, they do not care about the ARRL's rules for their game, nor do they have any power to prevent amateurs in Kosovo from operating (with a Z6 or any other prefix). There is no need to battle with the ITU in order to set the rules for the ARRL's private DXCC party.


> Politics and amateur radio:  Not an easy road to travel.
> 

It's only political if you make it political, e.g., by arbitrarily allowing the UN or ITU to set the rules for an ARRL contest. In contrast, eQSL will issue "confirmations" for Kosovo contacts, and they count towards eQSL awards. It's just that nobody (well, almost nobody) cares about those awards. It's the ARRL that is the issue, not the ITU, not the UN, and not Kosovo.

Rich KE1B
