No, it does not.  I will explain my understanding (I'm not a lawyer and I don't play one, ever).  My area of study, many years ago, was criminal law (and the courts systems).

Nation states (like the US, Canada) sign an international agreement like the mentioned WARC accord, BUT no international group (IARU) can define national laws (band plans).  For example, what is lawful for Canada (30 miles from me), is not always lawful here (i.e. phone in the US CW/data sections, outside Alaska).

The US band plan calls for no data (phone/image only) above 18.100 on the 17 meter band.

The ARRL created the band plan for the US, which is accepted (agreed upon) within the US ham community, though the ARRL has no legal standing for creating or enforcing laws.  Almost (and this may be key).

With ONE exception, that of the ARRL authorized and appointed observers (whatever the name is now) who can send letters and complaints WITH the force of law to miscreant stations to seek their return to the posted standards.  If that effort does not have an effect, THEN the FCC will use that ARRL data to come down on the station (they don't have a feather touch).

This is a legal quagmire since the ARRL itself has no ability to affect a license issued by the FCC.  (Are then then mall cops?)

Operating in opposition to the ARRL band plan, would fall under this means of enforcement.  So in effect, the ARRL band plan is supported by the FCC who DOES have the authority to cite and/or remove a station license.

It could be arguable in a court, but not before the FCC panel or administrative judge; they'd side with the ARRL (band plan).  One would have to go higher, into the Federal court system, IF the case was accepted.  Highly doubtful and not cheaply attempted.

However for the situation at hand, Bouvet is operating on a frequency NOT within their posted planned mode spectrum (which is legal); NOT using accepted current modes (the variant FT8 modes AFTER stating F/H only, also legal). 

The Bouvet team also states awareness of the US operator classes and abilities; which would presumably include the US data limitations above 18.110 MHz (NOT allowed in the US band plan as created by the ARRL).

Why these variations from 'the plan', would be a valid question.  Accidental in nature; reasons for local situations??

Perhaps the pilot can discern intent (a long satellite/email chat).  Clearly Bouvet was heard on 18.112 MHz in the US by stations not lawfully allowed to respond.  Had they used their posted spectrum, the US would have been included in (legally) calling.

Again, I'm not a lawyer and I am known to make some mistakes in understanding.
