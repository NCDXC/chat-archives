Paul,

They had been practically non-existent here -- until this morning. I heard them just before 13Z, very weak, and after several overs for confirmation had them in the log. It was a struggle. The QSB was deep and with a short period, so timing was difficult - but key. The op was doing the right thing, sending "CQ TX7T K" and nothing else. The successful ops in the states were doing the same - very short transmissions during a peak. Over the next hour, TX7T gradually got stronger, and finally they peaked as the sky turned pink to the West. The signal actually moved the S-meter! After that, they dropped like a rock. At 1405, they were gone. I think TX7T was hearing much better than we were hearing. Quite a few stations were calling from the West, but the Ws couldn't hear TX7T's reply - even sixes(!)

I don't think they worked many stations, but they were there and available. You have to wonder (maybe not) what it would be like if they had a beach-front suite!

Wayne, N7NG
