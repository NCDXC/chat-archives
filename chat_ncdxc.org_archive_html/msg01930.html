I've been using PSDR with my Flex-3000 for several years.  There are
probably test results comparing the traditional measures of filter
effectiveness -- isolation dB, steepness of response, etc.  for the
Flex/PSDR and other rigs.  I'm not sure how the rigs compare in those
numbers, but the PSDR software approach seems pretty potent after
using it for a few years.

But to me, the most prominent difference is in the flexibility to
design your filters as you operate.

The PSDR software has a bunch of predefined filters, with the typical
bandwidths, like 100/250/400/500/1000/etc.  So it looks comparable to
a radio with a knob and a bunch of settings.  But each of those
settings is configurable through the user interface (the PC screen -
just open a window for a filter and type in the numbers you want
instead of the factory defaults).   You can set any bandwidth you
like, by setting the low-end and high-end cutoffs, which are
independent.  This means you can do things like define a 1200 Hz
bandwidth filter, which is helpful in SSB contests on a crowded band.
You can also do things like set the low-end at 600Hz and the high-end
at 1800, which makes voices sound strange (no bass) but they're still
intelligible, and it's a good way to remove the effects of a very
strong nearby station's wide signal.   There's also a facility for
tunable notch filters, which let's you set up a filter of whatever
width and depth you like and anchor it to a RF frequency.  Good for
cutting out annoying carriers and birdies.  Because it's just
software, you can have multiple such filters active as you need them.

I'm not sure how flexible all the "knobby radios" are at this kind of
stuff, but the filter configurability is a characteristic I think
would be good for any comparison.   E.g., what can you do with Kx
filters?

>> I have a question for Elecraft fans, how do the filters available for
>> the Kx's compare with the electronic filters in an SDR?
>>
>> I have become totally spoiled by the filtering in PSDR, I am not sure I
>> would ever be happy giving it up.  But I am open minded, fill me in.

>
> The K3 and KX3 are SDR radios, but built with front panel controls. The IF
> filters are in DSP, and can be adjusted in 50 Hz increments. Two front panel
> knobs can set either bandwidth and shift, OR the LOW and HIGH audio
> frequencies corresponding to the filter skirts. To switch from one method to
> another you simply push the knob (like you would a button).
>
> The HARDWARE filters that you buy for the K3 are ROOFING filters that
> protect the DSP IF from overload by strong signals outside their passband,
> and you can select from a dozen different ones depending on your own
> preferences. The 400 Hz or 250 Hz 8-pole filters are all you need consider
> for CW, RTTY, and PSK. The K3 comes with either a 5-pole 2.7 kHz or 2.8 kHz
> filter that is also used for transmitting. Serious SSB contesters typically
> add 1.8 kHz (most popular) or 2.1 kHz roofers.
>
> If you don't do contesting, I'd suggest ordering a K3 or KX3 with no roofing
> filter, and add one only if you've got a close-in neighbor who blows your RX
> away in pileups. It's VERY easy to add one later. The exception to that is
> is your K3 has the second RX installed, which must be removed to insert a
> roofing filter into either it or the main RX. So if you're buying the second
> RX, I'd buy the roofers and install them then.
