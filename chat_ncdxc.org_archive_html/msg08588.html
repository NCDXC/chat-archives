On Sun,11/30/2014 9:42 PM, Al N6TA via Chat wrote:
> But why was the V31 so tough to work at the end of the contest??

Because he was a multiplier and had not been active much earlier in the
contest. In a contest logger, calls are plotted on the bandmap in colors. In
N1MM, grey means he doesn't count or that you've worked him on that band,
blue means you can work him, red means he's a multiplier, green means he's a
double mult (country and zone).

The key to working these pileups is to avoid them and come back when the
feeding frenzy. But even with those pileups, I've worked a lot of stations I
needed by 1) being early  2) carefully timing my call  3) sending fast (we
want to finish the Q fast, so will respond to a fast op before a slower one)
4) moving to the fringe of the pileup.

Here's my summary for the weekend. I ran full power, assisted (that is, I
used the cluster) and SO2R. I haven't figured out if I picked up any new
band-countries, but I did work a handful of EU long path on 80M this
morning, perhaps a new one. I worked nearly a dozen EU on 80 short path
Friday and Saturday night, but no new ones.

Note the QSO totals on 80 and 40 -- I've always been able to run Asia and
Oceania on those bands, and also on 160, but it's gotten even better since I
raised the high dipoles about 25 ft. I haven't done the stats, but I'd guess
that half of the Qs on those bands are JA and other Asia. 
Not much 160M activity, and nothing exotic -- I spent about 30 minutes there
for the 11 mults. It's like Sweepstakes and Field Day -- if you're on 160M,
you're losing. :)

One bonus thing though -- if you're into band-countries, guys who do
DXpeditions for contests often spend time before and after on the WARC bands
and 160M, so it's a great time to boost your totals. I picked up something
new on 160 Thursday evening.
