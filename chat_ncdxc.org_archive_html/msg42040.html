That’s an interesting approach to a disappointingly frequent problem. I think it might work well for European DXers, where 3Y0K’s signals will be heard above many of the callers. In North America, their signals may be much weaker than W/K callers. 
Imagine the scenario: you hear 3Y or their QSO partner exchanging simplex reports. You, and your ten closest friends all call on that frequency. If I were the DX op I’d try to complete as many exchanges as possible before the frequency is glutted and I move on. Actually tuning off the frequency after only one exchange to truly do S&P would consume too much time. 
After a few exchanges, the malignant DQRM will be replaced by an innocent, but equally destructive simplex pileup. 
Meanwhile, if the 3Y0J pileups are predictive of the coming week, it’s going to be tough to pick out either Bouvet or the successful caller in the wall-to-wall pileup within the DX operating range. If Bouvet has luckily pounced on me, will I hear them?
I think a HARM homing in on the DQRMer’s antenna might be more effective. 
I’m looking forward to hearing what others think before we’re locked in mortal combat in a few days. 
73 and GL to all!
Bruce, K3NQ

  1. DQRM strategy

This strategy may only be implemented if the DQRM is very bad and slows down our QSO rate significantly.

Normal CW/SSB pileup handling is listening UP 1 kHZ on CW and 5 kHz on SSB.

DQRM strategy is to work simplex on all bands within a 20 kHz bandwidth for CW and 30 kHz for SSB.

We will work simplex or “contest mode” within the 20 kHz window in CW. For example, operate SIMPLEX between 14010 and 14030 kHz.

In DQRM mode - we do S&P within a specified window like in any contest.

All stations are calling SIMPLEX. 3YØK tunes across the simplex window and works stations SIMPLEX.
