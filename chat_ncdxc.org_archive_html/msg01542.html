Of course "was I able to work them" is high on the list but off the top of my not yet caffeinated mind (we're on the road, slept in)...  

1) enough time on site to be useful to the masses, i.e. two weeks minimum. This allows for reasonable set up/tear down, op time and bad weather situations

1a) a large enough team to minimize operator fatigue and workload.  This makes it complicated for logistics. 

1b) realistic goals; i.e. the number of active stations at any given moment and enough resources to manage that; 160-10 on CW, SSB and RTTY are mandatory. 

2) controlling the hoards at all times; keeping the bandwidth reasonable (state up/down x-y and stay within that window). This might mean taking breaks, QSYing within the band or similar methods of stopping the unruly.  

2a). Being unpredictable on a working pattern within that window; work a station hop, work; move, no pattern. (I've watched the crowds move up and down the window following the DX pattern, obviously panadapters are common now.) randomize, always

3) keeping the QSO rate moderate to high at every opportunity; flowing with the propagation.  One Q every one to three minutes is highly unacceptable; you're there to make the most contacts in every mode, period.  Working WITH the propagation instead of flogging the dead band to work a continent that doesn't have a chance of hearing you. 

4) using a tool to 'read' the DX logbook to make sure that all zones/continents have had equal access (fair play) and then specific callouts to attempt fairness. Equal access is not underrated, it is part of the ham spirit and should be an inherent part of all DXers.  (If not then buy some cheese for that 'whine'; be fair or go home.)

5) LOTW upload is mandatory (if only for awards); waiting a few months for the upload is acceptable so the 'donation' based QSL effort has a chance (I know, it's expensive but that's another discussion).  

5a) Use of a real time log confirmation will help the overall load negating the need for 'insurance' dupes and will/should lower the pressure on the DX ops.  That isn't always possible.  (Pretty darned nerdy cool though!)

6). A good sense of situation during high stress i.e. tired, trying to satisfy the DX hunger, knowing to smile instead of showing crankiness.  This ties in directly to crowd control and adequate resources; one side MUST be in charge, it should (better) be the DX. 

7) updates of local issues (QRN, antenna blown over, amp quit, weather sucks) would help everyone to understand more and perhaps make things easier on the team.  At least everyone could empathize with their discomfort/situation and hopefully give the team a break.  A simple announcement on SSB would be sufficient as the clusters would spread the word; no local blog or Internet required that way. 

7a) BE FLEXIBLE!  Adapt and overcome.  

8) have fun and enjoy the moment; the memories will last a long time. Plan some down time activities besides eating and sleeping and repair. 

Once upon a time in my career days, I was a line Fire Captain in a paid dept.  This involved prep, planning, resource management, situational control/command and logistics every day and on every event (call); so I can relate to some of what goes into a good DX operation. 

The above means an increased workload for the team but success is already largely in their command.  The effort (doesn't) work(s) because of what they do, encounter and how they react. 

Plan for the worst; hope for the best. 

It's time for more coffee.
