It's 63' total, and here's the procedure for lowering it:

1. Loosen top guys. Put 10' ladder against the bottom section of telescoping mast. This is firmly anchored to a 10' redwood post cemented in the ground. It is firmly guyed at 10' and 55'.

2. Release the two section pins one after the other and slide each section down. Use work gloves and the pin to "brake" the section - so it comes down slowly and smoothly.

3. Undo bottom guys. Now the antenna is 43', and the steel collapsed mast is fairly heavy, but acts as a perfect counterbalance to the aluminum antenna. Walk it down onto a 6' tall ladder. The hinged base - just one 1/4 inch bolt has no problem.

It does take some muscle, because as you know, the coil is big and with the steel hat rods makes it a bit top heavy. But, even though steel is not the best idea for use with an aluminum antenna, the collapsed weight makes it much easier to maneuver that top heavy ma160v

I live in a wind protected "bowl". We get 35 mph winds once or twice a year. The hills around here are about 400 feet taller all around and they get much higher winds.

Grant Bingeman has a neat little book called "Short Antennas for 160M Radio" - published by the ARRL, and he suggests that you can use two short hatted verticals on 160m. I tried this last night and received signal reports on 80m (Mission Trail Net) of 1 "S unit" below the 63' vertical.

So, the height really helps. And the 76 buried radials - about half at 55' and the rest shorter, also is a big differentiator.

If I were to improve this, I would go all telescoping aluminum. Steel and aluminum mixed do cause some loss, but this antenna worked FT5ZM on 160, 80, 30, 17, and 12m. My nested 20-15m Moxon did the job on those bands, and the 40m phased array kicked it on 40.

The biggest surprise? The RX on the tall vert rivals any low band RX antenna I've tried (but with higher noise, but with enough Sig above the noise).

Fun, fun, fun!

I'm glad to see you run these experiments.  Nice job.  Please keep the results coming.

In the quest of adding 80m and 160m capabilities I also experimented with a Cushcraft MA160V and MA8040V.  I don't need 40m on the vertical since I already have a 40m beam.  I added a 80m coil and cap. hats to the stock MA160V.  The end result is an antenna that is both resonant on 80m and 160m without using any remote matching device.  On 160m it works just like a stock MA160.  On 80m the performance would be on par with the stock MA8040, which is ok with me.  On 80m the RF stops at just 28'.  On 160m the RF stops at 37'.  On-the-air tests have shown my 80m signal is about two S units down from a HyGain Hy-Tower.  I still have some extra tubing left and I'd love to extend the antenna between the base and the coils.  It seems like your antenna is working very well due to the extra height.  However at 37' my current antenna is already too big to handle easily.  So I'm curios to know what you are using to lower/raise the antenna with.  I'm thinking of getting one of those heavy duty DXE vertical raising fixture, the one with a winch.  This will allow me to experiment without the risk of damaging it.

I read your writeup on using the vertical on 30m.  I have never tried it and to my delight my tuner did tune the antenna on 30m.  I tried running some power and everything seemed fine.  I will play around on 30m tonight.

From my experiments I also learned about corona discharge at high power levels.  When running high powers I got a high SWR due to corona discharge coming off the ends of the capacity hats.  I never saw the discharge in person however it's the only explanation as SWR doesn't change based on power unless due to arcing or corona discharge.  I purchased some brass balls and I made them fit on the ends of the hat rods.  Problem solved!
