+ AA6YQ comments below

I know a lot of NCDXC members like DXLab Suite.

+ As an NCDXC-trained DXer, I developed DXLab explicitly to support DXing. Beyond all the usual things that most "logging" applications do, DXLab automatically maintains a database with one entry for each active station, as reported by spots from up to 4 DX clusters, a local packet cluster, the DX Summit web cluster, and one or more local instances of WSJT-X.

+ The entry for each active station records  its callsign, frequency, mode, when it was first reported, when it was most recently reported, DXCC entity, CQ zone, any grid square or IOTA/POTA/SOTA/WWFF tag mentioned in spot notes, the worldwide regions from which the station has been reported (AF, AS, EU, NA-E, NA-M, NA-E, OC, NA), the distance from your QTH to the closest reporting station, and short-path and long-path propagation forecasts generated by the (included) VOACAP engine.
 
+ You can configure DXLab for pursuit of the DXCC, IOTA, Marathon, VUCC, WAS, WAZ, or WPX awards, specifying the bands and modes you're chasing for each award.

+ DXLab maintains up-to-date databases of stations known to QSL via LoTW, and stations known to QSL via eQSL.cc.

+ Information contained in the "active database" can be simultaneously displayed in a table, on a world map, on a bandspread, on a panadaptor, or on a graph optimized to reveal current band openings.  For example:

https://www.dxlabsuite.com/Wiki/Graphics/SpotCollector/SpotDatabaseDisplayAnnotated.jpg

https://www.dxlabsuite.com/dxview/WorldMap.jpg

http://www.dxlabsuite.com/commander/Help/Spectrum-Waterfall.jpg

https://www.dxlabsuite.com/Wiki/Graphics/SpotCollector/PropagationViewTime.jpg

The table, bandspread, and panadaptor displays highlight the callsigns of stations with whom a confirmed QSO would advance your pursuit of one of the above awards. But as experienced DXers know, chasing spotted stations becomes less productive as your DXCC total passes ~250 entities. To maintain forward progress, the determined DXer must understand the seasonal propagation openings between his or her location and the locations of needed stations, and must learn the operating patterns of those need stations. DXLab's database of active stations both reveals band openings from your area to the locations of needed stations, and reveals the operating patterns of those needed stations -- identifying  the bands, modes, and times when you are most likely to hear a needed station *before* the cluster hordes arrive on frequency. For examples of this approach, see the "What's been QRV that I need?" and "Chasing Mount Athos" sections of this presentation that I delivered at Visalia in 2017:

https://www.dxlabsuite.com/Presentations/DXing%20with%20DXLab%20Visalia%202017%20v5.pdf

Since 2017, K1JT's FT8 mode has provided a wonderful new resource for identifying band openings, even if you don't care to make QSOs in that mode:

https://www.dxlabsuite.com/dxlabwiki/GettingStartedwithK1JTModesDirect

+ Note the nice 40m gray-line opening between my QTH near Boston and JA/HL, as well as PY.

http://www.dxlabsuite.com/dxview/DX%20Atlas.jpg

+ If you've just sat down in front of your radio and quickly want to see which bands are open to where? Click the "Scan DX" button:

http://www.dxlabsuite.com/dxview/Videos/ScanDX%202016-04-24/ScanDX.html

+ if you're interested and want to learn more about DXLab, see

https://www.dxlabsuite.com/dxlabwiki/GettingStarted

+ DXLab is entirely free, as was all that elmering I received from NCDXC members on the repeater and packet cluster back in the early 1990s.
