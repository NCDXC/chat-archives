On Tue, May 5, 2020 at 7:54 PM Rick Bates, NK7I via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

You can do that, but with FT8 or FT4, it's shooting yourself in the foot because a lot of others think the same way.

If you call on the DX frequency, it's a dog pile and while the software is good, even decoding multiple stations on the same frequency, why subject yourself to intentional competition and probably not being heard when if you move off that tone, you have a far better chance of success?  (Don't forget to click the box to hold your frequency when leaving F/H mode.)

To extend that point, try to pick a tone that is NOT used by the DX listening time slot; while it is only what YOU hear, by doing this you've up your odds again.

What I do (not that I'm a pro), is I use what tools are around (reverse spotting networks, PSKReporter) to see what MY propagation is and if I don't get through in a 'reasonable' period of time, I take a break, find a new 'clear' slot and move to it, then try again.  (That is about the only way one can crack the walls that you often can't hear.)  Generally, that works (exactly like figuring out where the DX CW station is listening when 'up' is the only hint given; except FT8 listens to a larger band segment).

I have moderate to good success with that, even last year when I was on simple vertical wire antennas.

It's also not always about raw power and large arrays, the FT8 software can be told to NOT hear zones, uber loud etc.  Some finesse will usually get you further and FT8 lets the pop gun stations have some really great traction on the air.  As with any other mode, PATIENCE is required.

Double LEFT click on the DX spot THAT YOU DECODE and you will automatically be on the correct time slot *until the DX changes* (it happens).  Be watchful, sometimes they QSY their tones or otherwise hop around the spectrum.

And to repeat what should be obvious about working anyone, let alone DX, if you can't hear (decode) them, then wait to transmit until you can.  That's part of the DX Code we follow, no?
