I used to think remote was cheating - you had to earn DXCC HR from your own back yard, dang it! And I am sure up until maybe 1950, that would have been a good rule. Check out some of the trends here regarding Honor Roll and DXCC:

http://dxccsleuth.files.wordpress.com/2013/02/dxcc_entity_counts_awards8.pdf

Then I realized I was being silly - that there are soooo many variables with DXCC Honor Roll - that you really aren't competing with others as much with yourself. Here are some:

+ What country do you live in?
+ What part of that country you live in?
+ Do you operate fixed, portable or remote?
+ What is your terrain at your QTH like?
+ How much can you afford to spend on that QTH?
+ What gear do you have - what can you afford? How good is your RX? How good are you ears?
+ How skillful are you - technically as well as on the air? Hows yer antenna?
+ Do you have any HOA's?

The technical and geo-political changes are almost shocking since the DXCC HR program started. I used to think "Hey - those guys in the 50's had it so easy - especially the year I was born - 1959". There were fewer entities to get on HR and the sunspots were being handed out as fast as free beer. No HOA's too! I was wrong - its all been relative - sometimes more so than others.

I started liking DXCC Honor Roll because it has a "track" or "cross country" aspect to it. It really is just me and myself in competition. There really is no level playing field - only a valiant effort at that. Same has been in the case in my career - which I got into because of ham radio. Again - I had to rise up and continually improve myself to get where I am today.

After proclaiming - in 2001 - when I started DXCC Honor Roll - "I am going to make HR in 10 years - in Lafayette - with 100 watts and home-brewed wire antennas", that started falling by the wayside after I hit about 275 or so. Then it became "do whatever you need to power and antenna wise. I also moved from Lafayette to Orinda - 7 miles away - (divorce) but going from the top of a ridge to the bottom of a ravine next to a creek. I was stuck at 325 for 3 years and almost threw in the towel. Enter HFTA - I was able to get past 325 because I now understood what the biggest "blocker" was - the shunting of signals by the surrounding hills. Once I understood this - I devised a strategy and moved past my "blocker". Thanks to Dean Straw - N6BV! Oh - and my new XYL likes ham radio - the ex XYL hated it.

I have never used a remote contest super station. I feel like a big dummy though - I missed FT5GA - Glorioso because I refused W6NV's - Oliver's offer to work it from his (much better) QTH and station - 2 miles down the road). If I had to do it over again - I would be at Oliver's for FT5GA - because I never heard a PEEP at my QTH from them - but hey SSN was ZERO then too. I can't tell you how many ESP QSO's I have made towards HR, but after looking at HR - that ends up being my badge of courage - that I am most proud of.

Again - I broke my own "2001 pontification", I did use two Deleted EU entities from the 70's - Czechoslovakia and Canal Zone. Why? Because I realized the rules that were in place in 2001 - are the rules I could follow "legally", and so - why not? I did feel a bit "dirty" at first - they felt like two "freebies".

I think the same applies to remote. If I could afford a contest super station on the top of some hill - hell yeah - it would have been much easier than being at the bottom of the gulch. And that one doofus who called me an alligator - (and who never has experienced what major signal shunting due to hills would "buck up"). ESP QSO' for an ATNO in nasty pileups is so hard - it stopped being much fun after 325, but I wanted to get this damned thing over with. 

But in the end, I played the game the way I wanted to play it - and all technically within the rules. Those rules did change - but not too significantly between 2001 - 2013. Getting the KZ5VV QSL card after 39 years is now a bragging right - not "cheating" as I thought it would have been in 2001. And the award is nice - but the "fishing stories" of how you made it and what the journey was like is the real award - and what you learn along the way are the real award / reward. 

The "stories" and "bragging rights" are what make the DXCC HR award the #1 coveted DX award out there - and by a country mile. Play it where it lies, and Gud DX to You!

73,
