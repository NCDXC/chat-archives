From the DX end, you can quickly spot the guy who just calls and calls, hoping he will get noticed, and get in the log. He’s never in synch with the DX Op, or anything else.  Like a Metronome. 

Not much that can be done about these guys.  They alway get worked, so the feedback is positive.

On Nov 11, 2014, at 10:15 AM, Steve Jones via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

Paul-
 
I think that if, when FT4TA had answered a DXer, and everyone else in the pileup were to have stood by, FT4TA would have put 100K QSOs in the log instead of 70K.
 
The more QRM you cause to the DX station who is trying to complete a QSO with someone else, the longer it will take the DX to complete that QSO.  That will only delay your own next chance to work him, putting you even closer to the moment the current path opening fades.
 
My favorite “cure” for this behavior I first heard on a Don Miller expedition.  Don was on a Pacific Island (or not!) trying to complete a QSO on SSB with a weak K4 on the east coast, while the whole West Coast continued to call.  After asking the W6’s to stand by several times, Don lost his patience with one very loud W6.  He said, “W6***, you will NOT be in the log tonight.  Now again, will everyone else please stand by.”  You could have heard a pin drop, as the weak K4 completed his Q with Don.  This kind of real-time pileup control is the only truly effective way to deal with those calling out of turn. 
 
Unfortunately, this treatment seems to be “too harsh” by most DX operators.  So we will continue to suffer the Bully Lids.  I don’t think peer pressure will discourage them at all.
