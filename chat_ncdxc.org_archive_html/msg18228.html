Hi Paul,

As I've mentioned here before, I spent a LOT of time on 160 last winter.  What I saw agrees with your assessment.  It generally created both late nights and early mornings.

Two things became apparent quickly.  These will be obvious to anyone else that has spent time on 160M, this was my first serious attempt on the band (and wasn't possible in my previous locale in CA due to local noise).

1)  Grayline MATTERS!  I don't understand the why of it, but the hour before/after the sunrise (at the DX) and the sunset (at my location at least); magic happens (a several dB boost in signals).  If both time periods aligned, magic indeed!

2)  Reception antennas are critical (required) to reduce noise, directional is best to null out noise in other directions.  (Even in my quiet locale.)

Producing a reasonable transmit signal is straightforward (add power, improve the antenna) but you have to hear them too and very often neither side is optimal or is one sided only (strong transmit, weak receive).

It also convinced me that one way propagation is real.  The return path is rarely equal to the path your signal takes.  It was frustrating to see (on chat) that others were calling/answering but I couldn't hear them but that my signal to them was easily copyable (at only 500-600 watts).

In the spring and summer, I did the same listening on 6M (small looped 1/2 wave) both in MSK144 (at night, early morning) and FT8 while I went about my day.  That allowed me to see short hop openings and some real DX.  Reoccuring openings also allow me to utilize that time window to make some contacts.  Some openings were mere minutes, others lasted hours.

What I would like to see is two stations (or more) with modified FT8 software that remains in signal strength exchange mode with each other, each transmission signal report based on that last heard.  That would provide both general beacons for band conditions and as the data was stored, quantify those conditions and dis/prove one way propagation exists (by showing disparity or variation between the station/s).

With most current radio receivers capable of hearing signals in the basement (noise floor limited); it also was blatantly clear that modes like FT8 hear FAR better than me, so while I like CW for exchanges, FT8 made the DXCC (in one winter on 160) possible.  With many of us with hearing aging issues, I don't expect this to change, FT8 et al will be popular.  [94 of the 114 entities worked on 160 have confirmed so far.  Some as far away as South Africa, which simply amazes me.]

73 all,

NCDXC friends, I wanted to share with you some observations of my 160 meter propagation studies with FT8. 

My approach has been to leave my radio on overnight at 1.840 with WSJT-X recording all of my decoding activities. 

Each morning, I take a few minutes to review what and when my station decoding signals over night while I was sleeping. 

Here is what I see. 

Right after my sunset, I am decoding DX in the Caribbean and South America. Within an hour of my sunset, I am decoding Western EU including the UK, EA, DL etc. This is not an every night occurrence but does often happen. 

As darkness moves Westerly, I decode stations in the South Pacific, H40, FO, 3D2 etc. 

As sunrise approaches my station, I am decoding ZL, VK, JA, BY, JT, HSO, UA, and Eastern Russia. 

These findings change with the seasons and the length of our day and nights and corresponding darkness. The strongest signals to me take place just at the DX's sunrise or just before my sunrise towards DX in the West. 

160 is a fascinating band!  About half of my DXCC on 160 is CW and the other half is FT8. 

Paul N6PSE
