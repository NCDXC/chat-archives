Guys, this technique really does work. I received this confirmation from Rusty W6OAT this morning:

I have just spent about an hour calling FT8WW on 20m FT8 using my "normal" method of calling about 1000 Hz above his frequency.  NADA!  So I decided to give your method a try.  I tuned my transceiver to 500 Hz below his announced freq (which then put his tones around 800 Hz on my waterfall and called him about 200 Hz below his transmit freq.  He answered me on my second call!  THANK YOU!

73,
Rusty, W6OAT

Also, yesterday morning Richard WB6EWM used this technique to call FT8WW at 144 on the waterfall and after two calls, he made his contact. The key is to find a clear spot as close to but lower than FT8WW's lowest transmit frequency. If he is at 312 for example, call at 250 or lower by moving your VFO to 14.084 and calling at 1250 (assuming you see FT8WW at 1350)

Good luck!

Paul N6PSE

On Sun, Dec 25, 2022 at 6:14 PM Paul N6PSE <pauln6pse@gmail.com> wrote:
I've worked FT8WW on 30 and 20 meters with about five minutes of calling. 

Here is how I did it:

1) I decoded him as ODD so I called Even (critical step) 

2) I saw that he was spotted at 10.131 and was 300-400 Hz on the waterfall. 

3) I purposely put my VFO at 10.130 which put him 1400 Hz +/- on the waterfall. 

4) Recognizing that he is NOT Fox and Hound but is using MSHV, I called him below his QRV frequency on the waterfall. Around 700 Hz. I had no competition and a clear signal to him. 

5) Amid the huge pileup and overlap of signals, he saw my signal in the clear and worked me quickly. 

6) I did the same on 20 meters. He was at 14.085. I put my VFO at 14.084 and saw him around 1300 Hz on the waterfall. I called around 800 Hz, completely in the clear and quickly had a QSO. 

Try this technique, it works marvelous as long as the DX is NOT Fox and Hound. 

73 and good luck!

Paul N6PSE
