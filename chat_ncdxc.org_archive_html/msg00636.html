 I think that Clubs such as the NCDXC, SWODXA, The Clipperton DX Club (France) and the National Capitol Cities DX Club are wise to offer an annual DXpedition of the Year award.
 
For the cost of a couple of plaques, you get most of the major DXpeditions each year paying attention to the wants and needs of your club members. Major DXpeditions pay attention to these clubs and try to satisfy the wants and needs of your members.
 
Since the ARRL is a national organization, involved in much more than DX, it may not make sense as Wayne as pointed out to offer such an award. But for small constituencies like the NCDXC here on the West Coast, it makes sense to give the major DXpeditions a reason to satisfy our needs.
 
As a club, it is important for us to make a proper selection and award. The award itself can be meaningless if it is awarded for a less than stellar DXpedition.

In the FWIW department, the naming of DXpeditions of the Year has been popular. SWODXA does it, NCDXC does it (and probably a few others), and the League has done it in the past. Early on in my tenure at ARRL, however, I stopped the practice of designating a DXpedition of the Year.
 
The reason made sense to me: As a DXpeditioner, I thought that it was simply unfair. There are so many aspects to DXpeditions – many we don’t even know about – that it’s just too simplistic to pick one over the other. It would be far easier to pick the ‘worst’ DXpedition of the Year. (I wouldn’t suggest that, of course.) It would be easy for an expedition to the very rarest place in the world to be the very worst expedition on all other counts. It has happened.
 
Perhaps it would be better to honor DXpeditioners for various aspects of their expeditions (as Yasme did for AA7JV and HA7RY after the Chesterfields), the best logistics, the best operating, the most unique callsigns in the logs (Club Log), the best low bands, working the West Coast, etc.
 
73, Wayne, N7NG
Jackson Hole

Not many responses to this weeks question.
 
3D2C was a really fun DXpedition to participate in. Particularly because the team got along so well together. It was fun having so many guys from the West Coast involved (N6NKT, AD6E, K6MKF, K9JM and K6SRZ)

But as much fun as 3D2C was, I could not give it serious contention for my vote as DXpedition of the Year:
 
1) As #35/#47 on the most wanted list, it was not rare enough. I give points to destinations that are more rare/needed.
 
2) At 6.5 days, it was too short compared to DXpeditions of 15-20 days or more.
 
3) Due to cost cutting measures, we really were not able to communicate to the outside world and give news reports etc.
 
4) We didn't have to climb any cliffs or eat instant coffee to stay awake.
 
5) Other than PY3MM's dislocated shoulder, we really didn't have any hardships. We did see Sharks while snorkeling!
 
6) I really like the fact that we were able to include a number of competent first time operators on the team. That was fantastic!
 
Anyway, I urge everyone to make good use of their DXpedition of the Year vote and to try and acknowledge the DXpeditions that really hit the ball out of the park!
 
Paul N6PSE
