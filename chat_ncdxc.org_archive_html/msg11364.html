Just one example from personal experience.

K1N Navassa
1.  Take along 5 US Fish & Wildlife Personnel for leadership, protection, and compliance.  Cost for two weeks:  $104,000 paid up front
2.  Charter helicopter service for multiple round trips to Navassa from Jamaica (the only way to get on the island):  Cost for two weeks:  $200,000 paid in advance
3.  Other costs:
a. Purchase of tables, chairs, shelters, generators, fuel, water, and food for 20 people for two weeks
b. Shipping all supplies in a container from Atlanta to Jamaica
c. Storage and Customs fees in Jamaica

Total cost for this “Caribbean” DXpedition:   > $400,000

Has nothing to do with inflation, wages, value of the dollar, price of gold, or airline tickets.  

Has everything to do with what it takes to get to a rare DX entity that faces these common 
“Can’t Go There”  challenges:

1. it’s physical remote.
2. it’s politically remote.
3. it’s financially remote.
4. it’s operationally remote.

Want to overcome these challenges to put 100,000 QSOs in the log?   Get out your checkbook.

BTW - the current DXpedition to VP8-land hired the only boat available to get near Antarctica:  The Braveheart.  Price not negotiable.

And guess which boat the upcoming VK0EK Heard Island team is using to get to the Indian Ocean?   Yep, The Braveheart.

What to go into the amateur radio DXpedition transportation business?

On Feb 5, 2016, at 8:27 AM, Richard (Rick) Karlquist via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

I keep reading about how much dxpedition costs have
gone up.  I have no specific knowledge about this,
but I do read the Wall Street Journal, and
I don't understand how this "inflation" can be
explained, in light of general economic conditions.
The commodity bubble of the past decade has run
its course and most commodities are back to
2002 levels, most importantly oil.  (The only
exception is gold, which is not just another
commodity, and in any event is only 30% above
the 1980 price).  Wages aren't going up much;
I know my last significant raise was around
2003.  Central banks are worried about deflation,
not inflation.  The US dollar is at a 10+ year
high, so prices denominated in dollars are
especially constrained.  Airline tickets don't cost more,
and there is a glut of world shipping capacity.
Yet somehow, we are being told that charter boats
and helicopter services can command premium
prices?  What is special about them?  Not
arguing, just trying to understand.

Rick N6RK
