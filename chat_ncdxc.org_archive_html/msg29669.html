I agree with Paul.  We have enough 'lowering of the bar' these days and to do operations 'next to'  (how far?) would take away from those who have attained the ranks in #1 Honor Roll. 

DXCC is not about showing up (participation awards); it's about accomplishing something.  That something, is very specific.  The operations take place ON that location; a DXpedition has feet, antennas and transceiver (and wheels, boats, copter landing zone) ON the stated location; close just doesn't count (except horseshoes, hand grenades and nuclear weapons).

A RIB seems a viable compromise since the team must land the craft, set up the station, then leave so the environment remains (almost) pristine (simpler for controlling agencies to tolerate).  Human time on 'protected sites' would be less than a typical scientific study, mere hours.

Operation of the RIB from the boat (or beyond if an internet dish is on the RIB) protects the environment, while the transceiver is on/at the stated location (else it's /mm and worthless for DXCC other than grids).  Bouvet would have been a prime example (with the right boat for landing, a significant challenge, not to mention anchoring from the tidal surges).  If a RIB is lost, that's sad but if a member of the team is lost, that's a disaster.  If piracy or a government reaction happens (the contested south China sea zone), leave the RIB and boogie off to safety.

A RIB might lower some of the DXpedition costs since less would be transported and the staffing needs are lower (more remote ops, less shuttling meals and supplies, fewer bunks required onboard).  With costs being so extreme, it matters.  Team comfort would be higher which makes it easier to get folks to join the team (a warm bed and hot meals, not shivering on a barren rock in a tent).

It's the same reason that platforms were built ON unlivable rocks that during any storm, would be under water (Scarborough?  I forget now), not merely anchored in nearby (FAR safer) waters.  Those ops were THERE and that is a not so subtle difference.  Being nearby, isn't the same.

There is no absolute way to know where the boat/landing truly is since man is also inventive enough to work around such limitations as mere technology.  Not via tech or pictures, one barren rock or reef or atoll looks a lot like any other to most.  Even for those landing, how do you KNOW you're on the correct remote flea spot in the middle of an ocean?  You trust the technology (even sextant, compass and charts).

What you are left with is an honor code and some faith in the team.  A few have had monumental disastrous failures (unlicensed operations from a boat dock moored in a slip adjacent to land comes to mind) and live in infamy, never again trusted.

Since it was grazed on in this discussion, I repeat my premise that the only valid DXCC 'entities' that should be counted are only places where human life is present or possible (which deletes Bouvet unless it becomes a research facility and most reefs and atolls) and only include those governments that potentially will (ever) allow ham radio (unlikely in the DPRK in our lifetime).  Even more so with each governmental agency in charge routinely says NO to any request, because it's simpler than trying to find ways to make a trip compatible for any other need than their myopic viewpoint.

(I include satellite operations via geosynchronous birds because it excludes a large part of the world.  It should not count.)

We each (of an age) have slots that are now in the 'deleted' column so it really takes nothing away if a few more spots migrate into that column; that shows an active life of the DXCC award system, it adapts to reality (albeit at glacial pace).  Yes, while I have Afghanistan confirmed, it should be moved to the deleted column, as one example (not again, in the foreseeable future).

Let's not dumb down DXCC; let's not minimize the efforts of those who have worked everything so others can 'feel good' and struggle less.  Let's have valid and high real bars to reach.  DXpeditions BEING there and doing successful operations is THE point. 

Being nearby is a participation award.

73,
Rick nk7i

But I disagree with your response.  There must be technology available to ensure a ship is where it is claimed to be.  If Ukraine can pinpoint the exact location of a Russian tank, there must be a way to do the same with a ship.  I worked Don Miller when he was on the sailboat and not on Minerva Reef (or was it Maria Teresa Reef?).  Later it was verified (photographically I believe) that he was anchored just offshore, so my radio contact was in every way equivalent to him being onshore.  I remember when Gus Browning “deceitfully” crossed from Nepal into Tibet.  That operation was approved by DXCC, although it probably shouldn’t have been!  My issue is DXCC’s current rule that an operation must be on the surface of a given island, and not just offshore.  If fraud, deceit, and spoofing can be overcome, I see no reason why that 300 feet (or whatever distance limit might be established) should make any difference to the resulting radio contact.  Please don’t reject my suggestion (and NA2AA’s) out of hand, but rather consider a technological solution to confirm a DXpedition surface ship’s exact location.  It must exist.  Here is one possibility:  https://www.okta.com/identity-101/gps-spoofing/

If position deceit issues could be overcome, is there any other reason to deny credit for an offshore operation?

Good morning Steve and NCDXC friends. 

 

Steve, Ken K6TA is now SK and I have replaced him as the Pacific Division DXAC representative. 

 

Personally, I feel that by allowing ship based (near island) contacts to count for DXCC is inviting fraud, deceit and cutting corners. 

 

There is no reason why an experienced DXpedition team, with the proper boat, crew and proper Rigid Inflatable boats, proper generators could not have made a very safe and very productive operation from Bouvet. For various reasons, they were unable to transport their generators and get their gear ashore. Having the team members float ashore in freezing waters was extremely unsafe and unwise. 

 

Currently, ship based AIS/GPS systems can be "spoofed" In other words, their output can be masked in a fraudulent manner to make a vessel appear somewhere that it is not. This is done on a regular basis by Rogue fishing fleets fishing in restricted/sensitive areas. 

 

Don Miller "cut corners" by chartering boats and operating from the vicinity of rare and remote places. This wasn't good then and won't be good now. It will open Pandora's box to more shenanigans. 

 

One of the things I have learned is that some men will do anything for glory. The thrill and excitement of being the first, or doing what others were unable to do before often clouds their judgement. The quest for glory makes honest men turn deceitful and they sacrifice their team's safety. 

 

We ought not make it easier for them to do that. 

 

Paul N6PSE

 

On Sun, Jun 4, 2023 at 10:12 AM Steve Jones via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

Dear Ken,

I was struck by Dave Minster’s take on the Bouvet operation in his May QST editorial.  I fully support his comment questioning why being anchored 300 feet offshore doesn’t currently count for DXCC.  I have been chasing DX since 1962 when I worked my first JA from California.  I’m now at 333 current entities, with a few more worked but not yet confirmed.  My joy in working DX and chasing ATNO’s on DXpeditions is based on fine tuning my station so that I have the best chance of Mother Nature allowing me to work the distant station.  My personal analogy is fishing:  You can have the perfect Powell fly rod, and the perfect fly you tied that very morning based on the bugs you just saw hovering over the water.  But if Mother Nature doesn’t put that trout right there where it can see your fly, there is no catch!  Sure, part of my DX chasing is competitive, when I use my skills to place my call at just the right place and time to get ahead of all the other callers.  But my main reward is knowing my little signal went through all those thousands of miles of ionosphere, aurora, absorptive D-Layer, maybe a CME or two, to finally reach that distant operator’s ears and have him/her answer me back through all those same vagaries of propagation.  Whether the DX station was on dirt or rock or on a ship’s deck few hundred feet offshore makes absolutely no difference to my radio operating accomplishment.  And allowing DX credit for ships anchored off islands with sensitive ecologies, where access is prohibited by governmental agencies, would be to promote protection of earth’s delicate environment, a positive value we should all support.  Please consider changing the DXCC Rules to allow for offshore DXpedition operations.

Vy 73,

Steve Jones

N6SJ
