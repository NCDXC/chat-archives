I think calling it split is misleading since it's all one VFO. You are setting your receive and transmit audio frequency within the pass band of a single VFO.
My understanding is it is irrelevant what the audio frequency of the transmitting station is if the VFO RF frequency puts the audio in the receive pass band. Likewise for transmit where you set the RF VFO is irrelevant - with the caveat that WSJT-x will shift the RF frequency in an attempt to center your audio frequency in the transmitter pass band.  This could very well put you outside where the DX is listening if you are not on the same RF frequency.
To prevent this confusion and chasing each other around the band, the standard frequencies where established to minimize bandwidth consumption and keep everyone on the same page. Each FT8 channel is only using about 50 Hz of spectrum so a ton of (clean) signals fit in a 3 kHz passband.
Does is work better to not transmit on top of the DX by splitting your receive and audio frequencies? Yes, usually.
In F/H does not matter as long as you are transmitting > 1000 Hz and if possible in the clear. Hard to say what's clear on the DX end.
If I am wrong anywhere here someone smarter will correct me.

I was going to cover this in my DX CLub Presentation last month, but since we couldn't meet and since many of you have asked me, I will cover it here:

1) What does it mean when N6PSE emails that the DX is at 2454 Hz?
IF your Rig is at 14.074, with your Mouse, click in the little field under the Decode button that has RX to the left of it. Type in 2454 here. Then you will be receiving the DX on the right half of your working screen. 

2) How do I go split in WSJT-X FT8?

While holding your Shift Key, move your Red cursor/Bracket into a clear place (dark Blue) on your Waterfall. Notice that it will be offset from where your Green cursor is. Keep your Green cursor where the DX is. 

If you want to move both your red/green cursors into a clear place on the Waterfall to call CQ, Hold the Control key down and click in a clear spot. 

Good luck!
