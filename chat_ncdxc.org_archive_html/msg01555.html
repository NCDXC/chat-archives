There were a lot of great responses to yesterday's NCDXC QoTW: I think most of the responses were right on the mark and I agree with much of what was suggested.
 
I feel that it is critical for a DXpedition to announce their goals and then try to exceed them. It is difficult for a DXpedition to be "all things to all folks" however you've got to do your best to satisfy the demand. We are always asked to do 6 meter EME but the demand is small and the gear required is larger than the need so it gets left behind.
 
Some DXpeditions (TX5K & XT2TT) are really raising the bar on real time log checking. DXA2 was very impressive but my sense is that it required considerable development and support manpower. I'm looking forward to asking the Italian DX Group about their real time logging from XT2. My sense is that they have developed something on their own.
 
I still prefer a daily (single) upload to Clublog.org for our DXpeditions. Sometimes with flaky Internet that is quite difficult as it is.  
 
Lastly, all DXpeditions must strive to be fair and equitable. No one continent or CQ Zone should feel that they didn't get a fair chance to make contacts. It's more understandable when propagation (or lack of) prevents a QSO but when the operators prefer one area over another to such a degree that one area feels disenfranchised, then the leadership should take a look at that.
 
Paul N6PSE

Obviously, if I'm in the log it must have been a good DXpedition.

DXpedition essentials

1) Maximize unique calls. But this may require some conditions. 1A0C and the recent Glorioso made much of their unique totals, although 75% of them were EU. The real essential is a large number of uniques in the target area(s). Both failed in their goal of working zone 3 stations.

2) A successful DXpedition should reduce the need (band/mode) in the targeted areas. These needs can come from ClubLog for EU stations and The DX Magazine survey for US stations, who are not well represented in ClubLog. If the need didn't drop by at least 25 places, the DXpedition was not successful.

3) Did the DXpedition pick one or two bands and stick there until there were no callers? 9M4SLL has spent the first two days chasing EU propagation. ClubLog shows only a small number of NA stations. Spratly is needed by NA stations, too.

4) Where the DXers treated fairly? By this I mean was good behavior rewarded. TX5K had as many as 7 transmitters at one time working EU only. On the LF bands (160/40m), NA Little Pistol stations just didn't make it. Often there would be 5 hours of EU with a short break, then JA.

Steve, K6UM
