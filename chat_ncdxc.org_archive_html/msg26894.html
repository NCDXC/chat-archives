New Book: QSL?

We are excited to announce our latest title: QSL? (Do You Confirm Receipt of My Transmission?).

Archived from the collection of designer Roger Bova, QSL? details a collection of over 150 ham radio QSL cards from a single operator, unveiling the rich graphic culture of the pre-internet medium.

QSL? is available to order now, shipping next month.
pre-order +

A topic mostly unknown to those outside of the amateur radio community, QSL cards represent a time in global communication before the Internet age, yet remind us of social media handles used online today.

Each QSL card prominently displays a typographic call sign on the front, often followed by technical contact data on the verso, transcribed by the receiving party.  

Produced by the radio operators themselves (or local printers), the results are broad in approach—some minimal, others maximal, hand-drawn, photographic, or a hybrid of media. As a collection, they let us see styles from around the world.
 
 
 
But this collection is special.

After discovering the stack of cards at an antique store in upstate New York, Bova began to research the repeating call sign he noticed as the receiver of each card — station W2RP.

It turned out to be the call sign of the late Charles Hellman of Hastings-on-Hudson, NY. As reported by the ARRL, Hellman “…may have not only been the oldest surviving radio amateur in The United States but, at 92 years, also may have been the longest licensed.”

Charles passed away in 2017 at the age of 106, and this book is a small window into the international community that he and millions of other were a part of.
 

QSL cards are displayed chronologically, starting in the late 1970s through to 1989. Each card is shown at 1:1 scale without any modifications to its original design, including both front and back sides.
 

Specifications

– 276 pages
– 300+ color plates
– 8 × 11″ / 203.2 × 279.4 mm
– Hardcover case binding
– Cloth cover with front and back tip-ins
– Silkscreened spine
– Stochastic offset printing
– Custom protective mailer
– Printed in Italy

 
 

As Marc Da Costa notes in the book’s introduction:

“The sending of physical QSL cards has been on the decline recently, but the ARRL bureau still handles hundreds of thousands of them each year.”

We hope this book can inspire a new generation of hams, designers, or collectors of visual culture to appreciate the slower methods of communication. 73!*
 

QSL? will ship in November, 2022.

As always, thank you for supporting Standards Manual.
Jesse & Hamish

*“73” is radio code for “best regards.”
