On Feb 4, 2014, at 9:48 AM, Jim Brown <k9yc@audiosystemsgroup.com> wrote:

On 2/4/2014 8:28 AM, Andreas Junge wrote:
Even just a rotatable dipole up 50 feet gives you an advantage by being able to point exactly to the maximum signal.

I am currently using a 6-20M rotatable SteppIR dipole up 60 feet. It loads up on 30M and I can make a S5 signal go away in the null.

Over the last year or so, I've been using NEC to study the effect of mounting height. The BEST 2-element antenna is good for 4 dB as compared to a dipole at the same height. Indeed, much lot of the benefit we have historically assigned to a beam is really the result of mounting it higher.

For example -- raising a 20M dipole or Yagi from 33 ft to 67 ft is good for 6 dB at 5 deg elevation, 5 dB at 10 deg, and 3 dB at 15 degrees. Raising it to 100 ft is good for another 3 dB at 5 degrees,1 dB at 10 degrees. Tom Schiller, N6BT, has long maintained that, based on his experience, improving our signal by only 2 dB can make a big difference in what we can work. The point I'm getting to is that I agree with Rich that there's more than one way to skin the cat, depending on the practicalities of our real estate and budget, and that we can get gain from height, from adding elements, and from doing both.

Before I had found a clearing in my dense redwood forest large enough to rotate a beam, I had only wire dipoles at about 100 ft for 20-10, and at 110 ft for 80 and 40. 80 and 40 are still high wires (two for each band, at right angles to each other), and I've added a reflector to both NE dipoles to make them 2-el with peak gain to about 50 degrees. My guess is that I'm getting about 3 dB from these wire Yagis.

I'm continuing work on a presentation that looks at these issues in a way that helps us make better decisions on antennas based on the limitations of our real estate, possible skyhooks, ground conductivity, and budget. It compares verticals and horizontal antennas at various mounting heights, studying the effect of these factors on their vertical pattern. I plan to show this work in the Paciifcon antenna forum this fall, and would be love to do it for our club.
