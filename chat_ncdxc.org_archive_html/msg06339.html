Correction - RCS-8V. 

Here is a little more information - maybe the reason why the Christman Feed using the RCS-8V wasn't as good as with the StackMatch II. When I tested a phased vertical array with the RCS-8V - I was using two wire elements. Now I use two aluminum elements with a nice taper from 1 1/2 inches to 3/8". 

It could just be that aluminum elements really are that much better than wire (?). Which then makes me wonder about a beefy solid aluminum element vs. a copper "stip". When I model antennas in EZNec or YW - its very apparent how the taper schedule - thickness and progression through the taper schedule affect the performance of the antenna. But there seems to be "something" beyond the computer model as well. 

I recently did an analysis of a 20M Moxon up 55' vs. a full sized 3 element mono band yagi up 45', and the results were very interesting. One thing I have no way of knowing is the performance of the "gaps" at the end of a Moxon driven element. I have a way to ensure that the gaps are consistent. But its a lot easier with an aluminum yagi to keep everything perfectly in line. 

I'm starting to see why my elmers had always stressed aluminum over wire - and most of you know how I was addicted to wire antennas for many years. I do understand that with more surface area ("purchase"?) - and with a form that maintains its shape - why aluminum array (yagi or phased verticals) would out-perform a wire array. 

This is why I love experimenting with antennas - I learn something new from each project. The one's that fail as much as the one's that are successes.

I have been very pleased with my 40M vertical array – 2 elements spaced ¼ w.l  apart and with 44 radials each. So much so, I added a  second array for 30M, and it also exhibits the same characteristics as the 40M array:
 
~ 12 dB F/B
~ 4 dB forward gain in the end fire directions, ~3 dB broadside
 
This is consistent on RX, and I have done TX tests with stations in VK/ZL and ZS. I use a reference vertical as well as a half wave rotatable dipole up 45’ – and the K3’s “S Meter”. Reports at the other end are what they are . . .
 
One thing that I have learned is that the Array Solutions StackMatch II switch is an important component in the phasing arrangement. I use the Christman Feed, and each vertical is tuned with an SWR of 1.5:1 or less – simply by sliding the first and second segments in the aluminum tubing up or down.
 
I tried using an MFJ RC8V switch with diodes, and the performance wasn’t nearly as good as with the StackMatch II. The StackMatch II is not magic – but does employ the use of a toroidal transformer – and the people at Array Solutions say it would “flatten the SWR”. Since my elements are tuned with low SWR – not sure how much that is true.
 
I also found that trying to phase two vertical elements by following the Array Solutions directions was way inferior than the Christman Feed. The Christman Feed seems to be the biggest factor in successfully phasing two verticals end fire and broadside when you have ¼ wl elements spaced ¼ wl.
 
But there is “something special” about the Array Solutions StackMatch II in this configuration – I just don’t know exactly what.
