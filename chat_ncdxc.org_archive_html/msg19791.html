I've had this happen to me recently, I was halfway through a Q with Kenya and a station would park right up against the DX's signal and hit it with all the power and antenna they had and my unfortunate luck I was right under the first hop and lost the DX station and even though my receiver even is a direct conversion SDR, it still couldn't recover them with a 40 over 9 signal so close and haven't heard them again on 20m today. For me it's a curse of being in the midwest, If it's not a wave of 6s and 7s calling east to EU and Africa, it's a wall of 1s, 2s and 3s calling west to AS and OC. I have noted there are some considerate operators that try to leave a little hole about a whole signal width wide on each side of the DX. Lately I've found I've worked most stations by leaving my TX in a clear spot either at the very bottom 200-400 Hz or the very top 2800-3000 Hz and let my RX float around as needed.

On Tue, May 5, 2020 at 4:20 PM Paul N6PSE via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:
Rick, this is a valid question. 

In the beginning (of FT8) we all worked the DX on his frequency. It worked okay as it was a new mode and we were all trying to figure it out. 

Nowadays, there are the sledge hammer guys and the scalpel guys. The sledge hammer guys click on spots with little thought and call the DX on his frequency. Most get the time sequence right but some don't. That was the issue of today/yesterday. IF you transmit 300Hz or more away from the DX (left or right) you avoid causing QRM to your W6 buddies. 

What I often see is that there are 2-3 (or more) guys calling the DX on his frequency. It is like a mini pileup and they all effectively cancel each other out. The sledge hammer guys do that. The scalpel guys make a quick surgical strike in a clear spot on the waterfall and get their contact quickly and effectively. My friend Mike Flowers-K6MKF is an amazing surgeon using the scalpel approach as I saw yesterday when we were trying to work Andorra. 

Lastly, I see a few guys who call and call and call (with High Power) when they cannot even hear or decode the DX. I think that is just fundamentally wrong and just adds to the clutter/noise on the bands that make it harder and harder to make fast/good contacts easily. 

I will never call any form of DX that I cannot hear. Listen listen listen works for me!

73, 

Paul N6PSE
