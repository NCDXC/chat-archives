On Thu, Jun 3, 2021 at 8:32 AM John Miller via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:

> Yes, we did.  I averaged 250 per hour several times on Baker.
>
> Here are my other reasons why FT8 plays such a pivotal role for
> DXpeditions:
>
>
>    - Important for little pistol stations especially in
>    antenna-challenged HOA or high-noise neighborhoods
>    - No other mode has the capability of working five different calls in
>    parallel in the same sub-band with queuing
>    - With FT8, very high rates are possible compared to RTTY = more
>    ATNO?s in the log.
>    - Easier to reach certain parts of the world under poor propagation
>    conditions and weak solar cycles
>    - Excellent mode for challenging bands like 6M and 160M
>    - You still get credit for a digital QSO toward awards, etc.
>    - Excellent mode for hearing or speech impaired hams
>    - DXpedition operators can easily operate 2 stations on separate bands
>    simultaneously
>    - Has tremendous remote operation possibilities
>    - DQRM = Deliberate QRMing is minimized
>
> One of the most important advantages of FT8 is that DQRM is minimized.  On
> CW and SSB especially, some operators (i.e., LIDS) transmit on the transmit
> frequency of a DXpedition station in order to intentionally disrupt the
> operation.  A DXpedition station, operating as a Fox, can move around low
> in the passband.  Since WSJT-X (FT8) can decode the entire passband, DQRM
> is almost completely eliminated.  This translates to faster rates and more
> quality QSOs in the log.
>
> 73
> John, K6MM

> Paul,
> Sorry to say you are wrong. FT8 is not inefficient when propagation is
> present. Quite the opposite. Do the math on a 4 stream F/H operation. Even
> accounting for less than perfect sequencing it will typically be higher
> than CW under equal good conditions.
> FT8 does not favor the big guns, it favors high SNR. Sometimes that's a
> big gun, most of the time not.
> There are also skills to using FT8 as a Hound and worth a discussion as a
> topic.
> Your best bet for high FT8 rates as a Fox is to pick high SNR stations. It
> will maximize the likelihood of a perfect sequence.
>
> The ranked highest rates I've seen on an expedition by a single op are:
> 1. Multi-station/multi-stream FT8
> 2. SSB
> 3. CW
> 4. RTTY
> 5. PSK
>
> That said I would rather do anything but FT8 as an expedition operator....
>
> 73,
> Steve
> W1SRD
>
>
> Paul, in all seriousness this time, here would be my recommendations:
>
>
>    1. I know that FT8 is very popular now but I also think it is used
>    unnecessarily and is inefficient (aka QSO rates) when propagation is
>    present.  I would like to see CW/SSB used first, RTTY, and FT8 last when
>    propagation for the standard modes is not working.  The reason I say this
>    is that a primary goal for the DXpedition is to make as many contacts as
>    possible.  CW/SSB QSO rates are MUCH higher (I think, tell me if I?m
>    wrong).  Handle the big guns early and get them out of the way for the
>    moderate and weaker stations.  I?m not against FT8 but I see it being used
>    when CW and SSB can be supported.  For example, the C5 and A25 recent
>    DXpeditions mostly used FT8 when propagation existed for CW/SSB.  Perhaps
>    keep an FT8 station on at all times (only one band at a time of course)
>    while the others operated the standard modes.
>    2. If FT8 is used, it tends to favor the big guns more than using
>    CW/SSB which can overcome big guns with more skill.  Perhaps in F/H, you
>    can first use SNR thresholds to pick out weak ones earlier.  That to me
>    would be an advantage of using FT8.
>    3. It would be great to know quickly if the contact was verified to
>    avoid dupes.  I?ve heard a lot of people making dupes on recent
>    DXpeditions.  But don?t know how expensive or practical this might be from
>    your location.
>    4. It would be nice to know who the operator was on your end.  I found
>    this out on my first VK0 qso and was able to have a nice dialogue with him
>    later.
>    5. Daily news releases with photos would be nice to see what is
>    happening on your end.  Sometimes those of us waiting, don?t understand or
>    appreciate the challenges that occur with storms, antennas, generators,
>    etc?and I see subsequents posts complaining and indicating impatience.
