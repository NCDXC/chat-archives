Sean Reed is the Team Physician on the 3Y0K Team. 

He wrote this story and posted it on Facebook along with some really spectacular photos. 

Bouvet Island is an uninhabited subantarctic volcanic island and dependency of Norway situated at the southern edge of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge. It is the world’s most remote island. Bouvet lies 1,700 km north of Antartica, 1,845 km south of Gough Island, and 2,520 km south-southwest of the coast of South Africa. It has an area of 49 km2, 93% of which is covered by a glacier. Landing on the island is very difficult, as it normally experiences high seas, constant winds and features a steep coast.
Bouvet Island was first sighted on January 1, 1739, by the French explorer Jean-Baptiste Charles Bouvet de Lozier. Due to the imposing cliffs and challenging weather conditions he was unable to land on Bouvet. The first confirmed landing on Bouvet Island happened much later, on December 2, 1825, by British sealer George Norris who claimed the island for the United Kingdom. On December 1st, 1927 the Norwegians landed on the island claiming it for Norway and the British after taking into consideration how useless the island is, relinquished their claim.
I love visiting remote places and so looked for ways to visit Bouvet island. I found that there has been at least one successful tourist landing in 2006 with Oceanwide Expeditions and a couple of Ham Radio operator expeditions that had made it onto the island (1989 and 2023). There were also a number of expeditions/trips that made it to Bouvet but were unable to land (Oceanwide in 2015 and Ham radio operators in 2018). In 2012 Olavtoppen—Bouvet’s highest peak—was summited by 9 climbers. The Norwegians have spent some summers studying the island from a prefabricated weather station but haven’t been on Bouvet in years. Visits are infrequent putting the total visitors to step foot on Bouvet between 100-200 people.
I found a website of a Ham Radio expedition leaving for Bouvet Island on February 1, 2026 by using ChatGpt. I recently finished my Emergency Medicine Physician training and so emailed the expedition leadership asking them if they wanted a doctor to join them. They did for a substantial financial contribution as they didn’t have enough funds to cover the 1.7 million USD cost of the expedition. I wired them my portion and excitedly took the position of team doctor.
After a 3 week delay on February 21st, 2026 we set sail from Cape Town on a medium sized icebreaker ship, the Argus. We were 12 ship crew, 3 helicopter pilots/mechanic, 14 Ham radio operators and 7 “support” team that included polar region wilderness experts. We spent 7 days on rough oceans with 5-10 meter waves before arriving at Bouvet. After waiting a day for a weather window, 7 of our group were able to land on the southeast corner of Bouvet called Kapp Fie via helicopter before the weather window closed. We had to wait another 2 days for another weather window to be able to land the rest of the team/equipment.
I was in the second group and found the first group to be cold, wet and battered as they didn’t have enough time to adequately set up camp before bad weather hit them. With our entire team now on the island we were able to put up the rest of camp (total 5 large tents and 5 small tents), eleven Ham radio antennas and then bunkered down for the next 14 days. We had multiple days of up to 70 mph (60 knots) winds and -2 to 4 degree Celsius weather. There was frequent rain and fog that would soak us. We did have one nice weather day while on Bouvet allowing everyone to take a helicopter ride around or over the island. I landed briefly on the top of Bouvet during my helicopter ride.
Four people in our group had taken a sailboat to Bouvet in 2023 on the last Ham Radio expedition. They had to swim to the island as they did not have a helicopter. They noted big changes to Kapp Fie. The glacier had receded a considerable amount and over a thousand Antarctic fur seals had moved up the mountain onto the newly exposed terrain. There were at least a hundred chinstrap and king penguins as well. Above camp there was a small lake where giant petrels liked to bathe. There was over a kilometer of exposed rock behind the camp I was able to explore on days when the winds weren’t too strong. We found two pieces of wood with a nail in them. No idea how they got there. We ventured a little onto the glacier but not too far due to fear of crevasses.
The Ham Radio operators managed to make 102,000 radio contacts with people around the world. I spent my days either watching wildlife, helping in camp or hiding from the weather. We had occasional ice bergs float by. Day 15 and 16 on the island we were able to pack up camp and get everyone back to the ship before a large storm hit the island. It’s only taking us five days to get back to Cape Town as the seas have been good.
At times Bouvet Island felt like a prison because there was no way off of it and the nearest humans were at least 1000 km away. It was a special experience though watching the animals on the beach and seeing the cold ocean stretch off into the horizon knowing that this is one of the few places untouched by civilization left in the world. Raw nature where no one is coming to help you (because no one can) if anything goes wrong.
Our group was awesome. We started our adventure as strangers and fortunately left as friends. From the medical perspective everything was good minus some sea sickness, diarrhea and a couple small scratches. Our helicopter pilots (who have worked in Antarctica, Nepal, South Sudan, etc.) reported this as their scariest and toughest flying job.
Bouvet island is remote and desolate. Its challenging weather and poor accessibility are fitting for the most remote island in the world. If you want to visit you will need to get permission to visit from the Norwegian Polar Institute and then charter your own boat that is capable of handling the rough conditions of the Southern Ocean. Good luck. It’s a true adventure.
