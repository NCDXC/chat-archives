I’m enjoying reading other’s reminiscences about their dummy load antennas and desktop thump-modulated rigs. I’ve been trying to recall similar personal horror stories, but I have an affectionate spot for all my past radios, just as I do for all of my past cars and bikes. In my radio world, when a new rig entered the house, it became a member of the family. Just like family members with unusual political beliefs or personal hygiene habits, I loved them anyway. My first receiver was a Heathkit regenerative SW receiver… despite reading that super-regenerative receivers had fabulous sensitivity, I could barely pull anything out of the squealing and hissing. That was replaced by a Hallicrafters S-19R…. that was a great receiver and cost $5. I have wonderful memories of SW listening and exotic cards showing up in the mail. One of the exotic cards was from Radio Peking… that triggered an FBI visit to the home. My mom had to sign a statement confirming that the “little red book” was unsolicited, and I was forever traumatized that my endless supply of CCP propaganda had been interrupted.

Next was a Hammarlund HQ-100… I still recall the substantial feel of the knurled plastic tuning knobs. That carried me into my ham career, well over 60 years ago, and developed my newfound skill at sending CW with my Vibroplex bug with my right hand, while continually compensating for drift with my left hand on the bandspread knob. It seemed perfectly natural, just like my uncle’s Mafia buddies smoking cigars in our living room… you gotta love ‘em the way God made ‘em!

My first transmitter was well above my pay grade: a Viking Valiant that my Elmer sold me for $75. I had to remove two of the three 6146s from radio to run a legal 75w. My novice career lasted less than a month, and I upgraded to General some after. The lonely 6146s went back into their sockets, and my DX career took off…. It was 6 years after the history-making 1957 solar cycle, and my only hope was 40m CW, early in the hours before sunrise. All being family members, I stayed with those radios until a local dentist decided to leave the hobby and sold me his two-digit 1957 S-line for pennies on the dollar. Again, the station was well above my pay grade and took all of my savings from my paper route, but wow! I never realized that radios could work that well. I loved everything about that station and still do. That lead to my current ownership of three full S-lines. Just like drum-braked cars and bikes of the fifties need to be gently driven, with full knowledge of their limitations (thank you, Harry Callahan), they are still an absolute pleasure to use.

My wife got me a Kenwood TS-430 for my birthday over 30 years ago, and I had never realized what a chore it was to adjust those pesky tuning and loading knobs. Just flip the switch and go… just like putting the lever in D, rather than wearing out your left leg! Since then, it’s been a succession of ever-more-complex Kenwoods, as well as computer integration with the station. My biggest gripe? Windows OS! Not any radios, not any antennas… just the sure and certain knowledge that Windows was out to get me and would succeed with the next update.

 

73, Bruce K3NQ

Last week, we were discussing antenna regrets. Many of us described antennas that we had bought that did not meet expectations. I described my Cushcraft R7 dummy load. 

 

How about Rig regrets?   I have a few.   I bought the first version of the Yaesu FT100 mobile VHF/UHF/HF radio. This is the rig that aspired to be the later FT857D or the FT-891 of today. 

 

 

My biggest rig regret was my first commercial receiver. I bought a new (and even then, obscure) Star SR-500 all-tube receiver (1967 or so). It seemed to be a bargain (I think it was ~$100).

 

 

 

 

Covered 160-6 meter (no general coverage), AM/CW/SSB, and lots of features: RIT, selectable IF bandwidth, noise limiter, etc.

 

The problem was not so much that the receiver was deaf (it wasn’t great but it wasn’t terrible), but that the frequency drifted all over the place. As the radio would warm up, signals would move a few kHz. It has a front panel knob for “calibrating” the frequency display, but I learned that it really was a band-aid for the fact that the frequency display was useless.

 

It used a pulley-and-cable mechanism to tune the radio (and move the red cursor), but there was SO MUCH SLACK AND BACKLASH, that you had to tune past a station, then try to come back to it, gingerly. Also, the tuning ratio was so bad (a tiny movement of the big knob would move the frequency by a LOT), that the RIT was almost necessary just to tune a station in. 

 

Fortunately, this was back in the days when most people used a separate transmitter and receiver, so it really wasn’t critical if both stations in a QSO were on the same frequency. With this receiver, that was almost guaranteed. 

 

Remember back in the early novice days, when we were required to use crystal control, and everybody just had a bunch of crystals on random frequencies in the allowed band? It was standard practice to call CQ, then tune up and down, perhaps 10 kHz or more (oh, wait: back then it was 10 Kc), looking for someone calling you back. That’s part of the reason why it was “best practice” to call someone with their callsign THREE TIMES, then “de”, then your callsign TWO OR THREE TIMES. If you just gave your call, one time (like many do today), it’s likely that the CQ’ing station would not know you were calling him, and would probably not be fast enough to tune up and down to find you before you stopped sending. Sending the other station’s call 3x gave him time to find you.

 

My advice to new hams back then was to allocate your radio budget heavily towards the receiver, and whatever is left for the transmitter. “If you can’t hear ‘em, you can’t work ‘em.” With the SR-500 above, often “you can’t tune them in”. 

 

Rich KE1B
