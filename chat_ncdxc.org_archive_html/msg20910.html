Rich and NCDXC friends, 

I am finding LoTW adoption rates among FT4/FT8 users to be very high and I am receiving fewer Bureau cards, even from the Japanese. I am seeing new and long time DXers on the bands. Some of the "Never FT8" old timers are
now doing FT8 and having fun. Adoption of FT4 seems to be on the upswing. 

I have become a huge fan of LoTW and I upload almost daily and my confirmation rate is very high. I dislike dealing with Bureau cards and look forward
to a time when LoTW replaces Bureau cards. I still like to send/receive direct cards for truly rare DX. 

Paul N6PSE

On Tue, Sep 22, 2020 at 11:21 AM KE1B via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:
I just received my semi-regular huge pile of paper QSLs from the W1 Bureau. (Between ordinary operating, contesting, Anna’s operating, and our many, many DXpeditions, I get hundreds, sometimes thousands of cards each year.)

I have a QSL policy stated on my QRZ page that, for Bureau QSLs, I will send a return card ONLY IF the other station is requesting a QSL for a new band or mode. This avoids the problem when stations (particularly Japanese), send a bureau QSL for EVERY QSO. There are some stations (usually contesters) that I have worked 30-40 or more times on the same bands and modes (e.g., lots of 20m CW). I send them a card for the first time on a given band/mode, but not again, unless they are requesting a card for a new band/mode.

As a result, of the zillions of Japanese cards I receive each year, I normally don’t have to send many responses. Until FT8 came around.

Obviously, since FT8 (and FT4) are “new band/modes”, even for stations that I have worked dozens of times, I send them new cards in response. (That’s what I get for spending a late afternoon running JAs on FT8!) However, I have noticed a unique trend lately.

Many, if not most, of the FT8 QSLs I am receiving are from JA stations that I HAVE NEVER WORKED BEFORE, on any band or mode. Unlike other modes, I usually have a lot of QSOs with the same stations, especially contesters. But FT8 is either:

(1) Bringing out new hams who don’t (or can’t) operate CW/SSB/RTTY and are just getting on the air via FT8, or

(2) Allowing hams with very modest stations (low power, simple antennas, less-than-ideal locations) to now be heard via FT8, where they would not be heard otherwise.

Are others seeing this as well, i.e., lots of new “first time ever” contacts with stations because of the FT8 phenomenon?

Rich KE1B
