> The standards are still evolving.
> 
> The basic rule is, don't do fox/hound in the standard subband. 
> However, the other bands aren't yet well defined, which makes 
> which one to use hard to tell. Sometimes the DX's web page will 
> give a frequency.
> 
> When I see a bunch of signals on my waterfall synchronized with 
> the regular FT8 band, I assume someone is running fox/hound and 
> go looking for them. So far, they have always been at an even 
> multiple of 1 KHz, so this is fairly easy.
> 
> Note that the DX (fox) will always transmit in the even phase, 
> and the hounds always during the odd phase.
> 
> Remember that the DX will be sending in up to 5 60 Hz wide bands 
> starting at about 300. You start your contact by calling the 
> from above 1000 Hz, so the pileup will all be above 1000 Hz, 
> helping to find the base frequency the DX is using.
> 
> When you call them back, the fox/hound mode in wsjt-x will 
> automatically QSY you to their frequency to send your report. 
> Then the DX will send RR73, perhaps packaged with a report to a 
> different station.
> 
> My rule is, if you have sent a report, but not seen the RR73, 
> log the QSO manually, but try to make an insurance QSO later 
> unless your QSO shows up on some online log upload.
> 
> 
> Just to add to the confusion, there is another version of the 
> FT8 software that will do the same trick of joining the RR73 
> with a report to another station. It runs in normal FT8 subbands 
> and sometimes you will see the DX transmitting in the odd phase.
> 
> You use normal (non fox/hound) wsjt-x to contact these stations, 
> but annoyingly, wsjt-x does not recognize the RR73 sent with 
> another station's report in normal mode. I took a bunch of flack 
> on the developer list when I suggested it learn this trick. The 
> jury is still out. But do be prepared to select the next message 
> manually and send it.
> 
> The paving crews still haven't come through the FT8 version of 
> the wild west, so sometimes you have to carry the load and 
> bounce over the ruts.
