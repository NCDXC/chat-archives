On 4/15/2013 2:38 PM, Jack Haverty. wrote:

I'm not sure how flexible all the "knobby radios" are at this kind of
stuff, but the filter configurability is a characteristic I think
would be good for any comparison.   E.g., what can you do with Kx
filters?
Quite a lot. The K3 and KX3 are SDRs with knobs. There are two knobs on the front panel to set the IF filter the way you like it, and there are two ways to use the controls. One way is for one knob to be bandwidth and the other to be shift. Push in on one of those controls and now you can set the same filter by using the left knob for the low audio frequency limit and the right knob for the high frequency limit. Either way the filter can be set in 50 Hz increments. If you buy a roofing filter and plug it into the radio, you go to a menu and tell the K3 what bandwidth the filter is and it will automatically switch that filter in when you go narrower than that filter.
The DSP also provides a notch function, either manual or automatic, and there is are separate 8-band graphic equalizers for TX and RX.
DSP does LOTS of other stuff, including audio processing, careful waveshaping of the CW signal so there are no clicks, and so on.
The K3 and KX3 use menus mostly for configuration, and those menus are rarely needed while you're operating unless someone has screwed with your radio while you weren't looking. : ) There are two levels of menus. The top level has only a few functions, like VOX, switching between front panel and rear panel jacks for the mic, and the equalizers for TX and RX. With those exceptions, everything you need is on a knob or button. The low level menu enables optional stuff, like the second RX, transverters, etc, does the roofing filter setup, does various geeky things like AGC time constants and slopes.
Except for AF and RF gains, the knobs are all soft rotary converters, not pots -- they talk to the microprocessor, and most are multi-function. For example, the Mic Gain knob also sets the keyer speed. If you're running AFSK or PSK from your computer, the Mic Gain knob becomes the Line Input level. And nearly all of these multiple functions are clearly labeled on the panel in different colors.
The last rigs I owned before my K3s were FT1000MPs, which I used for a couple of years. I found the FT1000MP menus difficult to use and understand, and there's only one audio processing setting that doesn't sound truly awful (luckily the good one is very good). The K3s are MUCH simpler to operate, the menus, when you need them, are much clearer, and the K3s are much more flexible. I've never had the opportunity to operate an SDR without knobs.
