Ditto on everything Rick said. Consider Bouvet… nothing went as planned, and the DXp was terminated early. The first few days of a DXp are exciting, and not to be avoided. Some of my TX5S slots were after a few calls… others were hard fought, and frequently not realized. I got up at 4 AM this morning and called TX5S on 80 for almost an hour. I smiled when I heard fellow NCDXCers in the pile-up, and groaned (good naturedly, I hope) when Al N6TA got ‘em but I didn’t. It’s a cliché, but anything worth having is worth fighting for, and when I finally get that 80m CW spot, I’ll cherish it! Jump in there, listen to what (and where) successful callers are doing, and give it a try. No one can fault you unless you’re not split, or on the wrong cycle. By their nature, DXps are unpredictable, and you gotta join everyone else and rack up those slots before the tsunami or the Houthis strike (just joking, guys).

Hi Will,

Some do that, I never did even when I had a peanut station (100 watts and a crappy wire non-resonant antenna barely above dirt).  A minimal station is HARD WORK.  

But some tidbits to consider:

  • You learn valuable operating skills from the struggle/s along the way which pay dividends later; life skills too (persistence and patience being HIGH on that list).  [You just have to remember to use them later, even in a larger station or in real life.]
  • You can't work them if you don't call them.  [A resounding "DUH!" was heard throughout the realm.]
  • DXing, like contesting is not passive, it is an ACTIVE set of skills.  GET BUSY!
  • If you don't call them (WHEN YOU HEAR THEM), you deny your shot at radio magic.  "Stuff" happens, both good AND bad; so take your shot; you lose nothing but time for trying and
  • Sometimes, you win; even against 'the big kids'.  Save that memory for a lesser day (or later in life).
  • Tomorrow doesn't come with a guarantee, only a potential expiration date.  The same for the DX (team or solo).  Do it NOW.

GL ES 73,
Rick nk7i
