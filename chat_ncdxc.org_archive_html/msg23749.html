+ AA6YQ comments below

I resisted for a long time in trying this out.  There's probably more controversy over this with comments such as "is this real ham
radio" etc.  We do like new technologies and in this world of low sunspots it fits the bill.  

+ I like the combination of panoramic reception (like PSK31) with high sensitivity.


After using it for a bit I think my only objection is how it's in the same classification of RTTY.  I think it should be separate,
they are just not the same and the rewards of achieving RTTY contacts are devalued by those on FT8.  But then again, maybe RTTY will
be going away. 

+ There's been a desire for a RTTY-only DXCC award ever since PSK31 et al became available ~20 years ago. If you want this, let your
ARRL Director know.


 I also find it very impersonal especially in USA to USA contacts.  

+ Try JS8Call; it's a conversational mode that employs K1JT's weak signal protocol:

http://js8call.com/


So, a couple of observations from this neophyte on what I've observed in working the FT8 world although I'm sure there's a lot more
experience out there you may want to add:

1.	Getting Odd/Even Mixed up:  many times, I hear stations calling the DX on the same even or odd instead of the opposite.

+ In WSJT-X, click on an entry in the "Band Activity" panel in which the station you're seeking is transmitting, and WSJT-X will
choose the correct time-slot.

3.	Call w/o copying:  There are hams calling the DX who in no way can possibly copy them.

+ That happens in every mode.

4.	Finding the right frequency:  Don't select an open frequency on the odd/even the DX is on, make sure to pick one on the
opposite.  I've seen people call on the DX frequency while he was transmitting too.

+ When calling a DX station, I look for a frequency other than the one the DX station is using that is clear on both time slots.
That can be challenging when the band is full, but you can waste a lot of time calling on a frequency that is busy.

6.	Above 1KHz:  I like to keep my transmit above 1.5KHz as most of the DX seems to be below that;  not sure why.

+ Some DX stations use equipment with limited TX passbands.

7.	Missing the DX calling you:  Sometimes someone sits right on top of the DX, hopefully because they didn't hear them, washing
them out so you can't decode

+ That happens in every mode. A nearby strong signal can also desensitize your receiver; use your rig's notch filter, if it has one.

8.	The DX missing you:  similarly, sometimes the DX can't hear you because someone started transmitting on top of you

+ That happens in every mode. It probably happened with Spark.

it pays to check once in a while whether your frequency is still free.  If no success switch frequencies.

+ When you and several other stations are calling a DX station and the DX responds to someone other than you, use that time to check
your transmit frequency to be sure it's still clear, or at least that your time-slot is still clear.

9.	Receiving direction:  I've noticed that you can copy stations in a direction not normally thought of say on CW.  There may
be other directions too but I'm not talking long path either.  I'm saying something at 335 degrees may be similar to 35 degrees,
especially to the middle east.  So sometimes I'm confused as to where to point the antenna since I can hear from multiple
directions.

+ Point your antenna to the short path unless you're intentionally trying to use the long path or a skew path. Doing so will
maximize your SNR when receiving, and will maximize your transmitted signal strength at the DX. FT8 is so sensitive that you can
often work stations off the back or side of your antenna, but the short path heading will optimize your ability to make the QSO.

11.	Transmitting during QSOs:  One nice thing is that you can keep calling before the DX qso is done, he may see you and pick
you up without having to call CQ, besides everyone else is calling all the time too.

+ Many DX stations keep a list of callers and work through it, but not always in first-come-first-served order. If 3Y0DX calls me
while I'm working JAs, he'll be the next station I call!


12.	Not getting a 73:  A number of stations do not respond with a 73;  don't understand that so I wonder if they logged it (as I
did).  Even a recent DXpeditition didn't send 73.  So I don't if all the contacts are real, and whether they never copied me.
Sometimes I've tried a second contact though and did get a 73, but in observing them they sometimes didn't do it 50% of the time.
So not sure what gives.

+ Sometimes your QSO partner's 73 gets clobbered by another station; some stations will only repeat a 73 two or three times before
they give up and move on to another QSO. If WSJT-X prompts me to log the QSO, I consider it complete; otherwise, I keep trying. 

+ For those using DXLab, it can directly interoperate with WSJT-X, color-coding callsigns in the WSJT-X Band Activity panel to
indicate both "award need" (DXCC, IOTA, Marathon, VUCC, WAS, WAZ, WPX) and eQSL/LoTW participation, and populating SpotCollector
with the callsigns your instance(s) of WSJT-X have decoded so you that can identify both propagation openings and operating
patterns. See

https://www.dxlabsuite.com/dxlabwiki/GettingStartedwithK1JTModesDirect
