It depends on the modulation. Doesn’t it?

If you are sending out a nice AM carrier wave with good filtering and a low symbol rate it should pretty much stay where it belongs and no issue.

And if they have one system up, transmitting occasionally and they have full bandwidth to make it work and a nice signal – great.

But as soon as one guy does this, the rest will have to do it or they die because he is 1.5mSec ahead of them on every trade.  So now you have every flash trader of every stock, bond and commodity crammed wall-to-wall in this small set aside bandwidth.  How do they get heard?  Sharpen the modulation all the way to square waves, crank the symbol rate, over modulate and crank the power.  Think CB.  That will get through.  Think square waves at a megawatt.  Fun.  Oakland would be good DX from my house!

Once upon a time I was control op for the W6UE/R Caltech ARC repeater in Pasadena.  Ambassador college was there too.  They has a gardening department.  One of the gardeners knew a little bit about electronics.  He had an FM radio station he wanted to listen to as he drove around in his little work vehicle.  Not powerful enough.  So he went up on the roof of the gardening building, strung up a dipole antenna, hooked it to a CATV wide band amplifier putting out about 300W, hooked his radio into an FM modulator, hooked the whole thing up and Voila!  He could hear his station all the time loud and clear.  Great, right?  So could everyone else within 20 miles on every frequency from DC to light with a peak every 20KC.  Fortunately he did not include a 100HZ PL tone in that or he would have burned up our repeater before I caught him.  His boss was really nice – he took the stuff and tossed it in the trash can after I explained how the communications Act of 1934 only really provides for felony penalties.

 

Whatever “testing” they have done so far is a phantasm.  They are not going to receive exclusive rights to this – it will be shared in some manner – either voluntarily or involuntarily.    Evil people may even spread spectrum it (making it worse) and never get caught.  They won’t ID because that represents data (money) for which they won’t be paid.  Once it is working on HF they will move it other places as well.  They are mining for gold and gold miners and their ilk are well known to be the most environmentally careless people on the planet.  They filled the SF Bay with mercury and killed countless victims over the years just so they could make $100 Million.  How much would it cost to clean up?  We’ll never know because we can’t afford to do it and we just spent $10 Trillion on infrastructure improvement.

 

Seriously -- get out there – write some ideas that you agree with down as reasons why to either not permit this at all or to require that it be seriously licensed and regulated and filtered and file them.  Don’t all just let someone else do the work and copy their submission because then the FCC staff will simply toss them all out or lump them all together.  Make them read the damned things – only way to do that is if they are all a bit different.  I have worked in government and that is absolutely how it works.

 

The more diverse filings the FCC sees that oppose this with cogent arguments the less likely it is to be permitted to move forward. They will argue that the “test” was successful, hurt no one and that the thing has been proven.  You have to show why that is a load of BS and requires further real world testing.  The ARRL won’t ride in and save us on this.  If it passes it isn’t going to go away because the ARRL doesn’t have the money to fight this.

W6DR

Understand that this is just an effort to save a little time – a very little amount of time  - in doing something that is already done at nearly the speed of light using fiber (and switches/retransmitters) with NO effect on hams or anyone else.  They want to bypass the fiber and switch delay to save, perhaps, a couple of milliseconds and carry out flashier “flash trades”.  The cost will be large but their potential profit can be shown to be huge (coming entirely at the expense of the flash traders already on fiber).  I don’t see a reason to give up ham radio for this nonsense.  It only benefits one group at the expense of another and if it is NOT allowed no one suffers.  It is not allowed by ITU so it cannot be allowed by FCC without violating the ITU regs – or changing them.

 

A good way to block it would be to do what was required by the FCC for Digital television – a 50db BP filter proposed by industry which the FCC then “doubled” to 100db.  Yes they did that.  It is very effective – but it is also very expensive.  These filters are the size of a large bus and located inside skyscraper radio rooms.  For HF they might wind up being a bit larger.  Make them build a 100db bandpass filter to contain their signal and don’t let more than 0.001uW/meter appear within 10 MHz of the sides of that filter.  Technical solutions are always the best way to go.  They avoid all the unpleasantries of motivation and intent.  Build it so we can’t hear you if we try or don’t build it at all.

 

These guys should pay a neutral third party expert such as  NIST or AEROSPACE Corp to come up with a technical solution, test it, and prove that it will be environmentally stable over a WIDE range of weather and atmospheric conditions.

 

W6DR

Art,

 

Thank you for bringing this important matter to the attention of our club members and other amateurs. 

 

Here's what I sent, based on your suggestions - others may find it useful as a template to revise and add their own comments:

 

"I am writing in opposition to RM-11953. This would allow a consortium of wealthy investors to transmit very high power (more than 10 kilowatt) signals adjacent to the frequency bands used by amateur radio operators in the US and around the world to provide emergency communications and other volunteer services that are important to the public.

I request that additional time be allowed for detailed and thorough study of the impact of this proposed rule. There are likely other alternatives that the petitioners could use for their business needs that do not require high power utilization of the limited radio frequency spectrum. If spectrum use is granted, the power and spectral limits should not exceed those of the adjacent amateur radio frequency bands.

Thank you for your consideration in permitting ongoing access by amateur radio to our valuable frequency spectrum.

Respectfully,
Andrew F Calman
Amateur radio callsign K6ZP"
