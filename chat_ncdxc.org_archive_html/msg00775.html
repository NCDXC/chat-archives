OMs and YLs-
 
I’m one of those who do the mega-DXpeditions. In fact, those are the only ones that interest me, because I like the process of a long, hard project with a lot of problem solving along the way. My principal experience has been 3Y0PI, XR0Y/Z, VK0IR, XR0X, K7C, and now with Clipperton (TX5K, Mar. 2013) and Heard Island (Jan. 2014) in preparation. So for what it’s worth I can share what it’s like from the organizational/funding point of view.
 
For me, a central requirement for assembling a top-rate team is keeping the cost to the participant reasonable. How much is reasonable? For the BIG ONE, Heard Island, the participant cost probably will be about $20k.
 
The problem is that for the really big ones the transportation cost is huge—about $1 million for the vessel and helicopter to get to Heard Island. Add 25% for additional expenses and you have the rough budget: $1.25 million. So how many people on the team? If you think of 25 participants, the cost per person is $50k, which of course is unreasonable. Unfortunately, the entire amateur radio DXing community worldwide can supply only about $100k (short of major angels) for a big event, so the inevitable conclusion is that it can’t be financed. This is, in fact, probably why HI hasn’t been activated since VK0IR (1997), when expenses were much more reasonable ($10k/participant).
 
Faced with this apparently impossible situation, my strategy was the following: Expand the total team to 50, of which about HALF (25) would be radio ops, and the other half would be environmental scientists, volcano climbers, glaciologists, adventurers, and so on. For a total team of 50, the cost per person is $25k, of which perhaps $20k would be provided by the individual participants. A radio team of 20-25 is enough to activate VK0 over a 3-week period. The non-radio team would pay roughly half the transportation cost; it would find its support from non-radio sources. The facilities overhead would be shared between the radio and science teams, hence reducing the per-person cost. The radio operation would be a full-service, no-compromises DXpedition embedded within the larger project, the Heard Island Expedition. Said another way, the DXpedition would share the boat with the non-radio group, and together they would make the expedition possible. Neither can do it alone; together they can.
 
So the Plan for getting Heard Island on the air is: A total team of 50 (25 radio ops and 25 scientists), 3 weeks on HI, cost $20k/person, leaving the expedition to raise a total of $250k. Of that, the radio community would supply $100k, the non-radio community another $100k, and we will have to find the last part where we can. But since the expedition is going to be of interest to millions worldwide, and it will generate a lot of publicity, we can expect these other funds to appear.
 
I suspect that this multi-purpose approach is also relevant to other DX destinations in which regulations, rather than finances, are a limiting factor. For instance, on our Clipperton trip next March we will be doing a variety of scientific projects in addition to the radio operations. While not compromising the radio operations, the scientific component was enough to excite the interest of two French scientists, who will join us on the island, and a French TV crew, who will come with us and produce a film for the THALASSA series. Commercial sponsors will appear if we generate enough exposure.
 
In summary, I believe that the biggest, hardest, most expensive destinations can still be activated for the Deserving, but not as pure radio-only operations. Sharing the space will be good for us, but it’s going to require thinking outside the classical model of DXpeditions. Who wants to share the space with a geologist? A aerobiologist? An algologist? How about a cryptozoologist? A hyperthermal exobotanist? What about a hydromicrolimnologicalvirologist? If there’s a volcano on the island, we may need someone who knows about pneumonoultramicroscopicsilicovolcanoconiosis. Of course, if we go to Llanfairpwllgwyngyllgogerychwyrndrobwllllantysiliogogogoch, Wales, we probably should take a Gammaracanthuskytodermogammarus loricatobaicalensis-ologist! In any case, multi-disciplinaryism probably is going to be a good thing.
 
Merry Christmas!
 
Bob KK6EK
