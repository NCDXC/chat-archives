No and no.  I know that I am in the minority here but here goes.
 
In the first place to qualify many of these flea speck ‘islands’ as a country is ludicrous.  To be a country, it should be currently inhabited, namely by people, not just visiting radio operators.  That means homes, places of work and living.  Just because a reef is exposed at low tide does not make it a country.  This premise is also good for larger islands, reefs or rocky shoals; they should be human occupied to qualify as a country.  If a location is so extreme that no one lives there, it’s not a country.
 
In the places that are no longer human occupied, the hazardous wastes or leftovers (unexploded bombs) need to be safely neutralized or removed.  Then let the ‘island’ return to its natural state.  Mankind excels at destruction but nature returns it to a relative normalcy over time if left alone.  To leave hazards there is to light the fuse on a time bomb, inexcusable behavior for an intelligent being.
 
The wildlife sanctuaries are just that, sanctuaries.  The US F&W (and Park system) are mandated to properly manage them, which in most cases means simply to leave them alone, period; no access, let nature be nature.  Not only for the indigenous and migrating birds, but also for marine life such as coral, fishes or sea turtles which require safe harbor AND shores.  Many species are also cornerstone species, so in effect, if we help them, we are saving ourselves from destruction.
 
There should be some pristine places left on this planet that are completely untouched by humans.  There aren’t many such places.  It’s bad enough that the flotsam from ships, tsunamis and intentional dumping pollutes and clogs the ecosystem killing the wildlife in numerous remote places (and IS a solvable issue but is frightfully expensive) but to add human traffic in those sensitive areas with a high potential of destruction is unconscionable.  Consider the extremes that were taken on the recent Antarctic DXpedition (the island name eludes me, but required NO sign of having been there, on a rocky desolate island that is a breeding ground for penguins and more) and then factor in an even more delicate ecosystem and you can see my concerns.
 
It’s not worth the risks of further damaging the delicate balance just so someone can prove they sent a radio signal.  It is not worth the struggle and monetary effort and extremes just to add another QSL card to the collection.  Some things are simply more important.
 
Mankind is damaging enough; let’s give the critters a chance to survive.  There are plenty of other places we can talk to with our radios.  We don’t need to be invasive to every tiny land (or reef) mass, just to prove we’ve been there.  The risk of damage is just too high.
 
73,
Rick wa6nhc

Here is your NCDXC Question of the Week (QoTW)
 
If you look at the latest most wanted DXCC list, you will see that US Islands managed by the US Fish & Wildlife Service are rapidly rising to the top of the list.
 
For some time now, the US F&W Service has made it virtually impossible for DXpeditions to activate these remote and rare islands.
 
Question: Should hams actively lobby their politicians and government representatives to open access to these islands?
 
Should organizations such as NCDXF/INDEXA expand their role to include lobbying on behalf of the DX Community?
 
 
What are your thoughts?
