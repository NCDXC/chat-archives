Well put, John. If an argument can be made that there is some benefit -- of any sort -- to the hams' presence on the island, then getting approval to operate from these locations becomes arguable.

And this also plays to Rick's earlier comments.  I agree with him in that we shouldn't be launching DXpeditions in order to activate some non-existent country at the expense of other species and/or the local environment.  But if we can find ways to contribute to ecological efforts, then permitting a DXpedition to one of these islands becomes a win-win situation for both the hams and the US government.

This certainly isn't a novel idea.  One current example is the upcoming Clipperton 2013 DXpedition; they are planning a number of environmental projects along with their activation of the island.  They've partnered with a non-profit research group for the scientific portion of the trip.

Here is your NCDXC Question of the Week (QoTW)
 
If you look at the latest most wanted DXCC list, you will see that US Islands managed by the US Fish & Wildlife Service are rapidly rising to the top of the list.
 
For some time now, the US F&W Service has made it virtually impossible for DXpeditions to activate these remote and rare islands.
 
Question: Should hams actively lobby their politicians and government representatives to open access to these islands?
 
Should organizations such as NCDXF/INDEXA expand their role to include lobbying on behalf of the DX Community?
 
 
What are your thoughts?
