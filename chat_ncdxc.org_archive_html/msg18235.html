The issue in my case started because the tilt plate never left the boom on disassembly at the SK house and that once the tower mast is attached, the Sharpie mark made after manufacture, had long faded.  So placement of the catch plate, was a guess, then trial and error.

The physics is pretty cute (one has to think it through carefully), a dangling metal hook is held down by gravity.   The tip of the hook hangs below (and inside once raised) the opening of the loop (a stainless clamp), so any vertical rise (antenna twist) of the hook snags the loop, which limits the twist.  This keeps the antenna parallel to the ground when hit by winds.

When lowering the tower, the hook (by gravity) moves away from the loop and allows the antenna to remain parallel (more or less) to the ground so complete lowering to ground level doesn't drive the elements into the dirt/concrete/roof.  It beats the heck out of needing a crane to (un)install the antenna.  The user can then work on the antenna, on the ground, no tower climb required.

I would have added this system anyway because my tower climbing day, are DONE.  I'm not young enough to bounce off the ground anymore.

It's the spacing between loop and hook tip (1/8-1/2") when vertical, that I'm have to guess at, my bad for not making fresh marks on the plate when it was not attached to the mast.  20:20 hindsight and ignorance.  Since the antenna exceeds 200 lbs, guessing (or one tower climb by the neighbor to make a final adjustment) is simpler than muscling the antenna around to make a mark.  My last guess was about an inch too low.

The website says over 300 sold (when the site was updated last).  Al is VERY responsive in answering email or shipping parts.  But if that number is still accurate, it may be a challenge to find others using it (they're also not cheap, this one approaches $1k but are very strong and solid).

I would like to hear from other tilt plate customers,
satisfied or not.  In 2014, I had a very long and excellent
discussion at Dayton with NN4ZZ about tilt plates and the Karlock
in particular.  I never understood the theory of operation
previous to that, but NN4ZZ sounded convincing.  I have
never heard of problems with it until now.

I built a homebrew tilt plate for my Monstir before NN4ZZ
offered the big size.  I invented my own locking mechanism
that works by using a 20 foot pole to reach up and lock
and unlock it while the tower is cranked down.  Made out of
repurposed hardware store parts.  It is "positively" locked,
regardless of wind, etc.  The Karlock always seemed
to be "too clever by half".
