On 11/26/2012 6:31 AM, paul@n6pse.com wrote:
How was your experience working PT0S? 

Excellent. 

  Did you have fun working them?  

I found it quite easy on 160, 80, and 40, far more difficult on the higher bands. It helps that those are the bands for which I have the best antennas, but it also helped that I read their website to understand their operating plans, and that I put enough time on these bands when NOT chasing a DX trip to understand propagation.  By the time they went QRT, I'd worked them on 160 CW, 80 CW, 75 SSB, 40 CW, 20 SSB, 17 CW, and 10 SSB. 

  Are you satisfied with your results/number of contacts?

Given that there were a total of four guys operating under VERY difficult conditions from halfway around the world, I am VERY satisfied.  The fact that they stayed on the air, and not only managed to almost always have a good signal, but also to hear very well under those conditions is a tribute to their physical and mental stamina, and to the superb engineering of George Wallner, AA7JV. 

Running some quick statistics -- more than 40,000 Qs by four guys averages to 10,000 per man.  In my memory, the only trips that have equalled that are previous 2-man trips by George and Tomi. And while we don't have access to data on costs, I'd bet that their costs per QSO are far less than trips other than their own.

As to those QRXs that turned into QSYs or QRTs -- those complaining ought to got to pt0s.com, read the blog, and tell us how well they might have responded under those circumstances.  I know for damn sure that I could not have done nearly as well. This was no walk in the park -- these guys were continually being bombarded by mother nature and having to pause to react, just to stay on the rocks!  

As to the emphasis on JA that some have complained about -- PY0 is very difficult to work from JA, and #7 most wanted there, while it is far easier to work from EU and NA.  Those who seem to be complaining most loudly about that are certain to be those most likely to have benefitted from N6PSE's dedication to listen for W6 from very difficult DX QTHs. 

This has my vote for DXpedition of the year.

73, Jim K9YC
