Jim,

To me a keyer is a necessary DXing tool.  Although I have learned to
recognize my call at 30 WPM, I can't send it at anywhere near that speed.
Also when sitting in a pileup for a long time, it is much easier to hit "F1"
every time you want to send.  A keyer allows me to send my call with a
"perfect fist" which in my mind increases the likelihood that the DX will
copy it correctly.  It also allows me to easily match the sending speed of
the DX.  A keyer (I use the microKEYER) has allowed me to earn CW DXCC
without a "fist".  

I would be missing quite a few rare DX QSOs (including BS7) without my
trusty keyer.

73,
- Craig, AE6RR

On 1/22/2013 9:44 AM, John Miller wrote:
> The K1EL Winkeyer Kit.

Since this is a DX club, I'm hesitant to suggest gadgets primarily designed
for contesting.  If, however, we open the category to include contesting
gadgets, I'll second the K1EL keyer, which operates both as a stand-alone
keyer with memories, and also as a computer controlled keyer for
single-radio and SO2R contesting.

I'll also add

1) the wonderful TXBPF six-band bandpass filter kits designed and
distributed periodically by Bob Henderson, 5B4AGN, a Brit living in Cyprus.
This filter set, using a design based on W3NQN's classic QST article (back
when QST actually published useful and accurate technical content), provides
performance nearly as good as filters hand-built by W3NQN himself, at at a
fraction of the cost.  My kits cost me about $420 each, including a built-in
band decoder and a buffered relay driver for antenna switching relays. It
took me at least 40-50 hours to build and test the two boxes, and I learned
a LOT.

2) The YCCC SO2R box, also sold as a kit, which includes a K1EL-style keyer.
Cost about $200.

3) The Yamaha CM500 boom mic headset, which is very comfortable, even for
long haul use in contests, sounds great on receive, and with a mic that
produces excellent competitive audio. Cost only $45, blows away "better
sound through marketing" products like Heil. Yes, cables and break and fail
-- one of mine did after three years of VERY hard use, but you can buy
CM500s for the cost of one Heil, and the CM500 sounds better on both TX and
RX and is much more comfortable!

4) If you run digital modes (RTTY, PSK, JT65, etc.) a good low cost stereo
USB audio interface from one of the established audio companies.  
Two such products are the Numark stereo interface (no model number, costs
about $35 at B&H Photo) and the Tascam US100 (I think that's the model
number), cost about $75. These interfaces are at least twice as good
decoding RTTY and JT65 as the standard sound cards built into my Thinkpads.

5) DXKeeper -- the champ of DX logging software, keeps track of awards,
prepares submittals for awards, imports logs from contest loggers, uploads
to and downloads from both LOTW and eQSL almost automatically. 
DXKeeper is part of FREE suite of ham software. I use two of the other
programs -- DXView to provide azimuth and distance headings once a call (or
prefix) is entered, and Commander to read frequency and mode from my radio
and plug it into DXKeeper.  Both programs can do a lot more, but those are
the features I use.

6) The VE7CC cluster software for users. Easy to use filters by band,
country (of both spotter and spot), even band segment, the display can be
color-coded by band, and can be customized for what info is provided
(including whether the spotted station uses LOTW and/or eQSL).  Free, google
on ve7cc to find it.

7) The new Elecraft KAT500 automatic antenna tuner. Three switched outputs,
rated 600W for SWR up to about 15:1, and legal limit for SWR less than about
3:1.  I'm using KAT500s with my legal limit Titan amps.  
$699, $50 cheaper as a kit that's easy to build. Good for 500W on 6M.

73, Jim K9YC
