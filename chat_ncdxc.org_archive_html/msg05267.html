I have been successful with the Old School method, either just parking on a frequency or listening for a 599 exchange and tail-ending.  It works, but it usually requires more time and patience (in a pileup like those for FT5, a LOT more). This approach has its limits, especially if you, like me, live in a high QRN area, or have the auditory acuity of a snail.

 

I have been running LP-PAN & CW Skimmer on my K3 for the past few months, and am still getting the hang of it.  In modest pileups, I have found it allows me to jump in with just one or two calls, tail-ending the last flagged "599," and snag my prey - including FT5ZM. With practice, you can also learn to read the waterfall, and begin to see familiar patterns - like a callsign + report + TU exchange, even when skimmer doesn't flag the "599." 

 

I am still too inexperienced (or slow) to be able to "lead the bird" - watching for DX Op patterns and anticipating their next move.  I am too easily caught up in the chase to get ahead of the curve.

 

I have found LP-PAN & CW Skimmer to be extremely effective tools for those running less than 500 Watts with a modest antenna setup. When the pileups get really, really ugly its effectiveness drops and you start to feel the limitations of limited screen "real-estate," slowing CPU's, limited RAM ... and declining reflexes. But this setup has helped me to jump from 20 confirmed DXCC to 110 confirmed in just the past three months.

 

Sorry for publishing an essay here ...

My experience tacks Steve and Mike’s comments.  I put the TS-990S pan-adapter on the Sub receiver and watch to see if I can figure out what is happening.  Sometimes it simply doesn’t work but often it does.  When I see (and hear) the station being worked I either tailgate if the OP is “sucking” contacts there or try to identify a pattern – visually.  Not always easy.

There are some world renowned ops where I know exactly how they work - and typically do this:

1) Move up slightly - they obvious are using a very narrow CW filter
2) ID either once a call - or every two calls
3) Go up to some high edge of the range
4) Crawl back down slowly again
5) Every now and then "reset" back to the bottom of the range

SOme will actually say QRX and when they come back - they are back at the bottom no matter where they left off.

This "reset" happens a lot with really big pileups.

WHen an op jumps around a lot - I just park it some where.

I am still a lot faster by tuning than looking at the P3.

Now there is one use of the P3 that is absolutely the BEST THING SINCE SLICED BREAD:

On 160M, where I have several noise sources - and have two RX antennas phased through an MFJ-1025, the P3 shows an amazing signal to noise waveform - where I know that I have dropped the noise - and yet there is still enough signal to hear the typical 160M (weak) signal. That alone makes the P3 VERY much worth the price. For DX-ing in a pileup? No - not nearly as high an ROI.

73,

Rich
KY6R
