Paul and All,

 

               Having spent a good part of my time trying trademark cases for the past 40 years, there are some things about trademarks that may help in evaluating the issues raised by Paul’s blog article:

 

1.      Trademarks are source-identifiers—they tell you who or what is the source of the product or service, even if you may not know the name of the source

2.      One can obtain a federally-registered trademark if they establish to the PTO’s satisfaction that the mark is source-identifying and that the applicant is the source

3.      Perhaps most importantly, trademarks are not like patents.  A patents give the owner the right to exclude completely others from practicing what is disclosed in the claims of  patent.  Trademarks, however, give the owner the right to exclude others from using the mark only if the junior use is likely to lead to confusion as to source, sponsorship or affiliation.  There are a number of factors the courts us in evaluating likelihood of confusion, but a big one is the relatedness of the products or services on which the mark is used.  For example, I could not use APPLE on computer keyboards but probably could use the mark on, say, furniture.  (There’s a related doctrine that protects truly famous marks from even unrelated uses).

 

I’m surprised that the PTO issued registrations for DX DINNER and DXPEDITION OF THE

YEAR.  When I get back from some appointments today, I’ll get on the PTO’s TESS database and look at the file wrappers for the two registrations you mentioned.  Even so, anyone else could use DX DINNER unless the owner could establish that the use of that mark, standing alone, is likely to make folks think that the junior user’s product or service is somehow related to or affiliated with the mark owner.  For example, I think NCDXC and SCDXC run no serious risk in using the term DX DINNER to describe the Friday evening event at Visalia.

 

               73 Kip W6SZN

John, I don’t believe that I am harsh or unfair to SWODXA. Up until recently, I was a dues paying member. I have insight into how they operate and how they think.

 

You can search on any trademark at this website and determine who owns it: http://www.uspto.gov/trademarks/

 

I believe it is just a matter of time that SWODXA will start enforcing their trademarks and forcing DX-World, NCDXC and others to use a different term for their DXpedition award.  Otherwise why would they go to the bother (and cost) to trademark these terms?

 

How can any good come from this club’s trademarking of terms that we use and enjoy in our hobby? Trademarks were intended to protect the names of companies, products and services. Trademarking a common term within a niche hobby is not what trademarks were intended to protect.

 

We all have our own opinions, and now you’ve read mine.
