A few additional words for the list:

Historical--official DX awards date to at least 1935, and chasing DX long prior to an award, and the DX entities we want to activate are part of the natural world we both want to preserve

Cultural--amateur radio is a hobby/passion that helped drive advances that lead to our modern age

Stewardship--Parks and the USFWS and Amateur Radio want to preserve the natural resources of these islands and maintain our radio and other natural heritage

Loss--gains are few, losses many.

Conservation--what has the Amateur Radio community done to protect natural resources, for activations and separately?  Why should agencies consider AR a trusted partner?

Comfort level--how do we build partnerships?

Bird Strike--how to assure safety of wildlife and birds.

The most effective organizational change in open organizations may be top down -and- bottom up.  There are many partners who must accept the direction, not just one management chain.  Environmental management is much like Amateur Radio management.

Rich

On Jan 7, 2013, at 7:50 AM, <paul@n6pse.com> <paul@n6pse.com> wrote:

Here is your NCDXC Question of the Week (QoTW)
 
If you look at the latest most wanted DXCC list, you will see that US Islands managed by the US Fish & Wildlife Service are rapidly rising to the top of the list.
 
For some time now, the US F&W Service has made it virtually impossible for DXpeditions to activate these remote and rare islands.
 
Question: Should hams actively lobby their politicians and government representatives to open access to these islands?
 
Should organizations such as NCDXF/INDEXA expand their role to include lobbying on behalf of the DX Community?
 
 
What are your thoughts?
