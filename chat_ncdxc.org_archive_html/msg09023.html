On Tuesday, January 27, 2015, PETER GAMBEE via Chat <chat@ncdxc.org> wrote:
Well, EP6T is now in the history books.  My results were disappointing - not a single Q, despite spending my early mornings staring at my panadaptor screen straining to spot even the trace of signal on 14.033.  Seemed the only times I had a decent copy on them, they were calling for JA’s.  Ugh.  Looking at comments from many operators on the spot clusters, Im pretty sure that this was a common experience for many hams around the globe.  I’ve noticed that the general theme of the “flame” posts is to blame the expedition team for either a) operational incompetence, or b) nationalistic bias - i.e., ‘it’s a European team, so they are only looking for Europeans’, etc. - 

It is a pretty safe bet that this kind of misplaced (idiotic conspiracy theory) anger fuels a good deal of the intentional QRM that plagues major expeditions.  There has been a great deal of discussion about this among hams on-line, in print and in convention presentations.  Much of this discussion is aimed at potential expedition teams and their operating practices, but I’d like to throw out an idea for an upcoming expedition team to consider:  A video documentary of a major DXpedition, including coverage of some of the mundane but important aspects of planning, funding, sponsorship, logistics, setup - and of course the on-air marathon, to get a feel for the sleep deprivation, stress, chaos and hard work on-the-air.  

This documentary could be distributed to radio clubs around the country, put on-line via the ARRL and others, and presented at regional hamventions.  It might give the rank-and-file ham community a better feel for all aspects of a major expedition, and help modify at least some of the negative behavior on-the-air.

The gear necessary to record and edit a video like this is small and light enough to be carried in a small camera bag, reasonably inexpensive, and might even offer an additional potential corporate sponsor.  It would only require that a member of the team be dedicated as camera op, and that someone with experience in video editing handle the post-production.  Granted, these aren’t small items, but might be worth thinking about.  Just food-for-thought.


73

- Peter Gambee
K6TTD

Peter,

As one who has been on both sides of the pile, I agree with your comments. This DXped offered the possibility of not only an ATNO  for newer DXers climbing the ladder, but an island group for IOTA, a new mode and/or a new band. Given that EP is not daily fare, many were looking forward to it. Too many, as it turns out.

For me, none of these possibilities have been realized. Because the path to Kish I. is particularly challenging for W6/W7, success has been mostly achieved by those best situated and equipped, as always, but that success has eluded many, if not most of the rest of us. I myself am blocked on long path (strike one) which has been a big negative, as has been having no 40m antenna capable of gain over the poles (strike two), meaning shorter, weaker openings. Then there is local noise and QRM in this age of switching power supplies (strike three). Add to that the troubles on the DXped side (noise, power issues, etc.) and you have the perfect storm. The team has, I am sure, done its best.

Most of us have less than perfect locations, and when the imperfections of small lots, noise,  unfortunate topography and/or other commitments (like a job or family) combine, success may be beyond reach and adding this counter may be something for the next solar cycle. The fact that, elsewhere, DXers are logging a dozen band-modes or more just turns the knife, so to speak, but it will at some point be their fate to encounter similar problems with other rare counters that are easy for us.

Alas, you can't win them all! But that makes success, when it comes, even more sweet.

Garry, NI6T
On 1/25/2015 5:00 PM, PETER GAMBEE via Chat wrote:
Don’t know about y’all, but EP6T has been a real tough one for me.  The only copy I’ve been able to get has been just above WSPR levels, and has tended to vanish without a trace before I could make contact.  
 
I have heard that the team has extremely high local QRM (S9+) from a nearby business that refuses to cooperate with the team.  They are only able to copy sigs that are 599 or better.  Also heard that their own power source (don’t know if it is local mains) is very unreliable, and has been dumping on them periodically, which accounts for the sudden QRT, often on all bands at once.  Add all of this to a declining solar flux, an extremely rare DXCC entity, AND lousy paths from Western North America at present, and you get the radio equivalent of the Great Wall of China for those of us in California without a full gallon and stacked mono-banders at 100’.  I knew this would be tough one, but this is making Amsterdam Island look like a cake-walk-in-the-park.  
 
I will be interested to read the after-action accounts in QST and elsewhere after the dust settles.  Given the flame and carp that I have been seeing on the clusters, I really feel bad for this group.  They have put a great deal of time, money and effort into this venture and it seems many hams just want to throw rocks at them.  What a shame.  I think far too many DX hunters around the globe have no idea what it is like to be on the receiving end of their calls, or their jeers.  Too bad some of these folks can’t do a “ride along” on a DXpedition to get a sense of what it really takes to stage something like this for THEIR benefit.  
 
- Peter Gambee
