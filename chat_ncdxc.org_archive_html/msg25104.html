FWIW here is what I do…

 

I am calling CQ

I get a call from someone – no contact yet

I send a report – co contact yet

I get a “REPORT” back – contact is logable

I will go through the motions of sending and receiving the ’73 but it is in the log

 

He is calling CQ

I send my call – no contact yet

He sends me a report – no contact yet

I send my report – contact is logable

It may go thru – it may not – I will continue to try to complete it properly but I have no control over the – shall I call them clowns? – who step on everyone’s frequency in the middle of an FT8 QSO.

 

At that point it is up to the other guy – some people clearly state on their QRZ page that under no circumstances is it a QSO until they have received multiple ‘73s – ok – fine – but that is not what I do – I really don’t care at all.  I will make the effort to play along but I am not going to spend 30 minutes trying to get a second ’73 out of someone once their signal has disappeared from the waterfall.

 

One thing to note – often you will get a call from someone – one call – and then they go silent.  I believe that most of that activity is due to someone trying to click on someone they really want to communicate with, but then the display scrolls and they click on you, then they realize the mistake and correct it leaving you sitting there wondering what happened.  But once they send a report I see it as being invested in the QSO.

 

In a VHF contest the QSO is complete when I know his call and grid and he knows my call and grid.  Done.  No 73s or 599s or anything.

 

At the end of the day it is up to the operator – but if you are going to have complicated moralistic rules on what is and what is not a QSO it is probably best to put it on your QRZ page so the other guy has fair notice and can at least try to meet you half way.

 

End of diatribe.

Hi John,

It's been a while.  We mostly agree on this question.

Even with 'loud' signals, sometimes there is a failure to decode.  That may happen on the RR73 of course.  It may have been sent but by not repeating it, the DX keeps the rate higher.  If your R-xx isn't seen, they send you a report again and you KNOW they didn't get your report.

The Rxx report is the ONLY time your signal report is seen by the other station in an FT-x exchange.  Any lack of completion response, is subject to question; did they get the report, quit or just move on?  (DXer purgatory)

I consider the RR73 equal to the CW "TU" or similar in phone, it means the reports have been correctly exchanged, completing the minimal contact.  RR73 (or RRR) does that in JT modes; it's more than a courtesy, it's closure.

Now on rarer DX, failure to decode RR73 is logged (as you do) in the hopeful category (similar in other modes too).  Sometimes you win, sometimes the band conditions do.  There is no clean answer, some is operator 'style'.

It doesn't mean that I don't try for an insurance Q either.  😉  After the LOTW 15 minute window, if conditions allow.

From what I've read of EME contacts; there is a LOT of presumption, guesswork and hope involved in confirmations.  Some border on 'dodgy' or questionable.

Instead of (or in addition to) screen captures, I suggest using the log that WSJT-X maintains in it's internal folders (File | Open directory folder).  EVERYTHING you hear and send, is in that (bloody awful HUGE) ALL.txt file (from memory).  You'll have to use Wordpad (in Windows) or something other than notepad (it'll gag) and it's not a fast search.

I've had to do that to add or deny confirmations on DX requests.  It's tedious at best but useful.

Good to see you again!
