CQWW 2013 was incredible!   Band conditions were better than any in recent memory. I focused 99% of my own attention on working 10 meters and it seems like the majority of contesters did as well. It was so exciting to find great propagation on a global scale all at the same time!  I was working PJ2’s in the Caribbean and then working JA’s, JT1’s and BY’s in the opposite direction all at the same time.
 
I heard some incredibly good and skillful operating and unfortunately some really rude and quite bad operating. I was really impressed with the skill and finesse of K6TU and K6XN.  I was in many pile ups with Stu and Ted throughout the weekend. They got in, they made their call, usually getting the DX on their first call and they were done. They were like highly skilled surgeons hitting their target and quickly moving on. I heard them over and over and was quite impressed.
 
On the flip side, interestingly, two of the rudest, worst operators I heard this weekend both reside in Oregon. One of them is famous for writing books on how to operate. When 7O2A went QRV this station kept constantly calling him no matter who the 7O replied to. After about thirty minutes of constant calling this operator finally got his contact and will retain his place on the #1 Honor Roll.  During Saturday, 7O2A was quite active and I was in the pile up. 7O2A came back “Who is the November Six?”  This Oregon station with a W2 call kept calling even though the 7O was clearly trying to work the N6.
 
I noticed a lot of erroneous posts on the DX Clusters. I saw many posts for an AP5. When I went to listen for him, I heard a very strong 8P5 on the frequency.  I bet there are going to be many guys who worked the 8P5 that thought they got an AP5 in Pakistan.
 
Fortunately, most operators were quite good and seemed conscientious and considerate.  The only country I had difficulty working on ten meters was Canada! I worked the whole world on ten meters but anytime I tried to work a VE1-VE7 I just could not hear them and they could not hear me. My guess is that we are just too close to each other. No problem working Mexico or other close in countries. Probably the easiest contact I made was with RI1ANU at the South Pole on ten meters.
 
All in all, a terrific weekend for Amateur Radio!
 
Paul N6PSE

I was listening to 7O2A on 20M SSB at the end of the contest. The pileup was the usual – and I hadn’t even bothered jumping in except for maybe a call every once in a while. Tuner uppers, people making commentaries, one guy asking for RTTY, constant callers, etc, etc. I was happy that I already had Yemen – that’s what crossed my mind.

 

Right at 0000z everyone just stopped calling him – thinking the contest was over. I waited just a bit and in the clear called him – and he came back to me. Without all of the “din” of the contest, I could hear him and he could hear me in a way that just wouldn’t have happened in the contest. I’ve got my fingers crossed that it wasn’t some wise guy impersonating him, but then it sounded legit – when he said he had to take down the antennas.

 

WOW. It sure does pay to listen, listen and listen. And be very selective when you transmit.

 

I remember working P5/4L4FN the exact same way on multiple bands – but on a published frequency that he was known to appear on, and before the pileup went nuts. It doesn’t always go this way – most of the time it’s just a slog in the pileups and that I get through once the herd of big guns has had their fun and the pileups thin out a bit. Patience. That’s the key.

 

73,

 

Rich
