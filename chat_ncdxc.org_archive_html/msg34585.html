It is more convenient, if the remote app runs on every platform that the user has at their fingertips.  Just food for thought, look at the larger picture.

I use linux, Windoze and iOS.  TeamViewer is available on each (and Android I presume) so I can use whatever device is convenient when I’m not in the station (another room, another state or country).  Other apps can do similar, but not all run on most platforms.  Flex for example, makes it pretty simple (just throw more money at it).

For example, I used an iPhone on a local RV park wifi, linked to my home station, and no one would know I was thousands of miles away, unless I told them.  As it should be.  They got the hint when I mentioned I was watching the sunrise alpenglow making the glaciers pink, on the Cook Inlet volcanoes and the moose that was walking through the camp while I was sipping my morning coffee …

It wasn’t Kansas Dorothy.  And it was fun.

That was also in the days of marginal 3G cell, which brings up a key point:
You’ll have to figure out how to make your station link, as solid as you can if on cell or wifi while remote; then modify your operating style so you don’t (can’t) leave your transmitter keyed for example.  You’ll want more than a simple audio link with VOX on, you want to see everything that is in play at your station or you put your license at risk or you could melt the transmitter.

You want FULL control of the station.  Many newer apps even give you panadapter display (HUGE advantage for remote,  CW/RTTY in particular). 

And you’ll want to tame latency, for CW at least.  (I use the rig memory keyer.)

Don’t forget your logbook access.  It doesn’t matter where YOU are, you log based on the transmitter location.

Bigger picture view…

What operating systems (client and remote host)? I’m generally very happy with Microsoft Remote Desktop (RDP protocol, mstsc client). They’ve put a lot of effort into optimizing it over the years and it keeps up with all the advances in Windows – multi-monitor, multiple different dpis, etc. There are some tricks to get it to do more advanced things (e.g. registry edits to be able redirect USB devices), but it’s generally solid. It also has the advantage of being built into Windows, so nothing to download or install.

 

For remote audio though, especially with a laggy connection, you’ll want something UDP-based instead of TCP, so I use Sonobus for audio, with RDP for everything else.
