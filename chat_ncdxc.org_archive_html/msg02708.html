It’s not simply a matter of “bending to the pressure” to change their entity admission process to allow Kosovo, and it’s not simply a matter of facilitating Ham Radio in Kosovo. (DO you really think the Kosovo hams would continue to use the YU8 prefix?) The DXCC criteria as adopted by the Board in 1998 provided more diversity in criteria than has existed since 2004. For a reason not related to the DXCC country criteria, the Board was “urged” -- and that’s an understatement – to remove the so-called IARU rule that allowed the DXCC program to recognize entities that were represented by an IARU society as Political Entities. It can be argued that this criteria left something to be desired, but the then President of the IARU approved, as he has since. That wasn’t the reason for its removal.

 

Since the demise of the IARU rule, DXCC criteria has been a hostage of the United Nations. If the U.N. refuses to recognize an entity as a member state to the extent that even the International Telecommunications Union (a United Nations agency) cannot issue telecommunications (radio, wireless and landline) prefix blocks an entity cannot become a DXCC entity. (Rule 1C doesn’t apply in these cases.) Such recognition requires approval (or at least not rejection) by the Security Council, the permanent members of which have a veto.

 

This is the unfortunate situation in which Kosovo finds itself. For at least five years, Kosovo has been forced to use a telephone country code belonging to another, small European country. It has been left in sort of a communications limbo. All of this is pending on-going negotiations aimed at a settlement that will allow Serbia to join the EU and relations to (technically) normalize between Serbia and Kosovo.

 

However, whether Kosovo is admitted as a DXCC country is not even the most important issue. Of far more importance is what happened to the DXCC rules in 2004. The process was forced after considerable argument. There was little consideration of the impact on the DXCC program. It was political, and in fact, Swains Island and the “new” DXCC rule in 2006 that facilitated it was a direct result, but that’s another story.

 

What is even more important is that the DXCC criteria was hobbled by the narrowing of the DXCC criteria. The DXCC program no longer has the ability to respond to similar situations in the world that are yet to come. Over 100 countries have recognized Kosovo. Almost ALL of the countries that do not recognize Kosovo do not because they have situations with discontented regions with populations that could consider a path similar to that taken by Kosovo. The UN and much of the world tried to discourage Kosovo from declaring a unilateral independence just for this reason. Many countries felt that it would set a dangerous precedent. It may have.

 

The question really should be: Should the ARRL begin immediately to study the DXCC criteria in view of the current world situation? Should ARRL change the DXCC criteria to be ready the next Kosovo? Or should it wait for eight years?
