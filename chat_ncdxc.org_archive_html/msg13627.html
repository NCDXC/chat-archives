The entire purpose of the form (typically wood) is to direct and hold the liquid concrete in the proper position until it hardens enough to stay there of its own accord.

As long as the final dimensions and total amount of concrete is within specified limits, I wouldn’t worry.

NOW, the MUCH MORE IMPORTANT element is the reinforcing material (rebar cage) that gets built inside the hole before the concrete is poured in. The strength and resiliency of the base will depend critically on the amount and arrangement of the rebar cage. Little things count here, e.g., one NEVER welds crosspieces in a rebar cage, you simply twist-tie them together with wire. That allows the cage to give and move without breaking; a necessary ability when the wind is whipping that long “moment-arm” of the tower with the big weight (antenna) at the far end.

Be sure that the rebar cage is built to spec, and that the mounts (typically big J-hooks or the like) for the tower base are EXACTLY right. This is especially important in a free-standing (no guys) tower, where ALL of the stresses must be absorbed in the base.

>From what I see in the Tashjian catalog, your tower requires a (minimum) 3.5’ W x 3.5’ L x 7.5’ D base (a little over 3 cubic yards of concrete) with a very specific rebar cage. To me, even that sounds a bit on the small side. For example, my US Tower 55’ crank-up (NON-heavy duty) has a 5.5’ x 5.5’ x 6.5’ base (about 7 cubic yards), and over 250 lbs of rebar in the cage. It has operated flawlessly since construction in 2008.

Again, the wooden forms are almost irrelevant. What is important is the concrete mass and shape (bigger is better), and the rebar cage (at LEAST what the manufacturer specs, and maybe more). Don’t pour the concrete until that cage is correct; you can’t undo it later.

Also, it’s a good idea to “vibrate” the concrete as it is being poured, to avoid any air bubbles from forming internally. There is a specific tool for this; it looks like a dildo for King Kong’s girlfriend.
