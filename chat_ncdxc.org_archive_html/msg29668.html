The COVID pandemic sure put a damper on our many Holiday DXpedition plans for the past few years, but Anna and I finally went back out, and returned to Anguilla for the fifth time, renewing our VP2EAQ and VP2EAR callsigns. As usual, we combined Caribbean beach fun, food, and relaxation with some radio operations.

My DXpedition gear was a K3S, running barefoot into a Buddipole. I generally configure the antenna as a vertical with a single counterpoise (an L-shaped dipole). Our location allowed me to position the antenna so that the horizontal counterpoise was literally over the edge of the Caribbean Sea, about 15-20 feet above sea level. That salt water makes an amazing difference. Setting the lengths of the vertical properly, it provided a perfect impedance match (1:1 SWR) across the entire band, for 10/12/15/17/20 meters, and just slightly higher on 30 meters (about 1.5:1 SWR). The radiation angle tends to be quite low for a vertical over salt water, making DX contacts quite easy.

The “shack” was a room in our rented villa that was intended as a game room (with a pool table, etc.), but it was just a few feet from the guard rail where I had mounted the Buddipole mast, so the total cable length from the radio to the antenna feedpoint was about 30 feet total, minimizing cable loss. We had no radio, antenna, or computer problems during the entire trip. (I was amazed at the Internet speeds we had available on a tiny island like Anguilla. I measured about 200 Mb/s download, and 100 Mb/s upload; at home I only get 9 Mb/s bidirectional!) So we had full cluster access, plus the ability to upload logs to Clublog, etc.

In past trips to Anguilla, we have seen almost equal propagation to EU and NA, and of course easy shots to SA. Not so this time. For some reason, there was very little propagation to North America and ABSOLUTELY NOTHING to the West Coast. (I thought I finally got California when I worked Jack Brindle W6FB, the former president of NCCC, but then remembered that he moved to Louisiana!) I’d be interested to hear if we were spotted on the West Coast, and if anyone tried to call us.

We made just over 2200 QSOs, with about 1000 of those in the CQ WW WPX CW Contest on May 27-28. Here’s the breakdown:

CW: 1629 Qs (73.8%); not surprising, with over 1000 of those in the contest

SSB: 186 Qs (8.4%)

FT4: 228 Qs (10.3%)

FT8: 166 Qs (7.5%)

And by band:

10m: 216 Qs (9.8%)

12m: 358 Qs (16.2%)

15m: 1227 Qs (55.5%)

17m: 108 Qs (4.9%)

20m: 266 Qs (12.0%)

30m: 34 Qs (1.5%)

Obviously, 15m was the “money band”, particularly during the contest. When not in the contest, we specifically tried to focus on 12/17/30m, since a lot of folks need those band/mode slots. 12 meters was particularly good. 

And by region:

SA and Caribbean: 98 Qs (4.4%), including one QSO with another Anguillan (my friend Teddy, VP2ETE)

Asia: 23 Qs (1.0%), with only 3 JAs!

Australia/New Zealand/Oceania: 30 Qs (1.4%), with 27 of 30 being Indonesia!

Africa/Atlantic Islands: 11 Qs (0.5%)

NA: 359 Qs (16.3%), almost completely East Coast and Deep South

EU and Middle East: 1688 Qs (76.4%)

What is it with Indonesia?? I have never seen so many, at all times of the day. And of the 27 Indonesian QSOs, there were 21 unique callsigns. I think there has been a resurgence of ham radio there. I particularly note that most of those QSOs were FT4/FT8, which is the easiest mode for DX’ing by new hams. Only 2 Indonesian QSOs were CW, and both were in the WPX contest.

With all of our travels to Anguilla, we now have a combined total of over 18,000 QSOs from there, the most of any of our DXpedition destinations.

The Anguillan weather was, as always, consistently pleasant. It rains almost every night, but it dries out as soon as the sun comes up and rarely rains during the day. Winds are almost constant, especially where we were, on the south side of the island. (We had a nice view of Sint Maarten from the back yard, just a few miles away.) Between the wind, rain, and salt spray, the antenna parts started corroding a bit by the end of the week, but didn’t cause any operational problems. I’ll take everything apart, and clean/lube all of the Buddipole parts before our next trip.

Of course, the food and beaches were spectacular, as always. Anguilla is famous for their crayfish, which are like junior-sized lobsters. I think Anna had it for dinner 3-4 times, and I had it once, along with Anguillan lobster, conch, and other seafood. Anguilla is unlike many of the other, more popular Caribbean islands; there are no cruise ships, no high-rise hotels, no casinos, and no high-end shopping, but there are beautiful villas for rent, a few excellent resorts, and a plethora of amazingly great places to eat.

A few years ago, the Caribbean was devastated with hurricanes, and many islands had severe damage to both structures and flora. (Barbuda was almost completely destroyed.) One side effect was that iguanas, which are not native to Anguilla (but are present on some other islands) were washed ashore from the storms. Now there are iguanas EVERYWHERE on Anguilla. A family of them took up residence in the backyard of the home we were renting.

I’m sorry we weren’t able to work any of our West Coast friends due to the bad propagation, nor could we hand out the VP2 prefix multiplier for the contest. But it was nice to travel back to our favorite island, and play a little radio.

The QSL requests are already piling in …

73 de Rich and Anna

VP2EAQ/VP2EAR
