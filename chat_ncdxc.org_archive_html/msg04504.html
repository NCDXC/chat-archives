I have seen the same thing on 40M going from 40' to 70' was THE difference
between being able to work SV2ASP/A or not even hearing him. Even at 70' -
the QSO was totally ESP - but possible. 

Using EZNec with the trace option is illuminating. I also did what you have
done in your presentations - and compared phased verticals with a dipole up
70'. The dipole wins on gain - the phased verticals on F/B (hi hi).

Finally, I modeled a 2 element 40M wire yagi using 20 and 22' spacing, equal
sized elements and length of each element between 65 - 70'. The feed is two
5/8 wl coax stubs - and using a stackmatch II switch. I am so impressed, I
will try this. And the antenna would "play well" on 30, 20, 17 and 15M (I
think the impedance gets a bit too high on 20M - but not terrible on the
other bands.

I can get away with a NE - SW orientation with this antenna since my only
goal is to just stay on Honor Roll. I have concluded that HR #1 is something
that may or may not happen - and I don't care either way. Most of what I
need is NE - SW.

Subject: [NCDXC Chat] Height of an 80M Dipole - Figure of Merit

I've recently been studying the effect of height on the DX performance of
horizontal 80M antennas. I modeled a simple half-wave dipole over two kinds
of soil -- "Sandy" and "Average," that are pretty much the range of what
most of us have -- and at heights from 40 ft to 130 ft in 10 ft steps. For
"Sandy" soil, every 10 ft of height is worth about 1 dB below
70 ft, and 0.9 dB per 10 ft between 70 ft and 130 ft. For the better
"Average" soil, the numbers are 5-10% larger.  In other words, raising a
dipole from 40 ft to 70 ft is worth 3 dB, going from there to 130 ft is good
for another 5.5 dB.

These numbers come from comparing radiation at 10 degrees elevation. In
general, the differences are pretty consistent from 15 degrees down to 1
degree. That is, if the advantage of height is 3 dB at 10 degrees, it's also
about 3 dB from 1 degree to at least 15 degrees.

A good way to think about this is to realize that a low dipole (less than a
quarter wave) is a cloud-warmer that will work pretty well for short
distances, while the closer the antenna gets to a half-wave, the more the
radiation is concentrated at the low angles we need to work most DX.

Obviously, these height numbers can be scaled to 40M by dividing by 2. 
That is, 65-70 ft is a close to ideal mounting height, and every 5 ft below
that loses 0.9 - 1 dB.

Think about this when you're deciding whether to use a beam or a high wire,
and/or how much the cost of a tower (or tree-climbers) is worth.  
A 2-el Yagi is good for about 3.5 dB, a 3rd element adds a dB or so. 
That 2-el Yagi at 40 ft is roughly equal to a dipole at 70 ft. Do both and
you're 6-7 dB better than the dipole at 40 ft. Raise an 80M dipole from 40
ft to 130 ft and add a reflector and your 100W radio yields 900W ERP.
