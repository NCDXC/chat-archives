On 1/17/2014 10:00 AM, Paul Ewing wrote:
This morning on 40 meters, I heard one ham telling another ham that he puts NoAlox anti-oxidant on his coax connections.
There's an interesting piece in QEX this month by W9UCW about the common failure of conventional wisdom to have any relationship to reality.
Two pieces of conventional wisdom that are dead wrong are 1) the oft-repeated statement that UHF connectors (PL259, SO239, etc.) are inherently lossy (I've seen numbers like 1dB per connector) thrown around), and that 2) the impedance discontinuity introduced by a UHF connector pair adds loss due to mismatch. Both are essentially false, but both are based upon a grain of truth.
The grain of truth for #1 is that there are lots of junk connectors sold into the ham and CB marketplace, and these connectors ARE lossy, they fall apart, go intermittent, etc. REAL PL259 connectors made by Amphenol are NOT lossy.
The grain of truth for #2 is that the UHF family of connectors date from the 1930s, when 30 MHz was considered a pretty high frequency, and the designers of the connector realized that electrical length of the connector splice wasn't great enough for the Zo of the connector to matter. Fast forward 80 years and we're working into the tens of GHz, and above about 1 GHz, the impedance discontinuity would matter a LOT.
When I made up cables for your Iraq trip, I spliced them all together with barrels -- a total of about 1,300 ft of very good coax with about 24 PL259s and 13 barrels -- and measured the loss by careful substitution using HP test gear. The loss at 500 MHz was a dB or so LESS than the data sheet value for 500 MHz.
I agree with others that a UHF junction that is wrench-tight and well-protected from moisture is plenty good enough. Wrench-tight is important -- it matters most for shielding effectiveness.
